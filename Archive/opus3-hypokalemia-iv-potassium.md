

**CASE TITLE**
Hypokalemia requiring IV potassium replacement in acute postoperative ileus.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 61-year-old woman is postoperative day two following an open right hemicolectomy for a cecal adenocarcinoma. Her past medical history includes well-controlled hypertension managed with hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily (held since admission), gastroesophageal reflux disease, and osteoarthritis. She has no history of renal disease or cardiac arrhythmia. Current postoperative orders include IV lactated Ringer's at 125 mL/hr, morphine PCA, ondansetron 4 mg IV every 6 hours as needed for nausea, and nasogastric tube to low intermittent suction for persistent postoperative ileus. She has had no oral intake since surgery. A Foley catheter is in place. The surgical team has written an order for potassium chloride 40 mEq IV in 250 mL normal saline to infuse over 2 hours via peripheral IV, with a repeat basic metabolic panel four hours after the infusion completes.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At the start of the day shift, the nurse assumes care. The patient reports diffuse abdominal bloating, mild nausea, and generalized weakness that she says is worse than yesterday. The nasogastric tube has drained 1,100 mL of bilious fluid over the past 12 hours. She has had no flatus or bowel movement. She appears fatigued and is lying still in bed. She reports mild burning at her peripheral IV site in the left forearm but says it has been there since last night.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline vital signs: T 37.2 °C, HR 96, BP 108/68, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Heart rhythm on bedside telemetry shows normal sinus rhythm with occasional premature atrial contractions. Abdomen is distended, firm, with hypoactive bowel sounds in all four quadrants. Nasogastric tube is patent and draining dark-green fluid. The left forearm peripheral IV site shows a 20-gauge catheter with mild erythema and tenderness along the vein proximal to the insertion site; no induration, no streak, no palpable cord. Skin turgor is slightly decreased. Mucous membranes are dry. Deep tendon reflexes are diminished bilaterally at the patellar and Achilles tendons. No muscle fasciculations. Foley catheter output over the past 8 hours is 280 mL (35 mL/hr average). The patient is alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Morning basic metabolic panel: sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 2.8 mEq/L, chloride 94 mEq/L, bicarbonate 30 mEq/L, BUN 28 mg/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, glucose 118 mg/dL, magnesium 1.4 mg/dL. Complete blood count: WBC 11,200/µL, hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL, platelets 198,000/µL. Morning ECG: normal sinus rhythm at 96 bpm, flattened T waves in leads II, III, aVF, and V4–V6, presence of U waves in V2–V3, ST segment within normal limits, QTc 488 ms.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 30 minutes after the nurse receives the potassium order. The nurse reviews the order for potassium chloride 40 mEq in 250 mL NS over 2 hours via peripheral IV. The nurse recognizes this concentration and rate exceed the facility's peripheral-IV policy, holds the infusion, and contacts the provider/pharmacist. The order is revised per facility protocol to an approved peripheral concentration and rate. Before proceeding, the nurse inspects the existing left forearm peripheral IV site and notes the erythema and tenderness described above. The patient winces when the nurse palpates proximal to the catheter. The nurse also notes the low magnesium of 1.4 mg/dL on the morning labs and the urine output of 35 mL/hr. Telemetry continues to show sinus rhythm with occasional PACs and the flattened T waves and U waves documented on the morning ECG. No new arrhythmias have occurred.

**Stage 2** — 1 to 3 hours into the shift. The nurse has restarted a new 20-gauge peripheral IV in the right antecubital fossa, confirmed adequate urine output, and initiated the revised potassium chloride infusion on an IV pump at the approved peripheral rate. Fifteen minutes into the infusion, the patient reports burning pain at the new IV site rated 5/10. The infusion pump is running without alarms. Vital signs: HR 92, BP 112/70, RR 16, SpO₂ 97%. Telemetry is unchanged. The nurse assesses the right antecubital IV site: no redness, no swelling, no leaking, vein is intact, blood return is present. The patient says the burning is "going up my arm." The nurse slows the infusion rate per policy and the burning decreases to 2/10 but does not resolve. Meanwhile, the surgical team has been notified of the low magnesium and has ordered magnesium sulfate 2 g IV in 100 mL NS over 2 hours. The nurse must decide how to manage the peripheral infusion discomfort and whether the current IV access is adequate for both infusions, and must determine the correct sequence and monitoring for the two electrolyte replacements.

**Stage 3** — 5 to 6 hours into the shift. The potassium infusion has been completed. The magnesium infusion is finishing. Repeat basic metabolic panel drawn four hours after the potassium infusion: sodium 140 mEq/L, potassium 3.3 mEq/L, chloride 97 mEq/L, bicarbonate 28 mEq/L, BUN 26 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, magnesium 1.8 mg/dL. Repeat ECG: normal sinus rhythm at 84 bpm, T waves slightly improved in amplitude in leads II, III, aVF, and V4–V6, U waves still faintly present in V2, QTc 462 ms. Vital signs: HR 84, BP 118/72, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Urine output over the past 4 hours is 160 mL (40 mL/hr). The patient reports the generalized weakness is mildly improved but still present. Deep tendon reflexes remain diminished. Telemetry shows sinus rhythm without PACs. The nasogastric tube has drained an additional 600 mL. The patient has had no oral intake.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* Before initiating the potassium infusion at Stage 1, the nurse must evaluate whether the existing left forearm peripheral IV site is appropriate for a concentrated potassium infusion. The site shows erythema, tenderness, and pain on palpation. *Correct action:* The nurse should not infuse potassium through this compromised IV site. The catheter must be removed and a new peripheral IV established in a different location with confirmed patency and blood return before starting the infusion. *Why:* Potassium chloride is a high-alert, vein-irritating/hypertonic medication that can cause serious tissue injury with extravasation. An already-irritated vein with signs of early phlebitis increases the risk of infiltration and extravasation. Confirming vascular integrity before infusing a high-alert medication is a fundamental safety check. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse reviews the morning labs and notes both the potassium of 2.8 mEq/L and the magnesium of 1.4 mg/dL. The nurse must determine whether the potassium replacement alone will be sufficient to correct the hypokalemia. *Correct action:* The nurse should notify the provider that the magnesium is low and advocate for concurrent magnesium replacement. Hypokalemia is refractory to potassium replacement when hypomagnesemia is present and uncorrected. *Why:* Magnesium is required for the normal function of the renal outer medullary potassium (ROMK) channel; when magnesium is depleted, renal potassium wasting continues regardless of how much potassium is administered. Correcting magnesium is essential to correcting and maintaining the potassium level. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* Before starting the potassium infusion at Stage 1, the nurse must verify that the ordered concentration and rate are within safe parameters for peripheral IV administration and must confirm that a critical safety rule about potassium administration is not violated. *Correct action:* The nurse confirms that for bedside nursing/NCLEX purposes, potassium chloride is never administered by IV push or IV bolus. The ordered concentration of 40 mEq in 250 mL (160 mEq/L) exceeds the generally recommended maximum peripheral concentration of 10 mEq/100 mL (or 80 mEq/L in many facilities), and the rate of 20 mEq/hr is at the upper limit for peripheral infusion. The nurse should verify this concentration against facility policy and, if it exceeds the approved peripheral limit, request either further dilution, a slower rate, or central venous access. At minimum, continuous cardiac monitoring must be confirmed before starting any IV potassium infusion at 10 mEq/hr or greater. *Why:* Rapid or concentrated IV potassium can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia, including ventricular fibrillation and asystole. The absolute prohibition on IV push potassium exists because a bolus delivers a lethal concentration to the myocardium. Peripheral veins also tolerate lower concentrations than central veins; higher concentrations cause chemical phlebitis and increase the severity of extravasation injury. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient reports burning pain traveling up the arm during the potassium infusion despite a patent IV with blood return and no visible infiltration. The nurse must decide the appropriate intervention. *Correct action:* The nurse should first pause or slow per policy, assess the site carefully, verify patency, and notify provider/pharmacy if pain persists. The nurse should consider applying a warm pack to the site to promote vasodilation and reduce venous irritation, and should contact the provider to discuss further dilution of the potassium, switching to a larger-bore or more proximal vein, or placing a central venous catheter if repeated infusions will be needed. The nurse should not discontinue the infusion entirely without a plan to complete the replacement, given the symptomatic hypokalemia with ECG changes. *Why:* Burning during peripheral potassium infusion is common and results from the chemical irritation of the vein intima by the potassium concentration. It does not necessarily indicate infiltration when the site is intact with blood return. Slowing the rate decreases the concentration of potassium contacting the vein wall per unit time, and warmth dilates the vein, increasing blood flow around the infusate. However, persistent pain requires reassessment and possible route change to prevent phlebitis and ensure the full dose is delivered. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse has both a potassium chloride infusion and a magnesium sulfate infusion to administer. The nurse must determine the correct monitoring requirements and whether both can run simultaneously through the same peripheral line. *Correct action:* The nurse should not piggyback magnesium sulfate into the same line as the potassium chloride without verifying compatibility, and should ideally run them through separate IV access to allow independent rate control. Continuous telemetry monitoring must remain in place throughout both infusions. The nurse should monitor deep tendon reflexes before and during the magnesium infusion (loss of patellar reflex is an early sign of magnesium toxicity), monitor respiratory rate (magnesium toxicity depresses respirations), and continue to monitor urine output (both electrolytes require adequate renal function for safe excretion of any excess). *Why:* Both potassium and magnesium affect cardiac conduction. Simultaneous rapid infusion without independent rate control could produce additive cardiac depression. Monitoring deep tendon reflexes during magnesium infusion is the bedside safety check for hypermagnesemia, and respiratory depression is the most dangerous consequence of magnesium toxicity. Adequate urine output (at least 0.5 mL/kg/hr) must be confirmed before and during IV potassium and magnesium replacement because impaired renal excretion increases the risk of hyperkalemia and hypermagnesemia. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the repeat potassium is 3.3 mEq/L (up from 2.8), the repeat ECG shows slight T-wave improvement but persistent U waves and a QTc of 462 ms (down from 488 ms), and the patient's weakness is only mildly improved. The nurse must evaluate whether the response is adequate or whether further intervention is needed. *Correct action:* The nurse should recognize that the potassium has improved but remains below normal (3.5–5.0 mEq/L), the ECG has partially but not fully normalized, and the patient remains symptomatic. The nurse should anticipate that additional potassium replacement will be needed, notify the provider of the repeat results, and advocate for continued telemetry monitoring and a follow-up potassium level after the next dose. The nurse should also note that the nasogastric suction continues to drain large volumes, representing an ongoing source of potassium and chloride loss that will require sustained replacement. *Why:* A single 40 mEq dose raises serum potassium by approximately 0.3–0.5 mEq/L in the absence of ongoing losses. This patient has continuous GI losses from nasogastric suction, which creates a persistent drain on potassium and contributes to metabolic alkalosis (reflected in the elevated bicarbonate), further driving potassium intracellularly. Partial ECG improvement confirms the replacement is working but is incomplete. Ongoing monitoring and replacement are required until the potassium normalizes, ECG changes resolve, and symptoms abate. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Infusing potassium through the compromised left forearm IV because it is still technically "in the vein" and the nurse wants to start the replacement quickly. This is unsafe because the signs of early phlebitis indicate vascular wall compromise, increasing the risk of extravasation of a high-alert, vein-irritating medication. Convenience does not override site integrity assessment for a high-alert drug.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Treating the hypokalemia without addressing the hypomagnesemia, assuming that potassium replacement alone will correct the deficit. This leads to refractory hypokalemia because ongoing renal potassium wasting cannot be stopped until magnesium is repleted. The nurse who does not flag the low magnesium misses a critical co-dependency.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Assuming that because the potassium order was written by a physician, the concentration and rate must be safe, and hanging the infusion without independent verification. Potassium chloride is a high-alert medication requiring independent double-check of dose, concentration, route, and rate. The nurse has an independent obligation to verify safety parameters regardless of who wrote the order.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Believing that IV push potassium might be acceptable in an urgent situation because the potassium is critically low. For bedside nursing/NCLEX purposes, potassium chloride is never administered IV push or IV bolus. A potassium of 2.8 mEq/L, while requiring prompt replacement, does not justify a route that can cause immediate cardiac arrest.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Discontinuing the potassium infusion entirely because of the burning sensation, without establishing an alternative plan to complete the replacement. While responding to patient discomfort is appropriate, abandoning the infusion leaves a symptomatic, ECG-confirmed hypokalemia untreated. The correct response is to address the discomfort (pause or slow per policy, assess the site carefully, verify patency, and notify provider/pharmacy if pain persists) while ensuring the potassium is still delivered.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Interpreting the burning as infiltration and removing the IV when the site shows no swelling, has blood return, and is infusing without resistance. This wastes functional access and delays treatment. Burning during peripheral potassium infusion is an expected chemical irritation, not necessarily infiltration, and should be managed with rate adjustment and comfort measures while maintaining vigilant site assessment.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Running both the potassium and magnesium infusions wide open through the same peripheral line to "get them in faster" because the patient is symptomatic. This eliminates independent rate control and risks additive cardiac conduction effects. Each infusion must be rate-controlled independently, and the combined cardiac effects require continuous telemetry.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the potassium rise from 2.8 to 3.3 mEq/L as adequate correction and not advocating for additional replacement, because the value "moved in the right direction." The potassium remains below normal, ECG changes persist, the patient is still symptomatic, and ongoing nasogastric losses guarantee continued depletion. Partial improvement is not resolution, and the nurse must communicate the need for continued replacement and monitoring.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

The learner should be able to assess peripheral IV site integrity and determine suitability before administering a high-alert, vein-irritating medication such as potassium chloride.

The learner should be able to identify the absolute contraindication of IV push potassium and verify that the ordered concentration and rate fall within safe parameters for the route of administration.

The learner should be able to recognize the relationship between hypomagnesemia and refractory hypokalemia and advocate for concurrent magnesium replacement.

The learner should be able to implement appropriate monitoring during IV potassium and magnesium infusions, including continuous telemetry, urine output assessment, respiratory rate, and deep tendon reflexes.

The learner should be able to evaluate the adequacy of electrolyte replacement by integrating repeat lab values, ECG changes, clinical symptoms, and ongoing sources of loss to determine whether further intervention is needed.
