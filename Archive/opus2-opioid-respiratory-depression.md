

**CASE TITLE**
Postoperative opioid-induced respiratory depression following open abdominal surgery.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old man with a history of obstructive sleep apnea (uses CPAP at home, brought his machine to the hospital), obesity (BMI 38), well-controlled hypertension on hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily, and gastroesophageal reflux disease on pantoprazole 40 mg daily underwent an open right hemicolectomy for a cecal mass 14 hours ago. Pathology is pending. He has no known drug allergies. His postoperative orders include morphine sulfate 4 mg IV every 3 hours as needed for moderate-to-severe pain, ondansetron 4 mg IV every 6 hours as needed for nausea, lactated Ringer's at 125 mL/hr, sequential compression devices bilaterally, incentive spirometry every 2 hours while awake, and continuous pulse oximetry. His CPAP is at the bedside but has not been applied since surgery because he has been mostly upright and awake. He received morphine 4 mg IV at 0200, 0500, and again at 0745 when he reported pain of 8 out of 10 with attempted repositioning. It is now 0830.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The night-shift nurse gives bedside handoff to the day-shift nurse at 0715. At that time the patient is awake, conversational, and rates his pain 6 out of 10 at rest. He is using his incentive spirometry independently. He tells the oncoming nurse that the morphine "takes the edge off but doesn't last." At 0745 he uses the call light requesting additional pain medication, reporting pain 8 out of 10 with movement. The night-shift nurse, still on the unit, administers the 0745 dose of morphine 4 mg IV before leaving. The day-shift nurse assumes full responsibility at 0800.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
At 0715 handoff: T 37.2 °C, HR 82, BP 138/84, RR 16, SpO₂ 95% on room air. Patient alert, oriented to person, place, time, and situation. Pupils 4 mm bilaterally, equal and reactive. Sedation score using the Pasero Opioid-Induced Sedation Scale (POSS): 1 (awake and alert). Abdominal dressing dry and intact with Jackson-Pratt drain containing 40 mL serosanguineous fluid. Bowel sounds absent. Foley catheter draining clear yellow urine, 280 mL over the past 4 hours. Sequential compression devices in place bilaterally, pedal pulses palpable.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Postoperative labs drawn at 0600: WBC 11,200/µL, hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL (preoperative 13.8 g/dL), hematocrit 34.2%, platelets 210,000/µL, sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 3.9 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, CO₂ 26 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, glucose 168 mg/dL, calcium 8.6 mg/dL. ABG not obtained at baseline. Coagulation studies within normal limits.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0830, 45 minutes after the third morphine dose. The day-shift nurse enters the room for a scheduled assessment and finds the patient slumped to one side in the bed with his eyes closed. He does not respond to the nurse saying his name at normal volume. When the nurse speaks loudly and applies a sternal rub, he opens his eyes briefly, mumbles "I'm fine," and drifts back to sleep within seconds. Vital signs: HR 68, BP 118/70, RR 8 and shallow, SpO₂ 89% on room air. Pupils are 2 mm bilaterally, constricted, but reactive. POSS score: 3 (somnolent, difficult to arouse). The pulse oximetry alarm is sounding. The IV of lactated Ringer's is infusing. The morphine PCA/PRN is not due again until 1045 but the patient's call light is within reach. The abdominal dressing remains dry. Jackson-Pratt drain output has not changed appreciably. Foley output over the past hour is 20 mL.

**Stage 2** — 0838, approximately 8 minutes after the nurse's initial findings. The nurse has repositioned the patient with the head of bed elevated to 30 degrees, applied supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula at 2 L/min, and performed continuous verbal and tactile stimulation. Despite these measures, the patient remains minimally responsive. He opens his eyes only to repeated loud verbal commands and does not follow instructions to take a deep breath. RR remains 7, SpO₂ has risen only to 91% on 2 L nasal cannula. HR 62, BP 108/66. POSS score remains 3. The nurse notes that the patient's respiratory effort is labored with audible snoring respirations and intermittent 6- to 8-second apneic pauses. The morphine was last administered 53 minutes ago. No additional opioids have been given. The rapid response team has been activated. The provider on call has been notified. Naloxone 0.4 mg/mL is available in the automated dispensing cabinet on the unit.

**Stage 3** — 0855, approximately 17 minutes after naloxone administration. The rapid response team arrived at 0842. Naloxone 0.04 mg (one-tenth of the 0.4 mg vial, diluted per protocol) was administered IV push at 0843, with a second dose of 0.04 mg given at 0846 when response was partial. By 0850 the patient is opening his eyes spontaneously, following commands, and taking deeper breaths when prompted. Vital signs at 0855: HR 78, BP 126/78, RR 14, SpO₂ 94% on 2 L nasal cannula. Pupils 3 mm bilaterally. POSS score: 2 (slightly drowsy, easy to arouse). He reports pain of 7 out of 10 at the surgical site and appears distressed. He asks, "Can I get something for this pain?" The rapid response team leader documents the event and discusses the plan with the primary surgical team. The morphine order is discontinued. The surgical team is considering multimodal analgesia alternatives. The naloxone duration of action is 30 to 90 minutes; the morphine administered at 0745 has an expected duration of 3 to 5 hours, meaning the opioid effect will likely outlast the naloxone.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse enters the room and encounters a patient who is difficult to arouse 45 minutes after an IV morphine dose. Multiple assessment findings are present: decreased responsiveness, RR 8, SpO₂ 89%, constricted pupils, decreased urine output, and dropping blood pressure compared to baseline. The nurse must determine which cluster of findings is most consistent with opioid-induced respiratory depression versus other postoperative complications such as hypovolemia, sepsis, or postoperative hemorrhage. *Correct action:* The nurse recognizes the constellation of somnolence with a POSS score of 3, respiratory rate of 8, oxygen desaturation to 89%, and bilateral miosis occurring within the expected peak window of IV morphine as classic opioid-induced respiratory depression. The temporal relationship to the third dose in 6.5 hours, combined with the patient's risk factors of obesity, obstructive sleep apnea, and age over 50, confirms the clinical picture. *Why it is correct:* Opioid-induced respiratory depression produces a characteristic triad of central nervous system depression, respiratory depression, and miosis. The timing after the third closely spaced morphine dose reflects drug accumulation. Obesity and obstructive sleep apnea independently increase sensitivity to opioid-mediated respiratory depression by reducing functional residual capacity and upper airway tone. Hypovolemic shock would present with tachycardia and would not explain miosis; sepsis would present with fever and tachycardia; hemorrhage would show tachycardia and changes in drain output or dressing. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse has identified opioid-induced respiratory depression. Multiple actions are possible: administer the next scheduled morphine dose if the patient requests it, reposition and stimulate the patient, apply oxygen, call the provider, activate rapid response, or administer naloxone. The nurse must determine the correct first actions and their sequence. *Correct action:* The nurse immediately withholds any further opioid administration, stimulates the patient with loud verbal and tactile stimulation, repositions with the head of bed elevated, and applies supplemental oxygen. These are the immediate bedside interventions that do not require an order and address airway, breathing, and circulation while the nurse simultaneously assesses the patient's response to stimulation. *Why it is correct:* Withholding the opioid prevents further respiratory depression. Stimulation and repositioning can temporarily increase respiratory drive and open the airway in a patient with obstructive sleep apnea. Supplemental oxygen addresses the hypoxemia. These are within independent nursing scope and must occur before escalation because they take seconds and may prevent further deterioration. Naloxone is not the first action at this point because the patient is still breathing, albeit inadequately, and the response to stimulation and oxygen has not yet been evaluated. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, despite repositioning, stimulation, and supplemental oxygen for 8 minutes, the patient remains minimally responsive with a respiratory rate of 7, apneic pauses, snoring respirations, and SpO₂ of only 91% on supplemental oxygen. The nurse must decide whether the current interventions are sufficient or whether escalation is required. *Correct action:* The nurse activates the rapid response team and notifies the provider immediately, recognizing that the patient has failed to respond to conservative measures and meets criteria for emergent intervention. The nurse prepares to administer naloxone by retrieving it from the automated dispensing cabinet so it is at the bedside when the rapid response team arrives or when a verbal or telephone order is obtained. *Why it is correct:* Failure to improve with stimulation and oxygen after several minutes indicates that the respiratory depression is too severe for conservative management alone. The apneic pauses and snoring respirations indicate upper airway obstruction compounding the central respiratory depression. Rapid response activation brings additional personnel, monitoring equipment, and prescriptive authority to the bedside. Preparing naloxone in advance prevents delay once the order is obtained. In many facilities, standing protocols or emergency algorithms authorize the RN to administer naloxone for opioid-induced respiratory depression with a respiratory rate below 8 or unresponsiveness; the nurse should follow the facility's protocol. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, naloxone is available in a concentration of 0.4 mg/mL. The nurse must determine the appropriate dose and route for a patient with opioid-induced respiratory depression who is postoperative and in pain. *Correct action:* The nurse anticipates or administers naloxone in small, titrated IV doses — 0.04 mg (one-tenth of the vial, diluted with 9 mL normal saline to yield 0.04 mg/mL) — repeated every 2 to 3 minutes until respiratory rate and level of consciousness improve, rather than administering the full 0.4 mg as a single bolus. *Why it is correct:* The goal of naloxone in a postoperative patient is to reverse the respiratory depression without completely reversing analgesia. A full 0.4 mg bolus in an opioid-dependent postoperative patient can precipitate acute pain crisis, severe sympathetic surge (hypertension, tachycardia, dysrhythmia), nausea and vomiting with aspiration risk in a fresh abdominal surgical patient, and potential wound dehiscence from sudden abdominal muscle contraction. Titrated low-dose administration allows the nurse to find the minimum effective dose that restores adequate ventilation while preserving some degree of pain control. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient has responded to two doses of naloxone 0.04 mg IV. His respiratory rate is 14, SpO₂ 94%, and he is awake and following commands. He is now reporting pain of 7 out of 10 and requesting analgesia. The morphine has been discontinued. The nurse must determine the priority concern for the next 60 to 90 minutes. *Correct action:* The nurse recognizes that the naloxone duration of action (30 to 90 minutes) is shorter than the remaining duration of the morphine administered at 0745 (which has an expected effect lasting 3 to 5 hours), meaning the patient is at risk for recurrent respiratory depression — renarcotization — once the naloxone wears off. The nurse maintains continuous monitoring of respiratory rate, SpO₂, and sedation level at least every 5 to 15 minutes for a minimum of 2 hours, keeps the remaining naloxone at the bedside, and does not administer any opioid analgesic even though the patient is requesting pain relief. *Why it is correct:* Renarcotization is the most dangerous period after naloxone reversal because the clinical team may perceive the crisis as resolved while the opioid is still active and the antagonist is wearing off. The morphine's duration exceeds the naloxone's duration by hours. Administering another opioid during this window would compound the risk. Non-opioid multimodal analgesia (IV acetaminophen, ice, repositioning, consideration of ketorolac if not contraindicated by the surgical team) can be offered for pain while opioids are held. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* Across the case, the nurse must consider which patient-specific risk factors contributed to this event and should be communicated during handoff, documentation, and discharge planning to prevent recurrence. *Correct action:* The nurse identifies and documents that this patient has at least four independent risk factors for opioid-induced respiratory depression — obstructive sleep apnea, obesity with BMI 38, age over 50, and receipt of three doses of IV morphine within 6.5 hours — and communicates these to the surgical team with a recommendation to reassess the analgesic plan. The nurse also ensures the CPAP machine is applied when the patient is sleeping once he is stable, as the obstructive sleep apnea was untreated during the postoperative period. *Why it is correct:* Opioid-induced respiratory depression is often multifactorial. Identifying modifiable contributors (closely spaced dosing, untreated OSA) and non-modifiable risk factors (age, BMI) allows the care team to adjust the analgesic regimen — such as using multimodal analgesia with reduced opioid doses, extending dosing intervals, or adding non-pharmacological interventions — and to ensure appropriate monitoring intensity. Failure to apply CPAP in a patient with known OSA receiving opioids removes a protective mechanism against upper airway obstruction. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1 data (risk factors) combined with Stage 3 data (event resolution and planning).

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse attributes the decreased responsiveness and low blood pressure to postoperative hypovolemia or fatigue and increases the IV fluid rate rather than recognizing the opioid toxicity pattern. This is wrong because the miosis, temporal relationship to morphine dosing, and respiratory depression pattern are specific to opioid effect; hypovolemia would produce tachycardia, not bradycardia with miosis, and increasing fluids without addressing the respiratory depression delays life-saving intervention.

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse notes the low SpO₂ but attributes it to the patient's baseline obstructive sleep apnea and simply applies the CPAP machine without further assessment. This is wrong because while OSA contributes to the clinical picture, the acute change in level of consciousness, respiratory rate of 8, and miosis represent a new and dangerous opioid-mediated process that CPAP alone will not reverse; positive pressure ventilation does not address central respiratory depression.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The patient had previously reported pain of 8 out of 10, and the nurse reasons that the sedation may indicate the pain is now controlled, so the nurse documents the findings and plans to reassess in 30 minutes rather than intervening immediately. This is wrong because a POSS score of 3 with a respiratory rate of 8 and SpO₂ of 89% constitutes a respiratory emergency requiring immediate action; delaying intervention to "watch and wait" allows further respiratory depression that could progress to apnea and cardiac arrest.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse increases the oxygen flow rate to 4 L/min and continues to stimulate the patient rather than activating the rapid response team, reasoning that the SpO₂ improved slightly from 89% to 91%. This is wrong because supplemental oxygen may temporarily mask worsening hypoventilation by maintaining SpO₂ while carbon dioxide continues to rise; the persistent somnolence, apneic pauses, and failure to sustain wakefulness indicate the patient needs pharmacologic reversal and a higher level of assessment, not just more oxygen.

Attached to Decision Point 4: The nurse administers the full 0.4 mg vial of naloxone as a rapid IV bolus to ensure the fastest possible reversal. This is wrong because a full-dose bolus in a postoperative patient abruptly reverses all analgesia, precipitating severe pain, sympathetic surge with hypertension and tachycardia, vomiting with aspiration risk in a patient with a fresh abdominal incision, and potential wound dehiscence. Titrated dosing achieves respiratory reversal while minimizing these complications.

Attached to Decision Point 5: After the patient responds to naloxone and appears alert with improved vital signs, the nurse discontinues continuous monitoring and resumes routine every-4-hour assessments, reasoning that the crisis is resolved. This is wrong because the naloxone will wear off in 30 to 90 minutes while the morphine effect persists for several more hours; without frequent reassessment, renarcotization can occur undetected and result in respiratory arrest.

Attached to Decision Point 5: The patient is in pain and distressed after naloxone administration, and the nurse administers a half-dose of morphine (2 mg IV) reasoning that a smaller dose will be safe now that the patient is awake. This is wrong because any additional opioid during the naloxone monitoring window compounds the opioid load that is already present and will re-emerge as the naloxone wears off; the appropriate action is to use non-opioid multimodal analgesia and await the surgical team's revised analgesic orders.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Recognize the clinical triad of opioid-induced respiratory depression — decreased level of consciousness, respiratory depression, and miosis — and distinguish it from other causes of postoperative deterioration.
- Identify patient-specific risk factors that increase susceptibility to opioid-induced respiratory depression, including obstructive sleep apnea, obesity, advanced age, and cumulative opioid dosing.
- Sequence the immediate nursing response to opioid oversedation: withhold further opioids, stimulate, reposition, apply oxygen, assess response, and escalate to rapid response and naloxone administration when conservative measures fail.
- Apply the principle of titrated naloxone dosing in a postoperative patient to reverse respiratory depression while minimizing the risks of acute pain crisis, sympathetic surge, and aspiration.
- Anticipate and monitor for renarcotization based on the comparative durations of action of naloxone and the offending opioid, maintaining vigilant reassessment after initial reversal.
