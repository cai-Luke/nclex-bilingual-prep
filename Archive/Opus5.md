**CASE TITLE**
Clostridioides difficile colitis in an immunocompromised older adult following broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Mrs. Dolores Fuentes is a 74-year-old woman currently on hospital day 14 of an admission that began with community-acquired influenza A complicated by hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP) diagnosed on hospital day 6. She was treated with a 10-day course of piperacillin-tazobactam (completed two days ago) and a concurrent 7-day course of intravenous levofloxacin (completed five days ago) for the HAP, which was caused by Pseudomonas aeruginosa isolated from a sputum culture. The pneumonia responded well to treatment — her most recent chest X-ray shows resolving bilateral infiltrates, and she has been weaned from supplemental oxygen to room air. She remains on the medical-surgical unit pending continued recovery and functional reconditioning. Her past medical history includes rheumatoid arthritis managed with methotrexate 15 mg weekly and prednisone 7.5 mg daily (she has been on chronic low-dose prednisone for three years), hypertension managed with amlodipine 10 mg daily, gastroesophageal reflux managed with omeprazole 20 mg daily, osteoporosis managed with calcium and vitamin D supplementation, and mild chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 1.1 mg/dL, eGFR 54 mL/min). She has no documented drug allergies. She received pantoprazole 40 mg IV daily during the acute pneumonia phase, which was transitioned back to her home omeprazole three days ago. She lives with her husband in a single-story home. Her functional status prior to admission was independent for all activities of daily living, but she has deconditioned significantly during this prolonged hospitalization and currently requires a rolling walker and standby assistance for ambulation. Physical therapy has been seeing her daily.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The day-shift nurse receives Mrs. Fuentes at 0700 on hospital day 14. The night nurse reports that the patient had five loose, watery stools between 2200 and 0600, with the frequency increasing through the night. The patient did not have diarrhea prior to last evening. Mrs. Fuentes reports diffuse abdominal cramping that worsens before each bowel movement, generalized malaise, and poor appetite since yesterday. She describes the stool as watery, greenish-brown, and foul-smelling. She denies visible blood in the stool. She says she "just can't stop going" and looks fatigued and uncomfortable.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (0700): Temperature 38.1 °C (new low-grade fever — she has been afebrile for four days since the pneumonia resolved), heart rate 94 and regular, blood pressure 108/62 (lower than her recent trend of 124/74), respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation, but notably more fatigued than yesterday. Skin warm, dry, with decreased turgor — tenting at the dorsum of the hand persists for 3 seconds. Mucous membranes dry. Lips cracked. Abdomen distended, diffusely tender to light palpation with no localized peritoneal signs (no guarding, no rebound tenderness, no rigidity). Hyperactive bowel sounds in all four quadrants. No palpable masses. Perianal skin intact but erythematous and moist — consistent with irritation from frequent watery stools. Stool in the bedside commode is liquid, greenish-brown, with a characteristic acrid odor distinct from typical stool. No visible blood or melena. Peripheral IV in the left forearm, 20-gauge, site clean without signs of infiltration or phlebitis. The patient has a three-day-old midline IV (PICC was removed after antibiotics completed). The nurse notes: the patient is an older adult, immunocompromised (chronic prednisone and methotrexate), recently completed two broad-spectrum antibiotics (one anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam and one fluoroquinolone — both high-risk for Clostridioides difficile infection), is on a proton pump inhibitor (an independent risk factor for CDI), has been hospitalized for 14 days (prolonged exposure to the healthcare environment), and now presents with new-onset watery diarrhea, fever, and abdominal distension. The findings to track across stages are stool frequency and character, temperature, white blood cell count, abdominal examination (distension, tenderness, peritoneal signs — escalation to toxic megacolon is the feared complication), heart rate and blood pressure (hydration and hemodynamic status), and renal function (dehydration risk in a patient with baseline CKD).

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn at 0600 as part of routine morning labs): WBC 21,400/µL with 84% neutrophils and 6% bands (leukocytosis with left shift — her WBC was 8,200 two days ago when the pneumonia was resolving; this abrupt rise is a new finding), hemoglobin 11.6 g/dL, platelets 204,000/µL, sodium 136 mEq/L, potassium 3.2 mEq/L (low — likely from diarrheal losses), chloride 98 mEq/L, bicarbonate 20 mEq/L (mildly low — metabolic acidosis from bicarbonate loss in diarrhea), BUN 28 mg/dL, creatinine 1.4 mg/dL (up from baseline 1.1 — pre-renal component from dehydration), eGFR 38 mL/min (decreased), glucose 142 mg/dL, lactate 1.6 mmol/L (mildly elevated), albumin 2.4 g/dL (decreased — chronic illness, poor nutrition, and protein-losing enteropathy), CRP 68 mg/L (elevated, new inflammatory marker). Stool studies have not yet been ordered. No blood cultures have been drawn. The WBC trajectory (8,200 → 21,400 over 48 hours), the creatinine trajectory (1.1 → 1.4), and the potassium trajectory (was 4.1 two days ago, now 3.2) are the critical trend values.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Hospital day 14, 0700 to 1200 (recognition of CDI risk, initial interventions, specimen collection). The nurse synthesizes the clinical picture at 0700 and identifies a high-probability Clostridioides difficile infection based on the convergence of multiple risk factors and the classic presentation. She does not wait for a provider order to initiate infection-control measures. Her actions in the first 60 minutes proceed as follows:

She immediately implements contact precautions, placing the patient in a single room (Mrs. Fuentes is already in a private room), posting a contact-precautions sign on the door, and donning gown and gloves before all patient contact. She notifies the charge nurse that she suspects CDI and requests that a dedicated bedside commode, blood pressure cuff, thermometer, and stethoscope be placed in the room and not shared with other patients. She recognizes that alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR) is ineffective against C. difficile spores and begins performing hand hygiene with soap and water on every room exit — she also alerts the nursing assistant and any other staff entering the room that soap-and-water handwashing is mandatory, not optional, for this patient.

She reviews the medication administration record and identifies loperamide 2 mg PO PRN, which was ordered five days ago for general symptom management when the patient reported mild loose stools (attributed at the time to antibiotic-associated diarrhea without CDI features). The nurse does not administer the loperamide. She recognizes that antimotility agents are contraindicated in suspected CDI because they slow intestinal transit, preventing the clearance of toxin-producing organisms from the colon, and may precipitate toxic megacolon — a life-threatening complication of CDI characterized by colonic dilation, systemic toxicity, and risk of perforation. She flags the order for provider discontinuation and documents her clinical reasoning for holding it.

She contacts the provider (the medical-surgical attending, Dr. Okafor) at 0730 via page and communicates using SBAR: "Mrs. Fuentes, 74, hospital day 14, immunocompromised on prednisone and methotrexate, completed piperacillin-tazobactam two days ago and levofloxacin five days ago. She developed eight watery, foul-smelling stools since last evening, new fever 38.1, WBC jumped from 8,200 to 21,400, potassium 3.2, creatinine up from 1.1 to 1.4, abdomen distended and diffusely tender without peritoneal signs. I suspect C. difficile infection. I have initiated contact precautions, held the loperamide, and I am requesting orders for stool C. difficile PCR, IV fluid resuscitation, potassium replacement, and empiric CDI treatment." Dr. Okafor agrees with the nurse's clinical reasoning and enters the following orders: stool specimen for C. difficile toxin PCR (nucleic acid amplification test), IV normal saline 250 mL bolus followed by maintenance at 125 mL/hr, potassium chloride 40 mEq IV in 100 mL over 4 hours (replace via IV due to GI losses and unreliable oral absorption), hold loperamide (confirmed), hold methotrexate (immunosuppressive — increases risk of severe CDI and impairs immune response; rheumatology to be notified), continue prednisone 7.5 mg daily (cannot abruptly discontinue chronic corticosteroid — adrenal suppression risk), blood cultures x2 if temperature exceeds 38.5 °C, and begin empiric oral vancomycin 125 mg four times daily pending PCR results (per IDSA/SHEA guidelines, empiric treatment is appropriate when clinical suspicion is high and the patient is symptomatic).

The nurse collects the stool specimen at 0815. She selects a liquid stool sample (only unformed stool should be tested — formed stool should not be submitted for C. difficile testing because it does not meet the clinical definition of diarrhea, and testing asymptomatic carriers produces false clinical positives). She labels the specimen, sends it to the laboratory, and documents the time of collection. She administers the IV potassium and normal saline bolus. She administers the first dose of oral vancomycin 125 mg at 0900.

By 1000, the patient has had two additional watery stools (total 10 since 2200). Vital signs: temperature 38.3 °C, heart rate 98, blood pressure 112/66, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96%. Abdominal distension unchanged; tenderness remains diffuse without peritoneal signs. The nurse continues strict intake and output, documents stool frequency and characteristics on a dedicated stool chart, applies a moisture barrier cream to the perianal skin, and offers clear liquids to encourage oral hydration. She pages physical therapy to notify them that the patient is on contact precautions — PT will need to gown and glove, use dedicated equipment in the room, and perform hand hygiene with soap and water. She contacts environmental services to request enhanced room cleaning with a sporicidal agent (sodium hypochlorite–based disinfectant or an EPA-registered sporicidal product) rather than the standard quaternary ammonium disinfectant used for routine cleaning. She explains to the environmental services coordinator that standard hospital disinfectants do not kill C. difficile spores and that a bleach-based or sporicidal product is required for all high-touch surfaces, the bathroom, the bedside commode, and the floor.

**Stage 2** — Hospital day 15, 0700 to 1600 (CDI confirmed, full isolation bundle, treatment monitoring). The C. difficile toxin PCR result returns positive at 0200 overnight. The night nurse verified the result and continued contact precautions. The day nurse receives the patient at 0700 and reviews the confirmed diagnosis. Mrs. Fuentes reports that she had six watery stools overnight (down from eight the previous night), still with cramping and the same acrid odor, but she feels "a tiny bit less miserable." She was able to eat crackers and drink ginger ale.

Vital signs at 0700: temperature 37.8 °C (down from 38.3), heart rate 88 (improving), blood pressure 118/70 (improving with hydration), respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 97%. Morning labs: WBC 18,600/µL (down from 21,400 — trending in the correct direction), potassium 3.6 mEq/L (improving with replacement), creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (improving), BUN 24 mg/dL, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L (normalizing), lactate 1.2 mmol/L (normalizing), albumin 2.3 g/dL (stable-low). Abdominal examination: distension mildly improved, tenderness diffuse but less severe than yesterday, bowel sounds active but less hyperactive, no peritoneal signs. Perianal skin erythematous with one small area of superficial denudation at the 6 o'clock position — the nurse applies a zinc oxide barrier cream and documents the skin breakdown.

The nurse now implements the full contact-isolation bundle with confirmed CDI, verifying that each element is in place and educating all staff and visitors who enter the room:

Gown and gloves for every entry into the room, regardless of whether the entrant plans to touch the patient — C. difficile spores are shed onto environmental surfaces and can be transmitted by contact with contaminated surfaces, not only direct patient contact. The nurse explains this to Mrs. Fuentes's husband, who visits daily, and provides him with gown-and-glove instruction plus the rationale. She explains that he should not sit on the bed, should avoid touching surfaces unnecessarily, and must wash his hands with soap and water before leaving the room.

Soap-and-water handwashing for every room exit by every person — the nurse posts a sign inside the room at the sink reinforcing this requirement and explains the rationale both to the patient and to a nursing student who is on the unit today. She explains: C. difficile exists in two forms — the vegetative (active, toxin-producing) form, which is killed by alcohol-based hand rub, and the spore form, which is not. C. difficile spores have a tough outer coat that is resistant to alcohol, most standard disinfectants, and even high temperatures. Spores are the transmissible form — they are shed in stool, contaminate skin and surfaces, and can persist in the environment for months. Alcohol-based hand rub does not disrupt the spore coat and therefore does not remove spores from hands. Only the mechanical friction of handwashing with soap and water physically dislodges and rinses away spores. This is the single most important distinction in CDI infection control and the reason ABHR is insufficient.

Sporicidal environmental cleaning — the nurse confirms with environmental services that the room is flagged for bleach-based disinfection (minimum 1,000 ppm sodium hypochlorite solution or an EPA-registered sporicidal product) at least twice daily and upon discharge. She explains the rationale: quaternary ammonium compounds ("quats") and phenolic disinfectants, which are the standard agents used for routine room cleaning, do not kill C. difficile spores. Only oxidizing agents — primarily hypochlorite (bleach) or hydrogen peroxide–based sporicidal systems — penetrate the spore coat and achieve sporicidal kill. The nurse verifies that the bathroom, bedside commode, bed rails, call light, overbed table, IV pole, doorknobs, and light switches are included in the twice-daily sporicidal cleaning protocol.

Dedicated patient-care equipment — the nurse confirms that the stethoscope, blood pressure cuff, thermometer, and pulse oximeter remain in the room and are disinfected with sporicidal wipes between uses. No equipment leaves the room for use with other patients.

The nurse continues to administer oral vancomycin 125 mg four times daily. She recognizes and can explain why the oral route is essential for CDI treatment: C. difficile infects the colonic mucosa and produces toxins A and B within the intestinal lumen. The treatment must achieve high intraluminal (within-the-colon) drug concentrations. Oral vancomycin is not significantly absorbed from the GI tract — it passes through the stomach and small bowel to reach the colon at high concentrations, where it kills vegetative C. difficile directly. By contrast, IV vancomycin is absorbed systemically and distributed via the bloodstream; it does not achieve meaningful concentrations in the colonic lumen because it is not secreted back into the gut in therapeutic amounts. Therefore, IV vancomycin is ineffective for CDI even though it is the drug of choice for systemic MRSA infections. This pharmacokinetic distinction — the need for intraluminal rather than systemic delivery — is the reason oral vancomycin (or fidaxomicin, an alternative oral agent) is the standard of care for CDI, and IV vancomycin has no role. The nurse would also understand that in patients who cannot take oral medications (e.g., ileus, toxic megacolon, post-colectomy), IV metronidazole is used as an adjunct because it does achieve some colonic secretion, and rectal vancomycin enemas may be considered — but these are exceptional circumstances.

At 1100, the nurse evaluates the CDI severity classification. The patient met severe CDI criteria at onset based on WBC >15,000, but she has no fulminant features and is clinically improving on oral therapy. The nurse notes that per the 2021 IDSA/SHEA guidelines, a WBC above 15,000 or a creatinine rise above 1.5 mg/dL are markers of severe CDI. Mrs. Fuentes's WBC on admission to the CDI episode was 21,400 (now trending down to 18,600), and her creatinine peaked at 1.4 (now 1.3). The nurse documents these severity markers and communicates them to Dr. Okafor, noting that the patient met severity criteria at onset (WBC greater than 15,000) but is improving. Dr. Okafor confirms that oral vancomycin 125 mg four times daily remains appropriate — if the WBC had continued to rise or if hypotension, ileus, or megacolon had developed, the classification would shift to fulminant CDI requiring a higher vancomycin dose (500 mg four times daily), IV metronidazole, and possible surgical consultation.

Throughout the day, the nurse monitors for signs of clinical deterioration that would indicate progression to fulminant CDI or toxic megacolon: worsening abdominal distension, increasing abdominal tenderness progressing to peritoneal signs (guarding, rigidity, rebound tenderness), absent bowel sounds (suggesting ileus), high fever (above 38.5 °C), hemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension), rising WBC (especially above 30,000 — an ominous sign), rising lactate, altered mental status, or new cessation of diarrhea without clinical improvement (paradoxical cessation of stool in the setting of worsening vital signs suggests colonic dilation and ileus, not resolution). None of these are present — the patient is gradually improving.

By 1600, Mrs. Fuentes has had four watery stools since 0700 (decreased from six overnight and eight the night before). She tolerated a soft diet at lunch. Vital signs: temperature 37.4 °C, heart rate 82, blood pressure 122/72, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 97%.

**Stage 3** — Hospital days 17–18 (clinical improvement, discharge preparation, patient education). By hospital day 17 (three full days of oral vancomycin therapy), Mrs. Fuentes has improved significantly. She reports two to three semi-formed stools per day (markedly improved from the peak of ten watery stools). No further cramping. Appetite returning. She is ambulatory with a rolling walker and standby assistance — PT has cleared her for discharge with home PT services.

Vital signs at 0700 on day 17: temperature 36.8 °C (afebrile for 48 hours), heart rate 76, blood pressure 126/74, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Labs (day 17): WBC 11,200/µL (normalized from the peak of 21,400), potassium 4.0 mEq/L (normalized), creatinine 1.1 mg/dL (returned to baseline), BUN 18 mg/dL, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L (normalized), lactate 0.8 mmol/L (normal), albumin 2.6 g/dL (slightly improved). Abdominal examination: soft, nontender, nondistended, normoactive bowel sounds. Perianal skin intact — the superficial denudation has re-epithelialized with barrier cream use.

The provider has written a discharge order for hospital day 18, pending completion of discharge teaching. Oral vancomycin will continue for a total of 10 days — the patient has completed 4 days inpatient and will complete the remaining 6 days at home. The methotrexate remains held pending rheumatology follow-up (to be resumed only after the CDI course is completed and stool has normalized — rheumatology has been consulted and agrees). Prednisone continues at 7.5 mg daily without change. All other home medications resume. The nurse does not order a repeat C. difficile PCR test to confirm "cure" — she recognizes that a test of cure is explicitly not recommended because the PCR may remain positive for weeks after successful treatment due to persistent spore shedding or residual DNA, and a positive result in an asymptomatic patient would not indicate active infection or require additional treatment. Testing should only be performed if symptoms recur.

The nurse conducts a comprehensive discharge education session with Mrs. Fuentes and her husband on hospital day 17 afternoon, using teach-back methodology. The topics covered, with confirmed patient understanding, include:

Completing the antibiotic course: oral vancomycin 125 mg four times daily for 6 more days, taken at evenly spaced intervals (approximately every 6 hours). The nurse ensures the patient and husband understand that the full course must be completed even though she feels better — stopping early increases the risk of recurrence. She provides a calendar card with dose times marked. She explains that this vancomycin is an oral medication specifically for the gut infection and is different from the IV vancomycin used for bloodstream infections — it works inside the intestine and is not absorbed into the body. She instructs the patient to store the oral vancomycin capsules at room temperature and to take them with or without food.

CDI recurrence risk: the nurse explains that recurrence occurs in approximately 15–25% of initial CDI episodes, and Mrs. Fuentes's risk factors for recurrence are substantial — her age (above 65), her immunosuppression (prednisone, methotrexate), her continued proton pump inhibitor use (omeprazole), and the severity of her initial episode. She teaches the patient and husband the specific signs and symptoms of recurrence: return of watery diarrhea (three or more unformed stools in 24 hours), abdominal cramping, fever, or blood in the stool. She explains that recurrence most commonly occurs within 2–8 weeks of completing treatment. She instructs them to contact the provider immediately if any of these symptoms develop — not to wait, and not to self-treat with leftover medications.

When to seek emergency care: the nurse teaches the danger signs that require immediate emergency department evaluation — severe abdominal pain with distension, inability to pass stool combined with worsening abdominal pain (possible toxic megacolon), high fever (above 101.5 °F / 38.6 °C), bloody diarrhea, dizziness or fainting (dehydration or hemorrhage), and confusion or lethargy.

Infection control at home: the nurse teaches that C. difficile spores can be shed in stool even after symptoms resolve and can survive on surfaces for months. She recommends: (a) thorough handwashing with soap and water after every bathroom use (alcohol-based hand sanitizer is insufficient, as they have now learned), (b) cleaning the bathroom — especially the toilet, sink handles, and frequently touched surfaces — with a dilute bleach solution (1 part household bleach to 9 parts water, freshly prepared) or a commercial bleach-based cleaner at least daily during the remainder of the antibiotic course and for several weeks after, (c) not sharing towels or personal care items, (d) washing soiled clothing and linens in hot water with bleach if fabric-safe, (e) Mr. Fuentes should wash his hands with soap and water after assisting his wife with toileting or handling soiled materials. The nurse reassures them that CDI is not transmitted through the air or through casual contact like sitting together — it is transmitted by spores on hands and surfaces that reach the mouth (fecal-oral route).

Dietary and medication guidance: resume a normal balanced diet as tolerated; maintain adequate fluid intake (at least 6–8 glasses of water daily) to prevent dehydration from any residual loose stools. Probiotics — the nurse explains that some evidence supports Saccharomyces boulardii or Lactobacillus-based probiotics for reducing CDI recurrence, but their use is not universally recommended, and the patient should discuss this with her provider before starting any probiotic, especially given her immunocompromised status (live organisms in probiotics carry a small risk of fungemia or bacteremia in immunosuppressed patients). She should not take any new antibiotic without her provider verifying it is necessary and appropriate — unnecessary antibiotic exposure is the single greatest risk factor for CDI recurrence. If a future provider prescribes an antibiotic for any reason, she should inform them of her CDI history so they can select the lowest-risk agent and consider prophylactic measures.

Follow-up appointments: the nurse confirms a follow-up visit with the primary care provider in one week, a rheumatology visit in two weeks (to discuss resuming methotrexate), and a GI follow-up only if symptoms recur. She confirms that the home health PT referral is in place and that the visiting nurse will begin the day after discharge.

On hospital day 18, Mrs. Fuentes is discharged home with her husband. The nurse performs a final assessment: vital signs normal, abdomen soft and nontender, two formed stools yesterday, perianal skin intact, ambulating with walker. Contact precautions remain in effect until the patient physically leaves the unit. The nurse contacts environmental services to request a terminal sporicidal clean of the room — all surfaces, including curtains, mattress, and any porous items, are to be cleaned with the sporicidal agent before the room is reassigned to a new patient.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *The situation / question:* At Stage 1, the nurse reviews the medication administration record and sees loperamide 2 mg PO PRN ordered for diarrhea. Mrs. Fuentes has had eight watery stools and is uncomfortable. The nurse must decide whether to administer the loperamide for symptom relief. *Correct action:* The nurse withholds the loperamide and notifies the provider to discontinue the order. She does not administer any antimotility agent in a patient with suspected or confirmed CDI. *Why it is correct:* Antimotility agents (loperamide, diphenoxylate-atropine) decrease intestinal peristalsis, which in CDI has two dangerous consequences: first, slowed transit retains toxin-laden stool in the colon, prolonging mucosal exposure to C. difficile toxins A and B and worsening colonic inflammation and injury; second, impaired motility increases the risk of toxic megacolon, a life-threatening complication characterized by acute colonic dilation (transverse colon diameter greater than 6 cm on imaging), systemic inflammatory response, and risk of perforation, peritonitis, and septic shock. Diarrhea in CDI, while distressing, is a protective mechanism that clears organisms and toxin from the colon. Suppressing it is counterproductive and dangerous. The nurse's responsibility is to manage the consequences of the diarrhea (perianal skin protection, fluid and electrolyte replacement, comfort measures) rather than suppress the diarrhea itself. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — the clinical presentation and the medication order are both available at initial assessment.

2. *The situation / question:* At Stage 1, the nurse initiates contact precautions and instructs all staff to use soap-and-water handwashing rather than alcohol-based hand rub. A nursing student on the unit asks why ABHR is insufficient when it is considered the gold standard for hand hygiene in healthcare settings. The nurse must provide the correct clinical rationale. *Correct action:* The nurse explains that C. difficile produces endospores — metabolically dormant structures with a multilayered protective coat composed of proteins, peptidoglycan, and a keratin-like outer layer. This coat renders spores resistant to the desiccating and denaturing action of ethanol and isopropanol, which are the active agents in ABHR. Alcohol disrupts lipid membranes and denatures proteins of vegetative bacteria, but the spore coat does not contain accessible lipid membranes and its structural proteins are cross-linked in a configuration that resists alcohol denaturation. Therefore, ABHR does not kill or inactivate C. difficile spores. Soap-and-water handwashing works by a different mechanism — it does not kill spores either, but the surfactant action of soap combined with the mechanical friction of rubbing and the rinsing action of running water physically dislodges spores from the skin surface and carries them down the drain. The efficacy depends on duration (minimum 20 seconds of friction) and technique (all surfaces of hands and fingers). This is why soap and water is mandatory — not merely preferred — for hand hygiene in CDI. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — the suspected CDI and the initiation of contact precautions provide the context.

3. *The situation / question:* At Stage 2, the nurse contacts environmental services to request sporicidal cleaning and is told that the housekeeping staff plans to clean the room with the standard quaternary ammonium disinfectant ("quat wipes") used throughout the hospital. The nurse must determine whether this is adequate and, if not, what to request and why. *Correct action:* The nurse informs environmental services that quaternary ammonium disinfectant is inadequate for CDI rooms and that a sodium hypochlorite–based (bleach-based) solution at a minimum concentration of 1,000 parts per million (ppm) — or an EPA-registered sporicidal agent — must be used for all surfaces in the patient's room, including the bathroom, at least twice daily and as a terminal clean upon discharge. *Why it is correct:* Quaternary ammonium compounds kill vegetative bacteria by disrupting cell membranes, but C. difficile spores lack the accessible membrane targets that quats act on. Spores persist on environmental surfaces (bed rails, light switches, toilets, floors) for months and serve as a reservoir for transmission to subsequent patients and to healthcare workers' hands. Sodium hypochlorite (bleach) at appropriate concentration is sporicidal — the hypochlorous acid generated in solution oxidizes and degrades the spore coat, achieving spore kill. Hydrogen peroxide vapor (HPV) systems are an alternative for terminal cleaning but are not available for routine daily use. Failure to use a sporicidal agent means that even after the patient is discharged, the room remains contaminated and the next patient admitted to that room is at increased risk for CDI acquisition. The nurse's insistence on the correct cleaning agent is a patient-safety intervention that protects not only the current patient but all future occupants of that room. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — the confirmed CDI diagnosis and the environmental services interaction provide the context.

4. *The situation / question:* At Stage 2, a medical student rotating on the unit asks the nurse why Mrs. Fuentes is receiving oral vancomycin for her C. difficile infection rather than IV vancomycin, since IV vancomycin is "the strongest antibiotic for serious infections." The nurse must explain the pharmacokinetic rationale for the oral route. *Correct action:* The nurse explains that the route of administration is determined by where the infection is located and how the drug reaches that site. CDI is an intraluminal infection — C. difficile colonizes and produces toxins within the colonic lumen, damaging the mucosal surface. Treatment requires high drug concentrations within the colon, not in the bloodstream. Oral vancomycin is minimally absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract (less than 5% bioavailability) — this is usually considered a limitation, but for CDI it is the therapeutic advantage, because the unabsorbed drug remains in the intestinal lumen at bactericidal concentrations throughout the colon. IV vancomycin, by contrast, is distributed systemically via the bloodstream and does not cross back into the intestinal lumen in meaningful concentrations. A patient receiving IV vancomycin for CDI would have therapeutic blood levels but subtherapeutic colonic levels — the drug would not reach the site of infection. This is why IV vancomycin is the drug of choice for MRSA bacteremia (a bloodstream infection) but is ineffective for CDI (a luminal infection). The same pharmacokinetic principle explains why fidaxomicin, another oral agent, works for CDI — it too is minimally absorbed and achieves high fecal concentrations. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues (applying pharmacokinetic reasoning to route selection). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — the confirmed diagnosis and the ongoing oral vancomycin therapy provide the teaching context.

5. *The situation / question:* At Stage 2, the nurse monitors for signs of progression to fulminant CDI or toxic megacolon throughout the day. She must identify which specific clinical findings would represent a critical change requiring emergent escalation versus findings that represent expected gradual improvement. *Correct action:* The nurse identifies the following as emergent red flags requiring immediate provider notification and possible surgical consultation: (a) new abdominal rigidity, guarding, or rebound tenderness (peritoneal signs suggesting perforation or impending perforation), (b) abrupt cessation of diarrhea combined with increasing abdominal distension and pain (paradoxical — suggests colonic dilation and ileus, not resolution), (c) fever above 38.5 °C with hemodynamic instability (tachycardia above 110, systolic blood pressure below 90), (d) WBC rising above 30,000 or falling to an unexpectedly low level in a previously leukocytotic patient (bone marrow exhaustion — an ominous sign), (e) rising lactate (above 2.2 mmol/L would indicate tissue hypoperfusion), or (f) new altered mental status. By contrast, findings consistent with expected improvement include: gradual decrease in stool frequency with progression from watery to semi-formed consistency, resolving fever, downtrending WBC, improving abdominal tenderness without new peritoneal signs, and stable or improving hemodynamics. *Why it is correct:* Fulminant CDI (formerly called severe complicated CDI) carries a mortality rate of 30–50% and may require emergent subtotal colectomy. The transition from severe to fulminant can be rapid. The nurse's ability to distinguish a worsening trajectory from an improving one — and to escalate immediately when the trajectory changes — is the critical safety function. The paradoxical sign of diarrhea cessation with worsening distension is particularly important because a less experienced nurse might interpret the absence of diarrhea as improvement. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — requires the confirmed CDI, the severity markers, and the ongoing monitoring data.

6. *The situation / question:* At Stage 3, the nurse is preparing the discharge education and must decide whether to order a repeat C. difficile PCR test to confirm that the infection has cleared before sending the patient home. *Correct action:* The nurse does not request or recommend a test of cure. She recognizes that repeating the C. difficile PCR in a patient whose symptoms have resolved is explicitly not recommended by IDSA/SHEA guidelines. *Why it is correct:* The C. difficile PCR detects the toxin gene (tcdB) in stool, not active toxin production. After successful treatment, patients commonly continue to shed C. difficile DNA and even viable spores for weeks to months (asymptomatic carriage). A positive PCR in an asymptomatic patient does not indicate active infection, does not require treatment, and would generate confusion, unnecessary additional antibiotic exposure, prolonged isolation, and patient anxiety. The clinical definition of CDI resolution is symptom-based: resolution of diarrhea (fewer than three unformed stools per day), absence of fever, and improvement in laboratory markers. Mrs. Fuentes meets all of these criteria. Testing should be repeated only if symptoms recur. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes (using clinical criteria rather than laboratory testing to define resolution). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3 — requires the clinical improvement data and the discharge context.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse administers loperamide because the patient is distressed by the frequency of diarrhea and because the PRN order is active in the system. She reasons that symptom relief is a nursing priority and that slowing the diarrhea will reduce fluid losses and perianal skin damage. This is wrong because antimotility agents in CDI trap toxin in the colon, worsen mucosal injury, and increase the risk of toxic megacolon. The nurse should address the consequences of diarrhea (IV fluid replacement, electrolyte correction, skin barrier protection) without suppressing the diarrhea itself. Comfort measures that do not impair gut motility — warm compresses, positioning, perianal hygiene — are appropriate alternatives.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse uses alcohol-based hand rub when exiting the CDI patient's room because it is more convenient, faster, and readily available at the doorway dispenser, and she reasons that she wore gloves and did not directly touch stool. This is wrong because C. difficile spores penetrate gloves at a measurable rate (glove micro-perforations), contaminate the hands even with gloving, and are not killed or removed by ABHR. Every exit from a CDI room requires soap-and-water handwashing regardless of glove use. The convenience of ABHR does not override its microbiological inadequacy for spore-forming organisms.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse instructs the patient's husband to use the hand sanitizer dispenser outside the room rather than the sink inside the room. This is wrong for the same reason — the wall-mounted ABHR dispenser does not eliminate spores. The husband must wash his hands with soap and water at the in-room sink before removing his gown and gloves, and ideally again outside the room. Visitors must receive the same standard of hand hygiene instruction as staff.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse accepts the environmental services plan to clean with quaternary ammonium wipes because "that's what we always use" and does not escalate the request for bleach-based cleaning. This is wrong because standard quaternary ammonium disinfectants are ineffective against C. difficile spores, and a room cleaned with quats remains a transmission reservoir. The nurse has both the knowledge and the professional obligation to insist on sporicidal cleaning and to escalate through the charge nurse or infection preventionist if environmental services does not comply.

Attached to Decision Point 4: The nurse, upon hearing the CDI diagnosis, suggests to the team that IV vancomycin should be added or substituted because "oral might not be strong enough for such a high white count." This is wrong because IV vancomycin does not reach the colonic lumen and would not treat the infection, regardless of severity. For fulminant CDI, the correct escalation is increasing the oral vancomycin dose to 500 mg four times daily and adding IV metronidazole (which does achieve some colonic secretion) — not switching to IV vancomycin. The nurse's suggestion would divert treatment from an effective route to an ineffective one.

Attached to Decision Point 5: On day 15, Mrs. Fuentes has only two stools between 0700 and 1500 (markedly fewer than the previous day), and the nurse documents this as "diarrhea resolving — patient improving" without performing an abdominal assessment or checking vital signs. This is wrong because a sudden decrease in stool output in CDI requires correlation with the abdominal and hemodynamic examination. If the abdomen is becoming more distended, tenser, or if bowel sounds are diminishing, the reduction in stool output may represent developing ileus and colonic dilation (early toxic megacolon), not improvement. The nurse must assess the abdomen and vital signs before attributing decreased stool frequency to clinical resolution.

Attached to Decision Point 6: The nurse orders a repeat C. difficile PCR on the day of discharge to "make sure it's cleared" before the patient goes home. This is wrong because a test of cure is explicitly not recommended — the PCR may remain positive for weeks in a clinically cured patient due to spore shedding or residual DNA. A positive result would generate unnecessary concern, potentially delay discharge, and might lead to inappropriate retreatment of an asymptomatic patient. Clinical resolution is defined by symptom improvement, not by a negative test.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

1. Identify the convergence of CDI risk factors (advanced age, broad-spectrum antibiotics, immunosuppression, proton pump inhibitor use, prolonged hospitalization) and the classic presentation (acute watery diarrhea, abdominal distension, leukocytosis, fever) to initiate contact precautions and specimen collection before awaiting a confirmed diagnosis.

2. Explain the microbiological basis for why alcohol-based hand rub is ineffective against C. difficile spores and why soap-and-water handwashing is mandatory, and apply this principle consistently in clinical practice including education of staff, students, and visitors.

3. Distinguish appropriate sporicidal environmental disinfection agents (sodium hypochlorite, hydrogen peroxide–based systems) from standard hospital disinfectants (quaternary ammonium compounds) and advocate for correct agent use in CDI rooms by communicating the rationale to environmental services and the care team.

4. Apply pharmacokinetic reasoning to explain why oral vancomycin is effective and IV vancomycin is ineffective for CDI, identifying the intraluminal site of infection as the determining factor for route selection, and differentiate this from systemic infections where IV vancomycin is appropriate.

5. Monitor for clinical signs of progression from non-severe to fulminant CDI — including the paradoxical sign of diarrhea cessation with worsening abdominal distension — and differentiate a worsening trajectory from an improving one using integrated assessment of stool pattern, abdominal examination, hemodynamic parameters, and laboratory trends.
