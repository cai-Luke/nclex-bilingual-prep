**CASE TITLE**
Immune checkpoint inhibitor–induced myocarditis with concurrent hepatotoxicity.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 61-year-old woman with metastatic melanoma is admitted to the oncology unit for her third cycle of combination immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy with nivolumab (anti–PD-1) plus ipilimumab (anti–CTLA-4). Her cancer was diagnosed eight months ago after excision of a Clark level V lesion on the left scapula; subsequent imaging revealed hepatic and pulmonary metastases. She has a history of well-controlled hypertension managed with amlodipine 5 mg daily and no prior cardiac disease. Echocardiogram before starting immunotherapy three months ago showed a left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) of 62% with no wall-motion abnormalities. Her first two cycles were notable only for grade 1 fatigue and a mild maculopapular rash on the trunk that resolved with topical corticosteroids. Current orders include nivolumab 3 mg/kg IV and ipilimumab 1 mg/kg IV to infuse today, ondansetron 4 mg IV PRN nausea, continuous telemetry during infusion, and baseline labs drawn this morning before treatment.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient arrives to the infusion area reporting that over the past four days she has noticed increasing fatigue beyond her usual level, a vague sense of chest heaviness that is worse when she climbs the stairs to her bedroom, and mild nausea with poor appetite. She attributes these symptoms to "the chemo catching up with me." She denies frank chest pain, syncope, orthopnea, lower-extremity edema, or fever. She appears mildly fatigued but is conversational, ambulatory, and in no acute distress.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline assessment on arrival: temperature 37.1 °C, heart rate 102 and regular, blood pressure 118/74, respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Heart sounds are present without murmur, rub, or gallop; lung fields are clear bilaterally. Abdomen is soft, mildly tender in the right upper quadrant on deep palpation, with no hepatomegaly appreciable at the costal margin. Skin shows faint residual erythema on the trunk from the prior rash; no new rash. Peripheral pulses are 2+ and symmetric; no peripheral edema. Telemetry at baseline shows sinus tachycardia at 102 beats per minute with normal P-wave morphology, PR interval 0.16 seconds, and QRS duration 0.09 seconds. The patient rates her chest heaviness as 3 out of 10 and states it is not pleuritic.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn this morning: WBC 7.2 × 10³/µL (differential: neutrophils 68%, lymphocytes 18%, monocytes 10%, eosinophils 4%), hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, platelets 198 × 10³/µL, sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.3 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 16 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 104 mg/dL, AST 88 U/L (normal up to 35), ALT 112 U/L (normal up to 40), alkaline phosphatase 95 U/L, total bilirubin 1.0 mg/dL, troponin I 0.18 ng/mL (normal < 0.04), BNP 245 pg/mL (normal < 100), CRP 3.8 mg/dL (normal < 0.5), TSH 2.1 mIU/L, LDH 310 U/L.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 2 hours after baseline labs are resulted, before the infusion begins. The nurse reviews the labs and notes the elevated troponin I, BNP, AST, ALT, and CRP in the context of new-onset exertional chest heaviness and resting tachycardia. The treating oncologist is notified. A 12-lead ECG is obtained and shows sinus tachycardia at 104 bpm with diffuse shallow ST-segment depressions in leads I, II, aVL, V4–V6 and a new first-degree AV block (PR interval 0.22 seconds, compared with 0.16 seconds on telemetry strip moments earlier — confirmed to be a change from her pre-treatment ECG on file, which showed a PR of 0.16 seconds). The oncologist places the scheduled ICI infusion on hold and orders an urgent transthoracic echocardiogram and a cardiology consultation. Repeat vital signs: T 37.2 °C, HR 106, BP 112/70, RR 22, SpO₂ 95% on room air. The patient reports her chest heaviness has increased to 5 out of 10 and she now feels short of breath when sitting upright. Right-upper-quadrant tenderness is unchanged.

**Stage 2** — 4 to 6 hours after initial presentation. Cardiology has evaluated the patient. The urgent echocardiogram reveals a new global reduction in left ventricular systolic function with an LVEF of 38% (down from 62% at baseline), diffuse hypokinesis, and no pericardial effusion. Repeat labs: troponin I 1.42 ng/mL (rising sharply from 0.18), BNP 580 pg/mL, AST 156 U/L, ALT 189 U/L, total bilirubin 1.4 mg/dL, CRP 7.2 mg/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL. Repeat 12-lead ECG shows new ventricular ectopy (frequent PVCs, 8–10 per minute) superimposed on sinus tachycardia with persistent ST depressions and new T-wave inversions in the lateral leads; the PR interval is now 0.26 seconds. Vital signs: T 37.3 °C, HR 112 (irregular due to PVCs), BP 104/66, RR 24, SpO₂ 93% on room air. The patient is now dyspneic at rest and reports worsening fatigue; she is diaphoretic and anxious. Cardiology and oncology jointly diagnose probable immune checkpoint inhibitor–induced myocarditis (grade 3–4 immune-related adverse event) with concurrent immune-mediated hepatitis (grade 2, trending toward grade 3). Orders are written to transfer the patient to a higher-acuity monitored cardiac unit, such as telemetry step-down or ICU per acuity/protocol, initiate high-dose intravenous methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily (pulse-dose corticosteroids), start supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula to maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94%, place two large-bore peripheral IVs, obtain a stat serum CK and CK-MB, and permanently discontinue all immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy. A cardiac MRI is planned for the following morning if the patient is stable enough to tolerate it. The nurse administers the first dose of methylprednisolone and titrates oxygen to 3 L/min via nasal cannula, achieving SpO₂ 95%.

**Stage 3** — 18 to 24 hours after initiation of pulse-dose corticosteroids. The patient has received two doses of methylprednisolone 1 g IV. Repeat vital signs: T 37.0 °C, HR 94 and regular, BP 114/72, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on 2 L nasal cannula. Telemetry shows sinus rhythm at 94 bpm with resolution of the frequent PVCs; PR interval has shortened to 0.20 seconds, though ST depressions persist. The patient reports chest heaviness at 2 out of 10 and can sit up and speak in full sentences without dyspnea. Repeat labs: troponin I 0.64 ng/mL (declining from 1.42), BNP 390 pg/mL (declining), AST 132 U/L (declining), ALT 148 U/L (declining), total bilirubin 1.2 mg/dL, CRP 4.1 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, CK 420 U/L (mildly elevated), blood glucose 218 mg/dL (new hyperglycemia attributed to high-dose corticosteroids). The patient's urine output has averaged 55 mL/hr over the last 8 hours. The care team discusses the treatment plan: a corticosteroid taper over several weeks, ongoing troponin and liver-enzyme monitoring every 6 to 12 hours, cardiology follow-up with a repeat echocardiogram in 48 to 72 hours, and the permanent discontinuation of combination ICI therapy. The oncologist notes that for this patient’s grade 3–4 ICI myocarditis, the treatment plan is permanent discontinuation and the patient should not restart checkpoint inhibitor therapy unless a future specialist oncology/cardiology team explicitly determines otherwise. A new concern at this stage is the steroid-induced hyperglycemia, which requires blood glucose monitoring every 4 to 6 hours and possible sliding-scale insulin coverage.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* Before the infusion begins at Stage 1, the nurse reviews the baseline labs and the patient's new symptoms (exertional chest heaviness, increased fatigue, resting tachycardia). The question is whether the nurse should proceed with the scheduled ICI infusion or withhold it and notify the oncologist. *Correct action:* The nurse should hold the infusion and immediately notify the oncologist of the elevated troponin I (0.18 ng/mL, more than four times the upper limit of normal), elevated BNP, transaminase elevations, new exertional chest symptoms, and resting tachycardia. *Why it is correct:* Immune checkpoint inhibitors can cause fulminant myocarditis, which carries a mortality rate of up to 25–50% when diagnosed late. An elevated troponin in a patient on combination ICI therapy with new cardiac symptoms is a red-flag cue that must be reported before administering another dose of the causative agent, because additional dosing could accelerate the immune-mediated injury. Nurses cannot independently discontinue a prescribed medication, but they have both the authority and the obligation to withhold a medication when assessment findings suggest potential harm and to seek clarification or new orders from the provider.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must interpret the constellation of findings — elevated troponin with new ST changes and first-degree AV block, elevated transaminases, elevated inflammatory markers, new exertional dyspnea — in a patient receiving combination ICI therapy. The question is what clinical hypothesis best explains the data. *Correct action:* The nurse should analyze these cues as most consistent with immune checkpoint inhibitor–induced myocarditis, likely with concurrent immune-mediated hepatitis, rather than acute coronary syndrome, viral myocarditis, or disease progression. *Why it is correct:* The combination of diffuse (not territory-specific) ST-segment changes, a new conduction abnormality (prolonging PR interval), troponin elevation, BNP elevation, and concurrent hepatic transaminase elevation in a patient actively receiving combination anti–PD-1/anti–CTLA-4 therapy is the hallmark presentation of ICI myocarditis with multi-organ immune-related adverse events. ACS remains a differential diagnosis for cardiology to evaluate, but it does not fully explain the diffuse ECG changes, new conduction delay, rising inflammatory markers, and concurrent transaminase elevation in the setting of combination ICI therapy. Viral myocarditis is possible but far less likely in this clinical context. The concurrent hepatotoxicity suggests a systemic immune-mediated process rather than a single-organ etiology.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient is hemodynamically deteriorating (falling BP, rising HR, worsening oxygenation, falling LVEF to 38%, rapidly rising troponin, new PVCs). Multiple urgent needs exist simultaneously: oxygen supplementation, corticosteroid administration, transfer to a higher level of care, and vascular access. The question is which nursing priority comes first. *Correct action:* The nurse should prioritize airway and oxygenation first (apply supplemental oxygen to maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94%), then facilitate the administration of high-dose IV methylprednisolone as rapidly as possible, because corticosteroid immunosuppression is the definitive treatment that suppresses immune-mediated myocardial injury. Concurrently, the nurse should ensure continuous telemetry, establish reliable IV access, and prepare for transfer. *Why it is correct:* Oxygenation takes immediate priority per the ABCs because the patient's SpO₂ has dropped to 93% and myocardial oxygen demand is already elevated from tachycardia. However, unlike many emergencies where stabilization alone is sufficient, ICI myocarditis demands urgent immunosuppression — every hour of delay in corticosteroids allows further T-cell–mediated myocyte destruction, which is irreversible. The nurse must therefore treat this as a time-critical medication administration. Transfer is important but should not delay the first dose of methylprednisolone if it can be given on the current unit.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must prepare a comprehensive plan for the patient's ongoing monitoring and management after the first dose of corticosteroids is given and the patient is being transferred to telemetry step-down. The question is what monitoring and nursing interventions should be included. *Correct action:* The nurse should generate a solution set that includes continuous telemetry with alarms set for arrhythmias and conduction changes (especially worsening AV block, which can progress to complete heart block in ICI myocarditis), hemodynamic monitoring every 1 to 2 hours (HR, BP, SpO₂, respiratory status), serial troponin levels every 6 to 12 hours to track trajectory, strict intake and output with assessment for signs of worsening heart failure (crackles, jugular venous distension, new peripheral edema, rising BNP), serial liver-enzyme monitoring, blood glucose monitoring (anticipating steroid-induced hyperglycemia), and having a defibrillator and emergency resuscitation equipment immediately available at the bedside. The nurse should also assess for signs of other immune-related adverse events (assess for overlapping myositis or myasthenia-like symptoms, because ICI myocarditis can overlap with neuromuscular immune toxicity). *Why it is correct:* ICI myocarditis can deteriorate rapidly to complete heart block, cardiogenic shock, or fatal arrhythmia. The conduction system is a common target of the immune infiltrate, so PR-interval trending is essential. Multi-organ immune toxicity is the rule rather than the exception with combination ICI therapy, so surveillance for additional organ involvement is warranted. Anticipating steroid side effects reflects safe medication management.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the oncologist writes an order to permanently discontinue all ICI therapy. The nurse must act on this order and communicate it effectively. The question is what the nurse's responsibilities are regarding this medication discontinuation. *Correct action:* The nurse should verify the ICI is held/discontinued in the MAR per the provider order; document the medication as not given with the clinical reason; notify pharmacy/oncology if the order remains active; ensure the adverse drug reaction is documented per facility policy; and educate the patient that the immunotherapy was stopped because of a serious heart-and-liver immune reaction. *Why it is correct:* Permanent discontinuation — not dose reduction, not rechallenge — is the standard of care for grade 3–4 ICI myocarditis, given the high fatality risk with re-exposure. The nurse's role includes ensuring the order is executed across all systems (MAR, pharmacy), educating the patient to prevent inadvertent re-exposure in the future, and documenting the adverse drug reaction prominently in the medical record. This is an active nursing intervention, not a passive acknowledgment of an order.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after 18–24 hours of pulse-dose corticosteroids, the nurse reviews the repeat vitals, labs, and clinical status. The question is how to determine whether the treatment is working and what new concerns have emerged. *Correct action:* The nurse should evaluate that the troponin has declined from 1.42 to 0.64 ng/mL, the BNP is trending down, the transaminases are beginning to decrease, the PVCs have resolved, the heart rate has decreased, the blood pressure has stabilized, and the patient's symptoms have improved — all of which confirm a positive response to immunosuppression. However, the nurse must also recognize two important caveats: the troponin and transaminases remain elevated (the patient is improving but not yet resolved, so monitoring must continue and corticosteroid taper must be slow and guided by biomarkers), and a new problem has appeared — blood glucose of 218 mg/dL, which is a predictable adverse effect of high-dose methylprednisolone and requires blood glucose monitoring every 4 to 6 hours and possible insulin coverage per protocol. The PR interval has improved but is still prolonged (0.20 seconds), so telemetry must continue. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating ICI myocarditis response requires tracking the same biomarkers used for diagnosis (troponin, BNP, ECG conduction pattern, LVEF). A declining troponin is the most important early marker of response. However, premature corticosteroid taper (before troponin fully normalizes) is associated with myocarditis flares, so the declining but still-elevated troponin should prompt the nurse to anticipate a prolonged taper. Recognizing steroid-induced hyperglycemia demonstrates that the nurse evaluates not only the target condition but also treatment-related complications.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Proceeding with the scheduled ICI infusion because the troponin elevation is "only mildly elevated" or because the patient attributes her symptoms to expected chemotherapy fatigue. This is unsafe because any troponin elevation above the upper limit of normal in a patient on ICI therapy with new cardiac symptoms must be treated as a potential myocarditis emergency. The patient's self-assessment of her symptoms as routine does not override the objective laboratory and assessment data. Administering additional ICI in the setting of evolving myocarditis could precipitate fulminant cardiac failure.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Holding the infusion but failing to communicate the elevated troponin and new symptoms to the oncologist urgently, instead planning to mention it at routine rounds. This is wrong because ICI myocarditis can progress from stable to fatal within hours; delayed notification delays diagnosis and life-saving immunosuppressive treatment.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Interpreting the ECG changes as acute coronary syndrome and prioritizing heparin administration and cardiac catheterization preparation rather than recognizing the immune-mediated etiology. The constellation is most concerning for ICI myocarditis with concurrent immune-mediated hepatitis; ACS may still be evaluated by cardiology, but the nurse must hold the ICI, notify the provider urgently, maintain monitoring, and avoid delaying immunosuppression when myocarditis is strongly suspected.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Attributing the transaminase elevations solely to hepatic metastases and failing to connect them to the cardiac findings as part of a multi-organ immune-related adverse event. This error misses the systemic nature of ICI toxicity and could lead to underestimation of the severity of the immune reaction.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Delaying corticosteroid administration to wait for the cardiac MRI results to "confirm" the diagnosis before treating. This is unsafe because ICI myocarditis treatment with high-dose corticosteroids should not be delayed for confirmatory imaging when the clinical picture is strongly suggestive. Cardiac MRI is helpful but can be obtained after treatment has been initiated. The mortality risk of untreated ICI myocarditis far exceeds the risk of empiric corticosteroids.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Failing to monitor for progression of the AV block (e.g., not trending the PR interval or not having transcutaneous pacing equipment available). ICI myocarditis has a well-documented propensity to progress from first-degree to complete heart block rapidly, and failure to anticipate this complication can result in hemodynamic collapse.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Documenting the ICI discontinuation as a temporary hold with plans to reassess for rechallenge after recovery. For this patient’s grade 3–4 ICI myocarditis, the treatment plan is permanent discontinuation and the patient should not restart checkpoint inhibitor therapy unless a future specialist oncology/cardiology team explicitly determines otherwise. Framing it as a hold could lead to inadvertent re-exposure.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the declining troponin as evidence that corticosteroids can be rapidly tapered or discontinued. Premature tapering of immunosuppression before troponin fully normalizes is associated with myocarditis relapse. The nurse should anticipate a slow, biomarker-guided taper over weeks and should not suggest or support early discontinuation based on initial improvement alone.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Ignoring the new blood glucose of 218 mg/dL because the patient has no history of diabetes. Steroid-induced hyperglycemia is a predictable and common adverse effect of pulse-dose methylprednisolone and can become severe if unmonitored. The nurse should initiate glucose monitoring per protocol and notify the provider for insulin-coverage orders.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the clinical cues of immune checkpoint inhibitor–induced myocarditis, including elevated troponin, new ECG conduction abnormalities, and declining ventricular function, and differentiate this presentation from acute coronary syndrome and other causes of troponin elevation.

Apply the principle that a suspected immune-related adverse event involving the heart requires immediate withholding of the causative ICI agent and urgent provider notification, even when symptoms appear mild or the patient minimizes their significance.

Prioritize the initiation of high-dose corticosteroid immunosuppression as a time-critical intervention in confirmed or strongly suspected ICI myocarditis, understanding that delays in treatment increase mortality.

Generate a comprehensive monitoring plan for a patient with ICI myocarditis that includes serial troponin trending, continuous telemetry with attention to conduction-interval changes, hemodynamic surveillance, multi-organ toxicity screening, and anticipation of treatment-related complications such as steroid-induced hyperglycemia.

Evaluate the effectiveness of immunosuppressive therapy by interpreting biomarker trajectories, ECG changes, and clinical status, while recognizing that initial improvement does not equate to resolution and that premature treatment de-escalation poses a relapse risk.
