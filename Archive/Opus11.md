**CASE TITLE**
Hyperactive delirium superimposed on baseline dementia in an older adult with urinary tract infection.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
An 82-year-old woman is brought to the emergency department by her daughter. She lives in an assisted-living facility and carries a diagnosis of mild Alzheimer-type dementia (baseline Montreal Cognitive Assessment score of 20/30 documented six months ago). At baseline she is oriented to person and place, manages her own meals and grooming with cueing, converses coherently with occasional word-finding pauses, and sleeps through the night. Her medical history includes hypertension, osteoarthritis, type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 500 mg twice daily, and a remote hip fracture repaired three years ago. She has a documented allergy to sulfonamides (rash). Current home medications include lisinopril 10 mg daily, acetaminophen 650 mg every six hours as needed for joint pain, metformin 500 mg twice daily, and a daily multivitamin. She has no indwelling urinary catheter and no recent hospitalizations. The daughter, who visits three times a week and is the patient's health-care proxy, reports that over the past 36 hours the patient has become "a completely different person."

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The daughter states that yesterday afternoon the assisted-living staff called to say her mother was "talking to people who weren't there" and had tried to climb over the bed rail. Overnight the patient slept only in brief, agitated bursts, shouting at unseen figures. This morning she did not recognize her daughter, refused breakfast, and pulled out her hearing aid. The daughter emphasizes that none of this is normal for her mother: "She has memory trouble, but she's never been like this." The patient was incontinent of urine twice overnight, which the daughter says is new. No recent falls, no head trauma, no new medications, no alcohol use.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 38.3 °C (101.0 °F) orally, heart rate 104, blood pressure 148/82, respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 96% on room air. The patient is restless in bed, intermittently pulling at her gown and the blood-pressure cuff. She opens her eyes spontaneously, makes eye contact briefly, then looks away and speaks to an empty corner of the room, calling out a name. She does not follow commands consistently — she will squeeze a hand on one attempt but not the next. She is oriented to person only (states her first name correctly but gives the year as 1987 and says she is "at home"). Skin is warm and dry, mucous membranes are dry, and skin turgor is decreased (tenting over the sternum resolves in four seconds). Heart sounds are regular and tachycardic without murmur. Lungs are clear bilaterally. Abdomen is soft, mildly tender in the suprapubic region on palpation; the patient grimaces and pushes the examiner's hand away. No costovertebral angle tenderness. A brief straight catheterization for a urine specimen yields 60 mL of cloudy, malodorous urine. No peripheral edema. No focal motor deficits noted; she moves all extremities spontaneously and symmetrically.

**LABORATORY DATA**
White blood cell count 14,200/µL with 82% neutrophils and 6% bands. Hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, platelets 210,000/µL. Sodium 146 mEq/L, potassium 4.0 mEq/L, chloride 108 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 32 mg/dL, creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (baseline from six months ago was 0.9 mg/dL), glucose 158 mg/dL. Lactate 1.6 mmol/L. Urinalysis: amber, cloudy, specific gravity 1.028, positive leukocyte esterase, positive nitrites, large bacteria, 50–100 WBC per high-power field, trace protein. Urine culture is sent and pending. Blood cultures × 2 are drawn peripherally and sent. A non-contrast CT of the head (obtained because of acute mental-status change) shows age-appropriate cortical atrophy; no acute intracranial pathology.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Emergency department, hours 0–4. The provider orders ceftriaxone 1 g IV every 24 hours (chosen because of the sulfonamide allergy), a 500 mL normal saline bolus over two hours, and maintenance IV fluids at 75 mL/hr. The patient is admitted to a medical-surgical unit. During transport she becomes more agitated: she attempts to stand on the stretcher, swings at the transporter, and shouts, "Get away from me!" The daughter is present and visibly distressed, asking, "Is this the dementia getting worse? Should we start thinking about a memory-care facility?" The nurse dims the lights in the room, speaks in a calm, low tone, reorients the patient gently, and places the bed in the lowest position with the call light within reach. The daughter's hearing aid is cleaned and replaced. A Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) screen is performed: acute onset — yes (confirmed by daughter's timeline); inattention — yes (unable to recite days of the week backward, loses focus after two items); disorganized thinking — yes (speech is tangential, she answers "the dog needs water" when asked where she is); altered level of consciousness — yes (fluctuates between hyperalert agitation and brief drowsy episodes within the same hour). CAM is positive. The provider is notified of the positive CAM. Vital signs at hour 3: T 38.1 °C, HR 98, BP 140/78, RR 18, SpO₂ 97%. Urine output via a brief re-check voided specimen is 40 mL in two hours (dark amber). The patient has taken a few sips of water but pushes the cup away.

**Stage 2** — Hours 8–16 on the medical-surgical unit. The IV saline bolus is complete. Repeat vitals at hour 10: T 37.6 °C, HR 92, BP 134/76, RR 16, SpO₂ 98%. The patient is less combative but remains confused: she now recognizes her daughter intermittently ("You look like my girl") but continues to misidentify the room and the time. She picks at her IV site repeatedly and has dislodged the saline lock once; the nurse re-secures it. She sleeps in 20-minute stretches, then wakes abruptly and calls out. The daughter has been at the bedside for ten hours and is exhausted, tearful, and again asks whether this is permanent. Repeat labs at hour 12: WBC 12,800/µL, sodium 143 mEq/L, BUN 28 mg/dL, creatinine 1.1 mg/dL, glucose 142 mg/dL. Urine output over the past four hours is 160 mL (light yellow). The urine culture preliminary report returns: greater than 100,000 CFU/mL of Escherichia coli, susceptibilities pending but historically sensitive to cephalosporins at this facility. The night-shift nurse documents a Braden Scale score of 14 (mild risk for pressure injury), noting the patient's sensory perception is slightly limited and her mobility is very limited due to agitation and confusion. The patient has not eaten since admission. A fall-risk assessment is high. Bed alarm is activated. The provider places an order for haloperidol 0.5 mg IV every 6 hours as needed for severe agitation that poses a safety threat, with a note: "Use only if non-pharmacologic measures fail and patient is at imminent risk of self-harm; reassess after each dose."

**Stage 3** — Hours 24–36. The patient has received one dose of haloperidol overnight after she climbed over the side rail despite the bed alarm and was found standing barefoot beside the bed. She calmed within 30 minutes and slept for three hours. Morning assessment: T 37.1 °C, HR 82, BP 130/72, RR 14, SpO₂ 98%. She is drowsy but arousable, follows simple one-step commands ("squeeze my hand," "open your mouth"), and recognizes her daughter by name. She still thinks she is at home and gives the year as "the nineties." She ate half of a soft breakfast with cueing. Urine output over the past eight hours is 320 mL (pale yellow). Repeat labs: WBC 10,400/µL, sodium 140 mEq/L, BUN 24 mg/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, glucose 130 mg/dL. The urine culture final report confirms E. coli sensitive to ceftriaxone. The nurse performs a repeat CAM: acute onset — yes (still within the acute episode); inattention — yes but improved (recites days of the week backward to Wednesday before losing track); disorganized thinking — improved (answers are relevant but slow); altered level of consciousness — mildly drowsy, no longer fluctuating between hyperalert and somnolent. CAM remains positive but the trajectory is improving. The daughter asks, "She's better but still not herself — when will she be back to normal?" The provider discusses a plan to transition to an oral antibiotic (cephalexin 500 mg every 6 hours) once the patient tolerates a full meal, with a target discharge back to assisted living in 48–72 hours if the delirium continues to clear.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the daughter asks whether this acute change represents worsening dementia or something else. Using the history provided by the daughter, the CAM results, the vital signs, and the laboratory data, determine the primary problem driving the patient's current presentation. *Correct action:* The nurse identifies the primary problem as delirium superimposed on baseline dementia, precipitated by a urinary tract infection. The acute onset over 36 hours, the fluctuating course, the inattention, the disorganized thinking, the positive CAM, the fever, the leukocytosis with bandemia, and the grossly abnormal urinalysis all point to an acute, reversible confusional state — not a progression of her underlying Alzheimer disease. *Why it is correct:* Dementia is chronic and insidious; it does not produce visual hallucinations, acute combativeness, and fever over 36 hours. Delirium is acute, fluctuating, and characterized by inattention — features the CAM is designed to detect. Identifying the cause (UTI with systemic inflammatory response) is essential because delirium is potentially reversible when the precipitant is treated, whereas misattributing the change to dementia progression could lead to premature goals-of-care changes and failure to treat the infection aggressively. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient is acutely agitated — attempting to stand on the stretcher, swinging at staff. Multiple immediate nursing actions are needed. Determine which actions are appropriate and which single action takes highest priority. *Correct action:* The highest-priority action is ensuring immediate physical safety: lowering the bed to its lowest position, staying with the patient, and using calm verbal de-escalation and reorientation. Simultaneously appropriate actions include dimming environmental stimuli, replacing the hearing aid to reduce sensory deprivation, and keeping the daughter at the bedside as a familiar presence. Restraints and sedation are not first-line. *Why it is correct:* In hyperactive delirium the patient is at high risk for falls and injury. Non-pharmacologic interventions are the first-line approach: reducing environmental triggers (noise, bright lights), correcting sensory deficits (hearing aid), maintaining orientation cues (familiar person, clock, reorientation), and ensuring the physical environment is safe. Physical restraints increase agitation and are associated with worsening delirium, prolonged hospitalization, and injury; they are a last resort, not a first response. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the provider orders haloperidol 0.5 mg IV every 6 hours PRN for severe agitation posing a safety threat, with the stipulation that non-pharmacologic measures must fail first. The nurse must determine what assessments are required before and after administering this medication if it becomes necessary. *Correct action:* Before administration the nurse verifies that non-pharmacologic interventions have been attempted and documented as ineffective, confirms the patient has no history of prolonged QT interval or Parkinson disease (haloperidol is contraindicated in both), and obtains a baseline ECG or confirms a recent one is on file per facility protocol. After administration the nurse monitors for hypotension, excessive sedation (using a standardized sedation scale), extrapyramidal symptoms (acute dystonia, rigidity), and QT prolongation. The nurse also reassesses the agitation within 30 minutes to evaluate effectiveness and documents the indication, the non-pharmacologic measures tried, and the patient's response. *Why it is correct:* Antipsychotics carry a boxed warning for increased mortality in older adults with dementia-related psychosis. Their use in delirium is reserved for situations where the patient is an imminent danger to self or others and non-pharmacologic strategies have failed. Haloperidol can prolong the QT interval and cause torsades de pointes; it can also cause extrapyramidal effects, excessive sedation, and hypotension — all of which are especially dangerous in a frail older adult. Documenting the clinical justification and the failure of alternatives protects the patient and meets regulatory and ethical standards for PRN psychotropic use. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action, evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* Across Stages 1 through 3, the patient's renal function, fluid status, and urine output change. Classify the trajectory of the acute kidney injury and determine whether the current interventions are effective. *Correct action:* The creatinine rose from a baseline of 0.9 to 1.3 mg/dL on admission (consistent with prerenal acute kidney injury from dehydration and infection), then improved to 1.1 at Stage 2 and 1.0 at Stage 3. BUN fell from 32 to 24, sodium corrected from 146 to 140, and urine output increased from 40 mL in two hours (Stage 1) to 320 mL in eight hours (Stage 3) with improving color. The trajectory confirms that the prerenal injury is resolving with IV hydration and antibiotic treatment of the underlying infection. *Why it is correct:* In older adults, dehydration and infection are common causes of prerenal azotemia. The elevated BUN-to-creatinine ratio (approximately 25:1 on admission), hypernatremia, concentrated urine (specific gravity 1.028), and decreased output are classic prerenal cues. Improvement in all four parameters after volume resuscitation confirms the kidney injury was volume-responsive and the interventions are working. Failure to track this trajectory could lead to either unnecessary nephrology consultation or premature discontinuation of fluids. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3 (requires comparison of values across all three stages).

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the daughter asks when her mother will return to normal. The patient is improved — following commands, recognizing her daughter, eating — but the CAM is still positive and she remains disoriented to place and time. The nurse must provide accurate, therapeutic communication about the expected course of delirium resolution in an older adult with underlying dementia. *Correct action:* The nurse acknowledges the daughter's distress, validates that the improvement is real and encouraging, and explains that delirium in older adults — especially those with pre-existing dementia — often takes days to weeks to fully resolve, even after the infection is treated. The nurse clarifies that the goal is return to the patient's *baseline* cognitive function (her pre-illness level, not a higher level), and that full resolution may lag behind the medical recovery. The nurse also explains that the assisted-living facility should be updated on the patient's current functional and cognitive status so that appropriate supervision is in place at discharge, and that follow-up cognitive assessment after recovery will help distinguish any residual delirium from the underlying dementia. *Why it is correct:* Delirium resolution is not instantaneous; in older adults with dementia, the median duration of delirium symptoms is 7–10 days, and some patients experience persistent subsyndromal symptoms for weeks. Setting realistic expectations prevents the family from interpreting slow improvement as permanent decline and making premature decisions about level of care. Discharge planning must account for the fact that the patient may still be delirious at discharge and will need closer supervision than her pre-illness baseline. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the night-shift nurse documents a Braden Scale score of 14 and a high fall-risk score. The patient has not eaten, is picking at her IV, and is sleeping only in brief agitated bursts. Determine the priority nursing interventions to reduce the risk of hospital-acquired complications in this patient. *Correct action:* The priority interventions are: implement a fall-prevention bundle (bed in lowest position, bed alarm active, non-skid footwear at bedside, call light within reach, hourly purposeful rounding), initiate pressure-injury prevention (reposition every two hours, place a pressure-redistribution mattress, keep skin clean and dry given urinary incontinence, apply moisture-barrier cream to the perineum), address nutritional intake (offer small frequent meals and fluids with cueing, consult a dietitian if intake does not improve within 24 hours, and continue IV fluids to maintain hydration), and minimize sleep disruption (cluster care activities, reduce nighttime noise and light, maintain a day-night cycle with curtains open during the day). *Why it is correct:* Hospitalized older adults with delirium are at compounded risk for falls, pressure injuries, malnutrition, and prolonged delirium from sleep deprivation. A Braden score of 14 indicates mild risk and mandates preventive interventions. The patient's agitation, confusion, incontinence, immobility, and poor intake create a convergence of risk factors that the nurse must address proactively — waiting for a complication to occur before intervening violates the prevention standard. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Attributing the acute behavioral change to progression of Alzheimer disease rather than recognizing it as delirium. This error leads to therapeutic nihilism — the nurse or family may accept the decline as inevitable and fail to pursue aggressive treatment of the reversible precipitant (UTI). The key distinguishing features are the acute onset, the fluctuating course, and the inattention, none of which characterize the slow, steady trajectory of Alzheimer disease.

- *(Decision point 1)* Dismissing the positive urinalysis as asymptomatic bacteriuria because the patient is an older woman. In this case the patient has systemic signs of infection (fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis with bandemia) and a temporally linked acute mental-status change, which together constitute symptomatic UTI. Asymptomatic bacteriuria is common in older women and should not be treated, but this presentation is not asymptomatic — the delirium is the symptom.

- *(Decision point 2)* Applying bilateral wrist restraints as the first intervention for the patient's combative behavior. Restraints increase agitation, prolong delirium, raise the risk of aspiration and strangulation, and are associated with worse outcomes in older adults. They are a last resort after all non-pharmacologic and pharmacologic strategies have failed and the patient remains at imminent risk of serious self-harm.

- *(Decision point 2)* Requesting an immediate sedative (benzodiazepine) for the agitation. Benzodiazepines are themselves a precipitant of delirium in older adults and worsen confusion, increase fall risk, and cause respiratory depression. They are not first-line for hyperactive delirium outside of alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal.

- *(Decision point 3)* Administering the PRN haloperidol without first attempting and documenting non-pharmacologic de-escalation. The order explicitly requires that non-pharmacologic measures fail first. Skipping this step exposes the patient to unnecessary medication risk and violates the conditions of the order.

- *(Decision point 3)* Failing to obtain or verify a baseline ECG before giving haloperidol. Without knowing the patient's QT interval, the nurse cannot safely administer a QT-prolonging medication. This omission could result in an undetected prolonged QT and a fatal arrhythmia.

- *(Decision point 4)* Interpreting the creatinine of 1.3 mg/dL as the patient's normal value because it is only mildly elevated, and therefore not recognizing the acute kidney injury. Comparing the admission value to the documented baseline of 0.9 reveals a significant acute rise. Failing to recognize the prerenal component could lead to inadequate fluid resuscitation or continuation of nephrotoxic agents.

- *(Decision point 5)* Telling the daughter that the patient should be "back to normal in a day or two" once the antibiotic finishes. This sets an unrealistic expectation. Delirium resolution in older adults with underlying dementia is typically prolonged, and overpromising leads to family distress and potential conflict when the patient does not recover on that timeline.

- *(Decision point 5)* Recommending immediate transfer to a memory-care facility based on the current presentation. The patient's current cognitive and functional status reflects the delirium, not her baseline dementia. Decisions about level of care should be deferred until the delirium has resolved and the patient's function can be reassessed against her true baseline.

- *(Decision point 6)* Focusing exclusively on fall prevention while neglecting pressure-injury risk and nutritional status. The Braden score of 14, the incontinence, the immobility, and the poor oral intake create a high-risk convergence. Addressing only one domain leaves the patient vulnerable to preventable harm in the others.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Differentiate delirium from baseline dementia using the Confusion Assessment Method, collateral history, and objective clinical data.
- Identify urinary tract infection as a common reversible precipitant of delirium in older adults and distinguish symptomatic UTI from asymptomatic bacteriuria.
- Prioritize non-pharmacologic interventions for hyperactive delirium and articulate the rationale for avoiding restraints and benzodiazepines in this population.
- Evaluate the trajectory of delirium resolution and prerenal acute kidney injury across serial assessments to determine whether interventions are effective.
- Provide accurate, therapeutic communication to family members about the expected time course of delirium recovery in an older adult with underlying dementia.
