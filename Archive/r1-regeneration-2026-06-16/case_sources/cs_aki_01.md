**CASE TITLE**
Prerenal acute kidney injury with hyperkalemia in acute-on-chronic kidney disease.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 65-year-old man is transferred from a skilled nursing facility to the emergency department. He has a documented history of type 2 diabetes mellitus managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily and hypertension managed with lisinopril 20 mg daily. His baseline renal function, documented three months ago at a primary care visit, shows a serum creatinine of 1.4 mg/dL with an estimated GFR of 48 mL/min/1.73 m², consistent with stage 3a chronic kidney disease. He also carries a diagnosis of osteoarthritis for which he occasionally takes over-the-counter ibuprofen, most recently two days ago per the facility medication administration record. The skilled nursing facility reports a confirmed norovirus outbreak; this patient has had profuse watery diarrhea and vomiting for approximately 72 hours. Oral intake has been minimal. The facility attempted oral rehydration but the patient could not keep fluids down. He was transferred when staff noted increasing confusion and near-absent urine output over the preceding 12 hours. On arrival, the emergency physician places orders for peripheral IV access, a 500 mL normal saline bolus to begin immediately, continuous cardiac monitoring, a 12-lead ECG, a basic metabolic panel, CBC, urinalysis with microscopy, a point-of-care blood glucose, and a bladder scan. The admitting provider writes to hold metformin and lisinopril pending renal function review.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient arrives on a stretcher, appearing dry and fatigued. He is oriented to person only, repeatedly asking where he is and what day it is. His lips are cracked and his mucous membranes are tacky. He reports diffuse abdominal cramping and nausea. He has not urinated in several hours. A family member at the bedside states he has been "not himself" for the past day and a half, increasingly drowsy and confused, and that the facility told her about the norovirus outbreak.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 37.2 °C, heart rate 112 and regular, blood pressure 88/54, respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Weight 82 kg (facility weight one week ago was 86 kg — a 4 kg loss). The patient is drowsy but arousable to voice; he follows simple commands but drifts off when not stimulated. Skin turgor is poor with tenting over the sternum lasting more than three seconds. Mucous membranes are dry. Capillary refill is four seconds in the fingertips. Jugular venous distension is absent; the neck veins are flat even when supine. Heart sounds are tachycardic without murmur, rub, or gallop. Lungs are clear bilaterally with no crackles or wheezes. Abdomen is soft, mildly tender diffusely, with hyperactive bowel sounds in all four quadrants. No peripheral edema. Extremities are cool to the touch with weak but palpable radial and dorsalis pedis pulses bilaterally. A bladder scan shows 40 mL of urine in the bladder, ruling out urinary retention. The 12-lead ECG shows sinus tachycardia at 112 bpm with peaked, narrow-based T waves most prominent in leads V2–V4 and leads II and III. The PR interval is 0.18 seconds and the QRS duration is 0.10 seconds — both within normal limits at this time. No ST-segment elevation or depression is noted.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Admission labs: sodium 134 mEq/L, potassium 6.1 mEq/L, chloride 96 mEq/L, bicarbonate 18 mEq/L, BUN 68 mg/dL, creatinine 4.2 mg/dL (baseline 1.4), BUN-to-creatinine ratio 16.2:1 (calculated), glucose 188 mg/dL, calcium 8.4 mg/dL, phosphorus 5.1 mg/dL, magnesium 1.6 mg/dL. CBC: WBC 11,200/µL, hemoglobin 13.8 g/dL (hemoconcentrated), platelets 210,000/µL. Venous blood gas: pH 7.30, pCO₂ 32 mmHg, lactate 2.8 mmol/L. Urinalysis: specific gravity 1.032, urine sodium 8 mEq/L, no protein, no blood, no casts on microscopy, trace ketones. Fractional excretion of sodium (calculated by the provider and documented): 0.4%.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 2 hours after arrival. The initial 500 mL normal saline bolus runs in over 30 minutes. The provider orders a second 500 mL bolus and then a continuous infusion at 150 mL/hr. The nurse places a Foley catheter; initial drainage is 30 mL of dark, concentrated urine. Over the next 90 minutes, urine output totals only 18 mL (approximately 0.15 mL/kg/hr). The patient remains confused, oriented to person only. Repeat vitals at 90 minutes: HR 106, BP 92/58, RR 18, SpO₂ 97%. The cardiac monitor continues to show peaked T waves. The nurse notes the patient's home medication list includes metformin, lisinopril, and ibuprofen as needed. The admitting provider has written to hold metformin and lisinopril but has not addressed the ibuprofen. The facility medication administration record confirms the patient received ibuprofen 400 mg two days ago. No new labs have been drawn yet at this point.

**Stage 2** — 2 to 6 hours after arrival. A second set of labs is drawn at the 4-hour mark. Results: potassium 6.4 mEq/L (rising from 6.1), creatinine 4.5 mg/dL (rising from 4.2), BUN 72 mg/dL, bicarbonate 16 mEq/L (falling from 18), lactate 3.2 mmol/L (rising from 2.8), glucose 162 mg/dL. The ECG now shows PR prolongation to 0.24 seconds and the QRS has widened to 0.12 seconds, in addition to the persistent peaked T waves. The patient has become more lethargic, responding only to direct verbal stimulation with brief eye opening and monosyllabic answers. He reports tingling in his fingers and muscle cramping in his calves. Urine output over the preceding 4 hours totals 45 mL (approximately 0.14 mL/kg/hr). The provider orders emergent hyperkalemia management per the facility's critical hyperkalemia protocol: calcium gluconate 1 g IV at the protocol rate for cardiac membrane stabilization, followed by regular insulin 10 units IV with 25 g dextrose (D50W 50 mL) IV push, and sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq IV over 30 minutes. Sodium polystyrene sulfonate 30 g orally is also ordered but the patient is actively nauseated. The provider places a nephrology consult and writes an order for strict intake and output with hourly urine measurement. Total IV fluid received to this point is approximately 1,500 mL.

**Stage 3** — 6 to 14 hours after arrival. The calcium gluconate was administered at hour 4.5; the insulin and dextrose were given at hour 5. By hour 8, repeat labs show: potassium 5.4 mEq/L (down from 6.4), creatinine 4.3 mg/dL (slight decrease from 4.5), BUN 70 mg/dL, bicarbonate 19 mEq/L, lactate 2.0 mmol/L, glucose 134 mg/dL. The ECG at hour 8 shows resolution of the PR prolongation (now 0.18 seconds) and the QRS has narrowed back to 0.10 seconds; peaked T waves are diminished but still present. Urine output from hours 6 to 10 improves to 35 mL/hr (approximately 0.43 mL/kg/hr). The patient is now oriented to person and place, answering questions in short sentences, and no longer drifting off. Blood pressure is 104/66, heart rate 92, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Skin turgor has improved. Nephrology has evaluated the patient and recommends continuing isotonic fluid resuscitation with a target urine output of greater than 0.5 mL/kg/hr, rechecking a metabolic panel every 6 hours, holding all nephrotoxic medications, and reassessing for renal replacement therapy if urine output does not continue to improve or if potassium rises again. By hour 14, urine output has reached 48 mL/hr (0.59 mL/kg/hr). Repeat potassium is 4.9 mEq/L. Creatinine is 3.8 mg/dL. The patient is alert, oriented to person, place, and time, and tolerating small sips of clear liquids without vomiting. Blood pressure is 112/70, heart rate 84.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* On initial assessment, the nurse reviews the admission vital signs, physical examination, and laboratory data together. Multiple findings are present — tachycardia, hypotension, poor skin turgor, flat neck veins, dry mucous membranes, peaked T waves on ECG, concentrated urine with a specific gravity of 1.032, urine sodium of 8 mEq/L, fractional excretion of sodium of 0.4%, BUN-to-creatinine ratio of 16.2:1, creatinine of 4.2 against a baseline of 1.4, potassium of 6.1, bicarbonate of 18, lactate of 2.8, hemoglobin of 13.8, clear lung sounds, and a bladder scan of 40 mL. The nurse must identify which findings are consistent with prerenal acute kidney injury from volume depletion, which reflect the dangerous hyperkalemia, and which are normal or unrelated baseline values. *Correct action:* The findings that together establish the prerenal AKI pattern are the concentrated urine (specific gravity 1.032), the very low urine sodium (8 mEq/L), the low fractional excretion of sodium (0.4%), the acute creatinine rise from 1.4 to 4.2, and the clinical signs of volume depletion (hypotension, tachycardia, poor skin turgor, flat neck veins, dry mucous membranes, weight loss of 4 kg, cool extremities with delayed capillary refill). The peaked T waves on ECG and the potassium of 6.1 reflect dangerous hyperkalemia. The clear lung sounds are a relevant normal finding — they confirm the absence of fluid overload, supporting the volume-depleted picture. The hemoglobin of 13.8 is mildly elevated from hemoconcentration, consistent with dehydration rather than a primary hematologic problem. The bladder scan of 40 mL rules out obstructive (post-renal) pathology. *Why it is correct:* In prerenal AKI, the kidneys are still functioning — they respond to decreased perfusion by avidly reabsorbing sodium and concentrating urine, producing a urine sodium below 20 mEq/L, a fractional excretion of sodium below 1%, and a high urine specific gravity. The BUN is elevated, but the BUN:Cr ratio is not classic for prerenal AKI; the prerenal pattern is supported primarily by profound volume depletion, urine sodium 8, FENa 0.4%, concentrated urine, and response to fluids. The peaked T waves are the earliest ECG manifestation of hyperkalemia and signal myocardial membrane instability. Recognizing these patterns as a coherent syndrome — not isolated abnormalities — is the foundation for all subsequent decisions. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Admission data (before Stage 1).

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse reviews the patient's home medication list — metformin 1000 mg twice daily, lisinopril 20 mg daily, and ibuprofen 400 mg as needed (last taken two days ago). The admitting provider has written orders to hold metformin and lisinopril but has not addressed the ibuprofen. The nurse must analyze the significance of each medication in the context of this patient's acute kidney injury and determine whether any action is needed regarding the ibuprofen. *Correct action:* All three medications are harmful in this clinical context and must be held. Hold metformin during this AKI/dehydrating illness because impaired renal clearance increases risk of accumulation and lactic acidosis. The elevated lactate and metabolic acidosis increase concern and require monitoring, but are also plausibly from hypoperfusion. Lisinopril must be held because ACE inhibitors reduce efferent arteriolar tone, which further decreases glomerular filtration pressure in a patient whose kidneys are already underperfused from volume depletion — continuing it would worsen the prerenal injury. The ibuprofen, though taken two days ago and not currently being administered, has not been addressed in the orders. The nurse should clarify with the provider that ibuprofen is explicitly held and documented as contraindicated during this AKI episode, because NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar dilation, reducing renal blood flow and directly opposing the compensatory mechanism that preserves GFR during hypovolemia. The nurse should also ensure the patient and family understand not to take any over-the-counter NSAIDs during recovery. *Why it is correct:* Each medication impairs renal perfusion or clearance through a distinct mechanism. The metformin risk is lactic acidosis from drug accumulation, not hypoglycemia — metformin does not cause hypoglycemia as a primary effect. The lisinopril risk is worsening prerenal physiology by removing the angiotensin II–mediated efferent arteriolar constriction that maintains filtration pressure when renal perfusion is low. The ibuprofen risk is blocking the prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar dilation that is a critical compensatory response to hypovolemia. The nurse's role is to verify that all nephrotoxic and renally cleared medications are held and to clarify any omission with the provider. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1 (when the medication list and the provider's incomplete hold orders are both visible).

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient has received approximately 1,500 mL of IV fluid but urine output remains critically low at 0.14 mL/kg/hr, creatinine has risen to 4.5, potassium has risen to 6.4 with progressive ECG changes (PR prolongation to 0.24 seconds, QRS widening to 0.12 seconds), and the patient is more lethargic with neuromuscular symptoms. The nurse must determine the most urgent problem requiring immediate intervention. Two competing concerns are present: the persistent oliguria suggesting the AKI may be progressing beyond a purely prerenal process, and the worsening hyperkalemia with ECG conduction changes. *Correct action:* The worsening hyperkalemia with progressive ECG changes is the most immediately life-threatening problem and takes priority. A potassium of 6.4 with PR prolongation and QRS widening indicates advancing cardiac conduction toxicity that can progress to ventricular fibrillation or asystole. While the oliguria and rising creatinine are serious and must be addressed, they do not pose the same minute-to-minute risk of cardiac arrest. The nurse should anticipate and prepare for emergent hyperkalemia management — specifically, the administration of calcium gluconate for cardiac membrane stabilization as the first intervention. *Why it is correct:* Hyperkalemia-induced cardiac conduction changes follow a predictable and dangerous progression: peaked T waves → PR prolongation → QRS widening → loss of P waves → sine wave pattern → ventricular fibrillation or asystole. This patient has already progressed past peaked T waves to PR prolongation and QRS widening, placing him at high risk for a lethal arrhythmia. The oliguria is a serious marker of renal injury, but it does not carry the same immediate risk of death within minutes. Prioritizing the hyperkalemia does not mean ignoring the renal failure — both are addressed, but the cardiac emergency comes first. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the provider has ordered the facility's critical hyperkalemia protocol: calcium gluconate 1 g IV at the protocol rate, then regular insulin 10 units IV with 25 g dextrose IV push, then sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq IV over 30 minutes, and sodium polystyrene sulfonate 30 g orally. The nurse must determine the correct sequence and identify any concern with the orders as written. *Correct action:* The calcium gluconate must be administered first because it stabilizes the cardiac membrane without lowering potassium — it buys time for the potassium-lowering measures to work. The insulin with dextrose is given next because it shifts potassium intracellularly within 15 to 30 minutes, producing the most rapid actual reduction in serum potassium. The sodium bicarbonate follows, also shifting potassium intracellularly though more slowly. However, the nurse should question the oral sodium polystyrene sulfonate order: the patient is actively nauseated and has been vomiting — oral administration risks aspiration and is unlikely to be retained. Question the oral SPS order because the patient is vomiting and because binders are not the immediate life-saving intervention. Clarify with the provider/pharmacy/nephrology about local protocol options such as SZC/patiromer when appropriate, or dialysis if hyperkalemia persists/rebounds. Additionally, the nurse must plan to monitor blood glucose closely after the insulin administration — the dextrose is given to prevent hypoglycemia, but blood glucose should be rechecked at 30 and 60 minutes post-insulin, and additional dextrose should be available. *Why it is correct:* Calcium gluconate does not lower potassium; it antagonizes the effect of potassium on the myocardial cell membrane, reducing the risk of arrhythmia while other treatments work. Its effect is rapid (onset within 1 to 3 minutes) but temporary (lasting 30 to 60 minutes), so potassium-lowering measures must follow promptly. Insulin drives potassium into cells via stimulation of the sodium-potassium ATPase pump. Administering an oral medication to a vomiting patient is both ineffective (the drug will not be retained) and unsafe (aspiration risk), and the nurse has a responsibility to clarify this order rather than attempt to administer it. The sequence — stabilize the membrane, then shift potassium, then eliminate potassium — follows a physiologically logical order from most urgent to definitive. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, with the hyperkalemia protocol ordered and the patient on continuous cardiac monitoring with a Foley catheter in place, the nurse must carry out the immediate interventions. Multiple actions are needed essentially simultaneously: administer the calcium gluconate, ensure IV access is adequate for the infusion, verify the insulin and dextrose are available and correctly prepared, place the patient on continuous ECG monitoring if not already done, position the patient safely given his altered mental status, and ensure hourly urine output documentation is occurring. *Correct action:* The nurse should administer the calcium gluconate 1 g IV at the ordered rate through a patent, well-positioned peripheral IV while observing the cardiac monitor for response — specifically watching for improvement in the PR interval and QRS width. The IV site must be assessed for patency and signs of infiltration before and during administration, because calcium gluconate is a vesicant that causes tissue necrosis if it extravasates. Simultaneously, the nurse should have the insulin, dextrose, and a glucometer at the bedside. After the calcium gluconate infuses, the nurse administers the insulin 10 units IV push followed immediately by the D50W 50 mL IV push, and sets a timer to recheck blood glucose in 30 minutes. The nurse raises the side rails and ensures the call light is within reach given the patient's altered mental status. Strict intake and output documentation continues with hourly urine measurements recorded. The nurse notifies the charge nurse of the patient's acuity and ensures the nephrology consult has been placed. *Why it is correct:* Calcium gluconate administration requires vigilant IV site monitoring because extravasation causes severe tissue injury. The medication must be given at the ordered rate — too rapid an infusion can cause bradycardia or cardiac arrest. Insulin-induced hypoglycemia is a predictable and preventable complication of hyperkalemia treatment; the dextrose is co-administered to offset it, but glucose monitoring must follow because the insulin effect outlasts the dextrose bolus. Fall prevention and safety measures are essential for a confused, lethargic patient. These actions occur as a coordinated set rather than a rigid sequence — the nurse manages them in parallel, with the calcium gluconate taking temporal priority because it addresses the immediate cardiac risk. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse evaluates the patient's response to treatment. Multiple parameters have changed: potassium has decreased from 6.4 to 5.4 and then to 4.9, creatinine has decreased from 4.5 to 4.3 and then to 3.8, the ECG has normalized (PR interval 0.18 seconds, QRS 0.10 seconds, peaked T waves diminishing), urine output has improved from 0.14 mL/kg/hr to 0.43 and then to 0.59 mL/kg/hr, blood pressure has improved from 92/58 to 112/70, heart rate has decreased from 106 to 84, mental status has improved from oriented to person only to fully oriented, and lactate has decreased from 3.2 to 2.0 mmol/L. The nurse must determine which findings confirm that the patient is improving and which findings still require close monitoring. *Correct action:* The findings that confirm improvement are the normalizing ECG (resolution of PR prolongation and QRS widening confirms the hyperkalemia-driven cardiac toxicity is resolving), the downward trend in potassium (6.4 → 5.4 → 4.9, now approaching the normal range), the improving urine output (now exceeding the 0.5 mL/kg/hr threshold that indicates adequate renal perfusion), the downward trend in creatinine (4.5 → 4.3 → 3.8, indicating the acute injury is beginning to reverse), the improving hemodynamics (blood pressure and heart rate normalizing), and the improving mental status (reflecting improved cerebral perfusion and metabolic status). However, the potassium at 4.9 is still at the upper limit of normal and could rebound as the insulin effect wears off — the nurse must continue to monitor potassium levels and the ECG. The creatinine, while trending down, remains far above baseline (3.8 versus 1.4), and the patient is not yet out of danger for requiring renal replacement therapy if the trajectory reverses. Urine output must continue to be monitored hourly. The nurse should also monitor for the return of vomiting or diarrhea, which could restart the volume depletion cycle. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes requires tracking the trajectory of the key parameters, not just their absolute values at a single point. A single improved potassium does not confirm sustained resolution — insulin-mediated intracellular potassium shift is temporary (duration 4 to 6 hours), and the total body potassium burden has not been eliminated, only redistributed. The creatinine trend is encouraging but the patient remains in significant AKI. The urine output crossing the 0.5 mL/kg/hr threshold is a meaningful positive sign that renal perfusion is recovering, consistent with the prerenal etiology responding to volume resuscitation. The improving mental status and hemodynamics corroborate the overall positive trajectory but do not eliminate the need for continued vigilance. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Interpreting the low urine sodium and concentrated urine as evidence that the kidneys are "failing" or "shutting down" rather than recognizing these as signs that the kidneys are functioning appropriately in response to volume depletion. In prerenal AKI, the kidneys are doing exactly what they should — conserving sodium and water in response to decreased perfusion. This is the hallmark that distinguishes prerenal from intrinsic renal injury, where the damaged tubules lose concentrating ability and urine sodium rises above 40 mEq/L.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Dismissing the peaked T waves on ECG as a nonspecific or anxiety-related finding rather than recognizing them as the earliest sign of hyperkalemia-induced cardiac toxicity. Peaked T waves in the setting of a potassium of 6.1 are a critical cue that demands immediate attention, not a benign variant.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Attributing the risk of metformin in AKI to hypoglycemia rather than lactic acidosis. Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia — it reduces hepatic glucose production and improves insulin sensitivity without stimulating insulin secretion. The danger of metformin in AKI is accumulation of the drug due to impaired renal clearance, which inhibits mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and promotes lactate production. The elevated lactate and metabolic acidosis increase concern and require monitoring, but are also plausibly from hypoperfusion.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Failing to address the ibuprofen because it was taken two days ago and is not a current scheduled medication. The nurse may reason that since the drug was taken in the past and is not being actively administered, it does not require action. However, the NSAID's effect on renal prostaglandin synthesis may still be contributing to the current renal insult, and more importantly, the medication must be explicitly held and documented as contraindicated to prevent inadvertent re-administration during the hospitalization or after discharge.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Prioritizing the oliguria and rising creatinine over the hyperkalemia with ECG changes, reasoning that "fixing the kidneys" will fix the potassium. While restoring renal function is essential for long-term potassium clearance, the immediate threat is the cardiac conduction toxicity. A patient can survive oliguria for hours while awaiting renal recovery or dialysis; a patient with progressive QRS widening can arrest within minutes.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Administering the insulin and dextrose before the calcium gluconate, reasoning that lowering the potassium is the primary goal. This is a dangerous sequencing error. Insulin takes 15 to 30 minutes to shift potassium intracellularly, during which time the myocardium remains vulnerable to arrhythmia. Calcium gluconate stabilizes the membrane within 1 to 3 minutes and must come first.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Attempting to administer the oral sodium polystyrene sulfonate to a vomiting patient without questioning the order. This risks aspiration and is unlikely to achieve the intended potassium elimination because the medication will not be retained. The nurse has a professional obligation to clarify orders that pose a safety risk.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Administering calcium gluconate rapidly as an IV push rather than at the ordered rate, reasoning that the situation is emergent and speed is paramount. Rapid IV calcium administration can cause bradycardia, hypotension, or cardiac arrest. Administer at the ordered/protocol rate with continuous ECG and IV-site monitoring; do not give as an uncontrolled rapid IV push.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the potassium of 4.9 as "normal" and discontinuing cardiac monitoring or reducing monitoring frequency. The potassium is at the upper limit of normal and may rebound as the temporary effects of insulin-mediated intracellular shift wear off. Continued ECG monitoring and serial potassium checks are essential until the potassium is sustainably in the normal range without ongoing shifting agents.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the improving urine output as definitive evidence that the patient no longer needs nephrology follow-up or consideration of renal replacement therapy. The creatinine remains nearly three times baseline, and the trajectory could reverse if the patient resumes vomiting, develops a new insult, or if the underlying injury has a component of acute tubular necrosis that has not yet declared itself.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the clinical and laboratory pattern of prerenal acute kidney injury — concentrated urine, low urine sodium, low fractional excretion of sodium, and signs of volume depletion — and distinguish it from intrinsic renal and obstructive causes.

Analyze the significance of each nephrotoxic medication (metformin, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs) in the context of AKI, including the specific mechanism of harm for each and the nurse's responsibility to verify that all are held and to clarify incomplete orders.

Prioritize hyperkalemia with progressive ECG changes as the most immediately life-threatening complication in AKI, taking precedence over the underlying renal failure in the acute management sequence.

Sequence emergent hyperkalemia interventions correctly — cardiac membrane stabilization with calcium gluconate first, followed by potassium-shifting agents, followed by potassium elimination — and identify unsafe administration practices including giving oral medications to a vomiting patient and infusing calcium gluconate too rapidly.

Evaluate the trajectory of recovery in prerenal AKI by tracking potassium trends, ECG normalization, urine output response to volume resuscitation, creatinine trajectory, and clinical indicators of perfusion, while recognizing that temporary improvements from shifting agents do not confirm sustained resolution.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the patient has received 1,500 mL of IV fluid with minimal urine output response, potassium has risen to 6.4 with progressive ECG conduction changes (PR prolongation and QRS widening), creatinine continues to climb, and the full clinical picture — 72 hours of GI losses, profound volume depletion, acute-on-chronic kidney injury, worsening hyperkalemia with cardiac toxicity, and three nephrotoxic medications on the home list — has fully declared.

Most likely condition: Prerenal acute kidney injury with life-threatening hyperkalemia. The combination of severe volume depletion from 72 hours of vomiting and diarrhea, hypotension, clinical signs of dehydration, a creatinine tripled from baseline, urine sodium of 8 mEq/L, fractional excretion of sodium of 0.4%, concentrated urine, and a potassium of 6.4 with progressive ECG conduction changes constitutes an unambiguous prerenal AKI with dangerous hyperkalemia as the immediately life-threatening complication.

Plausible competing conditions: (1) Acute tubular necrosis — tempting because the patient has not responded robustly to initial fluid resuscitation and the creatinine continues to rise, but wrong at this moment because the urine sodium remains very low (8 mEq/L), the fractional excretion of sodium is 0.4% (well below the 1% threshold), the urine is maximally concentrated, and the urinalysis shows no muddy brown granular casts — all of which indicate the tubules are still functioning and the injury remains prerenal. ATN would show urine sodium above 40 mEq/L, FENa above 2%, dilute urine, and characteristic casts. (2) Obstructive (post-renal) acute kidney injury — tempting in an elderly male with rising creatinine and oliguria, but wrong because the bladder scan showed only 40 mL, ruling out urinary retention, and there are no signs of hydronephrosis or obstruction; the clinical context of profuse GI losses and volume depletion fully accounts for the presentation.

Two priority actions: (1) Administer calcium gluconate 1 g IV at the protocol rate per the provider's order to stabilize the cardiac membrane against hyperkalemia-induced arrhythmia — this is the single most time-critical intervention because the ECG already shows PR prolongation and QRS widening, indicating advancing conduction toxicity that can progress to a lethal arrhythmia within minutes. (2) Administer regular insulin 10 units IV with 25 g dextrose IV push immediately after the calcium gluconate to shift potassium intracellularly and begin lowering the serum potassium level — this is the most effective rapid potassium-lowering measure available and must follow membrane stabilization without delay.

Two wrong actions: (1) Administering the insulin and dextrose before the calcium gluconate — wrong sequence because the insulin takes 15 to 30 minutes to shift potassium, during which time the myocardium remains unprotected against the conduction toxicity already visible on ECG; calcium gluconate stabilizes the membrane within 1 to 3 minutes and must come first. (2) Attempting to administer the oral sodium polystyrene sulfonate to the actively vomiting patient — wrong because the patient cannot retain the oral medication, making it ineffective for potassium elimination, and the attempt risks aspiration; question the oral SPS order because the patient is vomiting and binders are not the immediate life-saving intervention; clarify with the provider about local protocol options.

Two parameters to monitor: (1) ECG morphology — specifically the PR interval, QRS duration, and T-wave morphology — because normalization of these conduction parameters is the most direct and immediate indicator that the hyperkalemia-driven cardiac toxicity is resolving and the membrane stabilization and potassium-shifting interventions are working. (2) Hourly urine output — because recovery of urine output above 0.5 mL/kg/hr is the key indicator that renal perfusion is being restored by volume resuscitation, confirming the prerenal etiology and indicating that the kidneys are recovering their ability to clear potassium and other waste products; failure of urine output to improve despite adequate fluid resuscitation would signal progression to intrinsic injury and the need to reassess the plan.

Two irrelevant parameters: (1) Hemoglobin trend — while hemoglobin was mildly elevated on admission from hemoconcentration, tracking its trend is not a primary resolution marker for AKI and hyperkalemia; it reflects volume status but is less direct and reliable than urine output, creatinine, and potassium levels. (2) Urine specific gravity — while it was a useful diagnostic cue at admission to confirm the prerenal pattern, it is not the right parameter to track resolution; as the patient receives IV fluids, the urine will dilute regardless of whether renal function is truly recovering, making specific gravity an unreliable marker of improvement compared to urine output volume and creatinine trend.
