**CASE TITLE**
Late postpartum preeclampsia with severe features presenting after discharge.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 29-year-old primipara, gravida 1 para 1, delivered a healthy 3,400-gram infant vaginally at 39 weeks and 2 days gestation without complications. Her prenatal course was unremarkable: all blood pressures were documented below 130/80 throughout pregnancy, urine protein screens were negative at each visit, and she had no history of chronic hypertension, renal disease, autoimmune conditions, or gestational hypertension. She was discharged on postpartum day 1 with a blood pressure of 118/74, lochia rubra in expected amount, fundus firm at the umbilicus, and no edema. Discharge medications included prenatal vitamins, ibuprofen 600 mg every 6 hours as needed for perineal discomfort, and docusate sodium. She is breastfeeding. She has no known drug allergies. She lives with her partner and her mother, who has been helping with the newborn. She was instructed at discharge to call the postpartum triage line for any concerns including headache, vision changes, or blood pressure above 140/90 on her home cuff.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
On postpartum day 4, the patient calls the postpartum triage line at 10:15 AM. She reports a severe frontal headache rated 8 out of 10 that began when she woke this morning and has not responded to ibuprofen. She describes "seeing spots" intermittently, particularly when she looks at bright surfaces. She noticed significant puffiness in her face and around her eyes this morning, which her mother commented was markedly different from her appearance the day before. She denies chest pain, shortness of breath, epigastric or right upper quadrant pain, nausea, and vomiting. She reports her home blood pressure cuff reading is 162/108. She took the reading twice, five minutes apart, and both readings were similar. She is alert, speaking in full sentences, and oriented but sounds anxious. She states she almost did not call because her mother told her headaches and swelling were "normal after having a baby."

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Home assessment via triage call: patient reports blood pressure 162/108 (repeated and confirmed), severe frontal headache 8/10 unrelieved by ibuprofen, intermittent scotomata described as flashing spots, and new-onset facial and periorbital edema. She denies epigastric pain, right upper quadrant tenderness, dyspnea, and seizure activity. She reports her lochia has transitioned from rubra to serosa and is light in amount. She is voiding without difficulty and estimates adequate urine output. She has no calf tenderness, no chest pain, and no focal weakness or numbness. She is afebrile by touch. She reports sleeping poorly due to newborn care but denies confusion or altered mentation. Her speech is coherent and appropriate.

**LABORATORY DATA**
No laboratory data are available at the time of the triage call. Baseline labs from the delivery admission were within normal limits: hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, hematocrit 35%, platelets 238,000/µL, AST 22 U/L, ALT 18 U/L, creatinine 0.7 mg/dL, uric acid 4.1 mg/dL, LDH 162 U/L, urinalysis negative for protein.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1 — Triage call and emergency department arrival (postpartum day 4, 10:15 AM to 11:30 AM).**
The triage nurse instructs the patient to come to the emergency department immediately and not to drive herself. Her partner drives her. She is told not to take any additional medications at home and to bring her home blood pressure log. Upon arrival at 11:10 AM, the ED nurse obtains vitals: T 37.1 °C, HR 96, BP 168/112, RR 18, SpO₂ 99% on room air. A repeat blood pressure five minutes later is 164/110. The patient appears uncomfortable, squinting under the fluorescent lights, and reports the headache is now 9/10. Facial and periorbital edema is visually prominent and bilateral. Neurological assessment shows the patient is alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation; pupils are equal, round, and reactive; cranial nerves are grossly intact; deep tendon reflexes are 3+ at the biceps and patella bilaterally with two beats of clonus at the ankles; grip strength is equal and strong bilaterally. Heart sounds are regular without murmur. Lungs are clear bilaterally. Abdomen is soft, non-tender, with a firm fundus palpable just above the symphysis pubis. Lower extremities show 1+ pitting edema bilaterally. A urine specimen is obtained by clean catch: dipstick shows 3+ protein. IV access is established with an 18-gauge catheter in the left forearm. A Foley catheter is inserted; initial output is 150 mL of clear yellow urine. Stat labs are drawn: CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, LDH, uric acid, coagulation studies, and a protein-to-creatinine ratio on the urine specimen.

**Stage 2 — Acute treatment initiation (postpartum day 4, 11:30 AM to 1:00 PM).**
The obstetric provider is notified and arrives at bedside. Orders are received: magnesium sulfate 4 grams IV loading dose over 20 minutes, then 2 grams per hour continuous infusion for seizure prophylaxis; immediate-release oral nifedipine 10 mg now for acute blood pressure reduction, with the order specifying immediate-release capsule and not the extended-release formulation; repeat blood pressure every 5 minutes for 20 minutes, then every 15 minutes; seizure precautions including padded side rails, suction at bedside, dimmed lighting, and minimized stimulation. The nurse initiates the magnesium sulfate loading dose at 11:40 AM via infusion pump. At 11:45 AM, the patient takes the nifedipine 10 mg orally. Stat lab results return at 12:15 PM: hemoglobin 12.0 g/dL, hematocrit 36%, platelets 195,000/µL, AST 34 U/L, ALT 28 U/L, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, uric acid 6.8 mg/dL, LDH 198 U/L, INR 1.0, fibrinogen 380 mg/dL, urine protein-to-creatinine ratio 0.42. Serial blood pressures after nifedipine: 158/104 at 11:50 AM, 152/98 at 11:55 AM, 148/96 at 12:00 PM, 146/94 at 12:15 PM. The magnesium loading dose completes at 12:00 PM and the maintenance infusion of 2 grams per hour is running. The patient reports the headache has decreased to 6/10. She still sees occasional spots but less frequently. Deep tendon reflexes remain 3+ with clonus. Urine output via Foley is 60 mL in the first hour. The patient is anxious and asks whether she can continue breastfeeding while on these medications. She can usually continue breastfeeding while receiving nifedipine and magnesium, but she may need lactation support because magnesium therapy and illness/hospitalization can delay lactation.

**Stage 3 — Reassessment and stabilization (postpartum day 4, 1:00 PM to 3:00 PM).**
At 1:00 PM, blood pressure is 142/90. By 1:30 PM it is 138/88. The headache has decreased to 4/10 and the patient reports the visual spots have resolved. Facial edema is unchanged. Deep tendon reflexes are now 2+ bilaterally with no clonus. Respiratory rate is 16. Urine output over the second hour is 45 mL. The magnesium infusion continues at 2 grams per hour. The patient is calmer and resting in a dimly lit room. Repeat blood pressure at 2:00 PM is 140/86. At 2:30 PM, blood pressure is 136/84. The provider orders a transition plan: continue magnesium sulfate infusion for 24 hours from the loading dose, begin oral nifedipine extended-release 30 mg every 12 hours for sustained blood pressure control, and repeat the HELLP panel (CBC, AST, ALT, LDH, creatinine) in 6 hours. The patient asks when she can go home and whether this can happen again. The nurse provides education about the diagnosis of postpartum preeclampsia with severe features, the importance of the 24-hour magnesium course, blood pressure monitoring expectations, and the plan for close outpatient follow-up after discharge. The patient's mother, who is present, states she feels guilty for telling her daughter the symptoms were normal and asks what warning signs she should help watch for in the future.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* During the triage call at Stage 1, the nurse must identify which of the patient's reported findings — severe frontal headache unresponsive to analgesics, scotomata, new facial edema, blood pressure 162/108, light lochia serosa, adequate voiding, absence of chest pain, poor sleep — constitute an urgent clinical cluster requiring immediate action versus expected postpartum findings. *Correct action:* Recognize that the combination of severe headache, visual disturbances (scotomata), new-onset facial edema, and a blood pressure of 162/108 on postpartum day 4 constitutes a preeclampsia-with-severe-features cluster requiring emergent evaluation. The light lochia serosa, adequate voiding, absence of chest pain, and poor sleep from newborn care are expected postpartum findings and do not contribute to the urgent cluster. *Why it is correct:* Late postpartum preeclampsia — onset after 48 hours and up to 6 weeks postpartum — is an underrecognized entity. A blood pressure of 162/108 meets the severe-range threshold (systolic 160 or greater, or diastolic 110 or greater). Severe headache unrelieved by analgesics and scotomata are central nervous system warning signs of impending eclampsia. New-onset facial edema in the context of these findings reflects the endothelial dysfunction and capillary leak of preeclampsia. The fact that the patient was normotensive throughout pregnancy does not reduce the urgency; late postpartum preeclampsia can occur de novo. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, with the confirmed blood pressure readings of 168/112 and 164/110 in the emergency department, the nurse must interpret the blood pressure values in the context of the clinical presentation and determine the severity classification. The patient's labs are not yet available. *Correct action:* Classify the blood pressure as severe-range hypertension. Both readings have a systolic component at or above 160 and a diastolic component at or above 110, meeting the threshold for severe-range blood pressure. Combined with the CNS symptoms (headache, scotomata) and hyperreflexia with clonus, this presentation meets criteria for preeclampsia with severe features even before laboratory confirmation. The 3+ proteinuria on dipstick supports but is not required for the diagnosis when severe-range blood pressure and CNS symptoms are present. *Why it is correct:* The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists defines severe-range blood pressure as systolic 160 or greater or diastolic 110 or greater, confirmed on repeat measurement. Acute treatment must be initiated within 30 to 60 minutes of confirmed severe-range blood pressure to prevent stroke, placental abruption (not applicable postpartum, but the maternal cerebrovascular risk remains), and eclampsia. Hyperreflexia with clonus indicates CNS hyperexcitability and heightened seizure risk. Waiting for lab results to confirm the diagnosis before initiating treatment would be an unsafe delay. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, with lab results now available showing platelets 195,000, AST 34, ALT 28, LDH 198, and creatinine 0.9, the nurse must determine the primary working diagnosis among the competing possibilities: preeclampsia with severe features, postpartum hypertension without severe features, and HELLP syndrome. *Correct action:* The primary diagnosis is preeclampsia with severe features. HELLP syndrome is ruled out by the normal platelet count, normal transaminases, and normal LDH. Postpartum hypertension without severe features is ruled out because the blood pressure meets severe-range criteria and the patient has CNS symptoms (headache, scotomata) and hyperreflexia with clonus — these are defining features of severe disease. The creatinine has risen from baseline but remains below the renal severe-feature threshold (0.9, up from baseline 0.7), and elevated uric acid (6.8, up from 4.1) is consistent with the renal endothelial injury of preeclampsia but does not meet HELLP criteria. *Why it is correct:* HELLP syndrome requires hemolysis (elevated LDH, schistocytes, low haptoglobin), elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets — none of these are present. Postpartum hypertension without severe features would involve blood pressures above 140/90 but below 160/110 and the absence of end-organ symptoms. This patient exceeds both thresholds. The distinction matters because preeclampsia with severe features mandates magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis and acute antihypertensive therapy, whereas hypertension without severe features may be managed with oral antihypertensives alone and does not require magnesium. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2 (requires the lab results to rule out HELLP).

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse receives orders for magnesium sulfate and immediate-release oral nifedipine 10 mg. The patient asks whether the magnesium will bring her blood pressure down. The nurse must understand the purpose of each medication and identify the correct rationale for magnesium sulfate in this setting. *Correct action:* Magnesium sulfate is administered for seizure prophylaxis — to prevent eclamptic seizures — not as an antihypertensive agent. The nifedipine is the antihypertensive. The nurse should clarify to the patient that the magnesium protects against seizures while the nifedipine lowers the blood pressure, and that both are necessary. The order specifies immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg, not the extended-release (XL) formulation; the nurse should verify the correct formulation before administration because the extended-release form has a slower onset and is not appropriate for acute blood pressure reduction in this emergency. *Why it is correct:* Magnesium sulfate acts as a central nervous system depressant and raises the seizure threshold by blocking NMDA receptors and reducing neuronal excitability. It has a modest and inconsistent effect on blood pressure and is not classified or dosed as an antihypertensive. Confusing its role leads to the dangerous error of relying on magnesium alone for blood pressure control while omitting a true antihypertensive, leaving the patient at risk for hypertensive stroke. The distinction between immediate-release and extended-release nifedipine is critical: immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg produces blood pressure reduction within 10 to 20 minutes, whereas extended-release nifedipine has an onset of 2 to 3 hours and is intended for maintenance, not acute crisis management. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse is initiating the magnesium sulfate loading dose and the nifedipine simultaneously while implementing seizure precautions. Multiple actions must occur. The nurse must determine the priority set of actions and their appropriate grouping. *Correct action:* The priority actions at this moment are: administer the magnesium sulfate 4-gram loading dose via infusion pump over 20 minutes as ordered, administer the immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg orally, implement seizure precautions (pad side rails, place suction and oxygen at bedside, dim the room lights, minimize stimulation and visitors), and initiate serial blood pressure monitoring every 5 minutes for the first 20 minutes. These actions occur essentially simultaneously and are not a strict sequence — the magnesium infusion is started, the nifedipine is given, and the environmental precautions are set up concurrently. The nurse should not delay any of these actions to complete another first. Continuous monitoring of respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, level of consciousness, and deep tendon reflexes begins with the magnesium infusion. A Foley catheter is already in place for accurate intake and output measurement. *Why it is correct:* The initial goal is prompt reduction out of severe range while avoiding hypotension, and to establish seizure prophylaxis as rapidly as possible. These are concurrent priorities, not sequential steps. Delaying the antihypertensive to complete the full magnesium load, or delaying seizure precautions until medications are infusing, would leave the patient unprotected during the highest-risk window. The infusion pump ensures accurate magnesium dosing; manual drip calculation for a high-alert medication would be unsafe. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse reassesses the patient two hours after treatment initiation. Blood pressure has trended from 168/112 to 138/88, the headache has decreased from 9/10 to 4/10, scotomata have resolved, and deep tendon reflexes have decreased from 3+ with clonus to 2+ without clonus. Urine output is 45 mL in the second hour. The nurse must determine whether the patient is responding appropriately to treatment and identify which parameters confirm improvement versus which require continued vigilance. *Correct action:* The blood pressure trend below 160/110 (now 138/88) confirms effective acute antihypertensive response — the initial goal is prompt reduction out of severe range while avoiding hypotension; ongoing target/range follows protocol. Resolution of scotomata and reduction in headache severity indicate decreasing CNS irritability. The resolution of clonus and return to 2+ reflexes suggest decreased CNS irritability; continued presence of reflexes, stable respirations, alert mentation, and adequate urine output support absence of magnesium toxicity. These findings together confirm the patient is responding to treatment. The urine output of 45 mL in one hour requires continued monitoring — it is above the minimum threshold for safe magnesium clearance (generally 30 mL per hour per facility protocol) but is not robust, and the nurse should continue to track hourly output to ensure magnesium is being adequately cleared by the kidneys. The facial edema is unchanged, which is expected — edema resolves over days, not hours, and its persistence does not indicate treatment failure. *Why it is correct:* The acute treatment goals in preeclampsia with severe features are to prevent seizure (magnesium) and to reduce blood pressure to below 160/110 to prevent cerebrovascular injury (antihypertensive). Resolution of CNS symptoms (headache, scotomata) and normalization of reflexes are the clinical markers that seizure risk is decreasing. Blood pressure trending below the severe-range threshold confirms the antihypertensive is effective. Urine output is a dual-purpose parameter: it reflects renal perfusion (a marker of preeclampsia severity) and is essential for safe magnesium clearance, since magnesium is renally excreted and oliguria increases the risk of toxicity. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Dismissing the patient's symptoms as normal postpartum complaints — attributing the headache to sleep deprivation, the facial swelling to normal postpartum fluid shifts, and the elevated blood pressure to anxiety or pain — and advising the patient to rest, take ibuprofen, and call back if symptoms worsen. This is unsafe because it delays recognition of a life-threatening condition. Late postpartum preeclampsia is underrecognized precisely because patients and clinicians attribute its symptoms to the expected discomforts of the postpartum period. The combination of severe headache, scotomata, facial edema, and severe-range blood pressure is never normal postpartum and requires emergent evaluation regardless of a normotensive prenatal history.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Focusing on the absence of chest pain as reassuring and downgrading the urgency of the presentation. Chest pain is not a defining feature of preeclampsia with severe features; its absence does not reduce the severity classification when severe-range blood pressure and CNS symptoms are present. A nurse who anchors on the absent finding rather than the present cluster may inappropriately triage the patient to a routine follow-up appointment rather than the emergency department.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Waiting for laboratory results to confirm the diagnosis before initiating treatment for severe-range blood pressure. The 30-to-60-minute treatment window for severe-range blood pressure begins at the time of confirmed measurement, not at the time of lab confirmation. Delaying antihypertensive therapy to await a complete metabolic panel or platelet count exposes the patient to preventable cerebrovascular injury. Labs are drawn concurrently with treatment initiation, not as a prerequisite.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Diagnosing HELLP syndrome based on the elevated uric acid and a creatinine increase from baseline alone, without the defining triad of hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets. Uric acid elevation is common in preeclampsia and reflects renal tubular dysfunction but is not a HELLP criterion. Misclassifying the condition as HELLP when the platelet count, transaminases, and LDH are normal may lead to unnecessary interventions (such as preparing for emergent delivery in a postpartum patient, which is not applicable) and diverts attention from the actual priorities of seizure prophylaxis and blood pressure control.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Believing that magnesium sulfate is being administered to lower the blood pressure and therefore not recognizing the need for a separate antihypertensive agent. This is a common and dangerous misconception. If the nurse assumes the magnesium will control the blood pressure, the antihypertensive may be delayed or omitted, leaving the patient in the severe-range blood pressure zone and at risk for hemorrhagic stroke. Magnesium sulfate and the antihypertensive serve entirely different purposes and are both independently necessary.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Administering extended-release nifedipine (the XL formulation) instead of immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg for acute blood pressure reduction. The extended-release formulation has an onset of action of 2 to 3 hours and is designed for maintenance therapy, not acute crisis management. Using it in place of the immediate-release form would fail to achieve the blood pressure target within the required 30-to-60-minute window. The nurse must verify the formulation matches the order before administration.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the unchanged facial edema at Stage 3 as evidence that treatment is failing and escalating care unnecessarily — for example, requesting an increased antihypertensive dose or additional magnesium when the blood pressure and CNS symptoms have already improved. Peripheral and facial edema in preeclampsia resolves over days as the endothelial injury heals and fluid is mobilized; it is not an acute treatment-response marker. The parameters that confirm acute treatment response are blood pressure trend, resolution of CNS symptoms, reflex normalization, and urine output — not edema.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Sequencing the actions as a rigid linear chain — for example, completing the entire magnesium loading dose before giving the nifedipine, or completing all medication administration before implementing seizure precautions. These are concurrent priorities. The magnesium infusion runs over 20 minutes; the nifedipine is given orally at the same time; seizure precautions are environmental and can be set up simultaneously. Forcing a sequence where none exists delays one or more critical interventions during the highest-risk period.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the clinical cluster of late postpartum preeclampsia with severe features — severe headache, scotomata, new-onset edema, and severe-range blood pressure after 48 hours postpartum — and distinguish it from expected postpartum complaints, even in a patient who was normotensive throughout pregnancy.

Interpret severe-range blood pressure (systolic 160 or greater or diastolic 110 or greater) as requiring acute antihypertensive treatment within 30 to 60 minutes and initiation of seizure prophylaxis, without waiting for laboratory confirmation.

Differentiate preeclampsia with severe features from HELLP syndrome using the laboratory criteria of hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets, and from postpartum hypertension without severe features using the blood pressure threshold and presence or absence of end-organ symptoms.

Distinguish the purpose of magnesium sulfate (seizure prophylaxis) from the purpose of antihypertensive therapy (blood pressure reduction) and verify the correct nifedipine formulation (immediate-release, not extended-release) for acute management.

Evaluate treatment response in postpartum preeclampsia with severe features by tracking blood pressure trend below the severe-range threshold, resolution of CNS symptoms, normalization of deep tendon reflexes, and adequacy of urine output, while recognizing that persistent edema does not indicate treatment failure.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the emergency department assessment confirms severe-range blood pressure on repeated measurement, CNS symptoms (headache, scotomata), hyperreflexia with clonus, and a urine protein-to-creatinine ratio of 0.42 on postpartum day 4, and laboratory results have returned ruling out HELLP syndrome.

Most likely condition: Postpartum preeclampsia with severe features. The confirmed severe-range blood pressure (168/112 and 164/110), severe headache unresponsive to analgesics, scotomata, new-onset facial edema, hyperreflexia with clonus, and a urine protein-to-creatinine ratio of 0.42 occurring on postpartum day 4 in a previously normotensive patient constitute an unambiguous presentation. The late postpartum onset does not reduce the severity — delayed postpartum presentations often occur after discharge, making discharge teaching and triage recognition critical.

Plausible competing conditions: (1) HELLP syndrome — tempting because it can present postpartum and shares the hypertension and CNS symptoms, but wrong here because the platelet count is 195,000 (normal), AST and ALT are only mildly elevated and well below the HELLP threshold, and LDH is 198 (normal); the defining triad of hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelets is absent. (2) Postpartum hypertension without severe features — tempting because the patient is postpartum and hypertensive, but wrong here because both blood pressure readings exceed the severe-range threshold of 160/110, and the patient has CNS symptoms (headache, scotomata) and hyperreflexia with clonus, all of which are defining features of severe disease that exclude the "without severe features" classification.

Two priority actions: (1) Administer the magnesium sulfate 4-gram IV loading dose over 20 minutes as ordered for seizure prophylaxis — the patient's CNS symptoms and hyperreflexia with clonus indicate high seizure risk, and magnesium is the standard of care for eclampsia prevention. (2) Administer immediate-release oral nifedipine 10 mg as ordered to reduce blood pressure below 160/110 within 30 to 60 minutes — sustained severe-range blood pressure places the patient at risk for hemorrhagic stroke, and the antihypertensive addresses a different physiologic target than the magnesium.

Two wrong actions: (1) Withholding the antihypertensive because the magnesium sulfate infusion is running and the nurse expects it to lower the blood pressure — wrong because magnesium sulfate is not an antihypertensive agent; relying on it for blood pressure control leaves the patient in the severe-range zone and at risk for cerebrovascular injury during the critical treatment window. (2) Administering extended-release nifedipine (XL formulation) instead of immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg — wrong because the extended-release formulation has an onset of 2 to 3 hours and cannot achieve the required blood pressure reduction within 30 to 60 minutes; this substitution would leave the patient unprotected during the highest-risk period.

Two parameters to monitor: (1) Monitor BP trend — for acute antihypertensive response. (2) Monitor magnesium safety/seizure-risk markers — respiratory rate, LOC, DTR/clonus, urine output, and persistent headache/visual symptoms.

Two irrelevant parameters: (1) Facial edema — a student might track it as a marker of treatment response, but peripheral and facial edema in preeclampsia resolves over days to weeks as endothelial injury heals; its persistence in the acute treatment window does not indicate treatment failure and is not the parameter that confirms the crisis is resolving. (2) Lochia character and amount — a student might monitor it because the patient is postpartum, but lochia assessment is a routine postpartum concern unrelated to the preeclampsia emergency; the patient's lochia is already described as appropriately transitioning and light, and changes in lochia would not inform the evaluation of whether the acute hypertensive crisis and seizure risk are responding to treatment.
