**CASE TITLE**
Acute variceal hemorrhage in decompensated alcohol-related cirrhosis.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 55-year-old man with a known history of alcohol-related cirrhosis, classified as Child-Pugh B on his most recent hepatology visit three months ago, is brought to the emergency department by his family. His medical history includes portal hypertension with known esophageal varices identified on screening esophagogastroduodenoscopy eighteen months ago (grade II, no prior banding or sclerotherapy), ascites managed with furosemide 40 mg daily and spironolactone 100 mg daily, and a remote history of alcohol use disorder — he reports abstinence for the past four months, corroborated by his wife. He has no history of prior variceal bleeding, no known hepatocellular carcinoma, and no prior transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt. His home medications also include lactulose 30 mL twice daily for mild intermittent hepatic encephalopathy and a daily multivitamin. He takes no anticoagulants, no NSAIDs, and no beta-blockers — propranolol was discussed at his last hepatology appointment but was not started because of baseline systolic pressures in the low 90s. He has no known drug allergies.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient's wife reports that approximately forty-five minutes ago he vomited a large volume of bright red blood — she estimates roughly 500 mL based on what she saw on the bathroom floor and in the toilet. He had a second, smaller episode of hematemesis in the car on the way to the hospital. He reports feeling lightheaded, weak, and nauseated. He denies melena prior to today but has not had a bowel movement since yesterday morning. He denies chest pain, abdominal pain beyond his chronic baseline fullness, recent fever, recent alcohol use, or NSAID ingestion. He is alert, oriented to person, place, time, and situation, and is visibly anxious.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 118 beats per minute and regular, blood pressure 88/54 mmHg, respiratory rate 20 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 96% on 2 L nasal cannula. He appears pale and diaphoretic. Scleral icterus is present. Mucous membranes are dry and pale. There is dried blood around his mouth and on his shirt. Capillary refill is 3 seconds in the nail beds. Cardiac auscultation reveals tachycardia without murmur, rub, or gallop. Lungs are clear bilaterally with no adventitious sounds. The abdomen is distended and tense with a positive fluid wave and shifting dullness consistent with moderate-to-large-volume ascites. There is no focal tenderness, no rebound, and no guarding. A caput medusae pattern is visible on the anterior abdominal wall. Spider angiomata are present on the upper chest. Bilateral lower extremity pitting edema is noted to the mid-shin, 2+. There is no asterixis on sustained wrist extension. Neurologically he is alert, follows commands, and his speech is coherent and goal-directed. Skin shows scattered ecchymoses on the forearms bilaterally, which his wife says are chronic.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Complete blood count: WBC 6,200/µL, hemoglobin 8.1 g/dL, hematocrit 24.3%, platelets 72,000/µL, MCV 104 fL. Comprehensive metabolic panel: sodium 131 mEq/L, potassium 3.6 mEq/L, chloride 98 mEq/L, bicarbonate 20 mEq/L, BUN 32 mg/dL, creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (baseline 1.0 per outpatient records), glucose 102 mg/dL, total bilirubin 3.8 mg/dL, direct bilirubin 2.1 mg/dL, AST 68 U/L, ALT 42 U/L, alkaline phosphatase 134 U/L, albumin 2.4 g/dL, total protein 5.8 g/dL. Coagulation: INR 1.9, PT 22.4 seconds, aPTT 38 seconds. Lactate 3.4 mmol/L. Type and screen sent; crossmatch for 4 units packed red blood cells ordered. Ammonia level 62 µmol/L (reference range 15–45 µmol/L). Blood alcohol level undetectable. Urinalysis unremarkable.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 90 minutes after arrival.

Two large-bore (16-gauge) peripheral IVs are placed and a 500 mL normal saline bolus is initiated. The provider orders octreotide 50 mcg IV bolus followed by a continuous infusion at 50 mcg/hour, ceftriaxone 1 g IV once daily for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis prophylaxis (per standing hepatology protocol for any cirrhotic patient admitted with upper gastrointestinal hemorrhage), and pantoprazole 80 mg IV bolus followed by 8 mg/hour continuous infusion. The patient is made NPO. A nasogastric tube is placed and returns bright red blood with small clots, which does not clear after 500 mL of lavage. *(Compiler guard: Do not key pantoprazole or NG lavage as the central variceal-bleed intervention; the core interventions are resuscitation, octreotide/vasoactive therapy, ceftriaxone, airway/endoscopy readiness, restrictive transfusion, and band ligation.)* Gastroenterology is consulted for emergent esophagogastroduodenoscopy. The patient is positioned in a left lateral decubitus position with the head of bed elevated to 30 degrees for airway protection.

Repeat vitals 45 minutes after the fluid bolus: heart rate 112, blood pressure 92/58, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 97% on 2 L nasal cannula. He has another episode of hematemesis — approximately 200 mL of bright red blood. He remains alert but is increasingly anxious and reports feeling cold. Repeat point-of-care hemoglobin drawn 60 minutes after arrival returns at 7.2 g/dL. The provider orders transfusion of 1 unit packed red blood cells with a stated target hemoglobin of 7 to 8 g/dL per the facility's variceal hemorrhage protocol, which specifies a restrictive transfusion threshold to avoid exacerbating portal hypertension. Lactate is 4.1 mmol/L. Urine output via Foley catheter placed on arrival is 25 mL over the first hour.

**Stage 2** — 90 minutes to 4 hours after arrival.

The patient is taken for emergent esophagogastroduodenoscopy. Anesthesia provides monitored sedation with endotracheal intubation for airway protection given the ongoing hematemesis and risk of aspiration. Endoscopy reveals three columns of large esophageal varices in the distal esophagus with active spurting from one varix and a red wale sign on a second. No gastric varices are seen. No peptic ulcer disease is identified. The gastroenterologist performs endoscopic variceal band ligation, placing six bands across the three columns. Hemostasis is achieved endoscopically — no further active bleeding is visualized after banding.

Post-procedure, the patient is extubated without complication and transferred to the intensive care unit. Octreotide infusion continues. Vitals on ICU arrival (approximately 3 hours after ED presentation): heart rate 96, blood pressure 104/62, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 98% on 2 L nasal cannula. He has received 1 unit of packed red blood cells total. Post-transfusion hemoglobin is 7.8 g/dL. Lactate has decreased to 2.6 mmol/L. Urine output improves to 40 mL/hour. He is drowsy but arousable and oriented. No further hematemesis. A small amount of melena is noted on a rectal exam performed in the ICU, consistent with digested blood from the earlier hemorrhage.

Repeat labs 4 hours after arrival: sodium 133 mEq/L, potassium 3.4 mEq/L, chloride 100 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L, BUN 38 mg/dL, creatinine 1.5 mg/dL (rising from 1.3 at baseline), total bilirubin 4.2 mg/dL, albumin 2.2 g/dL, INR 2.0, platelets 68,000/µL, ammonia 78 µmol/L.

**Stage 3** — 12 to 24 hours after arrival.

Overnight the patient becomes increasingly confused and somnolent. At the 16-hour mark, the night nurse finds him difficult to arouse — he opens his eyes to voice and follows simple commands but gives disoriented answers, believing he is at home. He has developed asterixis on sustained wrist extension. His speech is slurred and he intermittently falls asleep mid-sentence. He has had no further hematemesis and no bright red blood per rectum; stool remains melanotic. Vitals: heart rate 82, blood pressure 108/66, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 97% on room air (nasal cannula weaned). Temperature 37.6 °C.

Repeat labs at 18 hours: hemoglobin 7.6 g/dL (stable), platelets 65,000/µL, INR 2.1, BUN 44 mg/dL, creatinine 1.8 mg/dL (continuing to rise), sodium 130 mEq/L, potassium 4.9 mEq/L, ammonia 124 µmol/L, total bilirubin 4.8 mg/dL, albumin 2.1 g/dL, lactate 1.8 mmol/L. Urine output has decreased to 15 mL/hour over the past 4 hours. The abdomen remains distended; the patient winces with deep palpation in the left lower quadrant but there is no rebound or rigidity. Temperature trends to 37.9 °C on recheck two hours later.

The ICU provider is notified. Orders include increasing lactulose to 30 mL every 2 hours titrated to 3 to 4 bowel movements per day, rifaximin 550 mg via nasogastric tube every 12 hours, holding furosemide and spironolactone pending renal reassessment given the rising creatinine and declining urine output, and a diagnostic paracentesis. If a diagnostic paracentesis was not already obtained early in the admission, the new tenderness, low-grade temperature, encephalopathy, and AKI make it urgent now to evaluate for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis despite prophylactic ceftriaxone.

Paracentesis results: ascitic fluid WBC 620 cells/µL with 78% neutrophils (absolute neutrophil count 484 cells/µL), ascitic fluid albumin 0.8 g/dL, ascitic fluid culture sent. The serum-ascites albumin gradient calculates to 1.3 g/dL (serum albumin 2.1 minus ascitic albumin 0.8), consistent with portal hypertension. The ascitic fluid absolute neutrophil count of 484 cells/µL exceeds the diagnostic threshold of 250 cells/µL for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis per the facility's hepatology protocol.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At initial presentation, the nurse reviews the full set of assessment findings and laboratory data. The patient presents with hematemesis, tachycardia, hypotension, ascites with shifting dullness, scleral icterus, spider angiomata, caput medusae, scattered ecchymoses, INR 1.9, platelets 72,000, albumin 2.4, and an elevated ammonia — alongside clear lung sounds, absence of asterixis, coherent speech, SpO₂ 96% on 2 L, and a normal glucose. The nurse must identify which findings together form the pattern of acute variceal hemorrhage in a decompensated cirrhotic patient and which findings are normal, stable, or unrelated to the acute emergency. *Correct action:* The findings that define the acute clinical picture are the hematemesis with hemodynamic instability (tachycardia 118, hypotension 88/54, diaphoresis, pallor, capillary refill 3 seconds), the stigmata of portal hypertension and chronic liver disease (ascites with fluid wave and shifting dullness, caput medusae, spider angiomata, scleral icterus), and the laboratory evidence of hepatic synthetic failure compounding hemorrhage risk (INR 1.9, thrombocytopenia 72,000, hypoalbuminemia 2.4). The clear lungs, absence of asterixis, coherent speech, and normal glucose are relevant as baseline normals but do not contribute to the acute hemorrhagic pattern. *Why:* In a patient with known cirrhosis and portal hypertension, massive hematemesis with hemodynamic compromise points to variceal hemorrhage as the source until proven otherwise. The coagulopathy and thrombocytopenia are not incidental — they reflect impaired hepatic synthesis of clotting factors and splenic sequestration from portal hypertension, both of which compound the hemorrhage. Recognizing the full constellation prevents the nurse from treating this as a simple upper GI bleed and ensures the variceal-specific interventions (octreotide, antibiotic prophylaxis, restrictive transfusion, emergent endoscopy) are anticipated. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation and baseline data.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the point-of-care hemoglobin returns at 7.2 g/dL after the initial fluid bolus, the patient has had a second episode of hematemesis, lactate has risen from 3.4 to 4.1 mmol/L, urine output is only 25 mL in the first hour, and the blood pressure has only marginally improved to 92/58 despite 500 mL of crystalloid. The nurse must interpret these findings together to determine the patient's hemodynamic trajectory and the adequacy of the current resuscitation. *Correct action:* These findings collectively indicate ongoing hemorrhagic shock with inadequate tissue perfusion despite initial resuscitation. The rising lactate reflects worsening anaerobic metabolism, the oliguria reflects renal hypoperfusion, and the marginal blood pressure response to a 500 mL bolus in the setting of continued active bleeding indicates that volume replacement is not keeping pace with losses. He is actively bleeding, unstable, lactate is rising, urine output is poor, and hemoglobin is near the restrictive threshold, so one unit PRBC is reasonable with a post-transfusion goal around 7–8/7–9, not 10. *Why:* Lactate is a marker of tissue oxygen debt — a rising lactate in the setting of hemorrhage indicates that oxygen delivery is failing to meet demand despite the fluid bolus. Oliguria below 0.5 mL/kg/hour (this patient's output is approximately 0.3 mL/kg/hour assuming a weight near 80 kg) reflects inadequate renal perfusion. The combination of these findings with persistent tachycardia and hypotension means the patient is not stabilizing and requires blood product resuscitation. Interpreting these values as a pattern of ongoing shock — rather than as isolated abnormalities — is the analytic step that drives the urgency of the next interventions. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse considers the differential for the source of the upper gastrointestinal hemorrhage. The patient has known cirrhosis with portal hypertension, documented grade II esophageal varices on prior endoscopy, massive hematemesis, and stigmata of chronic liver disease. He denies NSAID use and has no prior history of peptic ulcer disease. The nasogastric aspirate shows bright red blood that does not clear. The nurse must determine the most likely source of bleeding and the priority hypothesis guiding the plan of care. *Correct action:* Variceal hemorrhage is the primary hypothesis. In a cirrhotic patient with known portal hypertension, documented esophageal varices, and massive hematemesis, esophageal variceal bleeding is the most likely source and must be treated presumptively with variceal-specific interventions (octreotide, antibiotic prophylaxis, emergent endoscopy with band ligation) even before endoscopic confirmation. Peptic ulcer disease remains on the differential but is less likely given the absence of NSAID use, no prior ulcer history, and the presence of known varices with portal hypertension. The coagulopathy is a concurrent priority that compounding the hemorrhage but does not change the primary hypothesis — it is managed alongside the variceal-specific interventions, not sequentially before them. *Why:* Approximately 70% of upper gastrointestinal hemorrhage in cirrhotic patients originates from esophageal or gastric varices. The pre-existing documentation of grade II varices, the clinical stigmata of portal hypertension (ascites, caput medusae), and the volume and character of the hemorrhage all converge on varices as the source. Treating presumptively for variceal hemorrhage — particularly starting octreotide and antibiotics — is the standard of care because these interventions reduce mortality and re-bleeding even if the source turns out to be non-variceal, whereas delaying them to await endoscopic confirmation worsens outcomes. The coagulopathy (INR 1.9, platelets 72,000) is real and relevant but does not need to be fully corrected before endoscopy proceeds — aggressive correction with large-volume fresh frozen plasma risks volume overload and further elevation of portal pressure. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1 (the clinical picture and history are sufficient to establish the primary hypothesis before endoscopic confirmation in Stage 2).

4. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the provider has ordered a transfusion of 1 unit of packed red blood cells with a target hemoglobin of 7 to 8 g/dL per the facility's variceal hemorrhage protocol. The nurse must understand the rationale for this restrictive transfusion target and identify the correct set of concurrent interventions — including which interventions to anticipate and prepare for and which would be inappropriate. The available interventions include continuing octreotide, continuing ceftriaxone, transfusing to a hemoglobin target of 10 g/dL, administering fresh frozen plasma to normalize the INR before endoscopy, preparing for emergent endoscopy with band ligation, maintaining NPO status, and positioning for airway protection. *Correct action:* The correct concurrent set is: continue octreotide infusion (splanchnic vasoconstriction reduces portal pressure and variceal blood flow), continue ceftriaxone (antibiotic prophylaxis in cirrhotic GI hemorrhage reduces bacterial infectious complications and is standard care; guideline-cited evidence supports improved bleeding-related outcomes), transfuse packed red blood cells to the restrictive target of 7 to 8 g/dL per the variceal hemorrhage protocol (not to 10 g/dL), maintain NPO for emergent endoscopy, and position for airway protection. Transfusing to a hemoglobin of 10 g/dL is incorrect because liberal transfusion in variceal hemorrhage raises portal venous pressure and increases re-bleeding risk. Administering fresh frozen plasma solely to normalize the INR before endoscopy is not indicated — the INR of 1.9 reflects chronic hepatic synthetic dysfunction, not an acute coagulopathy that must be corrected before the procedure can proceed, and large-volume plasma transfusion risks volume overload and portal pressure elevation. *Why:* Randomized evidence demonstrates that a restrictive transfusion strategy (target hemoglobin 7–8 g/dL) in acute variceal hemorrhage improves survival compared with a liberal strategy (target 9–10 g/dL), primarily by avoiding the portal pressure increase that accompanies volume expansion with blood products. Octreotide reduces splanchnic blood flow and portal pressure, serving as a pharmacologic bridge to definitive endoscopic therapy. Ceftriaxone prophylaxis in cirrhotic patients with GI hemorrhage reduces bacterial infectious complications and is standard care; guideline-cited evidence supports improved bleeding-related outcomes. Endoscopy should not be delayed for coagulation correction in a patient whose coagulopathy is chronic and whose INR is below the threshold at which most endoscopists would require correction. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient has returned from endoscopy where band ligation achieved hemostasis. He is now in the ICU. Post-procedure hemoglobin is 7.8 g/dL after 1 unit of packed red blood cells, lactate has decreased from 4.1 to 2.6 mmol/L, urine output has improved to 40 mL/hour, heart rate has decreased from 112 to 96, and blood pressure has improved to 104/62. However, the creatinine has risen from 1.3 to 1.5 mg/dL, the ammonia has risen from 62 to 78 µmol/L, and the INR has ticked up from 1.9 to 2.0. The nurse must determine the priority nursing actions in the immediate post-banding period. *Correct action:* The priority actions are: (a) maintain the octreotide infusion — current variceal-bleeding guidance generally supports vasoactive therapy for 2–5 days after suspected/confirmed variceal hemorrhage, with local protocols often specifying the exact duration, to reduce the risk of early re-bleeding; (b) monitor closely for signs of re-bleeding (recurrent hematemesis, new bright red blood per rectum, rising heart rate, falling blood pressure, or a drop in hemoglobin on serial checks) — re-bleeding after band ligation occurs in up to 10–15% of patients within the first 48 to 72 hours; (c) continue ceftriaxone for the full prophylactic course; (d) monitor neurological status for emerging hepatic encephalopathy given the rising ammonia and the large protein load from intraluminal blood; and (e) monitor urine output and creatinine trends for hepatorenal physiology given the rising creatinine despite improved hemodynamics, and anticipate that hepatology/IR may evaluate for early transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) if high-risk features persist. The melena noted on rectal exam is consistent with digestion of the blood already in the GI tract from the earlier hemorrhage and does not by itself indicate re-bleeding — it must be interpreted in the context of stable hemoglobin and improving hemodynamics. *Why:* The post-banding period carries significant re-bleeding risk, and octreotide continuation is the pharmacologic safeguard during this window. The rising ammonia in the setting of a large intraluminal blood load (blood is a high-protein substrate that gut bacteria convert to ammonia) places this patient at high risk for hepatic encephalopathy — the absence of asterixis and the preserved orientation at Stage 2 are reassuring but represent a baseline that must be tracked. The creatinine rise from 1.0 (outpatient baseline) to 1.5 in the setting of hemorrhagic shock may reflect acute tubular injury from hypoperfusion, hepatorenal syndrome, or both — the improving urine output is encouraging but does not rule out ongoing renal injury. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient has developed confusion, somnolence, asterixis, and disoriented speech. His ammonia has risen to 124 µmol/L. His creatinine has continued to rise to 1.8 mg/dL with declining urine output (15 mL/hour). He has developed new abdominal tenderness and a low-grade temperature (37.9 °C). Paracentesis reveals an ascitic fluid absolute neutrophil count of 484 cells/µL, exceeding the facility protocol's diagnostic threshold of 250 cells/µL for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis. The hemoglobin is stable at 7.6 g/dL and there has been no further hematemesis. The nurse must evaluate the patient's overall trajectory and determine whether the current clinical picture represents expected post-hemorrhage recovery, a complication requiring new intervention, or both. *Correct action:* The patient is developing two recognized complications of variceal hemorrhage in cirrhosis, neither of which represents expected recovery: (a) hepatic encephalopathy — the confusion, asterixis, somnolence, and ammonia of 124 µmol/L represent a clinically significant decline from his Stage 2 neurological baseline (drowsy but oriented, no asterixis) and require aggressive lactulose titration and rifaximin, which have been appropriately ordered; and (b) spontaneous bacterial peritonitis — despite prophylactic ceftriaxone, the ascitic fluid neutrophil count of 484 cells/µL meets the diagnostic criterion, and the new abdominal tenderness with low-grade fever is consistent. The rising creatinine and falling urine output in the setting of SBP raise concern for hepatorenal syndrome, which carries high mortality in this population. The stable hemoglobin and absence of new hematemesis confirm that re-bleeding is not the current problem — the hemorrhage has been controlled, but its downstream complications are now the dominant clinical threats. The nurse should anticipate that the provider may broaden or change antibiotic coverage given the development of SBP despite ceftriaxone prophylaxis, should ensure lactulose is being administered and titrated to the target of 3 to 4 bowel movements per day, should perform and document serial neurological assessments (orientation, asterixis, level of consciousness) to track encephalopathy trajectory, and should closely monitor urine output and creatinine as markers of renal function. *Why:* Hepatic encephalopathy after variceal hemorrhage is precipitated by the large protein load from intraluminal blood, hypovolemia-related hepatic ischemia, and any concurrent infection — all three precipitants are present here. SBP occurs in up to 20% of cirrhotic patients hospitalized with GI hemorrhage even with antibiotic prophylaxis, and its development is an independent predictor of mortality and hepatorenal syndrome. The rising creatinine trajectory (1.0 → 1.3 → 1.5 → 1.8) in the setting of SBP and declining urine output is the classic presentation of hepatorenal syndrome, which requires early recognition because albumin infusion and vasopressor therapy (per provider order) may be initiated to prevent progression. Evaluating the patient's trajectory requires integrating the stable hemorrhage markers (hemoglobin, no new hematemesis) with the worsening systemic markers (encephalopathy, renal function, infection) to recognize that the clinical picture has shifted from hemorrhagic emergency to the metabolic and infectious complications of decompensated cirrhosis. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Failing to recognize the coagulopathy (INR 1.9, platelets 72,000) as part of the acute hemorrhagic picture and instead treating it as a chronic, stable finding that does not require attention. INR 1.9 and platelets 72,000 signal impaired hemostatic reserve and bleeding risk, but chronic cirrhosis INR is not an automatic cue for FFP or a reason to delay endoscopic hemostasis. Conversely, treating the absence of asterixis and the coherent speech as reassuring enough to lower the urgency of the presentation would be an error — these are baseline normals that may change, not evidence that the patient is stable.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Interpreting the marginal blood pressure improvement (88/54 to 92/58) after the fluid bolus as evidence that the patient is responding to resuscitation and is stabilizing. The rising lactate, persistent tachycardia, oliguria, and continued hematemesis all contradict this interpretation. A nurse who focuses on the slight blood pressure rise while ignoring the lactate trend and urine output may delay escalation of resuscitation or fail to communicate the inadequacy of the current response to the provider.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Attributing the hemorrhage to peptic ulcer disease rather than varices and therefore omitting or deprioritizing variceal-specific interventions (octreotide, antibiotic prophylaxis). While peptic ulcer disease is on the differential, the convergence of known cirrhosis, documented varices, portal hypertension stigmata, and massive hematemesis makes varices overwhelmingly more likely. Treating this as a generic upper GI bleed without variceal-specific pharmacotherapy would be a dangerous omission.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Transfusing packed red blood cells to a hemoglobin target of 10 g/dL based on the general principle that hemorrhaging patients need aggressive transfusion. In variceal hemorrhage specifically, liberal transfusion to a hemoglobin above 8 g/dL increases portal venous pressure and raises the risk of re-bleeding and death. The restrictive target of 7 to 8 g/dL stated in the facility's variceal hemorrhage protocol exists precisely because of this pathophysiology. A related error is insisting on correcting the INR with fresh frozen plasma before allowing endoscopy to proceed — this delays definitive hemostasis, risks volume overload, and is not supported by evidence in chronic liver disease coagulopathy where the INR does not reliably predict procedural bleeding risk.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Interpreting the melanotic stool noted on rectal exam as evidence of re-bleeding and urgently notifying the provider of a new hemorrhage. Melena in this context represents digested blood from the earlier massive hematemesis — it is expected and does not indicate new or ongoing bleeding when the hemoglobin is stable and hemodynamics are improving. Acting on this finding as if it were re-bleeding could lead to unnecessary transfusion (which would exceed the restrictive target and raise portal pressure) or unnecessary repeat endoscopy. The correct approach is to document the finding, correlate it with the stable hemoglobin and improving vital signs, and continue monitoring for true re-bleeding indicators (new hematemesis, hemodynamic deterioration, or a falling hemoglobin on serial checks).

Attached to Decision Point 6: Attributing the patient's confusion and somnolence to sedation effects from the endoscopy procedure (which occurred more than 12 hours earlier) rather than recognizing hepatic encephalopathy. The endoscopy sedation was provided under anesthesia with endotracheal intubation, the patient was extubated without difficulty and was oriented post-procedure at Stage 2, and the interval between extubation and the onset of confusion is far too long for residual sedation effects. The new asterixis, the rising ammonia from 78 to 124 µmol/L, and the clinical trajectory of progressive somnolence all point to hepatic encephalopathy precipitated by the intraluminal blood load and the developing SBP. A second error at this decision point is dismissing the low-grade temperature (37.9 °C) and mild abdominal tenderness as insignificant in a post-procedure patient, thereby failing to advocate for diagnostic paracentesis. In a cirrhotic patient with ascites, even subtle signs of peritoneal irritation with low-grade fever must prompt evaluation for SBP — the consequences of missed SBP (sepsis, hepatorenal syndrome, death) far outweigh the minimal risk of a diagnostic paracentesis.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the clinical pattern of acute variceal hemorrhage in a patient with decompensated cirrhosis, including the integration of hemodynamic instability, portal hypertension stigmata, and hepatic synthetic failure markers into a unified clinical picture.

Apply the rationale for a restrictive transfusion strategy in variceal hemorrhage, including the pathophysiologic basis for avoiding over-transfusion and the evidence against routine coagulopathy correction before endoscopy in chronic liver disease.

Identify the concurrent pharmacologic interventions required in variceal hemorrhage — octreotide for splanchnic vasoconstriction and ceftriaxone for antibiotic prophylaxis — and articulate why each is a standard-of-care intervention rather than an optional adjunct.

Evaluate a post-hemorrhage cirrhotic patient for the emergence of hepatic encephalopathy and spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, distinguishing these complications from expected post-procedural findings and recognizing their precipitants in the clinical course.

Integrate serial assessment data — neurological status, renal function trends, ascitic fluid analysis, and hemoglobin stability — to determine whether a cirrhotic patient's trajectory reflects recovery from the index hemorrhage or the development of new, life-threatening complications requiring escalation.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**

Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after the variceal hemorrhage has been controlled by band ligation and the hemoglobin is stable, but the patient has developed progressive confusion with asterixis, a rising ammonia level, worsening renal function, and paracentesis results confirming spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.

*Most likely condition:* Hepatic encephalopathy precipitated by the combined effects of a large intraluminal blood protein load from the variceal hemorrhage, developing spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, and acute kidney injury with impaired ammonia clearance. The clinical triad of progressive confusion with asterixis, ammonia rising from 62 to 124 µmol/L, and identifiable precipitants (blood in the GI tract, infection, renal failure) in a patient with known decompensated cirrhosis is unambiguously hepatic encephalopathy.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Residual sedation from endoscopy — wrong here because the patient was extubated without difficulty, was alert and oriented at Stage 2 more than 12 hours before the neurological decline, and has developed new asterixis and a markedly elevated ammonia, none of which are features of residual anesthetic effect. (2) Hypovolemic shock with cerebral hypoperfusion — wrong because the hemoglobin is stable at 7.6 g/dL, the blood pressure has normalized to 108/66, the heart rate has decreased to 82, and the lactate has fallen from 4.1 to 1.8 mmol/L, all indicating that the hemorrhagic shock has resolved; the confusion is not perfusion-related.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Administer lactulose as ordered (30 mL every 2 hours, titrated to 3 to 4 bowel movements per day) and rifaximin 550 mg via nasogastric tube every 12 hours to reduce ammonia production and absorption — these are the direct treatments for the hepatic encephalopathy and must be initiated and titrated without delay. (2) Notify the provider of the paracentesis results confirming SBP (ascitic fluid absolute neutrophil count 484 cells/µL). Anticipate IV albumin per SBP/HRS-risk protocol while antibiotic coverage is escalated or adjusted, since SBP has developed despite prophylactic ceftriaxone and may involve a resistant organism — treating the infection and mitigating HRS risk is essential because SBP is both a precipitant of the encephalopathy and an independent driver of hepatorenal syndrome and mortality.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Transfusing additional packed red blood cells to raise the hemoglobin above 8 g/dL because the patient "looks worse" — wrong because the hemoglobin is stable at 7.6 g/dL within the target range, the clinical deterioration is from encephalopathy and infection rather than hemorrhage, and unnecessary transfusion would raise portal pressure and increase re-bleeding risk. (2) Attributing the confusion to residual sedation and deferring lactulose administration or neurological reassessment until the next scheduled assessment — wrong because the 12-plus-hour interval since sedation, the new asterixis, and the ammonia of 124 µmol/L rule out sedation as the cause, and delaying treatment of hepatic encephalopathy risks progression to coma.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Serial neurological assessments — level of consciousness, orientation, presence or absence of asterixis, and ability to follow commands — because improvement or worsening of these findings directly tracks the trajectory of hepatic encephalopathy and determines whether lactulose titration is adequate. (2) Urine output and serum creatinine trend — because the rising creatinine (1.0 → 1.8) and declining urine output (40 → 15 mL/hour) in the setting of SBP raise concern for hepatorenal syndrome, and the trajectory of these values determines whether renal function is stabilizing with infection treatment or progressing toward a need for albumin infusion and vasopressor support.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Blood alcohol level — the patient's blood alcohol level was undetectable on admission and his wife corroborated his four months of abstinence; monitoring for acute alcohol intoxication is irrelevant to his current presentation of encephalopathy and SBP. (2) Lactate level — the lactate has already normalized from 4.1 to 1.8 mmol/L, confirming resolution of the hemorrhagic shock; continued tracking of lactate does not inform the current clinical questions about encephalopathy severity or renal function trajectory and would divert attention from the parameters that matter now.
