**CASE TITLE**
Guillain-Barré syndrome with progressive respiratory compromise following Campylobacter enteritis.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 32-year-old man with no significant past medical history presents to the emergency department. He works as a warehouse supervisor and was previously physically active with no chronic medications. Three weeks ago he experienced a four-day episode of bloody diarrhea with fever and cramping that resolved without antibiotics; stool culture at an urgent care visit at that time grew Campylobacter jejuni. He has no history of recent vaccination, no autoimmune disease, no prior neurological complaints, and no family history of neuromuscular disorders. He does not smoke, drinks alcohol socially, and has no drug allergies.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient reports five days of progressive tingling and pain in both feet that has ascended to involve both legs to the mid-thigh level. He noticed difficulty climbing the stairs at work two days ago and today had to grip the handrail to ascend a single flight. He describes the pain as a deep aching and burning in both calves and thighs that is worse at night. He denies any arm weakness, difficulty swallowing, facial drooping, visual changes, or shortness of breath at this time. He denies bowel or bladder incontinence or retention. He is able to walk independently but his gait is visibly unsteady.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 82, blood pressure 126/78, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Weight 85 kg. The patient is alert, oriented, and in no acute respiratory distress. Cranial nerves II–XII are intact. Upper extremity strength is 5/5 bilaterally in all muscle groups. Lower extremity strength: hip flexion 4/5 bilaterally, knee extension 4/5 bilaterally, ankle dorsiflexion 3/5 bilaterally, great toe extension 3/5 bilaterally. Sensation to light touch and pinprick is intact throughout but the patient reports subjective paresthesias in a stocking distribution to the knees. Deep tendon reflexes are absent at the Achilles and patellar tendons bilaterally; biceps and brachioradialis reflexes are trace (1+). Plantar responses are absent bilaterally. No clonus. No sensory level on the trunk. Rectal tone is normal. Gait is wide-based with bilateral foot drop. Romberg sign is positive. Skin is warm, dry, and intact. Bowel sounds are present in all four quadrants. Lung sounds are clear bilaterally with symmetric chest expansion. Heart rhythm is regular without murmur.

**LABORATORY DATA**
CBC: WBC 9,200/µL, hemoglobin 14.8 g/dL, hematocrit 43%, platelets 238,000/µL. BMP: sodium 140 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 96 mg/dL. Magnesium 2.0 mg/dL, phosphorus 3.4 mg/dL, calcium 9.4 mg/dL. CRP 0.8 mg/dL. ESR 12 mm/hr. TSH 2.1 mIU/L. Hepatic panel within normal limits. Urinalysis unremarkable. Creatine kinase 142 U/L (normal). MRI of the thoracic and lumbar spine with and without contrast: no cord compression, no epidural abscess, no transverse myelitis signal; mild enhancement of the cauda equina nerve roots noted.

Lumbar puncture results: opening pressure 14 cmH₂O, CSF protein 98 mg/dL (elevated; reference 15–45 mg/dL), CSF glucose 62 mg/dL (serum glucose 96 mg/dL; ratio normal), CSF WBC 3 cells/µL (normal; reference 0–5), CSF RBC 0, Gram stain negative, culture pending.

Baseline bedside pulmonary function: forced vital capacity (FVC) 3.6 L (42 mL/kg for his 85 kg weight; predicted normal approximately 4.8 L, so 75% of predicted), maximum inspiratory pressure (MIP, also called negative inspiratory force or NIF) −62 cmH₂O.

Nerve conduction studies and electromyography (performed by neurology): prolonged distal motor latencies, reduced conduction velocities, conduction block in the peroneal and tibial nerves bilaterally, and prolonged F-wave latencies — a pattern consistent with acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (AIDP), the most common variant of Guillain-Barré syndrome.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 12 hours after ED arrival.
Neurology is consulted and confirms the clinical and electrodiagnostic picture of GBS (AIDP variant). The provider orders IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day IV for five consecutive days (34 g/day for this 85 kg patient), to begin as soon as the infusion pharmacy prepares the first dose. Additional orders include: serial bedside FVC and MIP measurements every four hours, continuous cardiac telemetry monitoring, subcutaneous enoxaparin 40 mg daily for DVT prophylaxis, sequential compression devices to both lower extremities, gabapentin 300 mg orally three times daily for neuropathic pain, acetaminophen 1,000 mg every six hours as needed for additional pain, strict intake and output monitoring, and a bladder scan if the patient has not voided within six hours. The patient is admitted to a step-down/intermediate care unit with a 1:3 nurse-to-patient ratio.

At hour 4, the first serial FVC is 3.4 L (40 mL/kg) and MIP is −58 cmH₂O. The patient reports that the tingling has now reached his fingertips bilaterally. Upper extremity strength is now 4+/5 in grip and wrist extension bilaterally, down from 5/5 at presentation. Lower extremity strength is unchanged. Heart rate ranges 78–94, blood pressure 118/72 to 134/86. He rates his leg pain at 7/10 despite the first dose of gabapentin. He has voided 250 mL spontaneously. The first IVIG infusion is initiated at the prescribed rate without reaction.

At hour 8, FVC is 3.1 L (36 mL/kg) and MIP is −48 cmH₂O. The patient now reports mild difficulty taking a deep breath and says he cannot count to 20 in a single breath (he reaches 14). SpO₂ remains 97% on room air. Heart rate 96, blood pressure 142/90. He has developed mild facial weakness bilaterally — he cannot fully bury his eyelashes against resistance, and his smile is symmetric but weak. He can still swallow liquids without coughing. Respiratory rate is 18.

At hour 12, FVC is 2.6 L (31 mL/kg) and MIP is −38 cmH₂O. The patient is now using accessory muscles with deep inspiration. He reports increasing difficulty clearing secretions with cough. Single-breath count is 10. SpO₂ is 96% on room air. Heart rate 104, blood pressure 148/94, respiratory rate 20. Upper extremity strength is now 4/5 proximally and 3+/5 distally. Lower extremity strength has declined: hip flexion 3/5, knee extension 3/5, ankle dorsiflexion 2/5 bilaterally. He has not voided in the past four hours; bladder scan shows 420 mL. The provider is notified of the declining respiratory parameters and orders a Foley catheter for urinary retention. The neurology team and the ICU are notified of the trajectory.

**Stage 2** — 12 to 24 hours after admission.
At hour 16, FVC has fallen to 2.0 L (24 mL/kg) and MIP is −32 cmH₂O. The patient is visibly anxious, speaking in shorter sentences, and reports he feels like he is "breathing through a straw." SpO₂ is 95% on room air. Heart rate is 112, blood pressure alternates between 156/98 and 98/62 over a 30-minute period without positional change — labile blood pressure consistent with autonomic instability. Telemetry shows sinus tachycardia with occasional premature ventricular contractions. Respiratory rate is 24 and shallow. Bilateral facial weakness is more pronounced. He can no longer lift his arms above his head. Lower extremities are 2/5 proximally and 1/5 distally. He has absent gag reflex on the left side. Foley catheter output is 180 mL over the past four hours (approximately 0.5 mL/kg/hr). The second IVIG infusion is running. The ICU team accepts transfer for impending respiratory failure, and the patient is moved to the intensive care unit.

At hour 20 in the ICU, FVC is 1.6 L (19 mL/kg) and MIP is −26 cmH₂O. The patient can speak only a few words at a time. He is unable to cough effectively. SpO₂ is 93% on 2 L nasal cannula. Heart rate 118, blood pressure 162/100, respiratory rate 28 and labored. He is diaphoretic. Upper extremity strength is 3/5 proximally and 2/5 distally. He cannot lift either leg off the bed. The intensive care provider orders emergent endotracheal intubation for respiratory failure. Rapid sequence intubation is performed without complication; the patient is placed on mechanical ventilation with initial settings of assist-control mode, lung-protective tidal volume per respiratory therapy protocol, rate 14, FiO₂ 40%, PEEP 5 cmH₂O. Post-intubation ABG: pH 7.34, PaCO₂ 48 mmHg, PaO₂ 142 mmHg, HCO₃ 25 mEq/L. IVIG continues on schedule.

**Stage 3** — ICU days 2 through 5.
The patient remains intubated and mechanically ventilated. IVIG is continued through day 5 of the planned course (total five infusions completed). Autonomic instability persists: heart rate swings between 56 and 128 over hours, blood pressure ranges from 88/54 to 172/104, and telemetry captures two brief runs of supraventricular tachycardia lasting 8–12 seconds each, which self-terminate. The ICU team manages blood pressure fluctuations with short-acting agents titrated to response rather than long-acting antihypertensives, per the attending's order, because of the unpredictable swings. The patient develops an ileus on ICU day 3 with absent bowel sounds and abdominal distension; a nasogastric tube is placed to low intermittent suction and enteral feeding is held temporarily.

By ICU day 4, the weakness has plateaued: upper extremities are 2/5 throughout, lower extremities are 1/5 throughout, facial weakness is stable, and the gag reflex remains absent bilaterally. FVC on a brief spontaneous breathing trial is 0.9 L (11 mL/kg) and MIP is −18 cmH₂O — well below extubation thresholds. The patient communicates by blinking and mouthing words. He is tearful and expresses fear that he will never walk again. Pain is managed with scheduled gabapentin via the nasogastric tube (titrated to 600 mg three times daily) and low-dose IV hydromorphone 0.2 mg every four hours as needed; he rates pain at 5/10 with this regimen. DVT prophylaxis continues with enoxaparin and SCDs. Physical therapy and occupational therapy have initiated passive range-of-motion exercises. A speech-language pathologist has evaluated communication needs and secretion-management readiness. The patient remains NPO while orally intubated, with medications given via the feeding tube; swallowing will be reassessed after extubation or after tracheostomy when clinically appropriate.

By ICU day 5, the autonomic swings have begun to narrow: heart rate ranges 68–102, blood pressure 104/66 to 146/88. Bowel sounds have returned. The nasogastric tube is discontinued from low intermittent suction and enteral nutrition is restarted/advanced through a feeding tube per ICU protocol. The patient remains NPO while orally intubated; SLP continues communication support and will reassess swallowing after extubation or after tracheostomy when clinically appropriate. FVC on a spontaneous breathing trial is 1.1 L (13 mL/kg) and MIP is −22 cmH₂O — improved from the nadir but still below safe extubation parameters. Repeat labs: sodium 138, potassium 4.0, creatinine 0.8, albumin 3.2 g/dL, hemoglobin 13.4 g/dL. No signs of infection. The ICU team discusses the likely need for tracheostomy if FVC does not improve to at least 20 mL/kg within the next several days.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At initial presentation, the nurse reviews the history and assessment findings — progressive symmetric ascending weakness over five days, absent deep tendon reflexes, stocking-distribution paresthesias, a Campylobacter diarrheal illness three weeks prior, intact bowel and bladder function, no sensory level on the trunk, and a normal spinal MRI. Which constellation of findings should the nurse recognize as requiring urgent neurological evaluation for a rapidly progressive neuromuscular emergency?
*Correct action:* Recognize that the combination of ascending symmetric weakness with areflexia following a known Campylobacter infection is the classic presentation of Guillain-Barré syndrome and that the rate of progression (feet to thighs in five days) signals urgency. The post-infectious timing, the areflexia, the absence of upper motor neuron signs, and the lack of a sensory level distinguish this from spinal cord pathology. The nurse should communicate these findings to the provider as an urgent concern and anticipate orders for lumbar puncture, nerve conduction studies, and baseline bedside pulmonary function testing.
*Why it is correct:* Campylobacter jejuni is the most commonly identified infectious trigger for GBS, and the typical latency is one to four weeks. Ascending symmetric weakness with areflexia is the hallmark motor pattern of AIDP. The absence of a sensory level, preserved rectal tone, and normal spinal imaging rule out cord compression. Recognizing the pattern early allows initiation of immunotherapy and respiratory monitoring before the patient deteriorates — approximately 20–30% of GBS patients will require mechanical ventilation, and early recognition is the first step in preventing unmonitored respiratory failure.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues.
*Answerable from:* Initial presentation and assessment findings (before Stage 1).

2. *Situation:* The lumbar puncture results and nerve conduction studies are available. The CSF shows protein 98 mg/dL with only 3 WBC/µL — albuminocytologic dissociation. The NCS shows demyelinating features. The nurse also has the baseline FVC of 3.6 L (42 mL/kg) and MIP of −62 cmH₂O. How should the nurse interpret these diagnostic findings in the context of the clinical picture, and which monitoring parameters are most critical going forward?
*Correct action:* Interpret the elevated CSF protein with a normal CSF white cell count as albuminocytologic dissociation, which is the characteristic CSF finding in GBS and supports the clinical diagnosis. Recognize that the demyelinating NCS pattern confirms AIDP. Critically, identify that serial bedside FVC and MIP — not pulse oximetry or subjective dyspnea — are the essential respiratory monitoring parameters. The baseline FVC of 42 mL/kg is adequate but already below predicted normal (75% of predicted), and the trajectory of these values over the coming hours will determine whether the patient needs intubation. Hypoxemia and oxygen desaturation are late findings in neuromuscular respiratory failure because the lungs are intrinsically normal — by the time SpO₂ drops, the patient may be in imminent respiratory arrest.
*Why it is correct:* In GBS, the blood-nerve barrier breaks down, allowing protein to leak into the CSF, but the process is not infectious or inflammatory in the CSF space itself, so the cell count remains normal. This dissociation distinguishes GBS from infectious meningitis or myelitis (which would show pleocytosis). FVC and MIP directly measure the mechanical capacity of the respiratory muscles — the diaphragm and intercostals — which are the muscles threatened by ascending GBS. Pulse oximetry measures gas exchange, which is preserved until ventilatory failure is far advanced because the lung parenchyma is healthy. Relying on SpO₂ alone creates a false sense of security.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues.
*Answerable from:* Initial laboratory data and assessment findings (before Stage 1), with the understanding that the monitoring framework applies throughout the clinical course.

3. *Situation:* At the time of initial evaluation, the differential diagnosis includes GBS, acute spinal cord compression, transverse myelitis, and myasthenia gravis. The nurse must understand which condition is most consistent with the full clinical picture in order to anticipate the correct plan of care and communicate effectively with the medical team.
*Correct action:* Prioritize GBS (AIDP) as the most likely diagnosis. Spinal cord compression is ruled out by the normal spinal MRI, the absence of a sensory level, the preserved rectal tone, and the pattern of ascending peripheral weakness with areflexia rather than upper motor neuron signs (hyperreflexia, spasticity, Babinski sign). Transverse myelitis typically presents with a sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction early in the course, and MRI signal abnormality in the cord — none of which are present. Myasthenia gravis produces fatigable weakness that worsens with repetitive use and improves with rest, predominantly affects ocular and bulbar muscles initially, and does not cause areflexia — this patient's weakness is fixed, ascending, and accompanied by absent reflexes.
*Why it is correct:* The combination of post-infectious onset, ascending symmetric weakness, areflexia, albuminocytologic dissociation, and demyelinating NCS strongly supports AIDP. Each competing diagnosis has specific features that are absent in this patient. Correctly prioritizing the hypothesis directs the care plan toward immunotherapy and respiratory monitoring rather than toward surgical decompression (cord compression), high-dose corticosteroids (transverse myelitis), or acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (myasthenia gravis) — each of which would be inappropriate or harmful in GBS.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses.
*Answerable from:* Initial presentation, assessment findings, and laboratory data (before Stage 1).

4. *Situation:* The diagnosis of GBS is confirmed. The nurse must understand the treatment plan, anticipate the correct immunotherapy, and recognize which commonly proposed treatments are contraindicated. The provider has ordered IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day for five days. The nurse must also know the respiratory thresholds that will trigger intubation.
*Correct action:* Recognize that IVIG and plasmapheresis are the two evidence-based immunotherapies for GBS and are equally effective; the choice between them depends on availability, patient factors (such as IV access, IgA deficiency for IVIG, or hemodynamic instability for plasmapheresis), and institutional capability. Corticosteroids are not recommended as treatment for GBS; oral corticosteroids may delay recovery, and IV methylprednisolone alone or with IVIG has not shown benefit. The nurse should not anticipate or administer corticosteroids for this condition. The critical respiratory thresholds for intubation per the provider's parameters are FVC less than 20 mL/kg or MIP weaker than −30 cmH₂O (that is, a MIP value that is less negative than −30, such as −26 or −20 cmH₂O, indicating the patient cannot generate adequate inspiratory force). The nurse should understand that a less negative MIP number represents weaker inspiratory effort, not stronger.
*Why it is correct:* IVIG works by modulating the immune attack on peripheral nerve myelin; plasmapheresis removes circulating pathogenic antibodies. Both shorten the course and reduce the severity of GBS. Corticosteroids, despite their efficacy in many other autoimmune conditions, do not improve outcomes in GBS and may delay recovery — this is a critical distinction because the reflex to use steroids for an immune-mediated condition is strong but incorrect here. The 20/30 rule for intubation (FVC < 20 mL/kg, MIP weaker than −30 cmH₂O) reflects the point at which respiratory muscle fatigue is likely to progress to ventilatory failure, especially with concurrent bulbar weakness, dysautonomia, or rapid decline. Waiting for hypoxemia or hypercapnia to trigger intubation risks respiratory arrest.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions.
*Answerable from:* Stage 1 (when the treatment plan is initiated and the trajectory of FVC/MIP decline becomes apparent).

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, hour 20, the patient's FVC has fallen to 1.6 L (19 mL/kg) and MIP is −26 cmH₂O. SpO₂ is 93% on 2 L nasal cannula. He is speaking only a few words at a time, cannot cough effectively, and is diaphoretic with a heart rate of 118 and blood pressure of 162/100. The ICU team is at the bedside. The nurse must determine the priority actions at this moment.
*Correct action:* The FVC of 19 mL/kg has crossed below the 20 mL/kg threshold and the MIP of −26 cmH₂O is weaker than the −30 cmH₂O threshold — high-risk/intubation criteria are met in this clinical context. The nurse's priority actions are to prepare for emergent intubation (assemble intubation equipment, suction, confirm IV access, draw up rapid sequence induction medications as directed, position the patient, ensure continuous monitoring), assist with the intubation procedure, and then establish mechanical ventilation with post-intubation assessment including arterial blood gas. Simultaneously, the nurse should continue the IVIG infusion (it is not discontinued because of intubation), maintain continuous cardiac telemetry given the autonomic instability (labile blood pressure, tachycardia, PVCs), ensure DVT prophylaxis is current, and address the patient's anxiety with calm, clear communication about what is happening and why. The nurse should not delay intubation to obtain additional pulmonary function measurements, wait for further SpO₂ decline, or attempt non-invasive ventilation as a substitute for intubation in this clinical context — the trajectory is clearly downward and the thresholds have been crossed.
*Why it is correct:* High-risk respiratory parameters are met in the setting of rapid clinical decline. The clinical signs — inability to speak in full sentences, ineffective cough, accessory muscle use, diaphoresis — corroborate the objective measurements. The SpO₂ of 93% is a late finding that confirms the urgency but should not have been the trigger — the FVC and MIP crossed their thresholds before the SpO₂ dropped meaningfully. Autonomic instability (blood pressure swinging from 156/98 to 98/62 and now at 162/100 with tachycardia and PVCs) adds cardiovascular risk and is an independent indication for ICU-level monitoring. Continuing IVIG is essential because the immunotherapy is treating the underlying disease process; intubation addresses the respiratory complication but does not replace disease-modifying treatment.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action.
*Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, ICU day 5, the patient has completed the five-day IVIG course. The weakness has plateaued and is beginning to show the earliest signs of stabilization. Autonomic swings have narrowed. Bowel function is returning. FVC on a spontaneous breathing trial is 1.1 L (13 mL/kg) and MIP is −22 cmH₂O. The nurse must evaluate the patient's trajectory and determine whether the clinical course is consistent with expected recovery, continued deterioration, or a need for additional intervention.
*Correct action:* Evaluate that the patient is in the plateau phase of GBS — the weakness has stopped progressing, which is a positive sign, but respiratory function has not recovered sufficiently for extubation (FVC 13 mL/kg is well below the 20 mL/kg threshold; MIP −22 cmH₂O remains weaker than −30 cmH₂O). The narrowing of autonomic swings (heart rate 68–102 versus the prior 56–128, blood pressure 104/66–146/88 versus 88/54–172/104) indicates improving autonomic stability, which is a favorable trend. The return of bowel sounds and tolerance of enteral feeding suggests resolving ileus. However, the FVC trajectory — from a nadir of 0.9 L on day 4 to 1.1 L on day 5 — represents only marginal improvement, and the patient remains ventilator-dependent. The nurse should communicate to the patient and family that recovery from GBS is typically slow (weeks to months), that the plateau is expected and does not mean treatment has failed, and that tracheostomy may be necessary for prolonged ventilatory support. The nurse should continue to monitor FVC and MIP trends, maintain DVT prophylaxis, support rehabilitation efforts, manage pain, and assess the patient's psychological state — his tearfulness and expressed fear of permanent disability require psychosocial support, therapeutic communication, and possible referral to a mental health professional.
*Why it is correct:* GBS follows a characteristic course: progressive weakness (days to four weeks), a plateau phase (days to weeks), and then gradual recovery (weeks to months or longer). The plateau does not indicate treatment failure — IVIG shortens the course and reduces severity but does not produce immediate reversal. Evaluating outcomes in GBS requires tracking the trajectory of multiple parameters: respiratory function (FVC/MIP trends), motor strength (stabilization versus continued decline), autonomic stability (narrowing of heart rate and blood pressure variability), and functional recovery markers (swallowing, bowel function). A single improved value does not confirm recovery, and a single stable value does not confirm plateau — the trend over days is what matters. The psychosocial dimension is clinically significant: GBS patients who are cognitively intact but locked in by profound weakness are at high risk for anxiety, depression, and feelings of helplessness, and addressing these needs is within the nurse's scope and responsibility.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes.
*Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Failing to recognize the urgency of the presentation because the patient is still ambulatory and has a normal SpO₂. A nurse might assess the patient as "stable" because he can walk and his oxygen saturation is 98%, missing that the ascending pattern and areflexia indicate a rapidly progressive neuromuscular emergency. The ability to walk at presentation does not predict the trajectory — GBS can progress to complete paralysis and respiratory failure within hours to days, and the current ambulatory status is a snapshot, not a prognosis.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Relying on pulse oximetry as the primary respiratory monitor and feeling reassured by a normal SpO₂. In neuromuscular respiratory failure, the lungs are healthy — gas exchange is preserved until the respiratory muscles are too weak to ventilate, at which point the patient decompensates rapidly. SpO₂ may remain above 94% until the patient is in imminent respiratory arrest. FVC and MIP are the tools that detect declining ventilatory capacity before it becomes a crisis.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Interpreting the elevated CSF protein as evidence of bacterial meningitis and expecting antibiotic therapy. The CSF protein is elevated, but the normal WBC count (3 cells/µL) is the critical distinguishing feature — bacterial meningitis produces both elevated protein and marked pleocytosis (hundreds to thousands of WBCs). Albuminocytologic dissociation (high protein, normal cells) is characteristic of GBS, not infection.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Suspecting spinal cord compression despite the normal MRI and the absence of upper motor neuron signs. A nurse unfamiliar with the distinction between upper and lower motor neuron patterns might focus on the "ascending weakness" and assume cord pathology, potentially advocating for emergent surgical consultation rather than immunotherapy. The areflexia, the peripheral nerve distribution, and the normal cord imaging definitively exclude compression.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Expecting or advocating for corticosteroid therapy because GBS is an autoimmune/immune-mediated condition. The intuition that "autoimmune equals steroids" is deeply ingrained but incorrect for GBS — corticosteroids are not recommended as they may delay recovery and have not shown benefit. This is one of the most important disease-specific distinctions in GBS management. If a nurse sees an order for high-dose methylprednisolone, the appropriate action is to clarify the order with the provider, not to administer it.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Delaying intubation because the SpO₂ is "only" 93% and the patient is still conscious and speaking. The FVC and MIP have both crossed their respective thresholds (FVC < 20 mL/kg, MIP weaker than −30 cmH₂O), and the clinical trajectory is unambiguously downward. Waiting for SpO₂ to drop further or for the patient to become obtunded risks respiratory arrest. The intubation decision in GBS is based on the pulmonary function trend, not on the oxygen saturation at a single moment.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Attempting non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (BiPAP) as a bridge to avoid intubation in a patient with progressive neuromuscular weakness, absent gag reflex, and inability to clear secretions. Non-invasive ventilation is not appropriate when the patient cannot protect the airway, cannot cough effectively, and has a clearly declining trajectory — it delays definitive airway management and increases aspiration risk.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the plateau phase as treatment failure and advocating for a second course of IVIG or switching to plasmapheresis immediately. The plateau is the expected natural history of GBS, not evidence that IVIG did not work. A second course of IVIG is not routinely recommended after a full five-day course and is a provider-level decision based on specific clinical criteria — the nurse should not assume that lack of rapid improvement equals treatment failure.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Neglecting the psychosocial needs of the patient because the focus is on physiological monitoring. The patient is cognitively intact, aware of his paralysis, tearful, and expressing fear of permanent disability. Failing to address his emotional state, provide therapeutic communication, involve the family in care planning, and consider a mental health referral is a significant nursing omission. Psychosocial integrity is a core nursing responsibility, not a secondary concern.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the classic clinical presentation of Guillain-Barré syndrome — ascending symmetric weakness with areflexia following a preceding infection — and understand the urgency of the presentation based on the rate of progression, not the current functional status.

Interpret albuminocytologic dissociation in the cerebrospinal fluid as the characteristic diagnostic finding of GBS and distinguish it from infectious meningitis, and identify FVC and MIP as the critical respiratory monitoring parameters in neuromuscular respiratory failure rather than relying on pulse oximetry.

Differentiate GBS from competing diagnoses — spinal cord compression, transverse myelitis, and myasthenia gravis — based on specific clinical features, and prioritize the correct diagnostic hypothesis to guide the plan of care.

Apply the respiratory intubation thresholds for GBS (FVC < 20 mL/kg, MIP weaker than −30 cmH₂O) to determine when emergent intubation is indicated, and recognize that corticosteroids are not recommended as treatment for GBS despite its immune-mediated etiology.

Evaluate the trajectory of a mechanically ventilated GBS patient across the progressive, plateau, and early recovery phases by integrating respiratory function trends, autonomic stability, motor examination, and psychosocial assessment to determine whether the clinical course is consistent with expected recovery or requires escalation.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, hour 20, when the FVC has fallen to 19 mL/kg and MIP to −26 cmH₂O, both crossing the intubation thresholds, with progressive quadriparesis, autonomic instability, and loss of airway protective reflexes.

Most likely condition: Guillain-Barré syndrome (acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy) with impending respiratory failure requiring emergent intubation. The ascending symmetric weakness with areflexia, post-Campylobacter onset, albuminocytologic dissociation, demyelinating NCS pattern, and progressive decline in FVC and MIP over 20 hours constitute an unambiguous presentation of GBS that has now reached the critical respiratory threshold.

Plausible competing conditions: (1) Myasthenia gravis with respiratory crisis — wrong here because the weakness is fixed and ascending rather than fatigable and fluctuating, deep tendon reflexes are absent rather than preserved, and the post-infectious onset with albuminocytologic dissociation and demyelinating NCS are not features of myasthenia gravis. (2) Acute transverse myelitis with ascending paralysis — wrong here because there is no sensory level on the trunk, no early bowel or bladder dysfunction (urinary retention developed later with autonomic dysfunction, not as the presenting pattern of a cord syndrome), the spinal MRI shows no cord signal abnormality, and the CSF shows albuminocytologic dissociation rather than the pleocytosis typically seen in myelitis.

Two priority actions: (1) Prepare for and assist with emergent endotracheal intubation — both intubation thresholds have been crossed (FVC 19 mL/kg, MIP −26 cmH₂O), the patient cannot cough effectively, the gag reflex is absent on one side, and the respiratory trajectory is unambiguously downward; securing the airway is the immediate life-saving priority. (2) Continue the IVIG infusion on schedule and ensure uninterrupted administration through and after intubation — the immunotherapy is treating the underlying autoimmune attack on peripheral nerve myelin and must not be interrupted by the transition to mechanical ventilation; stopping IVIG because the patient is being intubated would deprive him of the disease-modifying treatment.

Two wrong actions: (1) Initiating non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (BiPAP) instead of proceeding to intubation — wrong because the patient has lost airway protective reflexes (absent gag on the left), cannot clear secretions, and has a clearly progressive downward trajectory; BiPAP does not secure the airway, increases aspiration risk in a patient who cannot protect it, and delays definitive intervention. (2) Administering high-dose intravenous corticosteroids in an attempt to halt the immune-mediated progression — wrong because corticosteroids are not recommended as treatment for GBS; oral corticosteroids may delay recovery, and IV methylprednisolone has not shown benefit; the reflex to treat an autoimmune condition with steroids is a common error that should be avoided in this disease.

Two parameters to monitor: (1) FVC and MIP trends on serial bedside measurements — these directly measure the mechanical capacity of the respiratory muscles and are the parameters that will determine whether the patient can eventually be weaned from the ventilator; an upward trend in FVC (toward 20 mL/kg) and a more negative MIP (toward −30 cmH₂O or stronger) will signal the beginning of respiratory recovery. (2) Heart rate and blood pressure variability as markers of autonomic stability — autonomic dysfunction is a major driver of mortality in GBS, manifesting as labile blood pressure, bradycardia, tachycardia, and arrhythmias; narrowing of the heart rate and blood pressure ranges over time indicates improving autonomic regulation and reduced risk of cardiovascular collapse.

Two irrelevant parameters: (1) SpO₂ alone as a measure of respiratory status — in neuromuscular respiratory failure with healthy lungs, SpO₂ remains normal until ventilatory failure is far advanced; it is a lagging indicator that does not detect the progressive loss of ventilatory muscle strength that FVC and MIP capture, and relying on it creates a false sense of security. (2) CSF protein level on repeat lumbar puncture — the CSF protein has already served its diagnostic purpose by demonstrating albuminocytologic dissociation; repeating the lumbar puncture to track protein levels does not inform the acute management decisions at this synthesis moment (intubation, ventilator weaning, autonomic management) and exposes the patient to an unnecessary invasive procedure.
