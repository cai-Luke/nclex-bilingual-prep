**CASE TITLE**
Spinal cord compression from metastatic cancer.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 61-year-old woman with a three-year history of stage IV non-small cell lung cancer with known osseous metastases to the thoracic spine (T6, T8) and ribs is admitted to the oncology unit from the emergency department. She was diagnosed with bone metastases eight months ago and has been receiving palliative radiation and systemic therapy. She has a history of well-controlled hypertension managed with amlodipine 5 mg daily and a 30-pack-year smoking history. Home medications include dexamethasone 4 mg PO daily (recently tapered from a higher dose after her last radiation cycle), oxycodone 10 mg PO every 6 hours as needed for back pain, ondansetron 4 mg PO every 8 hours as needed, enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneous daily for VTE prophylaxis, and a bisphosphonate infusion monthly. She presents because her back pain has changed in character and intensity over the past 48 hours, and she is having new difficulty walking. Current admission orders include IV normal saline at 75 mL/hr, continuation of home medications, neurological checks every 4 hours, fall precautions, and a stat MRI of the entire spine with and without contrast.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient reports that her usual mid-thoracic back pain, which she describes as a dull ache rated 4 out of 10 and typically relieved by oxycodone, has escalated over the past two days to a sharp, band-like pain across her mid-back radiating around both flanks, now rated 8 out of 10 and no longer relieved by her usual oxycodone dose. She states that this morning she noticed her legs felt "heavy and clumsy" when she tried to walk to the bathroom, and she stumbled twice. She denies any recent falls or trauma. She appears anxious and is lying very still in bed, reluctant to move because movement worsens the pain. She says she has not had a bowel movement in three days but attributes this to her opioid use. She notes that when she urinated this morning, the stream seemed weak and she felt she did not fully empty her bladder.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline vital signs: temperature 37.1 °C, heart rate 92 beats per minute, blood pressure 138/82 mmHg, respiratory rate 18 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 96% on room air. She is alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. Cranial nerves are intact. Upper extremity strength is 5 out of 5 bilaterally with normal sensation. Lower extremity motor strength is 4 out of 5 bilaterally in hip flexors and quadriceps and 4 minus out of 5 in ankle dorsiflexors, with the left side slightly weaker than the right. She has difficulty lifting her legs against gravity with sustained resistance. Deep tendon reflexes at the patellar tendons are hyperreflexic (3+) bilaterally; Achilles reflexes are 3+ bilaterally. Babinski sign is equivocal on the right and extensor (positive, upgoing toe) on the left. Sensation to light touch and pinprick is diminished below approximately the T8 dermatome, with a vague band of hyperesthesia at the T6–T7 level. Proprioception in the great toes is mildly reduced bilaterally. She has point tenderness on palpation over the T6 and T8 spinous processes. Rectal tone is present but diminished on exam performed by the admitting provider. A bladder scan shows a post-void residual of 280 mL. Skin is intact, warm, and dry. She has mild bilateral lower extremity edema (1+), which she says is chronic. Track lower-extremity motor strength, sensory level, bladder function/post-void residual or catheter output, reflexes/clonus, Babinski response, perineal sensation, and rectal tone or voluntary anal contraction across stages.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs: WBC 6,800 per microliter, hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL, platelets 188,000 per microliter, sodium 141 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, calcium (corrected) 10.1 mg/dL, albumin 3.2 g/dL, alkaline phosphatase 312 U/L (elevated, consistent with bone metastases), ALT 28 U/L, AST 32 U/L, glucose 118 mg/dL, PT/INR 1.0, aPTT 29 seconds. Urinalysis is unremarkable. Calcium, creatinine, and alkaline phosphatase provide oncology and treatment context; they should not distract from the urgent neurologic trajectory unless calcium becomes severely abnormal, which it does not in this case.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — approximately 3 hours after admission: The MRI results return and confirm an epidural mass at T7 with significant spinal cord compression. The tumor is arising from the known T6 and T8 vertebral body metastases and is causing greater than 50 percent canal narrowing with cord edema and signal change on T2-weighted images. There is no evidence of spinal instability or fracture-dislocation. The oncologist and neurosurgeon are notified. The provider orders an immediate increase of dexamethasone to 10 mg IV bolus now, followed by 4 mg IV every 6 hours (urgent MSCC dexamethasone protocol). The nurse reassesses the patient after the MRI and finds the following changes: pain has increased to 9 out of 10 despite receiving oxycodone 10 mg PO one hour ago. Lower extremity motor strength has declined to 3 out of 5 bilaterally in hip flexors and quadriceps and 3 minus out of 5 in ankle dorsiflexors. She can no longer lift her legs against gravity with sustained resistance and has noticeable foot drop on the left. Sensory level has risen slightly, with diminished sensation now beginning at the T7 dermatome. She reports new numbness in her perineal area. A repeat bladder scan, performed because the patient has not voided despite adequate fluid intake over 3 hours, shows 420 mL. Vital signs: temperature 37.2 °C, heart rate 98, blood pressure 142/86, respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 95% on room air. She is increasingly anxious, tearful, and asking, "Am I going to be paralyzed?"

**Stage 2** — approximately 8 hours after admission (5 hours after Stage 1): The dexamethasone bolus was administered and the scheduled doses are running. A radiation oncology consult has been placed, and a neurosurgical consult is evaluating the patient for possible surgical decompression versus emergent radiation. The nurse has obtained an order for and placed an indwelling urinary catheter, which drained 550 mL of clear yellow urine on insertion. Pain management has been escalated: the provider has ordered a hydromorphone PCA pump (0.2 mg demand dose, 6-minute lockout, no basal rate). The patient's pain is now 6 out of 10 on the PCA, improved from 9 out of 10. However, the neurological examination continues to worsen: lower extremity motor strength is now 2 out of 5 bilaterally — she can only move her legs with gravity eliminated. Ankle dorsiflexion is 1 out of 5 on the left and 2 out of 5 on the right. She cannot wiggle her left toes on command. Deep tendon reflexes at the patellar tendons are now 4+ with clonus (3 beats) at both ankles. Babinski sign is clearly positive (upgoing) bilaterally. Sensory level has risen to approximately T6; she reports she cannot feel the nurse's touch below her nipple line on the left and only vaguely on the right. Perineal sensation is absent. Rectal tone, when re-evaluated by the provider, is now absent. Vital signs: temperature 37.6 °C, heart rate 86, lower than prior after improved pain control and reduced anxiety., blood pressure 136/80, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Blood glucose check is 186 mg/dL (steroid-induced hyperglycemia). Repeat calcium (corrected) is 10.8 mg/dL — trending upward. The neurosurgeon recommends emergent surgical decompression (posterior laminectomy at T6–T8) given the rapid neurological decline, to be followed by postoperative radiation. Surgery is scheduled for this evening. The patient and her family are distressed; her adult daughter asks the nurse whether surgery will make her mother walk again.

**Stage 3** — approximately 30 hours after admission (postoperative day 1, roughly 18 hours after posterior laminectomy): The patient is transferred back to the oncology unit from the PACU. She is awake, alert, and oriented. Surgical site dressing at the posterior thoracic spine is clean, dry, and intact with a Jackson-Pratt drain in place with 40 mL of serosanguineous output over the past 8 hours. Vital signs: temperature 37.8 °C, heart rate 78, blood pressure 124/74, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 97% on 2 L nasal cannula. Pain is 5 out of 10 at the surgical site, managed on IV hydromorphone. On neurological reassessment: lower extremity motor strength is now 3 out of 5 bilaterally in hip flexors and quadriceps — improved from the 2 out of 5 seen preoperatively. Ankle dorsiflexion is 2 out of 5 bilaterally (improved on the left from 1 out of 5). She can wiggle all toes weakly on command. Deep tendon reflexes remain hyperreflexic (3+) but clonus is no longer sustained — only 1 beat at the left ankle, absent on the right. Babinski sign remains positive bilaterally. Sensory level has improved: she now perceives light touch beginning at T8 and pinprick at T9. Perineal sensation remains absent. The indwelling urinary catheter remains in place, draining clear yellow urine at 60 mL per hour. Blood glucose is 210 mg/dL; a sliding-scale insulin order is in place. Repeat labs: hemoglobin 9.6 g/dL (dropped from 10.8, expected surgical loss), creatinine 0.9 mg/dL (stable), corrected calcium 10.4 mg/dL (slightly improved from 10.8, bisphosphonate and steroids helping), alkaline phosphatase 298 U/L (stable). The dexamethasone is continued at 4 mg IV every 6 hours with a planned taper over the coming weeks. Radiation oncology plans to initiate adjuvant radiation to the T6–T8 field in 10 to 14 days once the surgical wound has begun to heal. The patient's mood is cautiously hopeful. She asks the nurse, "Will I need to learn to use a wheelchair?"

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. Situation and question: At the initial presentation, before MRI results are available, the nurse must recognize the clinical picture. Which constellation of assessment findings should prompt the nurse to suspect malignant spinal cord compression and treat it as a neurological emergency? Correct action: The nurse should recognize that the triad of escalating, unrelenting back pain at the level of known spinal metastases, new bilateral lower extremity weakness with upper motor neuron signs (hyperreflexia, positive Babinski), and a sensory level at T8 with bladder dysfunction (elevated post-void residual of 280 mL) in a patient with known vertebral metastases is the classic presentation of malignant spinal cord compression and constitutes an oncologic emergency requiring urgent intervention. The nurse should immediately communicate these findings to the provider using a structured handoff tool, emphasize the time-sensitive nature, and advocate for expedited MRI and neurosurgical consultation if not already ordered. Why it is correct: Malignant spinal cord compression results from tumor expansion into the epidural space, causing mechanical compression and vascular compromise of the spinal cord. Back pain is the earliest and most common symptom, present in over 90 percent of patients. Motor weakness, sensory changes below the level of compression, and autonomic dysfunction (bowel and bladder) follow. The degree of neurological function at the time definitive treatment begins is the single strongest predictor of functional outcome — patients who are still ambulatory at the time of treatment have the best chance of remaining ambulatory. Delays in recognition directly translate to worse neurological outcomes, making early cue recognition critical. Clinical judgment skill: Recognize cues. When it becomes answerable: Initial presentation data.

2. Situation and question: At Stage 1, the MRI confirms epidural compression at T7 with cord edema, and the patient's neurological status has declined (motor strength 3 out of 5, rising sensory level, inability to void with 420 mL residual, new perineal numbness). The provider has ordered dexamethasone 10 mg IV bolus. What is the priority nursing action at this moment? Correct action: The nurse should administer the dexamethasone 10 mg IV bolus immediately as ordered — this is the highest-priority pharmacological intervention and should not be delayed for non-urgent tasks. Simultaneously, the nurse should perform and document a thorough, timed baseline neurological assessment (motor strength in each myotome, sensory level, rectal tone status, bladder function) to serve as the reference point against which all subsequent examinations will be compared, notify the provider of the worsening neurological findings if not already communicated, and advocate for urgent definitive treatment (surgical decompression or radiation). Why it is correct: High-dose dexamethasone reduces vasogenic edema surrounding the compressed spinal cord, which can halt or slow neurological deterioration. Immediate administration is essential because every hour of ongoing compression increases the risk of irreversible ischemic injury to the cord. The timed, detailed neurological assessment establishes the trajectory of decline, which is the data the neurosurgeon and radiation oncologist use to determine whether emergent surgery is warranted over scheduled radiation. A rapidly worsening exam — as seen here — typically triggers surgical intervention. Clinical judgment skill: Take action. When it becomes answerable: Stage 1.

3. Situation and question: At Stage 1, the patient has not voided in 3 hours and the bladder scan shows 420 mL. How should the nurse interpret and act on this finding in the context of the overall clinical picture? Correct action: The nurse should recognize urinary retention as a neurologic finding caused by progressive thoracic cord compression, not as an isolated urinary issue or merely an opioid adverse effect. The nurse should notify the provider immediately, request an order for an indwelling urinary catheter, and document the finding as evidence of progressing cord compression. Why it is correct: Urinary retention in this thoracic MSCC case reflects interruption of descending autonomic pathways and is ominous because it occurs with worsening lower-extremity weakness, a rising sensory level, and new perineal sensory change. While opioids can contribute to urinary retention, the combination with progressive lower extremity weakness, a rising sensory level, and new perineal numbness points to cord compression as the cause. Failing to attribute the retention to cord compression could lead to a dangerous underestimation of the urgency of the situation. Decompressing the bladder with a catheter is also necessary to prevent overdistention injury and to allow accurate monitoring of urine output. Clinical judgment skill: Analyze cues. When it becomes answerable: Stage 1.

4. Situation and question: At Stage 2, the patient's neurological examination has worsened significantly despite initiation of high-dose dexamethasone (motor strength 2 out of 5, absent rectal tone, absent perineal sensation, clonus). The neurosurgeon recommends emergent surgical decompression. What are the priority nursing actions to prepare this patient for emergent surgery? Correct action: The nurse should prioritize the following in sequence: continue neurological assessments every 1 to 2 hours and document the trajectory to confirm ongoing decline and support the surgical decision; verify that the surgeon has discussed the procedure and obtained informed consent; witness the signature per policy; and notify the surgeon or anesthesia team if the patient or family has unresolved questions; verify NPO status and when the patient last ate or drank; ensure current labs, type and screen, and imaging are available and flagged for the surgical team; hold the scheduled enoxaparin dose and communicate the last dose timing to the surgical team and anesthesia; verify IV access is adequate (at least one large-bore peripheral IV); address the patient's blood glucose of 186 mg/dL per protocol and communicate the steroid-induced hyperglycemia to anesthesia; ensure the indwelling urinary catheter is in place and draining; and manage the patient's and family's emotional distress with honest, supportive communication, emphasizing that the goal of surgery is to prevent further neurological loss. Why it is correct: Emergent surgical decompression in the setting of rapidly progressive neurological deficit is time-sensitive, and the nurse's role is to systematically eliminate barriers to getting the patient to the operating room while maintaining safety. Holding anticoagulation is essential to reduce surgical bleeding risk. Communicating the steroid-related glucose elevation to the anesthesia team ensures appropriate intraoperative glucose management. Honest communication about realistic surgical goals (halting decline, not guaranteeing recovery) upholds the ethical principle of veracity and supports informed consent. Clinical judgment skill: Generate solutions. When it becomes answerable: Stage 2.

5. Situation and question: At Stage 2, the patient's daughter asks the nurse, "Will surgery make my mother walk again?" How should the nurse respond? Correct action: The nurse should provide an honest, compassionate response that acknowledges the family's fear and avoids making predictions beyond nursing scope, while reinforcing realistic expectations. An appropriate response would be: "I understand how frightening this is. The goal of surgery is to take pressure off the spinal cord and prevent further neurologic loss. Some patients regain function, and the fact that your mother still has some leg movement is encouraging, but the neurosurgeon is the best person to discuss expected recovery." The nurse should not offer false reassurance ("She'll be fine") or prognosticate definitively. Why it is correct: Prognostication regarding surgical outcomes is outside the nursing scope of practice and falls to the surgeon. However, the nurse has a therapeutic responsibility to address the emotional needs of the patient and family, provide accurate general information, and facilitate communication with the appropriate provider. The response balances honesty, hope grounded in the clinical reality (some residual motor function is a positive prognostic indicator), and appropriate referral to the surgeon. Clinical judgment skill: Take action (therapeutic communication as a nursing intervention). When it becomes answerable: Stage 2.

6. Situation and question: At Stage 3, postoperative day 1, the nurse reassesses the patient. Motor strength has improved from 2 out of 5 preoperatively to 3 out of 5, clonus has decreased, and the sensory level has dropped from T6 back to T8–T9. However, perineal sensation remains absent, the indwelling catheter is still required, Babinski signs are still positive bilaterally, and the patient asks about using a wheelchair. How should the nurse evaluate the effectiveness of the surgical intervention and plan ongoing care? Correct action: The nurse should recognize that the improvement in motor strength, reduction in clonus, and lowering of the sensory level are positive signs indicating that the surgical decompression has relieved some of the pressure on the cord and that the neurological trajectory has reversed from decline to partial recovery. However, the persistent absence of perineal sensation and ongoing bladder dysfunction indicate that autonomic and sacral-mediated bladder/bowel function has not yet recovered, and the continued positive Babinski signs confirm residual upper motor neuron dysfunction. The nurse should document the comparison between preoperative and postoperative neurological findings explicitly, continue neurological assessments every 4 hours, initiate a formal rehabilitation and physical therapy referral, implement a comprehensive bowel program (the patient has not had a bowel movement in at least 4 days now and has absent rectal tone), monitor for complications of immobility (deep vein thrombosis, pressure injury, pneumonia), maintain the skin integrity protocol with repositioning every 2 hours, continue to monitor blood glucose closely given ongoing high-dose steroids, and provide realistic, supportive communication about the recovery trajectory — which may take weeks to months, may plateau, and may not include full return of bladder and bowel function. Why it is correct: Evaluation of outcomes in spinal cord compression requires serial comparison of objective neurological findings against the established baseline and preoperative status. Motor improvement is the most clinically meaningful early indicator of successful decompression, while autonomic function (bladder, bowel, perineal sensation) typically recovers last and sometimes incompletely. The nurse's postoperative role extends beyond monitoring the surgical site to encompass the full spectrum of spinal cord injury nursing care: prevention of secondary complications, rehabilitation planning, and psychosocial support. Clinical judgment skill: Evaluate outcomes. When it becomes answerable: Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Attributing the patient's leg weakness and difficulty walking solely to generalized cancer-related fatigue or deconditioning rather than recognizing it as a neurological emergency. This is wrong because fatigue does not produce upper motor neuron signs (hyperreflexia, Babinski), a discrete sensory level, or acute urinary retention. Failing to recognize the urgency delays definitive treatment and worsens prognosis.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Assuming the back pain is simply a worsening of chronic cancer pain and focusing only on increasing analgesic doses without performing a neurological assessment. This is wrong because the qualitative change in pain — from a dull ache to a sharp, band-like, radicular pattern that is no longer responsive to the usual opioid dose — is a red flag for structural progression (epidural compression) rather than nociceptive bone pain alone. Treating pain without assessing neurological status misses the emergency.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Delaying administration of the dexamethasone bolus to complete a full admission assessment, recheck labs, or wait for pharmacy verification of a non-urgent medication. This is wrong because dexamethasone is the first-line pharmacological intervention in suspected or confirmed malignant spinal cord compression, and its benefit is time-dependent. Every delay contributes to further cord ischemia and irreversible neurological loss.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Attributing the urinary retention entirely to opioid use and managing it with conservative measures (running water, warm cloths) rather than recognizing it as cord compression-related autonomic dysfunction. This is wrong because in the context of progressive motor and sensory deficits, urinary retention is a neurological finding, not a medication side effect, and conservative measures will not address the underlying cause. Furthermore, delaying catheterization risks bladder overdistention injury.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Administering the scheduled enoxaparin dose because it is due on the medication administration record, without considering the emergent surgical plan. This is wrong because anticoagulation within the perioperative window increases the risk of epidural hematoma and surgical bleeding, which could cause additional spinal cord compromise. The nurse should hold the dose and communicate with the surgical team.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Telling the daughter, "Don't worry, the surgery will fix everything and she'll walk again," to provide comfort. This is wrong because it constitutes false reassurance, violates the principle of veracity, may undermine informed consent, and sets the family up for a devastating psychological blow if full recovery does not occur. Prognostication is outside nursing scope, and the nurse should facilitate realistic expectations and communication with the surgeon.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the persistent positive Babinski signs and absent perineal sensation as evidence that surgery failed, and communicating hopelessness to the patient. This is wrong because upper motor neuron signs may persist for an extended period after decompression, and autonomic function (bladder, bowel, perineal sensation) is typically the last to recover. The meaningful evaluation metric at postoperative day 1 is the trajectory of change — motor strength improved, clonus decreased, and sensory level improved, all of which indicate a positive response. Full recovery assessment occurs over weeks to months.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Failing to initiate a bowel management program because the nurse is focused on the surgical site and motor recovery. This is wrong because the patient has absent rectal tone, has not had a bowel movement in over four days, and is on opioids — she is at extremely high risk for neurogenic bowel complications including constipation, impaction, discomfort, skin/continence issues, and neurogenic bowel morbidity. A structured bowel program is an essential component of spinal cord injury nursing care.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the clinical presentation of malignant spinal cord compression — escalating back pain at the level of known metastases, new motor weakness with upper motor neuron signs, a definable sensory level, and bladder or bowel dysfunction — as an oncologic emergency requiring immediate action.

Differentiate neurological findings caused by spinal cord compression from symptoms attributable to other causes (opioid side effects, cancer-related fatigue, chronic pain) by integrating the pattern of motor, sensory, reflex, and autonomic findings.

Prioritize and implement time-sensitive nursing interventions for malignant spinal cord compression, including immediate corticosteroid administration, serial neurological monitoring, bladder management, and preparation for emergent definitive treatment.

Evaluate the effectiveness of surgical decompression by comparing serial postoperative neurological examinations against the preoperative baseline, distinguishing early positive indicators (motor improvement, decreased clonus, lowering sensory level) from findings expected to recover slowly or incompletely (autonomic function, Babinski sign).

Plan comprehensive postoperative nursing care for a patient with residual spinal cord dysfunction, including prevention of immobility complications, bowel and bladder management, rehabilitation referral, steroid side-effect monitoring, and therapeutic communication about realistic recovery expectations.
