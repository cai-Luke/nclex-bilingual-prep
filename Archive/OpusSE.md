**CASE TITLE**
Status epilepticus refractory to first-line benzodiazepine therapy.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 27-year-old woman with a three-year history of generalized tonic-clonic epilepsy, managed with levetiracetam 1,000 mg PO twice daily, is brought to the emergency department by paramedics. She lives alone and was found seizing by a coworker who went to check on her after she failed to appear at work. The coworker reports the patient mentioned feeling "off" the previous evening. Paramedics document that generalized tonic-clonic seizure activity was observed on their arrival and that four minutes of continuous convulsive activity had already elapsed per the coworker's estimate. The patient's medication bottles were found at home; the levetiracetam bottle appeared mostly full despite a fill date three weeks ago, raising concern for medication nonadherence. She has no other significant medical history and no known allergies. A peripheral IV (20-gauge, right antecubital) was established in the field and lorazepam 4 mg IV was administered seven minutes into the seizure, followed by a second lorazepam 4 mg IV dose at twelve minutes due to ongoing seizure. She is now arriving at the ED, approximately fifteen minutes into continuous seizure activity.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
On ED arrival the patient is on a stretcher in active generalized tonic-clonic seizure. She is unresponsive, with rhythmic bilateral upper- and lower-extremity tonic-clonic movements, jaw clenching, copious oral secretions, and urinary incontinence. There is no purposeful movement and no response to verbal stimuli. The seizure has not stopped despite two adequate doses of prehospital lorazepam. By definition — continuous convulsive seizure activity lasting longer than five minutes — this meets criteria for status epilepticus, and because the initial benzodiazepines have failed, it is now classified as established status epilepticus requiring urgent second-line therapy.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline on arrival: Temperature 38.3 °C (tympanic), heart rate 138 bpm and regular on the monitor, blood pressure 178/102 mmHg, respiratory rate 28 (shallow and irregular, partially obscured by motor activity), SpO₂ 88% on room air. Pupils are equal at 4 mm but reactivity is difficult to assess during convulsive activity. Skin is diaphoretic and flushed. Bite injury to the lateral tongue with blood-tinged saliva. No signs of head trauma. The single peripheral IV is intact and running normal saline at a keep-vein-open rate. Continuous cardiac monitoring shows sinus tachycardia without ectopy. End-tidal CO₂ (if capnography is available) is 52 mmHg, consistent with hypoventilation. Fingerstick blood glucose is 142 mg/dL. A point-of-care urine pregnancy test is obtained and returns negative.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn on arrival, results returning over the next 20–30 minutes): Sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.6 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, bicarbonate 16 mEq/L (low — lactic acidosis from prolonged muscle activity), BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 148 mg/dL, calcium (ionized) 1.14 mmol/L (normal), magnesium 1.8 mg/dL (normal), phosphorus 4.2 mg/dL, lactate 6.8 mmol/L (elevated), WBC 14,200/µL (stress leukocytosis), hemoglobin 13.1 g/dL, platelets 224,000/µL, CK 480 U/L (mildly elevated, expected to rise), levetiracetam serum level 4.2 µg/mL (subtherapeutic; reference range 12–46 µg/mL). Arterial blood gas: pH 7.24, PaCO₂ 48 mmHg, PaO₂ 68 mmHg, HCO₃ 16 mEq/L.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 8 minutes after ED arrival (approximately 15–23 minutes of total seizure duration). The patient remains in continuous generalized tonic-clonic status epilepticus. The nurse positions her on her side, suctions oral secretions, applies supplemental oxygen via non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min, and ensures IV access. SpO₂ improves to 92%. The emergency physician orders fosphenytoin 20 mg phenytoin equivalents (PE)/kg IV at a maximum rate of 150 mg PE/min as second-line therapy. The nurse begins the infusion on an infusion pump through the existing 20-gauge PIV, with continuous cardiac monitoring and blood pressure checks every 5 minutes during infusion. Fingerstick glucose is rechecked and is 136 mg/dL. At the 6-minute mark of the fosphenytoin infusion, seizure activity has not ceased. Repeat vitals: T 38.6 °C, HR 142, BP 172/98, RR 26, SpO₂ 93% on NRB. The patient remains unresponsive with ongoing tonic-clonic movements. No arrhythmia or hypotension noted during the infusion so far. A second peripheral IV (18-gauge, left forearm) is placed for additional access. Labs from the initial draw begin returning, confirming the subtherapeutic levetiracetam level and the metabolic acidosis.

**Stage 2** — 15 to 35 minutes after ED arrival (approximately 30–50 minutes of total seizure duration). The fosphenytoin infusion is completed. Ten minutes after completion, motor seizure activity persists — this now meets criteria for refractory status epilepticus (failure of both first-line benzodiazepine and adequate second-line antiepileptic drug). The physician is notified. Updated vitals: T 38.9 °C, HR 148, BP 164/96, RR 24 (still irregular), SpO₂ 91% on NRB (dropping despite supplemental O₂). End-tidal CO₂ 58 mmHg. The patient is developing increasing cyanosis around the lips, and secretions are copious despite ongoing suctioning, reflecting ongoing respiratory decline. The emergency physician calls for anesthesia/critical care for rapid sequence intubation and initiation of a continuous IV midazolam infusion for burst-suppression. The nurse prepares RSI medications (propofol, succinylcholine, and backup rocuronium) and equipment (endotracheal tube, laryngoscope/video laryngoscope, bag-valve mask, capnography). Repeat labs drawn at this point: lactate 9.4 mmol/L (rising), CK 1,220 U/L (rising significantly, raising concern for rhabdomyolysis), ABG pH 7.18, PaCO₂ 54 mmHg, PaO₂ 62 mmHg, bicarbonate 14 mEq/L. Potassium 5.1 mEq/L (rising from muscle breakdown). The patient is intubated successfully, placed on mechanical ventilation (FiO₂ 100%, tidal volume 6 mL/kg, PEEP 5 cm H₂O), and a continuous midazolam infusion is started at 0.2 mg/kg/hr per protocol after a 0.2 mg/kg IV bolus. An indwelling urinary catheter is placed; initial output is 40 mL of dark amber urine, which is sent for urinalysis and myoglobin. Continuous EEG monitoring is ordered and the electrodes are being placed. An arterial line is inserted in the right radial artery for continuous blood pressure monitoring and serial ABG sampling. Preparations are made for ICU transfer.

**Stage 3** — 35 to 70 minutes after ED arrival (approximately 50–85 minutes of total seizure duration and into the first ICU hour). After 10 minutes of the midazolam infusion, visible motor seizure activity ceases. However, continuous EEG reveals ongoing electrographic seizure activity (rhythmic spike-and-wave discharges) for an additional 8 minutes before the midazolam infusion rate is titrated upward to 0.3 mg/kg/hr and electrographic seizures finally terminate. The patient is now in a pharmacologically induced state of burst-suppression on EEG, which is the ordered ICU protocol target for this case. Post-intubation vitals begin to stabilize: T 38.4 °C (trending down with cooling measures), HR 112, BP 128/78 (now on vasopressor-free arterial-line monitoring), RR set at 16 on the ventilator, SpO₂ 99% on FiO₂ 60%. Urine output over the first ICU hour is 120 mL, which is below the target of 1–3 mL/kg/hr; the urine is tea-colored and urine myoglobin returns markedly positive. The nurse reports this, and IV isotonic fluids are titrated up toward 300 mL/hr per protocol/order for rhabdomyolysis management. Repeat labs at the one-hour ICU mark: lactate 5.2 mmol/L (falling), CK 2,800 U/L (still rising but expected to peak and then fall), potassium 5.3 mEq/L (stable, being monitored closely), creatinine 1.1 mg/dL (slight rise), ABG pH 7.30, PaCO₂ 38 mmHg (improved with mechanical ventilation), PaO₂ 142 mmHg (on FiO₂ 60%), bicarbonate 18 mEq/L (improving). The nurse maintains continuous EEG monitoring and reports any return of electrographic seizure patterns to the provider, keeps the head of bed elevated at 30 degrees, monitors the midazolam infusion, performs neurological assessments (pupil reactivity, response to stimuli) at least hourly, monitors urine output and color for rhabdomyolysis resolution, and maintains seizure precautions including padded side rails and suction at bedside.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. Situation and question: On ED arrival (prior to Stage 1 data), the patient is in active tonic-clonic seizure after receiving two 4 mg IV doses of lorazepam in the field, the last one approximately three minutes ago, with no seizure cessation. The nurse must recognize that this clinical picture constitutes established status epilepticus and that the initial benzodiazepine has failed, requiring escalation.
Correct action: Recognize the cues of ongoing convulsive activity despite adequate first-line benzodiazepine therapy, identify the situation as established status epilepticus, and anticipate that a second-line antiepileptic drug (such as fosphenytoin, valproate, or levetiracetam IV) will be ordered urgently.
Why it is correct: Status epilepticus is defined as continuous seizure activity lasting more than five minutes or two or more seizures without return to baseline consciousness. Once adequate benzodiazepine doses have failed, the Neurocritical Care Society and AES guidelines direct immediate administration of a second-line agent. Delay increases morbidity, mortality, and the likelihood of pharmacoresistance. Recognizing the failure of first-line therapy is the critical nursing cue that drives the urgency of escalation.
Clinical-judgment skill: Recognize cues.
When it becomes answerable: At initial presentation and the very beginning of Stage 1, when the nurse confirms seizure persistence after documented benzodiazepine administration.

2. Situation and question: At Stage 1, baseline labs return showing a levetiracetam level of 4.2 µg/mL (therapeutic range 12–46), lactate of 6.8 mmol/L, CK of 480 U/L, bicarbonate of 16 mEq/L, and ABG showing a mixed acidosis. The fingerstick glucose is 136–148 mg/dL. The nurse must analyze these cues in the context of status epilepticus to determine what is driving the seizure and what complications are developing.
Correct action: Analyze that the subtherapeutic levetiracetam level strongly supports medication nonadherence as a major contributing factor to the status epilepticus, while evaluation for infectious, toxicologic, metabolic, pregnancy-related, and structural causes continues. The elevated lactate and low bicarbonate reflect lactic acidosis from prolonged skeletal muscle hyperactivity. The mildly elevated CK signals early rhabdomyolysis risk. The normal glucose eliminates hypoglycemia as a cause.
Why it is correct: Medication nonadherence is a common major contributor to status epilepticus in patients with known epilepsy, and the subtherapeutic levetiracetam level strongly supports this. Identifying this helps the team target therapy (reload the patient's home antiepileptic and add a second agent) while continuing evaluation for other precipitants after stabilization. Lactate elevation and metabolic acidosis are expected consequences of sustained seizure-related muscle activity and do not independently require sodium bicarbonate infusion — they resolve when seizures stop. Early CK elevation flags the need for aggressive IV hydration to prevent myoglobin-induced acute kidney injury. Ruling out hypoglycemia is a mandatory early step because hypoglycemic seizures will not respond to antiepileptics.
Clinical-judgment skill: Analyze cues.
When it becomes answerable: Stage 1, when laboratory results return.

3. Situation and question: At Stage 1, fosphenytoin is being infused at 150 mg PE/min. The nurse must determine the priority monitoring actions during this infusion.
Correct action: The nurse must monitor for cardiac arrhythmias (continuous telemetry), hypotension (blood pressure every 5 minutes during infusion), and infusion-site reactions (purple glove syndrome is less common with fosphenytoin than phenytoin, but extravasation must still be monitored). The infusion rate must not exceed 150 mg PE/min. The nurse should also continue to observe for seizure cessation as the therapeutic endpoint of the drug.
Why it is correct: Fosphenytoin can cause QT prolongation, bradycardia, and hypotension through its effect on cardiac sodium channels, particularly at rapid infusion rates. Cardiovascular collapse is a known fatal complication of too-rapid administration. Continuous cardiac monitoring and frequent blood pressure assessment allow the nurse to slow or stop the infusion if hemodynamic compromise occurs. Simultaneously, the nurse must track whether the drug is achieving its goal — seizure cessation — to inform the team promptly if it fails, as this drives the next escalation step.
Clinical-judgment skill: Generate solutions (planning the monitoring strategy for a specific intervention).
When it becomes answerable: Stage 1, when the fosphenytoin infusion begins.

4. Situation and question: At Stage 2, seizure activity persists after completion of fosphenytoin, the patient's SpO₂ is dropping to 91%, end-tidal CO₂ is 58 mmHg, and secretions are worsening. Multiple problems compete for attention: refractory seizures, compromised airway, rising CK, and worsening acidosis. The nurse must prioritize.
Correct action: Prioritize airway and ventilation. Notify the provider that the airway is compromised and that the seizure is refractory. Prepare for rapid sequence intubation as the immediate priority while also preparing for initiation of a continuous infusion anesthetic (midazolam, propofol, or pentobarbital). Aggressive suctioning and jaw thrust or head-tilt-chin-lift to maintain airway patency should be performed while RSI supplies are assembled.
Why it is correct: Even though terminating the seizure is the ultimate goal, the patient cannot survive the seizure if she cannot oxygenate and ventilate. The falling SpO₂, rising CO₂, and copious secretions signal impending respiratory failure. Applying the ABCs framework, airway protection through intubation takes precedence. Furthermore, the continuous infusion agents used for refractory status epilepticus (midazolam, propofol) cause respiratory depression and require a secured airway before they can be safely administered. Therefore, intubation both protects the patient's breathing and enables the definitive seizure treatment.
Clinical-judgment skill: Prioritize hypotheses (determining which of several urgent problems demands first action).
When it becomes answerable: Stage 2, when the airway deterioration data and refractory seizure status are both apparent.

5. Situation and question: At Stage 3, motor seizure activity has stopped after midazolam infusion, but the continuous EEG still shows electrographic seizure activity for an additional 8 minutes. The nurse must decide whether the seizure has truly been controlled.
Correct action: Recognize that cessation of visible motor activity does not equal seizure termination. Report the ongoing electrographic seizure activity to the provider. Do not reduce or stop the midazolam infusion based on motor cessation alone. Anticipate an infusion rate increase until electrographic seizures terminate and burst-suppression is achieved on EEG.
Why it is correct: In status epilepticus, especially after prolonged seizure activity and the administration of neuromuscular blocking agents during RSI (succinylcholine), motor manifestations may cease while cortical seizure activity continues — this is called nonconvulsive status epilepticus. Ongoing electrographic seizure activity causes continued neuronal injury, excitotoxicity, cerebral metabolic demand, and secondary brain injury. Continuous EEG is the gold standard for confirming seizure termination. In this case, the ordered therapeutic target for the continuous midazolam infusion is burst-suppression on EEG, maintained per protocol before a slow taper.
Clinical-judgment skill: Evaluate outcomes (determining whether the intervention achieved its therapeutic goal using objective data).
When it becomes answerable: Stage 3, when continuous EEG data become available after motor cessation.

6. Situation and question: At Stage 3, the patient has tea-colored urine, a urine myoglobin that is markedly positive, and a CK of 2,800 U/L. The nurse must identify and act on this complication.
Correct action: Recognize rhabdomyolysis as a complication of prolonged status epilepticus. Ensure aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (normal saline, targeting urine output of at least 200–300 mL/hr per many protocols, or as ordered) to prevent myoglobin-induced acute tubular necrosis. Monitor urine output hourly via the indwelling catheter. Monitor potassium closely (myoglobin breakdown releases intracellular potassium). Report the findings and ensure serial CK and renal function labs are ordered.
Why it is correct: Sustained tonic-clonic muscle activity causes massive skeletal muscle breakdown, releasing myoglobin into the bloodstream. Myoglobin is freely filtered by the glomerulus and precipitates in renal tubules, especially in acidic and concentrated urine, causing acute kidney injury. The combination of rising CK, dark urine, and positive urine myoglobin confirms rhabdomyolysis. Early aggressive crystalloid hydration is the cornerstone of prevention of renal failure. Hyperkalemia from cellular lysis is a life-threatening complication that requires continuous monitoring.
Clinical-judgment skill: Take action (implementing nursing interventions for a newly identified complication).
When it becomes answerable: Stage 3, when the CK trajectory, urine appearance, and urine myoglobin result are available.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Error attached to Decision Point 1: Assuming the prehospital lorazepam "just needs more time to work" and waiting rather than escalating. This is wrong because benzodiazepines act within 2–3 minutes when given IV; if seizures persist beyond 5 minutes after an adequate dose, the drug has failed and delays in escalation are directly associated with increased mortality and pharmacoresistance.

Error attached to Decision Point 1: Administering a third dose of lorazepam IV in the ED instead of moving to a second-line agent. While a repeat benzodiazepine dose is often protocolized when the first dose fails, in this case two adequate IV doses were already given and failed. Repeatedly administering failed interventions wastes critical time and increases the risk of respiratory depression without effectively terminating the seizure.

Error attached to Decision Point 2: Attributing the seizure to hypoglycemia because "seizure patients always need glucose." The fingerstick glucose is normal. Administering dextrose to a normoglycemic patient is not indicated and diverts attention from the true precipitant (nonadherence). However, checking the glucose was appropriate and necessary.

Error attached to Decision Point 2: Interpreting the lactic acidosis as a primary metabolic problem requiring immediate bicarbonate infusion. The acidosis is a secondary consequence of prolonged seizure-driven muscle activity. It will correct when the seizures stop. Sodium bicarbonate is not routinely indicated for lactic acidosis from seizures and can worsen intracellular acidosis and cause sodium overload.

Error attached to Decision Point 3: Infusing fosphenytoin faster than 150 mg PE/min to "get the drug in quicker" because the patient is seizing. Exceeding the maximum infusion rate risks severe hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias (including heart block), and cardiovascular collapse. Speed of infusion does not outweigh safety.

Error attached to Decision Point 4: Prioritizing the rising CK or the metabolic acidosis over airway management. While rhabdomyolysis and acidosis are important, neither will matter if the patient suffers a hypoxic cardiac arrest from an unsecured airway. ABC prioritization must govern the sequence.

Error attached to Decision Point 4: Inserting an oral airway or placing anything in the mouth while the patient is actively seizing with jaw clenching. This risks dental fracture, aspiration of broken fragments, and injury to the nurse's hands. A nasopharyngeal airway is safer if any adjunct is needed prior to RSI; definitive airway management is endotracheal intubation.

Error attached to Decision Point 5: Stopping or reducing the midazolam infusion as soon as visible motor seizure activity ceases, without confirming electrographic seizure termination on EEG. This results in ongoing nonconvulsive status epilepticus and continued neuronal injury, which may go undetected without EEG. Neuromuscular blocking agents given during intubation can mask motor seizure activity, making this error especially dangerous.

Error attached to Decision Point 6: Failing to monitor urine output and color after seizure termination, assuming that once the seizure stops, the crisis is over. Rhabdomyolysis is a delayed complication that evolves over hours to days after the initial injury. Acute kidney injury from myoglobin deposition can be prevented with aggressive hydration but only if the complication is recognized and acted upon in time.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

1. Recognize the clinical criteria for status epilepticus and identify when first-line benzodiazepine therapy has failed, necessitating escalation to second-line agents.

2. Analyze laboratory and clinical data (subtherapeutic drug levels, metabolic acidosis, CK elevation, blood glucose) to identify the precipitating cause of status epilepticus and distinguish primary problems from secondary consequences of prolonged seizure activity.

3. Prioritize airway management over other competing problems (refractory seizures, metabolic derangements, rhabdomyolysis) when respiratory compromise is present, applying the ABCs framework in a rapidly deteriorating patient.

4. Differentiate motor seizure cessation from true electrographic seizure termination, and articulate the role of continuous EEG monitoring in confirming treatment effectiveness in refractory status epilepticus.

5. Identify rhabdomyolysis as a complication of prolonged status epilepticus, implement appropriate nursing interventions (aggressive IV hydration, urine output monitoring, serial CK and potassium surveillance), and evaluate the patient's response to treatment across multiple organ systems.
