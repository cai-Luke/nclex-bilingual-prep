# Home safety risks and anticipatory guidance at a nine-month-old well-child visit

## PATIENT BACKGROUND
Liam is a nine-month-old male presenting to the pediatric clinic for his scheduled well-child visit. He was born at 39 weeks via uncomplicated vaginal delivery, birth weight 3.4 kg. His growth has been appropriate, and he is up to date on immunizations through six months. He has no chronic medical conditions and no prior hospitalizations. He lives with his mother, Danielle (age 22, first-time parent), and her partner in a second-floor apartment. Danielle works part-time evenings; her partner provides childcare during those shifts. The family has no regular pediatrician — they have used walk-in clinics for prior visits — and this is their first visit at this practice. Danielle states she is here because "the WIC office said he needed his nine-month checkup." She appears engaged and affectionate with Liam but mentions she has not had much guidance on "baby stuff" since leaving the hospital.

## INITIAL PRESENTATION
Danielle is seated in the exam room holding Liam on her lap. He is alert, active, and exploring a set of car keys she has given him to chew on. A diaper bag on the chair contains a propped bottle of whole cow's milk with cereal mixed in, an open ziplock bag of popcorn, and a small unlocked cosmetics pouch visible at the top. Danielle reports Liam is "doing great — he's pulling up on everything now and trying to cruise along the couch." She says he sleeps in her bed because "he won't stay in the crib" and that she recently started him on whole milk "because it's cheaper than formula." She asks whether she can give him "a little Benadryl at night to help him sleep through" because her partner suggested it. She has brought a rear-facing infant car seat, which she mentions she recently turned forward-facing "since he's getting so big and he screams when he can't see me."

## ASSESSMENT FINDINGS
Weight: 8.9 kg (50th percentile). Length: 72 cm (55th percentile). Head circumference: 45 cm (50th percentile). Temperature: 37.1 °C tympanic. Heart rate: 128 beats per minute. Respiratory rate: 30 breaths per minute. SpO₂: 99% on room air. Skin is warm, dry, intact, with no bruising, burns, or unusual marks. Anterior fontanelle is open and flat. Tympanic membranes are pearly gray bilaterally with visible landmarks. Oral exam reveals two lower central incisors erupted; no oral lesions. Lungs are clear bilaterally. Heart has a regular rate and rhythm with no murmur. Abdomen is soft, non-tender, with no organomegaly. Musculoskeletal exam shows symmetric movement of all extremities with good tone. Neurological: Liam tracks objects across midline, transfers objects hand to hand, sits independently without support, pulls to stand at the exam table, babbles with consonant sounds ("ba-ba," "da-da" nonspecifically), and demonstrates a raking grasp progressing toward a pincer grasp. He exhibits stranger anxiety when the nurse reaches for him, turning toward Danielle and fussing. He does not yet wave bye-bye. Developmental screening (ASQ-3 completed by Danielle in the waiting room) is within normal limits for all domains.

## LABORATORY DATA
Hemoglobin: 10.8 g/dL (reference for age: 10.5–14.0 g/dL). Hematocrit: 33%. Lead level: 2 µg/dL, below the CDC blood lead reference value of 3.5 µg/dL; routine anticipatory guidance only. No other labs ordered at this visit.

## CLINICAL COURSE

### Stage 1 — During the initial history and observation (first 15 minutes of the visit).
As the nurse conducts the history, several safety concerns emerge simultaneously from Danielle's statements and from direct observation of the diaper bag contents. Danielle is cooperative and forthcoming. She volunteers that the apartment has an open staircase to the first floor and that they have not installed a baby gate "yet" because Liam "hasn't figured out the stairs." She mentions she keeps cleaning supplies under the kitchen sink with no cabinet lock because "he can't open doors yet." When asked about Liam's diet, she explains she switched from formula to whole cow's milk about three weeks ago because a neighbor told her it was fine after six months, and she mixes infant cereal into the bottle to "fill him up" so he sleeps longer. She also reports giving him small pieces of hot dog and grapes as finger foods. She reiterates her question about using diphenhydramine at bedtime. The nurse observes Liam mouth the car keys, which have a small decorative charm approximately 1.5 cm in diameter attached to the keyring. Danielle's tone throughout is warm and attentive; she repositions Liam when he squirms, responds to his vocalizations, and makes eye contact with the nurse. She asks several questions and appears genuinely interested in doing the right thing.

### Stage 2 — Midway through the visit (approximately 20 minutes in), after the nurse has begun teaching.
The nurse has started addressing the safety concerns. During the conversation, Danielle becomes tearful and says, "I feel like I'm doing everything wrong — nobody ever taught me any of this." She discloses that she aged out of foster care at 18, has no family support, and learned most of her parenting from online videos and her partner's suggestions. She states her partner "gets frustrated when the baby cries at night" and that the diphenhydramine idea was his solution. When the nurse gently explores this further, Danielle clarifies that her partner has never harmed Liam but "just gets annoyed and leaves the room," and that she is the one who always responds to nighttime waking. She adds that her partner sometimes watches Liam in the living room while she works and "lets him crawl around on the floor with whatever's out." She is not sure what is on the floor during those times. The nurse notices that Liam has now pulled the cosmetics pouch partially out of the diaper bag and is attempting to unzip it. Inside are a tube of lipstick, a compact mirror, and a small bottle of acetaminophen liquid (infant concentration, 160 mg/5 mL) with no child-resistant cap — Danielle explains she removed the cap "because it was hard to get off fast when he has a fever." The nurse asks Danielle what dose of acetaminophen she gives Liam. Danielle says, "I just give him about a tablespoon — that's what my neighbor said."

### Stage 3 — End of the visit (approximately 40 minutes in), during teach-back and care planning.
The nurse has provided structured teaching on the priority safety issues. Danielle has stopped crying and is engaged. The nurse asks Danielle to repeat back the key points. Danielle correctly states that Liam should stay rear-facing now and continue rear-facing until he reaches the rear-facing height or weight limit for that specific seat (noting many seats allow rear-facing beyond age 2), that he should sleep alone on his back in a crib with no loose bedding, and that she should not give diphenhydramine to help him sleep. When asked about the acetaminophen dose, Danielle says, "I use the syringe that came with it and give him... I think you said 5 mL?" — which is correct for his weight per the manufacturer dosing on the package (the package insert for this product lists 5 mL for infants 18–23 lbs, and Liam weighs 8.9 kg, approximately 19.6 lbs). However, when the nurse asks about food safety, Danielle says, "I'll cut the hot dogs into small pieces — like little circles — so he won't choke." She does not mention grapes. When asked what she will do if Liam gets into something he shouldn't, Danielle says, "I'll call 911 right away," but does not mention Poison Control. The nurse also asks about the staircase and cabinet locks; Danielle says she will "try to get gates and locks this weekend." The nurse reviews community resources and provides written materials, a Poison Control magnet (1-800-222-1222), and a follow-up appointment in one month. The nurse documents the visit, the teaching provided, the areas where Danielle demonstrated understanding, and the areas requiring reinforcement.

## KEY DECISION POINTS

1. **Situation:** At Stage 1, during the initial history and observation, the nurse must identify which of the many findings — the car keys with a small charm being mouthed, the propped bottle of whole cow's milk with cereal, the popcorn in the diaper bag, the forward-facing car seat, the bed-sharing, the open staircase without a gate, the unlocked cleaning supplies, the hot dogs and grapes as finger foods, the question about diphenhydramine for sleep, and the cosmetics pouch accessible in the bag — represent safety-relevant cues requiring nursing attention, and which observed behaviors (Danielle's warmth, responsiveness, eye contact, and engagement) are normal and reassuring findings.
   - **Correct action:** The car keys with the small charm (aspiration/choking hazard for a mouthing infant), the whole cow's milk before twelve months (nutritional risk and potential iron-deficiency contribution), the popcorn (choking hazard), the forward-facing car seat at nine months (should be rear-facing), the bed-sharing (safe-sleep risk), the unprotected staircase (fall risk in a pulling-to-stand and cruising infant), the unlocked cleaning supplies (poisoning risk), the hot dogs and whole grapes (choking hazards due to shape and texture), the diphenhydramine question (sedating an infant for caregiver convenience is unsafe and not indicated), and the accessible cosmetics pouch with medications (poisoning risk) are all safety-relevant cues. Danielle's affectionate interaction, responsiveness to Liam's cues, and willingness to ask questions are normal, positive caregiver behaviors and are not concerning findings. The stranger anxiety Liam displays is a normal developmental behavior for a nine-month-old and is not a cue of concern.
   - **Why it is correct:** At nine months, Liam is mobile, mouthing objects, pulling to stand, and beginning to explore — all of which amplify the danger of each identified hazard. Recognizing the full cluster of risks, rather than focusing on only one, is essential because multiple simultaneous hazards compound the overall danger. Equally important is recognizing that Danielle's engaged, affectionate parenting style and Liam's age-appropriate stranger anxiety are not concerning — misreading warmth as adequacy or stranger anxiety as a red flag would distort the clinical picture.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Recognize cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

2. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse must analyze the pattern of unsafe practices — switching to whole cow's milk before 12 months (specifically at 8 months/three weeks ago) on a neighbor's advice, using diphenhydramine on a partner's suggestion, turning the car seat forward-facing because the baby cries, mixing cereal in the bottle, offering choking-hazard foods — and determine whether this pattern is most consistent with a caregiver knowledge deficit, intentional neglect, or willful disregard for the child's safety.
   - **Correct action:** The pattern is consistent with a caregiver knowledge deficit rather than intentional neglect. Danielle is relying on informal, inaccurate sources (neighbor, partner, online videos) in the absence of consistent professional guidance. Her behavior toward Liam is warm, responsive, and attentive. She voluntarily discloses her practices and asks questions, which indicates openness rather than concealment. She has had no consistent pediatric medical home and states she has received little parenting education. While safety concerns must be documented and addressed, the current evidence supports a knowledge deficit rather than intentional neglect; the nurse should offer resources and education, and reassess safety, reserving escalation if the caregiver cannot or will not implement safety measures.
   - **Why it is correct:** Differentiating knowledge deficit from neglect determines the nursing approach. A knowledge deficit calls for nonjudgmental education, resource connection, and follow-up — not an immediate report to child protective services. The hallmarks here — voluntary disclosure, engagement, affection, absence of injury, and reliance on lay advice in the absence of professional teaching — point clearly to a gap in education, not intent to harm. However, safety must be established, and if the caregiver is unable or unwilling to implement necessary safety measures, escalation may become necessary.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Analyze cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

3. **Situation:** At Stage 2, after the nurse has identified the full scope of safety concerns, the nurse must determine which risk requires the most immediate intervention right now in the clinic, before any teaching begins. At this moment, Liam is actively pulling the cosmetics pouch out of the diaper bag, and inside it is an open bottle of infant acetaminophen (160 mg/5 mL) without a child-resistant cap. Danielle has also reported giving "about a tablespoon" of acetaminophen, which is 15 mL — three times the correct dose for his weight.
   - **Correct action:** The most immediate risk is the accessible, uncapped acetaminophen within Liam's reach right now. The nurse should calmly remove the pouch from Liam's reach immediately, then address the dosing error. The acetaminophen overdose risk is twofold: acute ingestion in the clinic if he opens the bottle, and chronic overdosing at home given Danielle's reported dose of 15 mL (approximately 480 mg) versus the correct dose of 5 mL (160 mg). All other hazards — the car seat, the bed-sharing, the staircase, the diet — are important but are not presenting an immediate danger to Liam at this moment in the exam room.
   - **Why it is correct:** Prioritization follows immediacy of harm. A nine-month-old with a developing pincer grasp who is actively manipulating an unzipped pouch containing an uncapped liquid medication could ingest a toxic amount within seconds. The reported home dosing of three times the recommended amount also represents a serious ongoing poisoning risk that must be corrected before the family leaves. The other hazards are real but are not producing immediate danger in the exam room at this moment.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Prioritize hypotheses.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

4. **Situation:** At Stage 2, after securing the acetaminophen and identifying the full set of risks, the nurse must determine the appropriate plan for caregiver education — specifically, how to structure the teaching given Danielle's emotional state (tearful, feeling she is "doing everything wrong"), her lack of family support, and the volume of safety topics to cover.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should first validate Danielle's feelings and acknowledge her strengths — she brought Liam for his well-child visit, she is asking questions, and she is clearly bonded with and attentive to her child. The nurse should use nonjudgmental, strengths-based language and avoid overwhelming Danielle with all corrections simultaneously. The teaching should be prioritized by severity of risk: correct the acetaminophen dosing error first (demonstrate the syringe, have Danielle practice, and provide written weight-band/package dosing), then address the choking hazards (remove the popcorn and charm, teach safe food preparation), then address the dietary safety concerns (stopping whole cow's milk and resuming formula, avoiding bottle propping and cereal in the bottle), then the car seat positioning, then safe sleep, then home safety (gates, cabinet locks, Poison Control number). The nurse should connect Danielle with community resources — parenting classes, a consistent medical home, home-visiting programs such as Nurse-Family Partnership if available — and schedule a follow-up visit. The nurse should not attempt to deliver all teaching in a single overwhelming session but should prioritize the highest-risk items, confirm understanding through teach-back, and plan for reinforcement at the follow-up visit.
   - **Why it is correct:** Effective health teaching requires meeting the learner where she is emotionally and cognitively. A caregiver who feels judged or overwhelmed will disengage. Strengths-based, prioritized teaching respects Danielle's capacity, builds trust, and increases the likelihood of behavior change. Prioritizing by severity ensures the most dangerous practices are corrected first even if time runs short. Connecting an isolated, under-resourced caregiver with ongoing support addresses the root cause of the knowledge deficit.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Generate solutions.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

5. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse must act on the acetaminophen dosing concern. Danielle has reported giving "about a tablespoon" — 15 mL, which delivers approximately 480 mg of acetaminophen per dose. Per the product/facility weight-band dosing table for infants 18–23 lb, Liam's instructed dose is 5 mL of 160 mg/5 mL acetaminophen. Danielle's tablespoon dose is 15 mL, or 480 mg, which is three times the instructed 160 mg dose. The nurse must determine the correct nursing action.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should ask Danielle how frequently she has been giving this dose and when the last dose was given, to assess for possible acute or cumulative toxicity. If Danielle reports recent or repeated dosing at this amount, the nurse should notify the provider and Poison Control immediately so they can determine whether a serum acetaminophen level and hepatic function labs are indicated. If the dosing has been infrequent and the last dose was not recent, the nurse should still notify the provider, document the reported dosing history, and correct the dosing practice before discharge. In either case, the nurse should demonstrate the correct dose using the syringe packaged with the medication, have Danielle return-demonstrate, discard the tablespoon as a measuring tool, and provide written weight-band dosing instructions. The nurse should also instruct Danielle to replace the child-resistant cap and store the medication out of reach and out of sight.
   - **Why it is correct:** Acetaminophen is the most common cause of pediatric poisoning from over-the-counter medications. A dose of 480 mg (three times the recommended 160 mg dose for his weight band) in an 8.9 kg infant significantly exceeds the recommended range and, if repeated, poses a risk of hepatotoxicity. The nurse's scope includes assessing the exposure history, notifying the provider of a potential toxicity concern, teaching correct dosing, and ensuring safe medication storage. The action must be taken now — before the family leaves — because the risk is ongoing at home.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Take action.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

6. **Situation:** At Stage 3, during teach-back, the nurse must evaluate whether Danielle's responses demonstrate adequate understanding of the safety teaching or whether specific areas require reinforcement before discharge. Danielle correctly states the car seat should remain rear-facing, that Liam should sleep alone on his back in a crib, that diphenhydramine should not be used for sleep, and that the acetaminophen dose is 5 mL by syringe. However, she says she will cut hot dogs "into small circles" and does not mention grapes. She says she will call 911 for a poisoning but does not mention Poison Control. She says she will "try to get" gates and cabinet locks "this weekend."
   - **Correct action:** The car seat, safe sleep, diphenhydramine, and acetaminophen dosing responses demonstrate adequate understanding — these do not require re-teaching. Two areas require immediate reinforcement before discharge: (1) Danielle's plan to cut hot dogs into circles is incorrect and still dangerous — hot dogs must be cut lengthwise first and then into small pieces to eliminate the cylindrical shape that conforms to and occludes a child's airway, and grapes must be quartered lengthwise for the same reason; the nurse must re-teach this and obtain a correct return demonstration of the concept. (2) Danielle should be taught to call Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) as the first contact for a suspected ingestion rather than 911, unless the child is unresponsive or not breathing — the Poison Control magnet should be reviewed and its use reinforced. The statement about "trying" to get gates and locks is a commitment-strength concern but not a knowledge gap — the nurse should problem-solve barriers (cost, transportation) and connect Danielle with resources that provide free safety equipment, such as Safe Kids coalitions or the clinic's own supply, rather than simply re-teaching the need.
   - **Why it is correct:** Teach-back is the evaluation step of the nursing process applied to health teaching. Correct responses confirm learning; incorrect or incomplete responses identify specific gaps that must be addressed before the teaching encounter ends. The hot dog error is particularly important because the cylindrical shape of a round-cut hot dog is the exact geometry that causes fatal airway obstruction in young children — a caregiver who believes she is cutting food safely but is actually preserving the dangerous shape has a false sense of security. The Poison Control gap matters because calling 911 for a non-emergent ingestion delays expert toxicology guidance and may result in unnecessary emergency transport, while failing to call Poison Control for a potentially serious ingestion delays critical early management advice.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Evaluate outcomes.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

## COMMON NURSING ERRORS

- Focusing on only one or two visible hazards — such as the car seat and the bed-sharing — while overlooking the less obvious risks like the small charm on the keys, the popcorn, the acetaminophen in the cosmetics pouch, or the hot dogs and grapes. A student may also incorrectly flag Liam's stranger anxiety as a developmental concern or a sign of inadequate socialization, when it is in fact a normal and expected behavior at nine months.
- Interpreting the pattern of unsafe practices as neglect and initiating or considering a child protective services referral. This is wrong because the clinical picture — voluntary disclosure, warmth, engagement, absence of injury, and reliance on informal advice in the absence of professional guidance — is consistent with a knowledge deficit in an under-resourced first-time parent, not with neglect. Mislabeling a knowledge gap as neglect damages the therapeutic relationship, may deter the caregiver from seeking future care, and is not supported by the evidence in this case. Conversely, a student might dismiss the seriousness of the safety risks because Danielle "seems like a good mom" — warmth and intent do not eliminate danger, and the hazards must still be addressed.
- Prioritizing the car seat orientation or the bed-sharing as the most immediate risk, because those are well-known safety topics, while failing to recognize that the uncapped acetaminophen actively being reached by the infant in the exam room is the most immediate danger at that moment. A student might also prioritize the dietary issues (whole cow's milk, cereal in the bottle) over the acute poisoning risk — nutritional concerns are important but are not immediately life-threatening in the way an accessible uncapped medication is.
- Delivering all safety corrections in a rapid, directive lecture without acknowledging Danielle's emotional state or strengths — this approach is likely to overwhelm and alienate a caregiver who is already tearful and feeling inadequate, reducing the effectiveness of the teaching. Alternatively, a student might avoid addressing the safety concerns directly because Danielle is upset, prioritizing emotional comfort over patient safety — the nurse must do both, not choose one.
- Correcting the acetaminophen dose verbally but failing to ask about the frequency and recency of dosing — this omits the assessment for possible toxicity that has already occurred. A student might also simply tell Danielle the correct dose without demonstrating the syringe and obtaining a return demonstration, which does not confirm the caregiver can actually measure the dose correctly. Another error is assuming the dosing has not caused harm because Liam appears well at the visit — acetaminophen hepatotoxicity can be clinically silent in early stages, and the absence of symptoms does not rule out cumulative injury if the overdosing has been repeated.
- Accepting Danielle's teach-back responses as uniformly adequate without identifying the specific gaps — the hot dog cutting method and the Poison Control omission. A student might hear "cut into small pieces" and assume the technique is correct without probing the shape, or might accept "call 911" as a sufficient poisoning response without teaching the role of Poison Control. Another error is treating Danielle's "I'll try to get gates this weekend" as a knowledge failure requiring re-teaching, when it is actually a resource-access barrier requiring problem-solving and referral — re-teaching what she already knows wastes time and may feel condescending.

## EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

- Identify multiple simultaneous home-safety hazards for a mobile infant by recognizing cues from caregiver history, direct observation, and developmental stage.
- Differentiate a caregiver knowledge deficit from neglect by analyzing the pattern of behavior, the caregiver's affect and engagement, and the context of the family's resources and support.
- Prioritize the most immediately dangerous safety risk when multiple hazards are present, using immediacy and severity of potential harm as the organizing principle.
- Plan and deliver nonjudgmental, strengths-based, prioritized safety teaching to an emotionally vulnerable caregiver, including correct acetaminophen dosing, choking-hazard food preparation, safe sleep, car seat positioning, and poison prevention.
- Evaluate caregiver understanding through teach-back, identifying specific gaps that require reinforcement versus barriers that require resource connection, and distinguishing adequate learning from incomplete or incorrect responses.

## BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the full scope of safety risks has been identified, the acetaminophen has been secured, and the nurse is determining the priority actions before the family leaves.

- **Most likely condition:** Caregiver knowledge deficit regarding infant safety, with multiple concurrent home hazards and an active medication-dosing error posing risk of acetaminophen toxicity. The pattern of unsafe practices — each traceable to informal, inaccurate advice in the absence of professional guidance — combined with Danielle's warmth, voluntary disclosure, and engagement confirms a teaching need, rather than an immediate child protective services report.
- **Plausible competing conditions:**
  1. *Child neglect as the primary explanation* — less likely here based on current evidence alone: Danielle's behavior demonstrates consistent attentiveness, affection, and willingness to learn; she brought Liam for his well-child visit voluntarily, discloses her practices openly, and asks questions; there are no signs of physical abuse or injury, and the unsafe practices stem from a lack of education. The nurse should continue assessment, document objectively, and escalate to protective services if safety measures cannot be established or if the caregiver refuses to implement essential changes.
  2. *Intimate partner violence (IPV) or coercive control driving the unsafe practices* — less likely here: there is insufficient evidence to conclude IPV or coercive control from this vignette alone. While the partner's frustration and suggestion of diphenhydramine are concerning, Danielle describes him leaving the room rather than displaying aggression, and she denies fear. The nurse should continue private screening and offer standard resources as clinically appropriate.
- **Two priority actions:**
  1. Assess the acetaminophen dosing history — frequency, recency, and total exposure — and notify the provider and contact Poison Control as directed if recent or repeated overdosing is reported, so they can determine if a serum acetaminophen level and hepatic function labs are indicated.
  2. Provide correct weight-band/package acetaminophen dosing instruction with syringe demonstration and return demonstration before the family leaves, and remove the immediate choking hazards (charm, popcorn) from Liam's reach.
- **Two wrong actions:**
  1. Initiating a child protective services referral based on the unsafe practices alone — wrong because the clinical evidence supports a knowledge deficit in an engaged, affectionate caregiver, not neglect; a CPS referral here would damage the therapeutic relationship and is not warranted by the findings.
  2. Deferring the acetaminophen dosing correction to the follow-up visit because Danielle is tearful and the nurse does not want to overwhelm her — wrong because the dosing error poses an ongoing risk of hepatotoxicity at home; this correction cannot wait, and the nurse must address it today while supporting Danielle emotionally.
- **Two parameters to monitor:**
  1. Danielle's teach-back accuracy at the follow-up visit — specifically whether she can correctly state and demonstrate the acetaminophen dose, describe safe food preparation (lengthwise cutting, avoidance of round/hard foods), and articulate the safe-sleep and car-seat practices, confirming that the teaching has been retained and applied.
  2. Liam's growth trajectory and hemoglobin at the follow-up visit — the premature switch to whole cow's milk and the cereal-in-bottle practice may contribute to iron-deficiency anemia and altered growth; trending his weight, length, and hemoglobin against today's baselines will confirm whether the dietary correction has been implemented.
- **Two irrelevant parameters:**
  1. Lead level — Liam's lead level is 2 µg/dL, below the CDC blood lead reference value of 3.5 µg/dL; while lead screening is part of routine well-child care, it is not the parameter that evaluates whether the identified safety risks are being resolved, and tracking it at this synthesis moment is not the priority.
  2. ASQ-3 developmental screening score — Liam's developmental screening is within normal limits today; while developmental monitoring continues at future visits, the ASQ-3 does not measure whether the home-safety hazards have been corrected or whether the acetaminophen dosing error has been resolved, so it is not the right evaluation tool for this clinical situation.
