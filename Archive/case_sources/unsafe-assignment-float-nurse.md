**CASE TITLE**
Unsafe assignment outside validated competency for a floated medical-surgical nurse.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Nurse Dara Okonkwo, RN, BSN, has worked on a 36-bed medical-surgical unit for four years. Her competency file documents validation in telemetry monitoring, PCA pump management, chest-tube care, and routine post-operative assessment. She has never been validated for vasoactive medication titration, hemodynamic monitoring via arterial lines, or neurological assessment protocols specific to the step-down (intermediate care) unit. Her facility's float policy states: "Floated nurses shall receive assignments consistent with their documented competencies. When patient acuity exceeds the float nurse's validated skills, the charge nurse must adjust the assignment or arrange for competency-matched support." The facility also has a chain-of-command policy: "When a patient safety concern is not resolved at the unit level, the nurse shall escalate to the nursing supervisor, then to the house administrator, documenting each step objectively."

Tonight is a twelve-hour night shift during a hospital-wide staffing surge caused by a respiratory illness outbreak. Census on the step-down unit is at capacity, and two step-down nurses have called in sick. The house supervisor floats Nurse Okonkwo to the step-down unit at 1845.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
Nurse Okonkwo arrives on the step-down unit at 1900 and receives a verbal assignment from the charge nurse, Marcus Reilly, RN. This step-down unit is credentialed to manage selected low-dose vasoactive infusions and arterial lines when assigned to nurses validated in those competencies. He assigns her three patients:

Client A — a 58-year-old man, postoperative day one after coronary artery bypass graft, transferred from the cardiac ICU to step-down two hours ago on a low-dose norepinephrine drip at 4 mcg/min titrated to maintain MAP above 64 mmHg, with an arterial line in the left radial artery for continuous blood pressure monitoring. He also has mediastinal chest tubes to a closed drainage system.

Client B — a 62-year-old woman, eight hours post-carotid endarterectomy, requiring neurological assessments every one hour per the unit's post-carotid-endarterectomy protocol (pupil reactivity, facial symmetry, speech clarity, grip strength, swallow function, and blood pressure parameters to maintain systolic between 100 and 150 mmHg). She is on a nicardipine drip at 5 mg/hr, titrated per protocol to keep systolic blood pressure within that range.

Client C — a 71-year-old woman, post-operative day two after right total knee arthroplasty, transferred to the step-down unit because no medical-surgical beds were available. She is hemodynamically stable, on oral pain medication, receiving routine incision care, and ambulating with physical therapy. Her care needs are consistent with a medical-surgical level of acuity.

The charge nurse tells Nurse Okonkwo: "I know it's a lot, but we're short. You'll be fine — the drips are already running, and the settings are in the chart. Just keep an eye on things."

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
At 1915, Nurse Okonkwo reviews the assignment sheet and begins her initial rounds.

Client A: T 36.8 °C, HR 92 (sinus rhythm on monitor), arterial line BP 108/62 (MAP 77), RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on 3 L nasal cannula. Norepinephrine infusing at 4 mcg/min via a right internal jugular central line. Mediastinal chest tube drainage is 30 mL of serosanguineous fluid over the past hour. Sternal dressing is dry and intact. Pedal pulses palpable bilaterally. He is drowsy but arousable and oriented to person, place, and time.

Client B: T 37.1 °C, HR 78 (sinus rhythm), BP 142/78 (cuff pressure; no arterial line), RR 16, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Nicardipine infusing at 5 mg/hr via a left antecubital peripheral IV. Pupils equal, round, reactive to light. Face symmetric. Speech clear. Grip strength equal bilaterally. Swallow intact. Neck incision dressing with a small amount of serous drainage. She is alert and conversational.

Client C: T 37.0 °C, HR 74 (sinus rhythm), BP 126/72, RR 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Right knee dressing clean, dry, and intact with mild swelling. Pedal pulse palpable on the right. She rates her pain at 3 out of 10 after her last dose of oral acetaminophen-hydrocodone. She is alert, oriented, and in no distress. Sequential compression devices are in place bilaterally while in bed.

Nurse Okonkwo notes that the norepinephrine and nicardipine drips both have titration parameters written as provider orders, and the unit's titration protocol binder is at the nurses' station. She has never independently titrated a vasoactive infusion. She has performed basic neurological checks on medical-surgical patients but has not been trained on the step-down unit's structured post-carotid-endarterectomy neuro protocol, which includes specific grading criteria and escalation triggers she is unfamiliar with.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Client A (drawn at 1800, one hour before Nurse Okonkwo's arrival): sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, glucose 148 mg/dL, hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL, hematocrit 32%, platelets 162,000/µL, INR 1.3, lactate 1.4 mmol/L, troponin I 0.42 ng/mL (expected post-CABG elevation).

Client B (drawn at 1700): sodium 140 mEq/L, potassium 3.9 mEq/L, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.8 mg/dL, glucose 112 mg/dL, hemoglobin 12.4 g/dL, hematocrit 37%, platelets 198,000/µL, INR 1.1.

Client C (drawn this morning): sodium 141 mEq/L, potassium 4.0 mEq/L, BUN 16 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, hemoglobin 11.6 g/dL, hematocrit 35%, platelets 210,000/µL.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1 — 1915 to 1945: The assignment is received and the nurse recognizes the problem.**

After completing her initial rounds, Nurse Okonkwo returns to the nurses' station. She has confirmed that all three patients are currently stable. She reviews the titration protocols in the binder and realizes she does not know how to zero and level an arterial line transducer, how to troubleshoot arterial line waveform dampening, or how to titrate norepinephrine or nicardipine according to the unit's protocols. She also reviews the post-carotid-endarterectomy neuro assessment form and finds that it includes a grading scale and specific escalation criteria — for example, any new facial droop, speech change, or unilateral grip weakness triggers an immediate provider notification and a STAT CT — that differ from the general neuro checks she performs on her home unit.

She approaches the charge nurse, Marcus, and says: "I've looked at the assignment. I'm comfortable with Client C — her care is within my documented competencies. But I've never been validated for vasoactive drip titration or arterial line management, and I haven't been trained on your post-carotid neuro protocol. I'm concerned about patient safety if I'm independently managing Clients A and B."

Marcus responds: "Everyone's stretched tonight. I can't rearrange the whole assignment. The drips are already set — just don't change them. If something goes wrong, call me and I'll come help."

Nurse Okonkwo recognizes that "just don't change them" does not resolve the safety concern: if Client A's MAP drops below 64, the norepinephrine will need to be titrated upward, and she is not competent to do so; if Client B's systolic rises above 150 or drops below 100, the nicardipine will need adjustment, and she is not competent to do that either. She also recognizes that the post-carotid neuro protocol requires her to identify and grade findings she has not been trained to assess reliably, and that a missed or misgraded finding could delay recognition of a post-operative stroke.

**Stage 2 — 1945 to 2100: The situation is not resolved at the unit level and escalation begins.**

At 2000, Nurse Okonkwo rechecks Client A. His arterial line BP reads 88/53 (MAP 64). The norepinephrine is still at 4 mcg/min. He is slightly more drowsy than at 1915 but still arousable and oriented. Chest tube output has increased to 50 mL over the past hour, and the drainage is now darker red. Heart rate is 102. SpO₂ remains 96%.

She notifies Marcus immediately: "Client A's MAP has dropped to 64, right at the threshold. His chest tube output has increased and the drainage is darker. His heart rate is up to 102. I need someone validated to assess whether the norepinephrine needs to be titrated and to evaluate the chest tube output trend."

Marcus comes to the bedside, assesses the patient, and increases the norepinephrine to 6 mcg/min per the titration protocol. He tells Nurse Okonkwo: "See, I just bumped it up. You could do that." He returns to his own three-patient assignment.

At 2030, Nurse Okonkwo performs the hourly neuro check on Client B using the protocol form as best she can. Client B's blood pressure is now 156/82. The nicardipine is still at 5 mg/hr. Nurse Okonkwo knows the systolic is above the 150 ceiling but is not confident in how to titrate the nicardipine — the protocol specifies increments of 2.5 mg/hr every five to fifteen minutes, with a maximum rate of 15 mg/hr, and she has never performed this titration. She also notes that Client B's speech seems slightly less crisp than at 1915, but she is uncertain whether this represents a true change or her own unfamiliarity with the patient's baseline and the grading criteria.

She pages Marcus again. He does not respond for twelve minutes. When he arrives, he increases the nicardipine to 7.5 mg/hr and performs his own neuro assessment, documenting speech as "clear, unchanged." He tells Nurse Okonkwo: "She's fine. You're overthinking this."

Nurse Okonkwo recognizes that the pattern is unsustainable: she has now needed Marcus to come to the bedside twice in ninety minutes for interventions she cannot independently perform, and Marcus has his own full assignment. She also recognizes that if she had not flagged the speech concern, and if it had been a true change, the twelve-minute delay in Marcus responding could have been clinically significant for a post-carotid patient.

At 2045, Nurse Okonkwo calls the house nursing supervisor, Patricia Huang, RN. She states: "I was floated from medical-surgical to the step-down unit tonight. I have been assigned two patients on vasoactive drips requiring titration and one post-carotid patient on an hourly neuro protocol. I am not validated for vasoactive titration, arterial line management, or the step-down neuro protocol. I have communicated this to the charge nurse twice. He has been coming to the bedside to perform the titrations, but he has his own full assignment and there was a twelve-minute delay in his response to my last call. I am concerned that this arrangement does not ensure timely, competent care for these patients. I am requesting reassignment or a competency-matched buddy nurse for Clients A and B. I am able to continue caring for Client C independently."

Supervisor Huang says she will come to the unit within fifteen minutes to review the situation.

**Stage 3 — 2100 to 2300: Resolution and documentation.**

At 2115, Supervisor Huang arrives on the unit. She reviews the assignment board, confirms Nurse Okonkwo's competency file does not include vasoactive titration or step-down neuro protocols, and speaks with Marcus. She restructures the assignments: a step-down-validated nurse, Keisha Williams, RN, takes over primary responsibility for Clients A and B. Nurse Okonkwo retains Client C and picks up a fourth patient — a stable post-operative cholecystectomy patient who was being held on the step-down unit due to the medical-surgical bed shortage. Both of these patients have care needs within Nurse Okonkwo's validated competencies.

At 2130, Nurse Okonkwo gives a bedside handoff report to Keisha for Clients A and B. She communicates the following: Client A's MAP trend (77 at 1915, 64 at 2000), the norepinephrine increase from 4 to 6 mcg/min at 2000, the increase in chest tube output and color change, and the heart rate increase to 102. For Client B, she communicates the blood pressure rise to 156/82, the nicardipine increase from 5 to 7.5 mg/hr at 2030, and her observation about the possible speech change that Marcus assessed as unchanged.

At 2145, Keisha reassesses Client B. She notes that Client B's speech now has a definite slurring quality compared to the immediate post-operative baseline documented in the recovery room note. Systolic blood pressure is 148 after the nicardipine increase. Keisha immediately notifies the surgeon per the post-carotid protocol and a STAT CT of the head is ordered.

At 2200, Nurse Okonkwo documents the events of the evening in a factual, objective manner according to the facility's assignment-objection and incident-reporting policies. Her documentation in the variance report includes: the time she received the assignment, the specific competency concerns she raised with the charge nurse and the times she raised them, Marcus's responses, the times she needed Marcus at the bedside for titration interventions, the twelve-minute response delay, the time she escalated to the nursing supervisor, the supervisor's response and the assignment restructuring, and the time of bedside handoff. She uses objective language throughout — she does not characterize Marcus's behavior as dismissive or retaliatory, and she does not editorialize about the charge nurse's judgment. She documents what was said, what was done, and when.

At 2230, Nurse Okonkwo completes an incident report per facility policy, documenting the assignment of patients requiring care outside her validated competencies and the steps taken to resolve the situation. The incident report is factual and does not assign blame.

Client C remains stable throughout the shift. The new cholecystectomy patient is comfortable, tolerating clear liquids, and ambulating to the bathroom independently.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, after completing initial rounds and reviewing the titration protocols and neuro assessment form, Nurse Okonkwo must identify which findings from the assignment and her own competency review are the significant cues that this is a patient safety concern rather than a routine float assignment. The relevant data includes: her competency file lacks validation for vasoactive titration, arterial line management, and the step-down neuro protocol; Client A requires active norepinephrine titration to a MAP target with arterial line monitoring; Client B requires nicardipine titration within a defined blood pressure range and hourly structured neuro assessments with specific escalation triggers; Client C's care needs are consistent with medical-surgical acuity; the facility float policy requires assignments consistent with documented competencies; and all three patients are currently hemodynamically stable at the moment of the assignment. *Correct action:* The significant cues are the mismatch between Nurse Okonkwo's documented competencies and the care requirements of Clients A and B — specifically, the vasoactive titration orders, the arterial line, and the structured neuro protocol with grading criteria and escalation triggers she has not been trained on. The fact that all three patients are currently stable and that Client C's care is within her validated competencies are contextual but do not negate the competency gap. The current hemodynamic stability of Clients A and B is not a reassuring cue — it is expected to change, and when it does, the nurse must be competent to respond. *Why:* The safety concern is not about the nurse's willingness to float or her general nursing skill. It is about the specific, documented absence of validation for interventions that these patients' orders require. A nurse who has never titrated norepinephrine cannot safely manage a post-CABG patient whose MAP is being maintained by that drip. A nurse unfamiliar with a structured neuro protocol's grading criteria and escalation triggers may fail to recognize or appropriately grade a change that requires emergent intervention. The facility's own float policy explicitly requires competency-matched assignments. *Skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, Nurse Okonkwo must analyze why the charge nurse's reassurance — "the drips are already running, just don't change them" — does not resolve the safety concern. *Correct action:* The charge nurse's statement assumes the drips will not need adjustment, but the clinical reality is that both drips have titration parameters precisely because they are expected to need adjustment. Client A's norepinephrine is titrated to maintain MAP above 64 — if MAP falls, the drip must be increased, and the nurse managing the patient must be competent to do so in a timely manner. Client B's nicardipine is titrated to keep systolic between 100 and 150 — blood pressure fluctuations are expected post-carotid endarterectomy, and the protocol exists because timely titration is essential to prevent hypertensive hemorrhage or hypotensive ischemia. The charge nurse's offer to "come help" is also insufficient because he has his own full patient assignment and cannot guarantee timely response. The core issue — that the assigned nurse lacks the competency to independently perform the interventions these patients' conditions are likely to require — remains unresolved. *Why:* Vasoactive drips are not static infusions. They are ordered with titration parameters because the patient's hemodynamic status is expected to fluctuate and the drip rate must be adjusted in response. A nurse who cannot independently perform those adjustments cannot safely be the primary nurse for that patient, regardless of whether the drip happens to be stable at the moment of assignment. The charge nurse's plan creates a de facto arrangement where every titration requires a second nurse to leave his own patients and come to the bedside — an arrangement that introduces delays and divides the charge nurse's attention from his own assignment. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, with Client A's MAP at the threshold and Client B's blood pressure above the protocol ceiling with a possible speech change, Nurse Okonkwo must determine the most important problem to address. Multiple concerns are present: Client A needs a titration she cannot perform, Client B needs a titration she cannot perform and may have a neurological change, the charge nurse's response time was twelve minutes on the last call, and the overall assignment structure remains unsafe. *Correct action:* After immediate patient-specific concerns have been escalated for qualified assessment, the management-of-care priority is the unsafe assignment structure. The individual clinical events (Client A's MAP drop, Client B's blood pressure and possible speech change) are symptoms of the underlying problem: a nurse without the required competencies is the primary caregiver for patients whose conditions demand those competencies. Addressing each event one at a time by paging the charge nurse is a reactive, unsustainable pattern that has already produced a twelve-minute delay. The priority is to escalate the assignment concern through the chain of command to achieve a structural solution — reassignment or a competency-matched buddy — rather than continuing to manage individual crises piecemeal. *Why:* Prioritization in management of care requires distinguishing between the immediate clinical need (which Marcus can address in the moment) and the systemic safety problem (which only a structural change can fix). Continuing to function in a reactive mode — calling Marcus each time a titration is needed — does not resolve the root cause and exposes patients to cumulative risk from response delays. The twelve-minute delay on the second call demonstrates that the workaround is already failing. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, Nurse Okonkwo must determine the appropriate set of actions to take to resolve the unsafe assignment. Several possible actions exist: refuse all patient care and leave the unit, accept the assignment as given and do her best, continue caring for Client C while requesting reassignment of Clients A and B, escalate to the nursing supervisor, call the provider directly to report the charge nurse, or file a complaint with the state board of nursing. *Correct action:* The appropriate actions are: (a) continue providing care for Client C, whose needs are within her competency, to avoid abandoning an accepted patient; (b) communicate her specific competency limitations to the charge nurse clearly and professionally, which she has already done; (c) propose a safe alternative — reassignment of Clients A and B to a validated nurse, or assignment of a buddy nurse who can perform the titrations and neuro assessments; (d) escalate to the nursing supervisor through the chain of command when the charge nurse does not resolve the concern; and (e) document the events objectively. Refusing all care and leaving the unit would constitute patient abandonment. Accepting the full assignment without escalation would be practicing outside her competency. Calling the provider to complain about the staffing assignment is not the next chain-of-command step. However, any suspected neurologic change must still be assessed and escalated according to the post-carotid protocol by a nurse competent to apply that protocol. *Why:* The nurse has a professional and legal obligation not to abandon patients already in her care (Client C was accepted when she began rounds), and she has an equally strong obligation not to practice outside her validated competency. These two obligations are not in conflict — they are resolved by continuing care within competency while escalating the structural problem. The chain of command exists precisely for situations where a unit-level safety concern is not resolved by the charge nurse. Escalation to the supervisor is the next step, not a punitive or adversarial action. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, Nurse Okonkwo calls the nursing supervisor. She must communicate the concern effectively. *Correct action:* State the facts in a structured, objective manner: identify herself and her home unit, state her documented competencies and the specific competencies she lacks, describe the assignment (two patients on vasoactive drips requiring titration, one post-carotid patient on an hourly neuro protocol), state that she communicated the concern to the charge nurse and describe his response, provide specific examples of the problem in action (two bedside calls in ninety minutes, a twelve-minute response delay), state what she is requesting (reassignment or a competency-matched buddy for Clients A and B), and affirm what she can continue to do (care for Client C and other patients within her competency). She does not characterize the charge nurse's motives, does not use emotional language, and does not threaten to leave. *Why:* Effective chain-of-command communication is factual, specific, and solution-oriented. The supervisor needs to understand the competency gap, the evidence that the current workaround is inadequate, and what resolution the nurse is requesting. Emotional or accusatory language undermines the communication and shifts the focus from patient safety to interpersonal conflict. Stating what she can do — not only what she cannot — demonstrates professionalism and helps the supervisor craft a workable solution. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after the assignment has been restructured and Nurse Okonkwo has handed off Clients A and B, she must evaluate whether the safety concern has been effectively resolved and determine what documentation is needed. At 2145, Keisha identifies a definite speech change in Client B that triggers the post-carotid protocol escalation. *Correct action:* The resolution is confirmed by the following: Clients A and B are now assigned to a nurse validated for vasoactive titration and the step-down neuro protocol; Nurse Okonkwo's current assignment (Client C and the cholecystectomy patient) is within her documented competencies; and the system worked — Keisha's detection of Client B's speech change and immediate protocol activation demonstrates that a competency-matched nurse was needed and that the reassignment improved patient safety. Patient records should contain patient-specific assessments, interventions, notifications, handoff details, and responses. The staffing/competency concern and chain-of-command steps should be documented through the facility’s assignment-objection, supervisor notification, or incident-report process according to policy. Do not document that an incident report or variance report was filed in the patient’s medical record. *Why:* Evaluation of outcomes in management of care includes confirming that the structural problem has been resolved, not just that the immediate crisis has passed. Keisha’s later finding may represent progression or clearer recognition using the unit protocol; either way, it demonstrates why competency-matched care matters. Factual documentation protects the patient (by creating a record for quality review), the nurse (by demonstrating appropriate professional conduct), and the institution (by activating the incident-reporting system). Subjective or retaliatory language in documentation undermines all three purposes. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- (Decision 1) Treating the current hemodynamic stability of Clients A and B as evidence that the assignment is safe — "they're stable right now, so there's no problem." This confuses the current snapshot with the anticipated clinical trajectory. Vasoactive drips exist because instability is expected; the competency concern is about what happens when the patient's status changes, not about the current moment. A nurse who makes this error will accept the assignment without raising concerns and will be unprepared when titration is needed.

- (Decision 1) Identifying the problem as "being floated" rather than as a specific competency mismatch. A nurse who frames the concern as displeasure with floating, rather than as a factual gap between her validated skills and the patients' care requirements, will have difficulty articulating the safety issue and may be perceived as uncooperative rather than advocating for patient safety.

- (Decision 2) Accepting the charge nurse's reassurance at face value — "he said just don't change the drips, so I'll watch and call if something happens." This error treats the charge nurse's workaround as a resolution when it is not. The nurse who accepts this plan has not resolved the competency gap; she has only deferred the problem to the moment when a titration is needed and the charge nurse may not be immediately available.

- (Decision 3) Continuing to manage each clinical event individually by paging the charge nurse rather than escalating the structural problem. This error treats the symptoms (each individual titration need) rather than the cause (the unsafe assignment). It is reactive rather than proactive and exposes patients to cumulative risk from response delays, as demonstrated by the twelve-minute gap.

- (Decision 4) Refusing all patient care and leaving the unit to avoid practicing outside competency. While the nurse is correct that she should not practice outside her competency, abandoning all patients — including Client C, whose care is within her validated competencies — constitutes patient abandonment. The nurse has accepted responsibility for these patients by beginning her assessment rounds. The correct action is to continue care within competency while escalating the structural concern, not to withdraw from all care.

- (Decision 4) Accepting the full assignment without escalation because the nurse fears being labeled uncooperative or insubordinate. This error prioritizes interpersonal comfort over patient safety. The nurse's professional obligation is to advocate for safe patient care, and the chain of command exists to provide a structured, non-adversarial pathway for doing so. Practicing outside validated competency to avoid conflict is not a safe or professional choice.

- (Decision 5) Using emotional, accusatory, or subjective language when escalating to the supervisor — for example, "Marcus doesn't care about patient safety" or "he's being dismissive and rude." This shifts the conversation from a factual safety concern to an interpersonal complaint and makes it harder for the supervisor to act on the clinical issue. Effective escalation is specific, factual, and solution-oriented.

- (Decision 6) Documenting the events using subjective or retaliatory language — for example, "the charge nurse was dismissive and put patients at risk due to his laziness" — rather than objective facts. Subjective documentation does not serve the quality review process, may constitute defamation, and undermines the nurse's credibility. Equally, failing to document at all — assuming the problem is resolved and no record is needed — deprives the institution of information needed for systemic safety improvement.

- (Decision 6) Concluding that the situation was not actually a safety concern because "nothing bad happened to Client A" — ignoring that Keisha's detection of Client B's speech change demonstrates exactly the kind of finding that requires a nurse familiar with the protocol's specific grading criteria. The near-miss is evidence that the concern was valid, not evidence that it was unnecessary.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the specific cues that distinguish an unsafe assignment (competency mismatch with patient care requirements) from a routine float assignment, including the significance of vasoactive titration validation and specialized assessment protocols.
- Analyze why a charge nurse's workaround ("just don't change the drips, call me if you need help") does not resolve the underlying competency and safety concern.
- Apply the chain-of-command process in the correct sequence — communicate with the charge nurse, propose a safe alternative, escalate to the nursing supervisor when unresolved — and distinguish appropriate escalation from abandonment or insubordination.
- Identify which actions are appropriate (continuing care within competency, escalating through chain of command, documenting objectively) and which are inappropriate (abandoning all patients, accepting an unsafe assignment without escalation, using retaliatory documentation language) in an assignment dispute.
- Document an assignment safety concern using objective, factual language and evaluate whether the resolution effectively addressed the root cause.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the charge nurse has failed to resolve the competency concern on two separate occasions, a twelve-minute response delay has occurred, and Client B's blood pressure has exceeded the protocol ceiling with a possible neurological change — the full scope of the unsafe assignment has declared itself.

- *Most likely condition:* Unsafe assignment outside the nurse's validated competency. The core problem is the structural mismatch between Nurse Okonkwo's documented competencies (medical-surgical level) and the care requirements of Clients A and B (vasoactive titration, arterial line management, structured post-carotid neuro protocol with specific grading and escalation criteria). The charge nurse's workaround has failed twice in ninety minutes, producing a twelve-minute response delay and leaving the nurse unable to independently perform the interventions these patients require.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Nurse unwillingness to accept a float assignment — wrong here: Nurse Okonkwo has not refused to float, has not refused to care for patients within her competency (she accepted Client C without objection), and has articulated specific, documented competency gaps rather than a general objection to working on the step-down unit. Her concern is factual and patient-centered, not preference-based. (2) Inadequate staffing as the primary problem requiring the nurse to simply manage with available resources — wrong framing: while the staffing shortage is the context, the safety issue is the assignment of patients to a nurse who lacks the validated competency to perform their required care; the solution is reassignment or buddy-nurse support, not acceptance of an unsafe competency mismatch as an inevitable consequence of short staffing.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Escalate through the chain of command to the nursing supervisor, communicating the specific competency gaps, the evidence that the charge nurse's workaround is inadequate (two bedside calls, twelve-minute delay), and a proposed safe alternative (reassignment of Clients A and B or assignment of a competency-matched buddy nurse). (2) Continue providing care for Client C, whose needs are within Nurse Okonkwo's validated competencies, to maintain continuity and avoid patient abandonment while the structural problem is being resolved.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Refusing all patient care and leaving the unit — this constitutes patient abandonment, including abandonment of Client C, whose care the nurse is competent to provide and has already accepted by beginning assessment rounds; abandonment does not resolve the safety concern and creates a new one. (2) Accepting the full assignment without further escalation because the charge nurse said it would be fine — this results in the nurse practicing outside her validated competency, which is unsafe for the patients and a violation of professional practice standards; the charge nurse's reassurance does not confer competency.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Whether Clients A and B are reassigned to a nurse validated for vasoactive titration and the step-down neuro protocol — this is the structural resolution that confirms the root cause has been addressed, not just the immediate symptoms. (2) Whether the nurse's remaining assignment (Client C and any additional patients) consists entirely of patients whose care needs are within her documented competencies — this confirms that the resolution did not simply shift the problem (e.g., by giving her a different patient outside her validated competencies) but actually matched assignment to competency.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Whether Client A's MAP remains stable after the norepinephrine increase — while clinically important for Client A's care, this does not evaluate whether the assignment safety concern has been resolved; a temporarily stable MAP does not mean the assignment is safe, because the next fluctuation will again require a competency the nurse lacks. (2) Whether the charge nurse expresses agreement or satisfaction with the reassignment — the charge nurse's interpersonal response is not a measure of whether the patient safety concern has been addressed; the relevant parameter is the structural change in the assignment, not the charge nurse's attitude about it.
