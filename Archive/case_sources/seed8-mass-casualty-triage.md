**CASE TITLE**
Mass-casualty START triage with chemical contamination in a resource-limited emergency department.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 22-bed community hospital emergency department operates with a single trauma bay, two resuscitation bays, and a staffing complement of three registered nurses, one emergency physician, and one physician assistant on the evening shift. The facility's emergency operations plan (EOP) designates this staffing level as "Level 1 — resource-constrained" and activates the hospital's mass-casualty incident (MCI) protocol when the number of acute casualties exceeds the number of available treatment spaces. The MCI protocol mandates Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment (START) triage at the point of entry, requires full external decontamination of any chemically contaminated casualty before entry into the treatment area, and directs that treatment priority follow START categories — red (immediate) before yellow (delayed) before green (minor) — with black (expectant) casualties receiving comfort measures only. The hospital's decontamination protocol specifies that a designated decontamination area with portable shower, containment, and personal protective equipment (PPE) for staff is maintained outside the ambulance entrance, and that no contaminated casualty may enter the ED until decontamination is complete and the casualty is re-triaged. The charge nurse on duty has completed annual MCI and hazardous-materials response training.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At 1847 on a Tuesday evening, the ED receives a radio alert from county emergency medical services: an industrial explosion and chemical release at a fertilizer-blending facility three miles away. EMS reports at least seven casualties en route by ambulance and private vehicle, with an unknown chemical exposure involving anhydrous ammonia. The first ambulance arrives at 1854 carrying two patients; three private vehicles arrive simultaneously, each carrying one to two individuals. Within eight minutes the ED is presented with seven casualties at the ambulance entrance. The charge nurse activates the MCI protocol, pages the hospital incident commander, and positions herself at the triage point with START triage tags.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
The charge nurse performs a rapid START assessment on each casualty at the triage point. The following findings are obtained in the order the casualties present:

Casualty A — a 52-year-old man, unresponsive, no spontaneous respirations. The nurse repositions the airway; no respirations resume. No pulse palpable at the radial or carotid site.

Casualty B — a 41-year-old woman, ambulatory, walking from a private vehicle, holding a blood-soaked towel over a 6 cm laceration on her left forearm. She is alert, oriented, and conversational. Respiratory rate 18, radial pulse strong, capillary refill 2 seconds. She reports she was outside the building when the blast occurred and was struck by debris. She has no chemical exposure and no respiratory complaints.

Casualty C — a 29-year-old man, carried on a backboard by EMS. He is responsive to voice, moaning, and following simple commands ("squeeze my hand") but not speaking in sentences. Respiratory rate 32, radial pulse present but weak and rapid, capillary refill 4 seconds. He has an obvious open fracture of the right femur with active bleeding controlled by a tourniquet applied by EMS. His clothing is intact and dry; EMS reports he was in an office area away from the chemical release.

Casualty D — a 36-year-old woman, sitting upright on a stretcher, nonambulatory due to severe respiratory distress, alert and oriented, coughing persistently with audible inspiratory stridor. Respiratory rate 32, radial pulse strong, capillary refill 2 seconds. Her clothing and hair are visibly wet with a pungent chemical odor consistent with ammonia. Her eyes are tearing and erythematous. She reports she was standing near the ammonia tank when it ruptured.

Casualty E — a 48-year-old man, lying on a stretcher, eyes open, following commands, speaking in short phrases between coughs. Respiratory rate 26, radial pulse present, capillary refill 2 seconds. He has partial-thickness burns over both forearms and the anterior chest (estimated 18% total body surface area). His clothing has been removed by EMS and he has been wrapped in a clean sheet; EMS reports no chemical contamination on his skin after clothing removal. He has no stridor.

Casualty F — a 24-year-old woman, ambulatory, walking independently, anxious and tearful but without visible injury. Respiratory rate 20, radial pulse strong, capillary refill under 2 seconds. She reports ringing in her ears from the blast and a mild headache. She was in the parking lot and has no chemical exposure.

Casualty G — a 60-year-old man, unresponsive, brought by private vehicle. He is apneic. After airway repositioning, he remains apneic. Radial pulse is absent; carotid pulse is faint and irregular. His clothing is soaked with a chemical odor. The driver reports the man was found next to the ruptured ammonia tank.

Baseline environmental note: the ED has two ventilators, one of which is currently in use for an existing ICU-hold patient. The blood bank has four units of O-negative packed red blood cells immediately available. The decontamination station is set up and functional. Two additional nurses have been called in but will not arrive for approximately 35 minutes.

**LABORATORY DATA**
No laboratory data are available at the triage point. Point-of-care testing will become available once casualties are moved into the treatment area. Baseline ED supplies relevant to the unfolding: the medication dispensing system contains standard resuscitation medications, including albuterol nebulizers, airway equipment, and burn-care supplies.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1 — Minutes 0–15: Triage-point sorting and decontamination initiation (1854–1909).**
The charge nurse completes START assessments on all seven casualties within six minutes. She must assign a triage tag color to each casualty and determine the order in which they will be moved into the treatment area. Simultaneously, she identifies that Casualties D and G have chemical contamination requiring decontamination before they may enter the ED.

Casualty D (the 36-year-old woman with stridor and chemical contamination) is moved by stretcher to the decontamination station. She is nonambulatory but cooperative. A nurse in appropriate PPE initiates the decontamination protocol: removal of all clothing, copious water irrigation for a minimum of 15 minutes per the facility's hazmat protocol, with attention to eyes and skin. During decontamination, Casualty D's stridor persists and her cough worsens. Respiratory rate increases to 36.

Casualty G (the 60-year-old man who remained apneic and chemically contaminated) remains outside the ED entrance. No pulse is palpable.

Casualty A (the 52-year-old man) remains pulseless and apneic after airway repositioning. No further resuscitative efforts are initiated per MCI protocol.

Casualty C (the 29-year-old man with the open femur fracture) is moved into the trauma bay as the first treatment-area patient. His tourniquet is assessed — it is effective, bleeding is controlled. IV access is established. His mental status remains responsive to voice only; he is now diaphoretic with a heart rate of 128 and blood pressure 88/62.

Casualty E (the 48-year-old man with burns) is moved into a resuscitation bay. IV fluid resuscitation is initiated per the Parkland formula order set. He remains alert, speaking in short phrases, with stable vital signs: HR 102, BP 118/74, RR 24, SpO₂ 94% on room air.

Casualties B and F (the two ambulatory, alert women with minor injuries) are directed to the waiting area with instructions to apply direct pressure (Casualty B) and to remain seated (Casualty F). They are told a nurse will assess them when resources allow.

**Stage 2 — Minutes 15–40: Treatment-area management and decontamination completion (1909–1934).**
Casualty D completes the 15-minute decontamination irrigation. Post-decontamination re-assessment: she remains alert and oriented but her stridor is now audible without a stethoscope, respiratory rate 34, SpO₂ 89% on room air, HR 112, BP 134/86. She is cleared to enter the ED and is moved into the second resuscitation bay. Humidified oxygen via non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min is applied. The emergency physician orders nebulized albuterol and IV dexamethasone. The nurse prepares airway equipment, continuous pulse oximetry, bronchodilator therapy, and difficult-airway backup. After the first albuterol treatment, SpO₂ improves to 92%, but stridor persists. The physician requests anesthesiology standby for possible emergent intubation.

Casualty C in the trauma bay: blood pressure has dropped to 82/58 despite 1 L of warmed normal saline. Heart rate 134. The tourniquet remains in place. The emergency physician orders activation of the massive transfusion protocol; the first two units of O-negative PRBCs are initiated. The orthopedic surgeon on call is contacted for emergent operative fixation. The nurse notes that the patient is now responding only to painful stimuli.

Casualty E in the resuscitation bay: after 500 mL of lactated Ringer's, vital signs are HR 98, BP 122/76, RR 22, SpO₂ 95% on 2 L nasal cannula. He is alert, oriented, and reporting pain at 8/10 over the burn areas. IV morphine is administered per order. Burn center transfer is initiated by the physician.

Casualty G: confirmed deceased outside the ED. The body is covered and left in place per the facility's MCI morgue protocol; identification and notification procedures are deferred until the acute phase resolves.

The two additional nurses arrive at 1929, four minutes ahead of the estimated 35 minutes. The charge nurse assigns one to Casualty D's resuscitation bay and one to begin formal assessment of Casualties B and F in the waiting area.

**Stage 3 — Minutes 40–75: Reassessment, re-triage, and resource reallocation (1934–2009).**
Casualty C returns from the operating room after emergent external fixation and hemorrhage control. Post-operative vitals: HR 108, BP 98/64, RR 20, SpO₂ 97% on 4 L nasal cannula. He has received four units of PRBCs total and is now responsive to voice, following commands. Urine output via Foley catheter is 25 mL over the past hour. The nurse monitors for signs of ongoing hemorrhage and transfusion reaction.

Casualty D: after two additional albuterol nebulizers and IV dexamethasone, stridor has diminished. SpO₂ is 94% on oxygen. Respiratory rate has decreased to 24. She remains alert and is now speaking in full sentences. Anesthesiology remains on standby but intubation has not been required. The nurse continues to monitor as pulmonary injury may continue to evolve over 18–24 hours; severe exposures require observation/hospitalization and monitoring for noncardiogenic pulmonary edema.

Casualty E: burn center has accepted the transfer. He is hemodynamically stable. Pain is controlled at 4/10. He is awaiting helicopter transport, expected within 20 minutes.

Casualty B: the newly arrived nurse has assessed the forearm laceration — it is a 6 cm linear wound, no tendon or neurovascular involvement, hemostasis achieved with direct pressure. Wound irrigation and closure with sutures are performed. Tetanus prophylaxis is administered. She is discharged with wound-care instructions.

Casualty F: assessment reveals bilateral tympanic membranes are intact, neurological examination is normal, headache is mild and resolving. She is observed for 30 minutes, then discharged with blast-injury precautions and instructions to return if symptoms worsen.

The charge nurse conducts a re-triage sweep of all remaining casualties at 2000. She updates the hospital incident commander: five casualties treated, two deceased (A and G), two discharged (B and F), one awaiting transfer (E), two remaining in the ED (C and D) with improving trajectories. She documents the triage decisions, resource utilization, and timeline in the MCI log.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the triage point during Stage 1, the charge nurse must rapidly assign a START triage category to each of the seven casualties based on the assessment findings obtained in the first six minutes. The findings include a range of presentations: two casualties with no spontaneous respirations after airway repositioning (A and G), one with altered mental status and signs of hemorrhagic shock (C), one with respiratory distress and chemical contamination (D), one with significant burns but stable hemodynamics (E), and two ambulatory casualties with minor injuries (B and F). The nurse must sort each casualty into the correct START category — red/immediate, yellow/delayed, green/minor, or black/expectant — using the START algorithm's decision tree: can the patient walk? If not, is the patient breathing? If not breathing, does repositioning the airway restore respirations? If breathing, is the respiratory rate above 30? Is capillary refill greater than 2 seconds or radial pulse absent? Can the patient follow simple commands?

*Correct action:* Casualty A — black (expectant): apneic after airway repositioning, pulseless, no resuscitation attempted under MCI protocol. Casualty B — green (minor): ambulatory, alert, isolated extremity laceration with controlled bleeding, normal vital parameters. Casualty C — red (immediate): not ambulatory, breathing with respiratory rate 32 (above 30), capillary refill 4 seconds, signs of hemorrhagic shock. Casualty D — red (immediate): nonambulatory due to severe respiratory distress, breathing with respiratory rate 32 (above 30). Her stridor and chemical exposure further confirm a compromised airway warranting immediate intervention. Casualty E — yellow (delayed): not ambulatory, breathing with respiratory rate 26 (under 30), capillary refill 2 seconds, radial pulse is present and he is following commands and speaking. He has significant burns requiring treatment but is hemodynamically stable and not in immediate life-threat. Casualty F — green (minor): ambulatory, alert, no visible injury, normal vital parameters. Casualty G — black (expectant): apneic after airway repositioning, no radial pulse, chemical contamination; under MCI protocol with one ventilator available and multiple red-category casualties, resuscitation is not initiated.

*Why it is correct:* The START algorithm is a rapid, objective sorting tool designed for mass-casualty events. It uses a fixed decision tree — walking, breathing, perfusion, mental status — to categorize casualties in under 60 seconds each. The algorithm intentionally overrides conventional clinical judgment (where the sickest patient receives the most resources) in favor of allocating scarce resources to the greatest number of salvageable patients. Casualties who are apneic after a single airway-repositioning attempt are tagged black because the resources required for full resuscitation (ventilator, dedicated nurse, medications) would be consumed by one patient at the expense of several salvageable ones. The two ambulatory casualties with minor complaints are tagged green and deferred — their injuries are real but not time-sensitive. The distinction between red and yellow turns on whether the casualty has an immediate life-threat (airway compromise, respiratory rate above 30, poor perfusion, or altered mental status) versus a serious injury that can tolerate a short delay without death.

*Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues.

*Answerable from:* Stage 1 (the triage-point assessment findings provide all data needed for START categorization).

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the charge nurse must articulate why the triage decisions she has just made differ from conventional ED triage and why certain casualties who would receive aggressive intervention in a normal ED shift are instead categorized as expectant. Specifically, Casualty A is pulseless and apneic — in a conventional ED with full resources, CPR and advanced cardiac life support would be initiated immediately. Casualty G is apneic with chemical contamination — in a conventional setting, aggressive airway management and decontamination would proceed simultaneously. The nurse must analyze why the MCI context changes these decisions.

*Correct action:* In a mass-casualty incident where the number of casualties exceeds available resources, the governing ethical and operational principle shifts from "do everything possible for the sickest patient" to "do the greatest good for the greatest number." This means that casualties whose survival requires a disproportionate share of scarce resources — prolonged CPR, the only available ventilator, a dedicated nurse for one patient — are categorized as expectant (black) so that those resources can be directed to casualties who are salvageable with timely but less resource-intensive intervention. Casualty A meets the START black criteria objectively (apneic after airway repositioning), and initiating CPR would consume one nurse entirely, removing her from the care of Casualties C, D, and E, all of whom have survivable injuries if treated promptly. Casualty G similarly meets black criteria (apneic after airway repositioning), and his chemical contamination adds a further resource burden — decontamination before any treatment could begin, requiring PPE-equipped staff who are needed elsewhere. The expectant category is not an abandonment of care; it is a triage-level decision that comfort measures are provided but resuscitative resources are not allocated. This analysis — connecting the resource constraint to the ethical framework to the specific patient outcomes — is what distinguishes disaster triage from routine practice.

*Why it is correct:* The principle of utilitarian resource allocation in disaster medicine is codified in MCI protocols precisely because the instinct to resuscitate the most critically ill patient is powerful and deeply trained. Recognizing that this instinct must be overridden — and understanding the ethical reasoning behind the override — is essential to effective disaster nursing. The analysis also requires the nurse to understand that the expectant category is not permanent in all MCI systems; if resources later become available, re-triage may upgrade a patient. But at the moment of initial sorting with three nurses and seven casualties, the allocation must favor the salvageable.

*Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues.

*Answerable from:* Stage 1 (the resource inventory and the triage-point findings together provide the data; the MCI protocol stated in the background provides the framework).

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, with START tags assigned, the charge nurse must decide which red-category casualty enters the treatment area first. Both Casualty C (hemorrhagic shock, open femur fracture, tourniquet in place, altered mental status, declining blood pressure) and Casualty D (chemical contamination with worsening stridor, requiring decontamination before entry) are tagged red/immediate. Only one trauma bay and two resuscitation bays are available. The charge nurse must prioritize.

*Correct action:* Casualty C enters the treatment area first. Casualty D must complete decontamination before she can enter the ED, and decontamination takes a minimum of 15 minutes per the facility's hazmat protocol. Casualty C has no contamination barrier, is already on a backboard, and has an immediately life-threatening hemorrhage with declining hemodynamics despite a tourniquet — delay risks irreversible hemorrhagic shock. Moving Casualty C into the trauma bay immediately while Casualty D undergoes decontamination outside is the decision that treats the most salvageable casualties in the shortest time. This is not a judgment that Casualty C is "sicker" than Casualty D in the abstract; it is a judgment that Casualty C can receive definitive treatment *now* while Casualty D cannot enter the treatment space yet regardless of priority, so the sequencing maximizes the use of the available treatment window.

*Why it is correct:* Disaster triage prioritization is not simply "red before yellow." Among red-category casualties, the next layer of prioritization considers which patient can most benefit from immediate intervention — the concept of salvageability and treatability. Casualty C's hemorrhage is treatable with resources on hand (IV access, blood products, surgical consultation), and delay worsens his prognosis minute by minute. Casualty D's decontamination is a hard prerequisite that cannot be bypassed — bringing a contaminated patient into the ED would expose staff and other casualties to secondary ammonia exposure, potentially creating more casualties and degrading the treatment environment. The decontamination interval is therefore not wasted time for Casualty D; it is a necessary step, and during that interval the treatment area is used for Casualty C.

*Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses.

*Answerable from:* Stage 1 (the contamination status, the decontamination protocol requirement, and the hemodynamic data are all available at the triage point).

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the charge nurse and the arriving reinforcement nurses must determine which actions are appropriate under the MCI protocol and which represent conventional-care reflexes that would consume scarce resources without benefit. The team is managing Casualty C (hemorrhagic shock, now in the trauma bay receiving blood), Casualty D (post-decontamination, now in a resuscitation bay with worsening stridor), and Casualty E (burns, stable, in the second resuscitation bay). The charge nurse must identify the set of actions that are appropriate disaster-phase nursing actions versus those that are not.

*Correct action:* Appropriate disaster-phase actions include: (a) initiating the massive transfusion protocol for Casualty C per physician order, because hemorrhage control is the immediate life-saving intervention and the blood bank inventory supports it; (b) applying oxygen via non-rebreather mask and administering nebulized albuterol to Casualty D immediately upon ED entry, because airway management for ammonia inhalation injury is time-sensitive and uses readily available resources; (c) having airway equipment and bronchodilators available at Casualty D's bedside per the facility's hazmat protocol, because bronchospasm from ammonia exposure may progress rapidly; (d) initiating IV fluid resuscitation for Casualty E per the Parkland formula order set, because burn resuscitation is protocol-driven and does not require disproportionate staffing; (e) directing Casualties B and F to the waiting area with basic self-care instructions (direct pressure, seated observation), because their green-category injuries do not warrant immediate nursing resources during the acute phase; (f) requesting anesthesiology standby for Casualty D rather than attempting blind intubation, because the chemical inhalation injury may cause laryngeal edema making intubation difficult, and the anesthesiologist is a better-matched resource for that procedure.

Actions that represent inappropriate conventional-care reflexes in this MCI context include: (a) initiating CPR on Casualty A or Casualty G — both are tagged black, and full resuscitation would consume one or two nurses and the only available ventilator, directly compromising care for the three red/yellow casualties; (b) performing a comprehensive head-to-toe nursing assessment with full documentation on each casualty before initiating treatment — in MCI mode, the START assessment *is* the initial assessment, and treatment of red-category casualties begins immediately based on the identified life-threat; (c) assigning a nurse to remain with Casualty B to perform wound closure during the acute phase — wound closure is important but is a green-category intervention that can safely wait 30–60 minutes; (d) attempting to decontaminate Casualty G's body as a treatment priority — the patient is deceased, and decontamination of remains is a post-acute-phase task handled per the facility's morgue protocol.

*Why it is correct:* MCI nursing requires continuous resource-allocation judgment. Every action must be weighed against the opportunity cost — the nurse-minutes and supplies consumed by one action are unavailable for another. The appropriate actions listed above share a common feature: each addresses an immediate or near-immediate life-threat in a salvageable casualty using available resources proportionate to the benefit. The inappropriate actions share the opposite feature: each consumes disproportionate resources for no survival benefit (CPR on expectant casualties), delays life-saving treatment for a non-urgent task (comprehensive documentation before treatment), or misallocates nursing time to a deferrable intervention (wound closure during the acute phase).

*Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions.

*Answerable from:* Stage 2 (the post-decontamination status of Casualty D, the hemodynamic trajectory of Casualty C, and the arrival of reinforcement nurses are all Stage 2 data needed to evaluate the full action set).

5. *Situation:* At Stage 1 transitioning into Stage 2, the charge nurse must sequence the steps for managing Casualty D, who is chemically contaminated and has worsening respiratory distress. The steps involved are: (a) don appropriate PPE before approaching the contaminated casualty, (b) remove all of the casualty's clothing and personal items and bag them as contaminated waste, (c) perform copious water irrigation for the full 15-minute protocol duration with attention to eyes and skin folds, (d) perform a post-decontamination assessment to confirm no residual contamination and re-triage the casualty, (e) move the casualty into the ED treatment area, and (f) initiate airway and respiratory treatment (high-flow oxygen, nebulized albuterol, IV dexamethasone per physician order). These steps have a clinically mandated sequence that cannot be rearranged without creating a safety hazard.

*Correct action:* The correct sequence is: first, don appropriate PPE (the nurse cannot safely approach a chemically contaminated casualty without respiratory and skin protection — failure to do so risks incapacitating the nurse and creating an additional casualty); second, remove all clothing and personal items (clothing traps the chemical against the skin and continues the exposure — removal eliminates an estimated 75–90% of surface contamination); third, perform the full 15-minute copious water irrigation (dilution and removal of the remaining chemical from skin and eyes — the duration is specified by the facility's hazmat protocol and must not be shortened even if the casualty appears improved, because ammonia causes progressive tissue injury with continued contact); fourth, perform the post-decontamination reassessment (confirm that no chemical odor or residue remains, re-assess vital signs and respiratory status, and re-triage — the casualty's condition may have changed during the 15-minute decontamination interval, and the re-triage determines whether she remains red or has deteriorated further); fifth, move the casualty into the ED treatment area (only after confirmed decontamination — premature entry risks secondary contamination of the treatment environment, staff, and other casualties); sixth, initiate airway and respiratory treatment inside the ED (do not contaminate the ED; provide only decon-zone emergency support that trained, PPE-equipped staff can safely perform, then move to the ED after decontamination for definitive monitoring, medications, and airway management).

*Why it is correct:* The decontamination-before-treatment sequence is a foundational principle of hazardous-materials response in healthcare. It protects the treatment environment, the staff, and other patients from secondary exposure. The specific order within the decontamination process — PPE first, then clothing removal, then irrigation — follows the logic of progressive contamination reduction while maintaining rescuer safety. Skipping or shortening any step creates a specific, identifiable hazard: approaching without PPE risks a second casualty (the nurse); irrigating over clothing traps the chemical; cutting the irrigation short leaves residual chemical that continues tissue damage; entering the ED before confirmation of decontamination exposes the entire department. The post-decontamination reassessment is placed before ED entry because the casualty's clinical status may have changed — she may now require a different level of intervention than initially anticipated, and the re-triage informs which treatment space she is assigned to.

*Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action.

*Answerable from:* Stage 1 and Stage 2 (the contamination status is identified at Stage 1; the completion of decontamination and the post-decontamination reassessment occur at Stage 2, and the full sequence spans both stages).

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the acute phase of the MCI is resolving. The charge nurse must evaluate whether the triage and resource-allocation decisions made during the event were effective. She reviews the outcomes: five of seven casualties received treatment; two were categorized as expectant and died; of the five treated, two are stable and being discharged (B and F), one is stable and awaiting transfer (E), and two remain in the ED with improving trajectories (C and D). She must determine which parameters confirm that the disaster triage was effective and that resources were appropriately allocated.

*Correct action:* The effectiveness of disaster triage is evaluated by whether salvageable casualties survived and received timely, appropriate intervention — not by whether every casualty survived. The key parameters are: (a) all red-category (immediate) casualties who were salvageable received treatment within the resource constraints and are alive with improving clinical trajectories — Casualty C's hemorrhage is controlled and he is post-operative with improving hemodynamics, and Casualty D's airway compromise is improving with treatment and she has not required intubation; (b) the yellow-category casualty (E) received appropriate initial stabilization and is being transferred to a higher level of care (burn center) in stable condition — the delay inherent in yellow categorization did not result in clinical deterioration; (c) green-category casualties (B and F) were safely deferred during the acute phase and received definitive care once resources became available, with no adverse outcomes from the delay; (d) no secondary contamination events occurred — no staff members were exposed to ammonia, no other casualties were contaminated inside the ED, and the decontamination protocol was completed fully before Casualty D entered the treatment area; (e) scarce resources (the single available ventilator, the limited blood supply, nursing staff) were allocated to salvageable casualties and were not consumed by expectant-category resuscitation attempts.

The parameters that would indicate triage failure include: a salvageable casualty who deteriorated or died because resources were diverted to a non-salvageable patient; a yellow-category casualty who decompensated to unsalvageable during the delay because the delay was too long or reassessment was missed; secondary contamination of staff or the treatment environment; or green-category casualties who had occult serious injuries missed by the rapid START assessment.

*Why it is correct:* Disaster triage effectiveness is measured against the utilitarian framework that governs it — greatest good for the greatest number. The metric is not zero mortality (which is unachievable in a true MCI) but rather whether the mortality that occurred was limited to casualties who were non-salvageable given the available resources, and whether all salvageable casualties received the care they needed in time to survive. The absence of secondary contamination is a safety metric specific to hazmat MCI events. The successful deferral of green-category casualties without adverse outcome confirms that the START algorithm correctly identified them as safe to wait. Reviewing these parameters allows the charge nurse to complete the MCI documentation, contribute to the after-action review, and identify any system failures for future improvement.

*Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes.

*Answerable from:* Stage 3 (the post-acute outcomes, the resource-utilization data, and the final status of all casualties are Stage 3 information).

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

(Decision 1) Tagging Casualty A or Casualty G as red/immediate and initiating CPR. This is the most common and most consequential error in disaster triage — applying the conventional-care reflex of "pulseless patient = code blue" to an MCI setting. Under START, a casualty who is apneic after a single airway-repositioning maneuver is tagged black. Initiating CPR would consume one to two nurses and the only available ventilator for a patient with a very low probability of survival, directly depriving Casualties C, D, and E of the resources they need to survive. The error stems from the deeply ingrained training that CPR is always initiated for a pulseless patient, which is correct in a resourced setting but incorrect under MCI triage rules.

(Decision 1) Tagging Casualty E as red/immediate instead of yellow/delayed. The 18% TBSA burns are visually dramatic and the patient is clearly in pain, which can lead a nurse to over-triage. However, Casualty E is breathing with a respiratory rate under 30, has a present radial pulse, is following commands, and is hemodynamically stable. His injuries are serious and require treatment, but they are not immediately life-threatening in the next 30–60 minutes. Over-triaging him to red would move him ahead of or alongside Casualties C and D, consuming a treatment space and nursing time that the truly immediate casualties need.

(Decision 2) Believing that the expectant category means "do not care for at all" or that it is equivalent to a do-not-resuscitate order. The expectant category means that resuscitative resources are not allocated, but comfort measures (pain management, covering, emotional support if conscious) are still provided when resources allow. Conversely, a nurse who cannot accept the expectant category and diverts resources to a non-salvageable casualty out of moral distress is making an understandable but operationally harmful error.

(Decision 3) Prioritizing Casualty D for immediate ED entry over Casualty C because Casualty D has an airway problem and "airway comes first in ABCs." This error applies the individual-patient ABC assessment framework to a multi-casualty resource-allocation decision where it does not govern. Casualty D cannot enter the ED until decontamination is complete regardless of her airway status — bringing her in contaminated would endanger the entire department. Casualty C can enter immediately and has a hemorrhage that is worsening minute by minute. The correct prioritization accounts for treatability and access, not just the ABC hierarchy applied to a single patient in isolation.

(Decision 4) Performing a full head-to-toe assessment with comprehensive documentation on each casualty before initiating any treatment. In conventional nursing practice, assessment precedes intervention, and thorough documentation is a professional standard. In an MCI, the START assessment *is* the initial assessment, and treatment of life-threats begins immediately based on the identified category. Delaying blood product administration for Casualty C or airway treatment for Casualty D in order to complete a full nursing assessment and chart it would waste critical minutes. Documentation in MCI mode is abbreviated and concurrent — triage tags, brief notes, and the MCI log — with full documentation completed retrospectively after the acute phase.

(Decision 5) Skipping PPE and beginning decontamination irrigation immediately to save time, reasoning that the patient's worsening stridor makes speed more important than rescuer safety. This error is dangerous because ammonia exposure can incapacitate the nurse through inhalation or skin contact, creating a second casualty and reducing the already-strained workforce. PPE donning takes approximately two minutes and is a non-negotiable first step. Similarly, shortening the 15-minute irrigation to 5 minutes because the patient "looks clean" risks leaving residual chemical on the skin and in the eyes, causing continued tissue injury and risking secondary contamination when the patient enters the ED.

(Decision 5) Initiating nebulized albuterol at the outdoor decontamination station before completing irrigation and moving the patient inside. While the respiratory distress is concerning, administering aerosolized medication in an open-air decontamination area with active chemical contamination is ineffective (the medication disperses in open air) and potentially unsafe (the nebulizer equipment becomes contaminated). Respiratory treatment begins after decontamination is complete and the patient is inside the ED with appropriate monitoring.

(Decision 6) Judging the MCI response as a failure because two patients died. In disaster triage, the expectation is not zero mortality — it is that mortality is limited to non-salvageable casualties and that all salvageable casualties survive. If the two deaths (Casualties A and G) were both non-salvageable by START criteria and all five salvageable casualties survived with appropriate care, the triage was effective. Evaluating disaster response by conventional mortality metrics leads to inappropriate guilt, moral injury, and potentially to future over-triage (tagging non-salvageable patients as red), which paradoxically increases overall mortality by diverting resources.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Prioritize among multiple red-category casualties based on salvageability, treatability, and access constraints (including decontamination requirements), rather than applying the individual-patient ABC hierarchy to a multi-casualty resource-allocation decision.
- Sequence the decontamination-to-treatment process correctly — PPE, clothing removal, irrigation, post-decontamination reassessment, ED entry, then definitive treatment — and explain why each step must precede the next.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of disaster triage using utilitarian outcome metrics (salvageable-casualty survival, absence of secondary contamination, appropriate resource allocation) rather than conventional zero-mortality expectations.
