**CASE TITLE**
Cytokine release syndrome following CAR-T cell infusion.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old man with relapsed diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) is admitted to the bone marrow transplant and cellular therapy unit for axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel) CAR-T cell infusion. He previously received two lines of chemoimmunotherapy with initial response followed by progressive disease. His past medical history includes well-controlled hypertension managed with lisinopril 10 mg daily and type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily; metformin was held 48 hours before admission per protocol. He has no history of cardiac disease, pulmonary disease, or neurological conditions. Lymphodepleting chemotherapy with fludarabine and cyclophosphamide was completed three days ago, and the CAR-T cell product was infused this morning at 0800 without immediate infusion reaction. Standing orders include continuous telemetry, strict intake and output, neurological assessments every 4 hours using the ICE (Immune Effector Cell-Associated Encephalopathy) scoring tool, vital signs every 4 hours for the first 24 hours then escalating to every 2 hours or more frequently if any signs of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) emerge, tocilizumab 8 mg/kg IV available at bedside for CRS management per institutional protocol, and dexamethasone readily accessible. IV normal saline is infusing at 75 mL/hr for maintenance hydration. The patient has a double-lumen peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC) and a Foley catheter for accurate output measurement.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At 1200, four hours after infusion, the patient is resting comfortably in bed. He reports mild fatigue, which he attributes to the lymphodepleting chemotherapy. He is alert, oriented to person, place, time, and situation, conversational, and in no acute distress. He denies headache, chills, nausea, dyspnea, or muscle aches. His wife is at the bedside and confirms he seems like himself.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline at 1200: Temperature 36.8 °C (oral), heart rate 78 and regular sinus rhythm on telemetry, blood pressure 132/78, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Lungs clear to auscultation bilaterally, no adventitious sounds. Heart sounds regular, no murmur or gallop. Abdomen soft, non-tender, with normoactive bowel sounds. Skin warm and dry, no rash. Capillary refill less than 2 seconds. ICE score 10 out of 10 (fully intact). Urine output 280 mL over the past 4 hours, clear yellow. No peripheral edema. PICC site clean, dry, and intact with brisk blood return from both lumens. These findings — temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, SpO₂, ICE score, and urine output — are the parameters whose trajectory across the subsequent stages is clinically decisive.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn at 1000, two hours post-infusion: WBC 0.3 × 10³/µL (profoundly pancytopenic from lymphodepletion), ANC 0.1 × 10³/µL, hemoglobin 9.8 g/dL, platelets 45,000/µL, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, glucose 148 mg/dL, AST 28 U/L, ALT 32 U/L, total bilirubin 0.8 mg/dL, LDH 220 U/L, CRP less than 5 mg/L, ferritin 320 ng/mL, fibrinogen 310 mg/dL, INR 1.0, D-dimer 0.4 µg/mL FEU. The CRP, ferritin, and fibrinogen trajectory over the coming days will be critical markers of CRS severity and evolution. The creatinine, LDH, liver enzymes, and coagulation parameters will signal end-organ involvement if the syndrome escalates.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 36 hours post-infusion (Day 2, approximately 2000): The nurse arrives for evening assessment and finds the patient lying in bed with a blanket pulled up, shivering intermittently. He reports feeling "achy all over, like a bad flu" with onset over the past 2 to 3 hours. He says he felt fine at the 1600 assessment except for mild fatigue. His wife reports he has been less talkative in the past hour. Vital signs: temperature 38.9 °C (oral), heart rate 104 and sinus tachycardia on telemetry, blood pressure 118/72 (down from baseline of 132/78), respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Skin is warm and flushed with mild diaphoresis. No rash. Lungs remain clear. ICE score is 10 out of 10; he is oriented and follows commands appropriately, though he is slower to respond. Urine output over the past 4 hours is 160 mL, decreased from baseline. The provider is notified and orders stat labs: CRP 86 mg/L (markedly elevated from less than 5), ferritin 1,450 ng/mL (elevated from 320), fibrinogen 280 mg/dL (mildly decreased from 310), LDH 380 U/L, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, AST 40 U/L, ALT 38 U/L. Vital sign frequency is increased to every 2 hours. The provider grades this as CRS Grade 1 (fever without hypotension or hypoxia) and orders acetaminophen 650 mg PO for symptom management while maintaining close monitoring. The nurse is instructed to call immediately if the patient develops hypotension, requires supplemental oxygen, or shows any neurological change.

**Stage 2** — 48 hours post-infusion (Day 3, approximately 0800): Over the night shift, the patient's fever has escalated to 39.8 °C despite scheduled acetaminophen. He is now rigoring. He tells the nurse he feels dizzy when he sits up and reports a mild headache. On assessment, heart rate is 122, sinus tachycardia, blood pressure 88/52 (mean arterial pressure 64), respiratory rate 26, SpO₂ 93% on room air. Lungs have faint bibasilar crackles. He is oriented to city and hospital but is uncertain of the year and month. ICE score has dropped to 7 out of 10, with points lost for orientation to year and month (2 points) and the inability to name one of three objects (1 point). His wife states, "He doesn't seem right — he keeps losing his train of thought." Urine output over the past 4 hours is 80 mL. The telemetry monitor alarms for sustained sinus tachycardia. Repeat labs: CRP 245 mg/L, ferritin 8,200 ng/mL, fibrinogen 180 mg/dL, LDH 620 U/L, creatinine 1.6 mg/dL, AST 78 U/L, ALT 65 U/L, total bilirubin 1.4 mg/dL, D-dimer 2.8 µg/mL FEU, potassium 5.1 mEq/L. The provider upgrades the grading to CRS Grade 2 (fever with hypotension not requiring vasopressors and/or hypoxia requiring low-flow oxygen) with concern for concurrent immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS). Orders include tocilizumab 8 mg/kg IV over 60 minutes (first dose), a 500 mL normal saline IV fluid bolus, initiation of supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula at 2 L/min targeting SpO₂ greater than or equal to 94%, and preparation for possible ICU transfer. The provider also orders dexamethasone 10 mg IV every 6 hours for the concurrent neurotoxicity.

**Stage 3** — 60 hours post-infusion (Day 3, approximately 2000, 12 hours after tocilizumab and initiation of dexamethasone): The patient received the tocilizumab infusion without incident and has received three doses of dexamethasone. On reassessment: temperature 37.6 °C (down from 39.8), heart rate 96 (sinus rhythm, improved from 122), blood pressure 108/68 (up from 88/52 and now stable without vasopressors after one additional 250 mL bolus), respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on 1 L nasal cannula (weaned from 2 L). The patient is alert and oriented to year, month, city, and hospital. ICE score has improved to 9 out of 10 (one point off for naming; he was unable to name one of the three objects but could write a full sentence and follow commands). His wife says, "He seems much more like himself." Lungs are clear; crackles have resolved. Urine output over the past 4 hours is 220 mL (improving). Repeat labs: CRP 110 mg/L (still elevated but trending down from 245), ferritin 5,400 ng/mL (down from 8,200), fibrinogen 240 mg/dL (improving from 180), LDH 420 U/L, creatinine 1.2 mg/dL (improving from 1.6), AST 52 U/L, ALT 48 U/L, D-dimer 1.6 µg/mL FEU, potassium 4.4 mEq/L. The provider continues dexamethasone on a taper schedule, maintains tocilizumab availability for a second dose if the patient re-spikes fever or becomes hypotensive again, and asks the nurse to continue frequent vital signs and neurological assessments every 4 hours. ICU transfer is deferred for now, pending sustained stability.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. The situation and question: At Stage 1, 36 hours post-infusion, the patient develops fever to 38.9 °C, tachycardia, myalgias, and rigors. He is in the post-CAR-T monitoring period and is profoundly immunosuppressed from lymphodepleting chemotherapy. The nurse must determine the most likely etiology driving this new presentation and differentiate it from other causes of fever in a neutropenic patient. The correct action is to recognize these findings as the classic initial presentation of cytokine release syndrome. Fever is the hallmark and often earliest sign of CRS, typically appearing 1 to 14 days post-CAR-T infusion (most commonly days 1 to 3 for axi-cel). The clinical picture — fever with constitutional symptoms (myalgias, rigors, fatigue) accompanied by a dramatically elevated CRP from less than 5 to 86 mg/L and elevated ferritin from 320 to 1,450 ng/mL — forms a constellation that points to CRS rather than isolated neutropenic fever or sepsis alone, though concurrent infection must always remain on the differential and blood cultures should be drawn per neutropenic fever protocol. The reason this is correct is that CRS results from massive activation of T cells and subsequent release of cytokines including interleukin-6, interferon-gamma, and tumor necrosis factor-alpha. The CRP surge is driven primarily by IL-6 and is a sensitive (though not specific) biomarker of CRS onset and severity. Ferritin elevation reflects macrophage activation in the inflammatory cascade. Recognizing the pattern — temporal relationship to infusion, fever as the defining feature, biomarker trajectory — is the first step in managing CRS. This exercises the clinical-judgment skill of recognizing cues. It becomes answerable from Stage 1 data.

2. The situation and question: At Stage 1, given that the patient is profoundly neutropenic (ANC 0.1) and now febrile, the nurse must determine what actions are required in addition to CRS monitoring. The correct action is to anticipate and ensure that blood cultures (peripheral and from each PICC lumen) are drawn and empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics are initiated per the institution's neutropenic fever protocol, even though CRS is the suspected primary etiology. Infection and CRS are not mutually exclusive and can be clinically indistinguishable in the early stages. This is correct because neutropenic fever is a medical emergency regardless of the presumed cause. In the post-CAR-T setting, patients are at extremely high risk for bacterial sepsis due to the profound immunosuppression from lymphodepleting chemotherapy. CRS management does not replace infection workup; they are concurrent pathways. Delaying antibiotics to "see if it's just CRS" can be fatal. The clinical-judgment skill exercised is generating solutions, because the nurse must identify that two clinical pathways — CRS management and neutropenic fever management — must run simultaneously rather than sequentially. This becomes answerable from Stage 1 data.

3. The situation and question: At Stage 2, the patient has progressed to fever 39.8 °C, hypotension with blood pressure 88/52, hypoxia with SpO₂ 93% on room air, declining ICE score from 10 to 7, and dramatically worsening biomarkers. The nurse must determine the priority of actions among the multiple new orders: tocilizumab infusion, fluid bolus, supplemental oxygen, dexamethasone, and preparation for ICU transfer. The correct action is to first apply supplemental oxygen and administer the IV fluid bolus to address the immediate physiological threats (impaired oxygenation and hypotension), then administer tocilizumab as the definitive CRS-targeted therapy, and then administer dexamethasone for the concurrent neurotoxicity. ICU transfer preparation occurs in parallel but does not delay pharmacological intervention. This is correct because the ABCs framework mandates stabilizing oxygenation and perfusion before administering immunomodulatory therapy. Oxygen corrects the immediate hypoxemia; the fluid bolus addresses cytokine-mediated vasodilation and capillary leak causing the hypotension. Tocilizumab, an IL-6 receptor antagonist, is the first-line pharmacological treatment for CRS Grade 2 or higher and blocks the downstream effects of IL-6, but it takes time to exert its effect and will not immediately reverse hypotension or hypoxia. Dexamethasone targets the neurotoxicity component, which is important but not the most immediately life-threatening finding compared to the hemodynamic instability. The clinical-judgment skill exercised is prioritizing hypotheses, because the nurse must triage multiple simultaneous concerns and sequence interventions correctly. This becomes answerable from Stage 2 data, because the hypotension, hypoxia, and neurological decline are not present in Stage 1.

4. The situation and question: At Stage 2, the patient's ICE score has dropped from 10 to 7 with disorientation to year and month, and word-finding difficulty on the naming task, along with a new headache and cognitive slowing reported by his wife. The nurse must determine whether this represents worsening CRS, a new complication, or both, and what the appropriate monitoring response is. The correct action is to recognize these findings as probable immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS), which frequently co-occurs with CRS but is graded and treated as a distinct entity. The nurse should continue serial ICE scoring at the increased frequency ordered, perform and document a focused neurological assessment including level of consciousness, pupillary response, and motor strength, and communicate the specific ICE score change to the provider so that ICANS grading can be updated. The nurse should also raise the head of the bed to at least 30 degrees and institute seizure precautions, as ICANS carries a risk of cerebral edema and seizures. This is correct because ICANS is mediated by cytokine-driven endothelial activation and blood-brain barrier disruption, leading to cerebral edema. It is distinct from CRS in its pathophysiology and treatment: tocilizumab does not cross the blood-brain barrier and therefore does not treat ICANS effectively, which is why dexamethasone (which does cross the blood-brain barrier) is added when neurotoxicity is identified. The ICE assessment tool is specifically validated to detect early ICANS, and serial trending is essential because neurotoxicity can progress rapidly to obtundation, seizures, or life-threatening cerebral edema. The clinical-judgment skill exercised is analyzing cues, because the nurse must differentiate ICANS from simple CRS progression and understand why a separate treatment pathway is triggered. This becomes answerable from Stage 2 data, since the ICE score and neurological findings do not change until Stage 2.

5. The situation and question: At Stage 2, the nurse reviews the lab trends and must determine which combination of findings most strongly supports escalating CRS severity with end-organ dysfunction. The correct action is to identify the cluster of CRP rising from 86 to 245 mg/L, ferritin surging from 1,450 to 8,200 ng/mL, fibrinogen falling from 280 to 180 mg/dL, creatinine rising from 1.0 to 1.6 mg/dL, and transaminases rising with AST going from 40 to 78 U/L and ALT from 38 to 65 U/L as the laboratory signature of escalating CRS with early end-organ involvement. The falling fibrinogen in the context of a rising D-dimer suggests consumptive coagulopathy. The rising creatinine and liver enzymes indicate renal and hepatic stress from cytokine-mediated inflammation. This is correct because CRS severity correlates with cytokine burden, reflected by CRP and ferritin trajectories. Fibrinogen is consumed in cytokine-driven coagulopathy (akin to disseminated intravascular coagulation), and a falling fibrinogen is an ominous trend that may precede bleeding complications, particularly dangerous in a thrombocytopenic patient. The creatinine rise reflects cytokine-mediated renal hypoperfusion and direct tubular injury. Trending these values together, rather than in isolation, gives the nurse the full picture of CRS trajectory. The clinical-judgment skill exercised is analyzing cues across the laboratory data set. This becomes answerable from Stage 2, because the trajectory requires comparison of Stage 1 and Stage 2 values.

6. The situation and question: At Stage 3, 12 hours after tocilizumab and dexamethasone initiation, the nurse must evaluate whether the patient is genuinely improving or whether the clinical picture is falsely reassuring. The correct action is to confirm that the improvement is genuine by verifying that multiple independent parameters are trending favorably in concert: temperature has decreased from 39.8 to 37.6 °C, hemodynamics have stabilized with blood pressure 108/68 without vasopressors, oxygen requirements are being weaned, ICE score has improved from 7 to 9, urine output has increased from 80 to 220 mL per 4 hours, and laboratory markers are trending in the correct directions — CRP declining, ferritin declining, fibrinogen recovering, creatinine falling. However, the nurse must also recognize that CRP may be an unreliable marker after tocilizumab administration, because tocilizumab blocks IL-6 signaling and causes a rapid suppression of CRP that may mask ongoing inflammation or concurrent infection. Therefore, the nurse should not rely solely on a dropping CRP to determine improvement but must integrate it with the full clinical picture and other biomarkers, particularly ferritin and the clinical assessment. The patient should continue on frequent monitoring because CRS can recrudesce, especially between 12 and 72 hours after a single tocilizumab dose. This is correct because tocilizumab's mechanism — blocking the IL-6 receptor — directly disrupts the signaling pathway that drives CRP production, meaning CRP loses its reliability as a standalone CRS biomarker after tocilizumab. Ferritin, clinical hemodynamics, urine output, and neurological status become more reliable indicators. Additionally, CRS recurrence is well-documented after a single tocilizumab dose, which is why a second dose must remain available and monitoring cannot be deescalated prematurely. The clinical-judgment skill exercised is evaluating outcomes. This becomes answerable from Stage 3, because the response data and the nuance about post-tocilizumab CRP interpretation require Stage 3 findings.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

For Decision Point 1: Attributing the fever, tachycardia, and constitutional symptoms solely to neutropenic sepsis and failing to recognize the CRS pattern. While infection must always be considered, ignoring the temporal relationship to CAR-T infusion and the CRP and ferritin trajectory delays CRS-specific monitoring escalation. Conversely, another error is dismissing the fever as "expected CRS" and failing to appreciate that the patient simultaneously meets criteria for neutropenic fever workup. Both errors stem from failure to hold two differential diagnoses simultaneously.

For Decision Point 2: Withholding or delaying empiric antibiotics because "this is probably CRS, not infection." This is dangerous because CRS and sepsis can be concurrent, and delayed antibiotics in a neutropenic patient carry significant mortality risk. Another error is drawing blood cultures but then waiting for results before starting antibiotics, which violates the urgency principle of neutropenic fever management.

For Decision Point 3: Prioritizing tocilizumab administration over immediate oxygen application and fluid resuscitation. While tocilizumab is critical, it does not immediately reverse hypoxemia or hypotension, and delaying basic stabilization to prepare and infuse a biologic agent violates the ABCs framework. Another error is administering a vasopressor before attempting fluid resuscitation; per CRS grading, Grade 2 CRS involves hypotension responsive to fluids, and escalating to vasopressors prematurely would reclassify the patient to Grade 3 without first attempting the less invasive intervention.

For Decision Point 4: Failing to perform or document serial ICE assessments and instead relying on a general statement like "patient seems confused" without quantifying the neurological change. The ICE tool exists specifically to standardize and trend neurotoxicity, and vague documentation delays recognition of ICANS progression. Another error is assuming tocilizumab will treat the neurotoxicity, leading the nurse to question or not advocate for the dexamethasone order. Tocilizumab does not cross the blood-brain barrier and is ineffective for ICANS; corticosteroids are the cornerstone of ICANS management.

For Decision Point 5: Interpreting the falling fibrinogen in isolation and initiating bleeding precautions while ignoring the rising creatinine and transaminases. While bleeding precautions are appropriate, the error is failing to report the full constellation of end-organ involvement to the provider, which may affect the decision to escalate CRS grading or transfer to the ICU. Another error is focusing only on the CRP elevation as "proof" of worsening CRS without integrating the coagulation, renal, and hepatic parameters, which provide information about end-organ impact that CRP alone does not.

For Decision Point 6: Assuming the patient is out of danger based on a single reassessment showing clinical improvement and advocating for reduced monitoring frequency. CRS can recrudesce, and the first 72 hours after tocilizumab require sustained vigilance. Another error is relying on the CRP trend as the primary indicator of resolution after tocilizumab has been given, not understanding that tocilizumab's mechanism disrupts CRP production and makes it an unreliable standalone marker. A student might also erroneously discontinue seizure precautions because the ICE score improved, not recognizing that ICANS can fluctuate and seizure risk persists until neurotoxicity fully resolves and corticosteroids are tapered.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the hallmark signs, symptoms, and biomarker patterns of cytokine release syndrome following CAR-T cell therapy, including the temporal relationship between infusion and symptom onset, and differentiate CRS from other causes of fever in the immunocompromised host.

Apply the CRS grading framework to determine the severity of cytokine release syndrome based on the presence or absence of fever, hypotension, and hypoxia, and correlate the grade with the appropriate first-line pharmacological intervention, specifically tocilizumab for CRS and dexamethasone for concurrent ICANS.

Prioritize and sequence nursing interventions during CRS escalation using the ABCs framework, ensuring immediate stabilization of oxygenation and hemodynamics before administering targeted immunomodulatory therapy.

Differentiate immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome from CRS progression by using the ICE assessment tool, and understand why tocilizumab is insufficient for ICANS management and corticosteroids are required.

Evaluate the effectiveness of CRS treatment by integrating clinical findings, hemodynamic trends, neurological status, urine output, and laboratory biomarker trajectories while recognizing the limitations of CRP as a monitoring tool after tocilizumab administration.
