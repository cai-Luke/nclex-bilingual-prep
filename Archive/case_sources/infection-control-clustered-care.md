# Cross-contamination prevention during clustered care of clients with different transmission-based precautions

## PATIENT BACKGROUND
The setting is a 28-bed medical-surgical unit during a weekday day shift. The nurse is assigned to four clients whose rooms are adjacent along one corridor. The unit's infection-prevention policy, posted at each doorway and reviewed at every shift huddle, states the following standing rules: (1) alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR) is the default hand-hygiene method except when hands are visibly soiled or the client has a known or suspected spore-forming organism, in which case soap and water is required; (2) dedicated or single-use equipment is required for any client on contact precautions — if a device must be shared, it is cleaned and disinfected using appropriate agents (such as an EPA-registered sporicidal disinfectant for spore-forming organisms like *C. difficile*) and allowed the manufacturer-specified wet-contact time before entering the next room; (3) PPE is donned outside the room and doffed inside the room (gloves first, then gown) before exiting, with hand hygiene performed immediately after removal; (4) visitors are offered a brief precautions orientation by the nurse at the first visit of each shift, including demonstration of hand hygiene and any required PPE; (5) soiled linen from any isolation room is placed in a water-soluble inner bag, then into a labeled outer bag, at the point of use.

Client A — Room 12. A 71-year-old woman, post-op day 2 after right hemicolectomy for colon cancer. She developed profuse watery diarrhea overnight; stool PCR returned positive for *Clostridioides difficile* infection (CDI) at 0545. She is on contact precautions. Orders include oral vancomycin 125 mg every 6 hours, IV lactated Ringer's at 125 mL/hr, strict intake and output, and daily basic metabolic panel. Her surgical incision is clean, dry, and intact with a Jackson-Pratt drain in place. She has a dedicated electronic thermometer and a dedicated blood-pressure cuff in her room.

Client B — Room 14. A 58-year-old man admitted 36 hours ago with community-acquired pneumonia. Sputum cultures are pending; he is on empiric ceftriaxone 1 g IV every 24 hours and azithromycin 500 mg IV daily. He is on standard precautions only. He has a peripheral IV in his left forearm, a continuous pulse oximeter, and supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula at 3 L/min. His wife visits daily and typically stays most of the shift.

Client C — Room 16. A 44-year-old man admitted with a large stage 3 pressure injury on his sacrum, culture-positive for methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA). The wound is open and draining moderate amounts of purulent exudate through the dressing. He is on contact precautions. Orders include IV vancomycin managed by pharmacy per facility protocol, wound care with wet-to-moist normal saline dressings every 12 hours, and a specialty low-air-loss mattress. He has a dedicated stethoscope and blood-pressure cuff in his room. His adult daughter has been helping reposition him and has been observed entering and leaving the room without gowning.

Client D — Room 18. A 29-year-old woman admitted with confirmed pulmonary tuberculosis (TB), sputum acid-fast bacilli (AFB) smear positive. She is in a negative-pressure airborne infection isolation room (AIIR). She is on airborne precautions plus standard precautions. Orders include a four-drug regimen (isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) administered orally, daily sputum AFB smears, and hepatic function panel every week. She has a dedicated stethoscope, thermometer, and blood-pressure cuff. Her mother, who speaks English as a second language but communicates conversationally, has been visiting and asks the nurse whether she can take her daughter outside in a wheelchair for fresh air.

The charge nurse informs the day-shift nurse at 0700 that the unit's portable bladder scanner is needed for Client A (who has not voided in 7 hours post-catheter removal) and then for Client B (who reports suprapubic fullness). Only one bladder scanner is available on the unit.

## CLINICAL COURSE

### Stage 1 — 0700 to 0900: Shift organization and first rounds.
The nurse plans the order of room visits for morning assessments, medication administration, and the bladder scan. At 0730, while the nurse is assessing Client A (wearing gown and gloves, having performed hand hygiene with soap and water before donning), the nursing student steps into Room 12 wearing gloves but no gown. The student picks up the dedicated blood-pressure cuff, takes Client A's blood pressure, then removes her gloves inside the room and touches the door handle with bare, unwashed hands. Observing this in real time, the nurse immediately stops the student before she exits the room, directs her to wash her hands with soap and water, and arranges for the door handle to be disinfected.

At 0745, the nurse needs to use the portable bladder scanner on Client A. After scanning Client A (post-void residual: 410 mL — the provider is notified and orders straight catheterization), the nurse must decide how to handle the bladder scanner before it goes to Client B's room.

Client C's daughter is in Room 16 when the nurse arrives at 0800. She is sitting at the bedside in street clothes, no gown or gloves, and has been helping her father shift position. She tells the nurse, "I've been coming every day — nobody told me I needed to put anything on." The wound dressing is saturated and needs to be changed.

### Stage 2 — 0900 to 1200: Escalating complexity.
At 0915, Client D's mother approaches the nurse at the nurses' station and asks, "My daughter is feeling cooped up. Can I take her outside in a wheelchair for just ten minutes? She says the fresh air will help her cough." The mother is not wearing a mask.

At 0945, the nurse enters Client C's room to perform the wound dressing change. While setting up supplies, the nurse notices that the dedicated stethoscope from Room 16 (Client C, MRSA contact precautions) is sitting on the counter of the clean utility room across the hall. A housekeeper reports she saw another staff member carry it out of Room 16 about 20 minutes ago and set it down next to the clean supply cart.

At 1030, Client A reports a new episode of large-volume watery stool. The nurse, already gowned and gloved in Room 12, assists with perineal care and linen change. The soiled linens are bundled into a standard linen bag (not a water-soluble inner bag). The nursing student, observing from the doorway, asks whether that matters.

At 1100, Client B's wife arrives for her daily visit. She stops at Room 12's open doorway, sees the contact-precautions sign, and asks the nurse (who is exiting Room 12 after doffing PPE), "My husband is right next door — should I be worried about catching something?" She appears anxious.

## KEY DECISION POINTS

1. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse observes the nursing student entering Client A's room (CDI, contact precautions) wearing gloves but no gown, then removing gloves inside the room and touching the door handle with bare, unwashed hands. Among all the actions the student performed, identify which specific behaviors are breaches of contact-precautions protocol.
   - **Correct action:** Three distinct breaches occurred — entering the room without a gown (contact precautions require gown and gloves), failing to perform hand hygiene immediately after glove removal, and touching the door handle with contaminated bare hands. Using the dedicated blood-pressure cuff was not a breach — the cuff is dedicated to that room and its use is appropriate.
   - **Why it is correct:** Contact precautions require both gown and gloves to be donned before entry to prevent contamination of clothing and skin. Hand hygiene immediately after PPE removal is the critical step that interrupts transmission; *C. difficile* spores are not eliminated by ABHR alone, so soap and water is required in this room, making the omission doubly significant. Touching the door handle with unwashed hands after caring for a CDI patient creates a fomite that can transmit spores to every subsequent person who touches that handle.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Recognize cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

2. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse must use the portable bladder scanner on Client A (CDI, contact precautions) and then on Client B (standard precautions). Determine the correct procedure for handling the shared equipment between the two clients.
   - **Correct action:** After use on Client A, the nurse cleans any visible soil from the bladder scanner if present, then disinfects the probe, cord, screen/controls, handle, and all touched surfaces with an EPA-registered sporicidal disinfectant effective against *C. difficile* spores, allowing the product label's wet-contact time to elapse before bringing the scanner into Client B's room. The nurse doffs PPE inside Room 12 per protocol, performs hand hygiene with soap and water (required for CDI), and then handles the cleaned scanner with clean hands to transport it.
   - **Why it is correct:** *C. difficile* spores survive on environmental surfaces for months and are resistant to alcohol-based cleaners. The unit policy requires either dedicated or properly disinfected equipment; since only one bladder scanner is available, cleaning and then disinfecting the device with an EPA-registered sporicidal disinfectant effective against *C. difficile* spores (List K) and allowing full wet-contact time is the only option to prevent transmission of hardy spores. Skipping the contact time or wiping the device with ABHR alone would leave viable spores on the scanner. The sequence — clean and disinfect the device while still in PPE, doff PPE, wash hands with soap and water, then transport the clean device — prevents recontamination.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Generate solutions.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

3. **Situation:** At Stage 1, Client C's daughter has been entering and exiting Room 16 (MRSA, contact precautions) without gown or gloves and has been repositioning her father, making direct contact with him and the bed linens near the draining wound. The nurse must address this. Determine the correct nursing action.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse provides nonjudgmental, clear teaching to the daughter: explains that her father's wound contains a resistant bacterium that can spread by direct contact, that a gown and gloves are required each time she enters the room, demonstrates donning and doffing, shows her where the PPE supply is located outside the door, and asks her to perform hand hygiene with ABHR (acceptable here — MRSA is not a spore-former) after doffing. The nurse validates the daughter's caregiving efforts before correcting the practice, acknowledging that she was not previously informed.
   - **Why it is correct:** Visitors are a recognized vector for MRSA transmission between rooms. The unit policy requires the nurse to orient visitors to precautions at the first visit of each shift. The daughter's lack of PPE during direct contact with a draining MRSA wound and contaminated linens creates a high risk of carrying MRSA on her clothing and hands to common areas and potentially to other clients' environments. A nonjudgmental approach preserves the family's trust and promotes adherence; blaming the daughter for a system failure (no one taught her) is neither accurate nor effective.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Take action.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

4. **Situation:** At Stage 3 (1230), the charge nurse asks the nurse to evaluate the shift's infection-control breaches retrospectively. The nurse must prioritize which of the observed breaches (the student's bare-hand contact in Room 12 at 0730, the soiled-linen bagging error at 1030, or the removal of Client C's dedicated stethoscope to the clean utility room discovered at 0945) posed the greatest risk for cross-contamination to other clients. Prioritize the breaches.
   - **Correct action:** The contaminated stethoscope in the clean utility room poses the greatest cross-contamination risk.
   - **Why it is correct:** A dedicated stethoscope from a room with a client who has a draining MRSA wound is potentially contaminated from contact with the client or their environment. Placing it in the clean utility room — the area where supplies are distributed to all clients on the unit — creates a direct pathway for MRSA to reach multiple clients via any staff member who touches the stethoscope or the surfaces it contacted, and via any supplies stored nearby. The door-handle contamination in Room 12 is a serious breach but is limited to one surface in one doorway; the linen-bagging error is a process deviation that primarily affects laundry-handling staff. The stethoscope breach has the widest radius of potential exposure and the most direct contact pathway to vulnerable clients.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Prioritize hypotheses.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

5. **Situation:** At Stage 2, Client D's mother asks to take her daughter (active pulmonary TB, AFB smear-positive) outside in a wheelchair for fresh air. Determine the correct response.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse explains that while the patient's AFB smears remain positive, she must stay in the negative-pressure AIIR to prevent airborne transmission of TB to other clients, staff, and visitors. The patient cannot leave the room for a non-essential trip. If medically necessary transport were required, the patient would wear a surgical mask and be transported via the most direct route with advance notification to receiving areas. The nurse acknowledges the mother's concern for her daughter's well-being, validates the difficulty of isolation, and offers alternatives within the room (opening the window blinds, facilitating video calls, arranging diversional activities). The nurse also teaches the mother that she should follow the facility's visitor policy for airborne precautions, offering/arranging instruction and an N95 respirator if available, and explains that a surgical mask does not reliably protect the visitor from inhaling airborne TB droplet nuclei inside the room, but is instead used on the patient only during medically necessary transport outside the AIIR.
   - **Why it is correct:** Airborne precautions for smear-positive pulmonary TB require the patient to remain in a functioning AIIR until discontinuation criteria are met. A common CDC-based framework requires three consecutive negative AFB sputum smears collected 8–24 hours apart (with at least one early-morning specimen), along with adequate effective therapy and clinical improvement, before release from airborne precautions. Removing the patient from the AIIR while smear-positive would expose everyone in the corridor, elevator, and outdoor area to airborne *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* droplet nuclei. The mother's request is understandable and the nurse's response must be compassionate and educational, not dismissive. Educating the mother on exposure risk and offering/arranging appropriate visitor respiratory protection (such as an N95 respirator with instruction) is consistent with CDC airborne precautions and unit visitor policy.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Analyze cues (the nurse must interpret the AFB smear status, the AIIR requirement, and the mother's request in context to determine what is and is not permissible).
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2 (the Stage 2 data confirms the AFB smear remains positive and the AIIR is functioning, which are the facts needed to answer definitively).

6. **Situation:** At Stage 3, the charge nurse asks the nurse to evaluate whether the infection-control interventions implemented during the shift were effective.
   - **Correct action:** Effective outcomes include: Client C's daughter demonstrated correct donning and doffing of gown and gloves and verbalized understanding (behavioral change confirmed); Client D's mother verbalized understanding of AIIR requirements and was observed donning an N95 respirator before re-entering the room (behavioral change confirmed); the contaminated stethoscope was retrieved, the clean-utility counter/supply-cart surfaces it contacted were disinfected, nearby potentially contaminated supplies were handled or removed per policy, and the stethoscope was disinfected with full wet-contact time before being returned to Room 16 (environmental breach corrected); the bladder scanner was properly disinfected between Client A and Client B with no evidence of cross-contamination. Ongoing risks include: Client A's CDI diarrhea continues at the same frequency, so contact precautions and vigilant hand hygiene with soap and water remain essential with every encounter; Client D remains AFB smear-positive, so airborne precautions and AIIR confinement must continue with no exceptions for non-essential transport; the nursing student's practice errors require follow-up education and supervised return demonstration before independent client contact in isolation rooms.
   - **Why it is correct:** Effectiveness of infection-control interventions is measured by observable behavioral compliance (visitor and staff PPE use, hand hygiene technique), environmental cleanliness (equipment in the correct location, properly disinfected), and absence of new transmission events. Identifying ongoing risks is equally important — declaring success prematurely (for example, assuming the stethoscope retrieval alone resolved the MRSA exposure without assessing whether any supplies in the clean utility room were contaminated) would be an incomplete evaluation.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Evaluate outcomes.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

## COMMON NURSING ERRORS

- Identifying the student's use of the dedicated blood-pressure cuff as a breach — it is not; the cuff is dedicated to Room 12 and its use is correct practice. This error reflects a misunderstanding of what "dedicated equipment" means and would cause the learner to miss the actual breaches (no gown, no hand hygiene, contaminated door handle).
- Focusing on the student's failure to use ABHR after glove removal while overlooking that soap and water — not ABHR — is required for CDI. The student failed to perform any hand hygiene at all, but even if she had used ABHR, it would have been the wrong method for a *C. difficile* room. A learner who corrects only the omission without specifying the correct agent misses the CDI-specific requirement.
- Wiping the bladder scanner with an alcohol-based wipe, generic non-sporicidal disinfectant, or ABHR, or failing to follow the label's wet-contact time. Alcohol is ineffective against *C. difficile* spores; only an EPA-registered sporicidal disinfectant (such as List K products) with full contact time reliably kills spores. This error stems from treating all disinfection as a quick wipe-and-go step.
- Telling the daughter she should not touch her father or help reposition him — this is unnecessarily restrictive and undermines the family's role in care. The correct action is to teach her to do so safely with appropriate PPE, not to prohibit family involvement. Alternatively, scolding the daughter for not gowning when no staff member had previously taught her is blaming the visitor for a system gap.
- Ranking the soiled-linen bagging error as the highest-risk breach because it involves CDI spores. While CDI spores are hardy, the linen-bag error primarily affects downstream laundry handlers (who already use standard precautions) and does not create a direct transmission pathway to other clients on the unit. The stethoscope in the clean utility room is a direct fomite pathway to multiple clients and is the higher-priority breach.
- Ranking the door-handle contamination above the stethoscope breach. The door handle is a single surface in one location and can be addressed with a single disinfection; the stethoscope in the clean utility room may have contacted multiple surfaces and supplies, creating a wider contamination radius.
- Telling the mother that the patient can go outside briefly if she wears a surgical mask — this is incorrect. A surgical mask on the patient reduces but does not eliminate droplet nuclei dispersal, and removing a smear-positive TB patient from the AIIR for a non-essential trip violates airborne precautions regardless of masking. The AIIR's negative pressure is the engineering control that contains airborne particles; a mask is a supplement for necessary transport, not a substitute for the AIIR.
- Telling the mother she does not need a mask inside the AIIR because the negative pressure protects her (negative pressure prevents room air from escaping into the corridor but does not protect anyone inside the room from inhaling droplet nuclei), or teaching the mother to wear a surgical mask inside the room as a reliable means of visitor protection. A surgical mask does not filter out airborne TB droplet nuclei; visitors should be offered and instructed on N95 respirators or appropriate respirator protection per facility visitor policy.
- Declaring infection-control interventions fully effective because no new infections were identified during the shift. The absence of new infections within a single 8-hour shift is not a reliable indicator of effectiveness — transmission events from today's breaches (especially MRSA colonization) may not manifest for days. Effectiveness at this point is measured by process indicators (corrected behaviors, cleaned equipment, sustained compliance) rather than outcome indicators (new infection rates), which require longer surveillance.

## EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

- Select appropriate PPE (type and combination) based on the transmission-based precaution category and the specific organism involved, distinguishing contact, airborne, and standard precautions.
- Recognize specific infection-control breaches in real time — including PPE omissions, hand-hygiene method errors, equipment misplacement, and linen-handling deviations — and differentiate true breaches from correct practices.
- Prioritize among multiple simultaneous infection-control breaches by evaluating the scope, directness, and severity of each breach's transmission risk to other clients.
- Sequence the handling of shared equipment between clients on different precaution levels, including the correct disinfection method, wet-contact time, and hand-hygiene technique for spore-forming organisms.
- Teach visitors about isolation precautions using a nonjudgmental, educational approach that preserves family involvement in care while ensuring compliance.

## BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the contaminated stethoscope has been discovered in the clean utility room, the student's breaches in Room 12 have been observed, the daughter's unprotected visits to Room 16 have been identified, and the mother's request to remove the TB patient from the AIIR has been received — the full scope of the shift's infection-control challenges is now visible.

- **Most likely condition:** Active cross-contamination risk from a contaminated dedicated device (MRSA-positive stethoscope) introduced into the clean utility room, creating a direct fomite pathway to multiple clients and supply lines on the unit.
- **Plausible competing conditions:**
  1. *The CDI door-handle contamination from the nursing student's bare-hand contact* — plausible because CDI spores are exceptionally hardy and the door handle is a high-touch surface, but wrong as the highest-priority breach here because the exposure is limited to one surface in one doorway and can be resolved with a single targeted disinfection, whereas the stethoscope breach has already potentially contaminated a shared supply area serving the entire unit.
  2. *The soiled-linen bagging error in Room 12* — plausible because CDI-contaminated linen improperly bagged could expose laundry handlers, but wrong as the priority because the exposure is downstream (laundry department) rather than an immediate lateral transmission risk to other clients on the unit, and laundry handlers already use standard precautions as a baseline.
- **Two priority actions:**
  1. Immediately remove the stethoscope from the clean utility room, disinfect the contacted clean-utility surfaces, assess/remove nearby potentially contaminated supplies per policy, disinfect the stethoscope with full wet-contact time, and return it to Room 16.
  2. Provide immediate corrective education to the staff member who removed the dedicated stethoscope from the contact-precautions room, and notify the charge nurse so the breach can be documented in the unit's infection-control event log for surveillance follow-up.
- **Two wrong actions:**
  1. Disinfecting only the stethoscope itself and returning it to Room 16 without cleaning the counter and assessing nearby supplies — this leaves residual MRSA contamination on the clean utility room surfaces, which defeats the purpose of the retrieval.
  2. Waiting until the end of the shift to address the stethoscope breach because the wound dressing change in Room 16 is due and seems more urgent — the dressing change is a scheduled task that can wait minutes, while the contaminated stethoscope in the clean utility room is an active, expanding exposure that worsens with every minute it remains there and every person who enters that room.
- **Two parameters to monitor:**
  1. Visitor and staff PPE compliance on subsequent room entries for the remainder of the shift — direct observation of correct donning and doffing confirms that the teaching interventions (to the daughter, the mother, and the nursing student) produced sustained behavioral change, not just a single correct demonstration.
  2. Equipment location and cleanliness at each room entry — verifying that dedicated devices are in their assigned rooms and that shared devices are properly disinfected between uses confirms that the system breach has been corrected and is not recurring.
- **Two irrelevant parameters:**
  1. Client B's sputum culture result (*S. pneumoniae*) — while clinically relevant to Client B's pneumonia treatment, it does not inform the infection-control evaluation because Client B is on standard precautions and *S. pneumoniae* community-acquired pneumonia does not require transmission-based precautions; tracking this culture result does not help evaluate whether cross-contamination has been prevented.
  2. Client C's vancomycin monitoring results — this is a pharmacotherapy parameter managed by pharmacy per facility protocol, not a measure of cross-contamination control; tracking drug levels or adjustments does not measure whether the stethoscope breach has been contained.
