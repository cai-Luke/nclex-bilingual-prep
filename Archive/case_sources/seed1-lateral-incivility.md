**CASE TITLE**
Lateral incivility causing a client-safety communication breakdown on a medical-surgical unit.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Mrs. Dolores Vega is a 71-year-old woman admitted two days ago for hypertensive urgency (BP 218/114 on arrival to the emergency department) superimposed on a history of chronic kidney disease stage 3b (baseline creatinine 1.5 mg/dL, eGFR 37 mL/min), heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, and type 2 diabetes. She was stabilized in the ED with IV hydralazine and transitioned to oral antihypertensives. Her current medication orders, updated by the hospitalist at 1400 today, include amlodipine 10 mg PO daily (increased from 5 mg this morning after a blood pressure trend of 168–174/92–96 over the preceding 24 hours), lisinopril 20 mg PO daily (unchanged), metformin 500 mg PO twice daily (held per pharmacy renal-dose flag pending repeat labs), and sliding-scale insulin per protocol. Telemetry monitoring is in place. The plan is to observe her blood pressure response to the amlodipine dose increase over the next 24 hours before considering discharge.

The unit is a 32-bed medical-surgical floor. The charge nurse today is Marcus Chen, RN, BSN, who has been on the unit for six years. The day-shift primary nurse for Mrs. Vega is Renee Talbot, RN, a senior nurse with twelve years of experience on this unit. The oncoming night-shift nurse is Priya Anand, RN, who completed orientation and received her license four months ago. Marcus has noticed increasing tension between Renee and Priya over the past several weeks — brief, clipped exchanges, Priya appearing flustered after interactions with Renee, and Renee making comments in the break room about "new grads who slow everyone down."

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At 1915, Marcus is rounding near the nurses' station when the bedside handoff between Renee and Priya for Mrs. Vega takes place. Marcus can hear and observe the exchange from the doorway. The handoff proceeds as follows:

Renee speaks rapidly, arms crossed, and does not make eye contact with Priya or the patient. She states: "Vega, room 312, hypertension, CKD, the usual. Vitals have been fine. Meds are on the MAR — you can read." When Priya asks whether there were any medication changes today, Renee rolls her eyes visibly — Mrs. Vega notices and looks down at her blanket — and says, "I just told you, it's on the MAR. I'm not going to spoon-feed you." Renee then turns and walks out of the room without waiting for questions. She does not mention the amlodipine dose increase from 5 mg to 10 mg that was ordered at 1400 and that she administered at 1700, nor does she mention the hospitalist's instruction to monitor the blood pressure response closely over the next 24 hours. In the hallway, as Renee passes Marcus, she says loudly enough for nearby staff to hear, "Good luck tonight — she'll be calling you every five minutes to hold her hand."

Priya, visibly shaken, does not pursue Renee for clarification. She reviews the MAR but, unfamiliar with Mrs. Vega's baseline regimen, does not notice that the amlodipine dose was changed today. She proceeds with her other assessments.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Mrs. Vega's baseline assessment at the start of night shift (1930), performed by Priya:
T 36.8 °C, HR 78 and regular, BP 152/88 (left arm, sitting), RR 16, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Alert and oriented ×4. Heart sounds: regular rate and rhythm, S1/S2 present, no S3 or murmur appreciated. Lungs: clear bilaterally, no crackles. Abdomen soft, non-tender, normoactive bowel sounds. Lower extremities: trace bilateral pedal edema (unchanged from admission). Skin warm and dry. Patient is calm but quiet; when Priya asks how she is feeling, Mrs. Vega says, "I'm fine, dear," and does not volunteer further information. Telemetry shows normal sinus rhythm without ectopy. Pain 0/10. Voiding without difficulty.

The 1700 amlodipine 10 mg dose is documented as administered in the MAR. The next scheduled amlodipine dose is at 0900 tomorrow. Lisinopril 20 mg was given at 0900 today; next dose is at 0900 tomorrow. No PRN antihypertensive is currently ordered.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Labs drawn at 0600 today: sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.4 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, CO₂ 22 mEq/L, BUN 34 mg/dL, creatinine 1.6 mg/dL (up from 1.5 at admission), eGFR 34 mL/min, glucose 148 mg/dL, calcium 8.8 mg/dL, magnesium 1.9 mg/dL, phosphorus 4.2 mg/dL. CBC: WBC 7,200/µL, Hgb 11.4 g/dL, platelets 198,000/µL. BNP 320 pg/mL (mildly elevated, stable from admission value of 340). Urinalysis: no protein, no blood, specific gravity 1.018.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 1915–2000, the handoff and its immediate aftermath.

The events described in the Initial Presentation have just occurred. Marcus witnessed the entire handoff exchange from the doorway. He observed: Renee's crossed arms and lack of eye contact during report; the eye-roll directed at Priya's question about medication changes, which Mrs. Vega witnessed; the dismissive verbal responses ("you can read," "spoon-feed"); the failure to verbally communicate the amlodipine dose change and the monitoring instruction; the departure without opportunity for questions; and the demeaning hallway comment about Priya. Marcus also notes that Priya did not advocate for a complete handoff — she accepted the incomplete report and moved on.

**Stage 2** — 2200, the care gap reaches the client.

Priya has been taking vital signs on her routine q8h schedule. At 2200 she obtains Mrs. Vega's vitals: T 36.9 °C, HR 92, BP 186/102, RR 18, SpO₂ 96%. Mrs. Vega reports a mild headache, rated 3/10, that started "a little while ago." She is alert and oriented ×4, no visual changes, no chest pain, no shortness of breath. Telemetry shows sinus tachycardia at 92, no ST changes.

Priya is concerned about the blood pressure elevation but is unsure of the context. She looks at the MAR again and this time notices the amlodipine dose change. She reviews the hospitalist's 1400 progress note, which explicitly states: "Monitor BP q4h × 24 hours to assess response to dose increase; call if SBP > 180 or < 110, or if symptomatic hypotension." She realizes that the intensified monitoring was never communicated and the care plan still reflects routine q8h vitals. Because she was unaware of the new order, the 1800 q4h check was missed, and this 2200 check is the first recognized abnormal value. She also realizes there is no PRN antihypertensive order to address the current spike.

Priya feels anxious about calling the hospitalist at this hour and is uncertain whether the blood pressure warrants a call. She approaches Marcus at the nurses' station and says, "Mrs. Vega's BP is 186/102 and she has a headache. I just found out the amlodipine was increased today and I was supposed to be checking her pressure every four hours. The 1800 check was missed. I don't know what to do."

Marcus verifies the provider parameter in the chart. The hospitalist's note specifies to call if SBP > 180. Mrs. Vega's SBP is 186. The call threshold has been crossed.

**Stage 3** — 2215–0200, intervention and resolution.

Marcus instructs Priya to notify the hospitalist now, per the stated call parameters, and to report the current blood pressure, the headache, and the missed monitoring intervals. Priya calls the hospitalist, who orders hydralazine 10 mg IV × 1 now, followed by a repeat blood pressure in 30 minutes, and reiterates q4h blood pressure monitoring. The hospitalist also asks to be called back if SBP remains above 170 after the hydralazine.

Priya administers hydralazine 10 mg IV at 2230. At 2300, Mrs. Vega's vitals: HR 80, BP 162/90, RR 16, SpO₂ 97%. Headache has resolved. At 0200, BP 154/86, HR 76, no symptoms. Priya documents the q4h checks and updates the care plan to reflect the current amlodipine dose and the monitoring schedule.

Meanwhile, Marcus addresses the interpersonal and systems issues. At 2315, after the patient is stabilized, he speaks with Renee by phone (Renee is now off shift). He describes the specific behaviors he observed — the dismissive language, the eye-roll in front of the patient, the hallway comment, and the failure to communicate the medication change and monitoring order — and their impact: a missed dose change, missed monitoring, and a blood pressure spike that exceeded the ordered call parameter. He does not characterize Renee's personality or take sides; he focuses on the behaviors and the outcome. Renee is initially defensive ("She should have read the chart — that's basic nursing"), but Marcus restates that verbal communication of significant changes during handoff is a unit expectation and a patient-safety standard, and that the behaviors he observed do not meet professional conduct expectations. He informs Renee that he will document the event per the unit's civility and professional-conduct reporting pathway and that a follow-up meeting with the nurse manager will be scheduled.

Marcus also speaks privately with Priya at 2330. He acknowledges that the handoff she received was inadequate and that she was placed in a difficult position. He also addresses Priya's responsibility: when a handoff feels incomplete, the receiving nurse must advocate for clarification — ask direct questions, request a structured SBAR summary, or escalate to the charge nurse before the outgoing nurse leaves the unit. He reviews the importance of checking for recent order changes at the start of every shift, particularly for the admitting diagnosis. He does not blame Priya for the care gap but ensures she understands her role in preventing recurrence.

Marcus ensures documentation boundaries are clearly maintained. Priya documents objective clinical facts in the patient chart: the blood pressure, headache, provider notification, order received, hydralazine given, response, and monitoring plan. Marcus completes a unit incident/safety report detailing the communication failure and the missed monitoring that led to the unmanaged blood pressure spike. Separately, Marcus documents the specific incivility details he witnessed and the conversations he held through the facility's professional-conduct reporting pathway, notifying the nurse manager by secure message for formal follow-up.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, during and immediately after the bedside handoff, Marcus must identify which specific behaviors and omissions in the exchange between Renee and Priya are significant cues requiring intervention. The handoff includes Renee's rapid delivery with crossed arms and no eye contact; the statement "Meds are on the MAR — you can read"; the visible eye-roll when Priya asks about medication changes, witnessed by Mrs. Vega; the statement "I'm not going to spoon-feed you"; departure without allowing questions; the hallway comment "she'll be calling you every five minutes to hold her hand"; the omission of the amlodipine dose change; and the omission of the hospitalist's q4h monitoring instruction. Priya's quiet acceptance of the incomplete report is also present in the scene.
*Correct action:* The significant cues are: the dismissive verbal statements directed at Priya ("you can read," "spoon-feed," "hold her hand"), the eye-roll performed in front of the patient, the failure to verbally communicate the amlodipine dose increase and the q4h monitoring order, and the departure without opportunity for questions. Priya's failure to advocate for a complete handoff is also a relevant cue, though it is a different category of concern (a new nurse's response to intimidation, not incivility itself). Renee's crossed arms and rapid speech, while contributing to the overall tone, are less specific as isolated cues than the verbal content and the concrete omissions.
*Why it is correct:* The dismissive language, the eye-roll in front of a patient, and the hallway comment are textbook manifestations of lateral incivility — they demean a colleague, undermine professional communication, and in this case directly contributed to a care omission. The failure to communicate a same-day medication change and a specific monitoring order during handoff is not merely rude; it is a patient-safety failure. Recognizing these as a connected pattern — incivility producing a communication breakdown that reaches the client — is the first step in the charge nurse's clinical judgment.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues.
*Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, Marcus must distinguish which of the observed behaviors represent lateral incivility (horizontal violence) versus behaviors that, while perhaps brusque, fall within the range of assertive or acceptable professional communication. The behaviors to classify include: (a) "Meds are on the MAR — you can read"; (b) the visible eye-roll in front of the patient when asked a clarifying question; (c) the hallway comment "she'll be calling you every five minutes to hold her hand"; (d) the failure to verbally communicate the amlodipine dose change and monitoring order; (e) speaking rapidly during handoff; (f) a hypothetical alternative in which a senior nurse says to a new nurse privately, "I need you to check the MAR before handoff so we can focus on changes — let's make that our routine."
*Correct action:* Behaviors (a), (b), and (c) are lateral incivility: they are dismissive, demeaning, and intended to belittle a colleague. Behavior (d) — the omission of critical information — is a patient-safety failure; whether it was deliberate withholding (an extension of the incivility) or carelessness, it is a serious professional lapse, and in the context of the other behaviors it is consistent with a pattern of disregard. Behavior (e) — speaking rapidly — is not, by itself, incivility; it may reflect time pressure and is not inherently demeaning. Behavior (f) — the private, constructive redirect — is assertive professional communication: it sets an expectation, is respectful, and is directed at improving practice.
*Why it is correct:* Lateral incivility is defined by behaviors that demean, disrespect, or undermine a colleague — it is about the intent and impact of the communication, not merely its brevity or directness. Assertive communication can be direct and even firm without being demeaning; the distinction lies in whether the message respects the colleague's professional standing and is aimed at improving care versus humiliating or dismissing the person. Classifying these correctly prevents the charge nurse from either over-reacting to normal directness or under-reacting to genuine incivility.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues.
*Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, when Priya reports to Marcus that Mrs. Vega's BP is 186/102 with a headache, that the amlodipine dose change was not communicated, and that the 1800 q4h blood pressure check was missed, Marcus must determine the priority problem. The competing framings are: (a) the interpersonal conflict between Renee and Priya is the primary issue and must be resolved before anything else; (b) the client-safety communication breakdown — the missed medication-change communication, the missed monitoring, and the current blood pressure spike exceeding the call threshold — is the priority problem; (c) Priya's inexperience and failure to independently identify the dose change is the primary issue.
*Correct action:* The priority problem is the client-safety communication breakdown. Mrs. Vega's SBP of 186 exceeds the hospitalist's stated call threshold of 180, she is symptomatic with a headache, and two monitoring intervals have been missed. The interpersonal conflict is the root cause and must be addressed, but the immediate priority is closing the active care gap — ensuring the provider is notified and the patient receives appropriate intervention now.
*Why it is correct:* Client safety always anchors the priority. The incivility is serious and must be addressed, but it is the upstream cause; the downstream effect — a patient whose blood pressure is unmonitored and now above the call threshold with symptoms — is the immediate clinical danger. Addressing the conflict first while the patient's blood pressure remains unmanaged would be a misplaced priority. Similarly, focusing on Priya's inexperience, while a legitimate teaching point, does not address the active clinical need.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses.
*Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, Marcus must determine the full set of appropriate charge-nurse actions to address both the immediate care gap and the underlying incivility. The candidate actions include: (A) instruct Priya to notify the hospitalist now, per the stated call parameters, reporting the current BP, the headache, and the missed monitoring; (B) speak with Renee privately (by phone, since she is off shift) to describe the specific behaviors observed and their impact on patient care, focusing on the behaviors and the outcome, not on personality; (C) speak with Priya privately to acknowledge the inadequate handoff she received and to coach her on her responsibility to advocate for a complete report and to check for recent order changes; (D) document the event per the unit's civility and professional-conduct policy and complete an incident report for the communication failure and missed monitoring; (E) notify the nurse manager for follow-up; (F) publicly reprimand Renee at the next staff meeting to set an example; (G) tell Priya that Renee "is just like that" and to ignore it in the future; (H) decide not to document the incident because Renee is a senior nurse with a good clinical record and the BP spike was caught before serious harm occurred.
*Correct action:* Actions A, B, C, D, and E are all appropriate components of the charge nurse's response. Action A addresses the immediate care gap. Actions B and C address the conduct and the contributing factors through private, behavior-focused conversations. Actions D and E ensure organizational follow-through and accountability. Actions F, G, and H are inappropriate: a public reprimand is itself unprofessional and escalates conflict; dismissing the behavior normalizes incivility and abandons the new nurse; and failing to document removes accountability and allows the pattern to continue.
*Why it is correct:* Structured conflict resolution requires addressing the immediate safety concern first, then intervening with each party privately, focusing on observable behaviors and their impact rather than on character, and documenting per policy to create accountability and a record for the nurse manager. The charge nurse's role is not to punish or to smooth over — it is to protect the client, reset professional expectations, and ensure the system responds.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions.
*Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, Marcus must choose the immediate first action from among the appropriate interventions identified above. The care gap is active: Mrs. Vega's SBP is 186, above the call threshold, she has a headache, and no PRN antihypertensive is ordered.
*Correct action:* The immediate first action is to instruct Priya to notify the hospitalist now, per the stated call parameters, and to report the current blood pressure, the headache, and the context of the missed monitoring. Closing the active care gap takes precedence over the conduct conversations, the documentation, and the manager notification — all of which are necessary but not time-critical in the way the patient's unmanaged blood pressure spike is.
*Why it is correct:* When a client-safety issue and a professional-conduct issue coexist, the safety issue is addressed first. The hospitalist's note explicitly states to call if SBP exceeds 180; the threshold has been crossed and the patient is symptomatic. Delaying the call to address the interpersonal conflict, to complete documentation, or to debrief with Priya would leave the patient without intervention during a period of risk. Once the call is made and the order is obtained, Marcus can turn to the conduct and systems issues.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action.
*Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after the immediate interventions — the hospitalist notification, the hydralazine administration, the private conversations with both nurses, the incident report, and the manager notification — Marcus must evaluate whether the interventions have been effective. He is looking for evidence that both the care gap and the communication breakdown have been resolved.
*Correct action:* Effective resolution is demonstrated by: Mrs. Vega's blood pressure trending downward (186/102 → 162/90 → 154/86) with resolution of her headache, confirming the clinical intervention worked; Priya performing and documenting q4h blood pressure checks on schedule for the remainder of the shift, confirming the monitoring order is now being followed; the nursing care plan updated to reflect the current amlodipine dose and the q4h monitoring schedule; and Priya demonstrating, in subsequent handoffs, the ability to ask clarifying questions and verify recent order changes. Longer-term markers — which Marcus would assess over the coming shifts — include complete and professional handoffs between Renee and Priya (and all staff), no further care omissions attributable to communication failures, and documentation that the nurse manager follow-up meeting occurred.
*Why it is correct:* Evaluation of outcomes must address both dimensions of the problem. The clinical outcome (blood pressure response, symptom resolution, adherence to the monitoring schedule) confirms the care gap is closed. The process outcomes (updated care plan, Priya's changed handoff behavior, absence of further omissions) confirm the communication breakdown is being corrected. A single improved blood pressure reading without evidence that the systemic communication failure has been addressed would be incomplete evaluation — the root cause would remain unresolved and the pattern would likely recur.
*Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes.
*Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Focusing only on Renee's tone or body language (crossed arms, rapid speech) while overlooking the concrete omissions — the failure to communicate the amlodipine dose change and the q4h monitoring order. The omissions are the cues with direct patient-safety consequences; the tone is concerning but the information gap is what reaches the client. Conversely, dismissing the eye-roll and dismissive language as "just personality" and focusing only on the omission misses the pattern of incivility that caused the omission.

- *(Decision point 2)* Classifying all of Renee's behaviors as equally problematic, including the rapid speech, which conflates normal time-pressure communication with incivility. This over-classification weakens the charge nurse's intervention by diluting the focus. Alternatively, classifying the hallway comment ("hold her hand") as harmless venting rather than incivility — it was made publicly, within earshot of other staff, and it demeans a colleague; it is not private frustration.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing the interpersonal conflict as the primary problem and spending time mediating between Renee and Priya before addressing Mrs. Vega's blood pressure. The conflict is the root cause and must be addressed, but the patient's SBP is above the stated call threshold and she is symptomatic — the active clinical danger takes precedence. Another common error is framing Priya's inexperience as the primary problem ("she should have caught the dose change on her own"), which shifts accountability away from the communication failure and the incivility that produced it.

- *(Decision point 4)* Choosing to publicly reprimand Renee at a staff meeting, believing that a visible consequence will deter future incivility. Public reprimand is itself a form of incivility, violates the principle of addressing conduct privately, and is likely to escalate the conflict rather than resolve it. Another error is deciding not to document the incident because "no real harm came to the patient" — the blood pressure spike exceeded the ordered call parameters and the 1800 monitoring interval was missed; the event meets the threshold for incident reporting, and failure to document allows the pattern to continue without organizational awareness.

- *(Decision point 5)* Choosing to speak with Renee first (by phone) before instructing Priya to call the hospitalist, reasoning that understanding Renee's perspective will help manage the situation. This delays closure of the active care gap. The conduct conversation is important but not time-sensitive in the way the unmanaged blood pressure spike is. Another error is Marcus deciding to call the hospitalist himself rather than coaching Priya through the call — while well-intentioned, this removes a learning opportunity and does not build Priya's competence in provider communication; the charge nurse's role is to ensure the call happens and to support the primary nurse in making it, not to take over the primary nurse's responsibility.

- *(Decision point 6)* Evaluating resolution based solely on Mrs. Vega's blood pressure returning to an acceptable range without confirming that the systemic issues have been addressed — the care plan updated, the monitoring schedule followed, and the communication pattern changed. A single improved vital sign does not demonstrate that the communication breakdown has been resolved; the root cause remains intact and the same failure can recur with the next patient. Another error is assuming that one conversation with Renee constitutes resolution of the incivility pattern — sustained behavioral change requires follow-up, documentation, and manager involvement, not a single corrective phone call.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the specific verbal and nonverbal behaviors that constitute lateral incivility (horizontal violence) in a clinical setting and distinguish them from assertive professional communication.
- Analyze how lateral incivility produces concrete patient-safety failures — not merely interpersonal discomfort — by tracing the path from a dismissive handoff to a missed medication change, missed monitoring, and an unmanaged blood pressure spike.
- Prioritize the active client-safety concern (the unmanaged blood pressure spike exceeding the call threshold) over the interpersonal conflict when both demand the charge nurse's attention simultaneously.
- Apply structured conflict-resolution principles within the charge nurse's scope: private behavior-focused conversations, documentation per policy, incident reporting, and manager notification — distinguishing these from inappropriate responses such as public reprimand, normalization of the behavior, or failure to document.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of the intervention across both dimensions — clinical outcome for the patient and process outcome for the communication system — recognizing that resolution of the vital-sign abnormality alone does not constitute resolution of the underlying problem.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after Priya has reported Mrs. Vega's BP of 186/102 with headache, the missed amlodipine dose-change communication, and the missed q4h monitoring — the full picture of a client-safety communication breakdown driven by lateral incivility has declared.

*Most likely condition:* Client-safety communication breakdown resulting from unresolved lateral incivility. The pattern is complete: a senior nurse's dismissive, demeaning handoff behavior directly caused the omission of a same-day antihypertensive dose change and a specific monitoring order, resulting in missed blood pressure checks and a symptomatic blood pressure spike above the provider's stated call threshold.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Isolated knowledge deficit in the new nurse — wrong here: while Priya did not independently catch the dose change, the root cause is not her inexperience but the deliberate withholding of information during a handoff that was truncated by incivility; a complete handoff would have communicated the change regardless of Priya's experience level. (2) Routine interpersonal friction requiring no formal intervention — wrong: the behaviors observed are not mere personality clashes; they include demeaning language in front of a patient, public disparagement of a colleague, and a concrete care omission; this pattern meets the definition of lateral incivility and has produced a patient-safety event that requires documentation and organizational follow-up.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Instruct Priya to notify the hospitalist immediately per the stated call parameters (SBP > 180), reporting the current blood pressure, the headache, and the missed monitoring intervals — closing the active care gap is the first priority. (2) Initiate structured conflict resolution by speaking privately with Renee (by phone) to describe the specific behaviors observed and their direct impact on patient care, focusing on behavior and outcome rather than personality, and informing her that the event will be documented per the unit's civility and professional-conduct policy.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Addressing the interpersonal conflict with Renee before instructing Priya to notify the hospitalist — this delays closure of the active care gap while the patient's blood pressure remains above the call threshold and she is symptomatic; client safety must precede conduct management. (2) Deciding not to document the incident or notify the nurse manager because the blood pressure spike was caught before serious harm occurred — this removes organizational accountability, allows the incivility pattern to continue, and fails to create the record needed for follow-up; the event involved a care omission where the blood pressure exceeded the call parameter and meets the threshold for incident reporting.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Completeness and professionalism of subsequent handoffs involving these nurses — specifically, whether medication changes and monitoring orders are verbally communicated, whether the receiving nurse has the opportunity to ask clarifying questions, and whether the exchange is conducted respectfully. This is the direct marker of whether the communication breakdown has been resolved. (2) Adherence to the q4h blood pressure monitoring schedule and absence of further care omissions for Mrs. Vega and other patients — this confirms that the specific safety failure has been corrected and that the systemic communication process is functioning.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Whether Renee and Priya become personally friendly or socialize during breaks — the goal of structured conflict resolution is professional, safe communication, not personal rapport; monitoring friendship confuses interpersonal warmth with professional accountability and is not the right measure of resolution. (2) Mrs. Vega's serum creatinine trend — while relevant to her overall medical management, the creatinine is not the parameter that evaluates whether the communication breakdown has been resolved; it tracks her chronic kidney disease, not the handoff failure that is the subject of this intervention.
