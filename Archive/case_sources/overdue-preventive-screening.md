# Overdue preventive screening and health literacy barriers in a middle-aged adult

## PATIENT BACKGROUND
Maria Delgado is a 52-year-old woman presenting to an outpatient primary care clinic for a new-patient visit. She has not seen a primary care provider in approximately six years. She works full-time as a hotel housekeeper on a rotating schedule that includes weekends, and she is the primary caregiver for her 78-year-old mother who lives with her. She has no significant past medical history on file. Her only current medication is ibuprofen, which she takes as needed for shoulder pain from work. She has no known drug allergies. She reports that she had a Pap smear "a long time ago, maybe ten years," and she has never had a colonoscopy or a mammogram. She is unsure of her vaccination history but thinks she had a tetanus shot after cutting her hand at work "about five or six years ago." She does not recall ever receiving a shingles vaccine. She has employer-sponsored health insurance but states she has a high deductible and is concerned about costs. She completed high school in the United States and speaks English fluently. She filled out the clinic's intake paperwork but left several sections blank, writing "not sure what this means" next to the family history section and the preventive-care questionnaire.

The clinic uses the following preventive-care protocol, which is posted in each exam room and available to clinical staff:

### Clinic Preventive-Care Protocol Used for This Visit
- **Cervical Cancer Screening:** For average-risk women aged 30–65, Pap with HPV co-testing every 5 years or Pap alone every 3 years.
- **Breast Cancer Screening:** Mammography every 1 to 2 years beginning at age 40, with shared decision-making about frequency.
- **Colorectal Cancer Screening:**
  - *Average Risk:* Screening begins at age 45. Options include annual fecal immunochemical test (FIT) or colonoscopy every 10 years.
  - *Increased Risk (First-degree relative diagnosed with colorectal cancer before age 60):* Colonoscopy screening is recommended beginning at age 40 or 10 years before the relative's age at diagnosis, whichever is earlier. Repeat colonoscopy every 5 years if the initial colonoscopy is normal. Stool-based tests (such as FIT) are not recommended as the first-line screening option for high-risk/increased-risk individuals.
- **Immunizations:**
  - *Recombinant Zoster Vaccine:* Two-dose series for adults aged 50 and older regardless of prior chickenpox history.
  - *Influenza Vaccine:* Administered annually in the fall.
  - *Tdap Vaccine:* Tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis booster every 10 years.

## INITIAL PRESENTATION
Maria arrives on time for her appointment. She is pleasant but visibly uncomfortable in the clinical setting, fidgeting with her purse strap and avoiding eye contact when the nurse begins the intake interview. When asked why she scheduled the visit, she says, "My daughter kept telling me I need to get checked. I feel fine, honestly. I don't know why I need all these tests when nothing is wrong." She adds, "The last time I went to a doctor they used a lot of words I didn't understand and I just nodded along. I don't want that again." She denies any current symptoms — no chest pain, shortness of breath, weight loss, bleeding, lumps, or changes in bowel habits. She reports occasional mild heartburn after spicy food and the chronic shoulder soreness. She sleeps about five to six hours per night due to her mother's nighttime needs. She drinks one to two glasses of wine on weekends and has never smoked. Her mother was diagnosed with colon cancer at age 58 and had surgery; Maria is unsure of the type or stage. Her father died of a heart attack at age 71. She has one older sister, age 56, who is healthy.

## ASSESSMENT FINDINGS
T 36.8 °C oral, HR 76 and regular, BP 124/78, RR 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Height 163 cm, weight 74 kg, BMI 27.8. She is alert, oriented, and cooperative. Skin is warm and dry with no lesions or rashes. Head and neck examination is unremarkable; thyroid is not enlarged. Heart sounds are regular without murmurs. Lungs are clear bilaterally. Abdomen is soft, non-tender, with active bowel sounds in all quadrants. No palpable masses. Musculoskeletal examination reveals mild tenderness over the right supraspinatus without swelling or limited range of motion. Breast examination is deferred pending mammography scheduling per clinic protocol. Neurological screening is grossly intact. She appears to read the clinic pamphlet slowly, holding it at arm's length, and sets it down after a few seconds.

## LABORATORY DATA
Point-of-care fingerstick glucose (non-fasting): 118 mg/dL. Urine dipstick: specific gravity 1.020, pH 6.0, no protein, no glucose, no blood, no leukocyte esterase, no nitrites. A lipid panel and fasting glucose are ordered for a return visit. No other labs are drawn today. The clinic's validated health-literacy screening tool (Newest Vital Sign) is administered: Maria scores 1 out of 6, consistent with a high likelihood of limited health literacy.

## CLINICAL COURSE

### Stage 1 — During the initial visit (0–45 minutes).
The nurse reviews Maria's intake forms and notes the blank sections. When the nurse asks Maria about her family history of colon cancer, Maria says, "My mom had something in her intestines, they took part of it out. I don't really know the details — she doesn't like to talk about it." The nurse explains that her mother's history is important for deciding which screenings Maria needs. Maria nods but does not ask questions. The nurse begins discussing the preventive screenings that are overdue based on Maria's age, sex, and family history: cervical cancer screening, breast cancer screening, and colorectal cancer screening. The nurse also identifies that Maria is due for an influenza vaccine (it is early October), and based on her age, she is eligible for the recombinant zoster vaccine series. Her tetanus-diphtheria-pertussis status needs clarification. When the nurse mentions the word "colonoscopy," Maria's expression changes. She says quietly, "My mom almost died from that cancer. I don't want to know if I have it. And I can't take a day off work for that — they'll fire me." She crosses her arms and looks toward the door. The nurse notes Maria's fear and the clear schedule and financial constraints she faces, alongside her high likelihood of limited health literacy.

### Stage 2 — Later in the same visit (45–75 minutes), after the nurse has used plain-language teaching and therapeutic communication to address Maria's concerns.
The nurse spends time using simple, concrete language to explain what a screening test is — "a test to look for a problem before you feel sick, so it can be caught early when it's easiest to treat" — and has specifically addressed Maria's fear about colorectal cancer by acknowledging her feelings, normalizing her worry, and explaining that finding something early is very different from her mother's experience. Maria's posture has relaxed. She uncrosses her arms and looks at the nurse. The nurse asks Maria to use teach-back to check her understanding of the recommended screening and vaccine plan. Maria says, "So you're saying I need the Pap smear because I might have cancer down there, and the mammogram because I might have breast cancer, and the colonoscopy because my mom had it. And the influenza shot is so I don't get the flu. And my blood sugar is fine so I don't need to worry about anything else."

The nurse notes that Maria's teach-back is partially correct but has significant gaps: she frames every screening as "because I might have cancer," showing she does not distinguish between screening and diagnostic testing. She also omits any mention of the shingles vaccine or the need to follow up on the fasting labs (lipid panel and fasting glucose) ordered. The nurse immediately addresses these gaps. The nurse uses a plain-language smoke-detector analogy: "Screening is like checking the smoke detectors in your house before there is a fire — we are looking when everything seems fine, not because we think something is wrong. If the detector goes off, then we do more tests to find out why." Maria's expression shows relief: "Oh, so you're checking before there's a problem, not because you think I have cancer." The nurse confirms this is correct and repeats the explanation of the shingles vaccine and fasting labs. Maria now says she understands.

The nurse reviews the list of recommended screenings and vaccines and asks Maria to help prioritize them based on her schedule and concerns. Maria says she can do things that happen in the clinic today — like a vaccine — but she is worried about scheduling anything that requires a day off work or causes high expenses. The nurse provides information about the clinic's financial counselor and explains that under her insurance plan, most preventive screenings are covered without a copay. Maria asks, "Wait, so if they find something during the colonoscopy, does it stop being free?" The nurse explains, "For many plans, a screening colonoscopy and medically necessary polyp removal during that screening are covered without cost-sharing, but coverage details can depend on network, anesthesia, facility billing, and how the claim is processed. The financial counselor can review your exact plan before you schedule." Maria nods and says, "That makes sense. I want to talk to that person."

The nurse administers the influenza vaccine in the left deltoid per standing order. Maria tolerates it without difficulty. The nurse then begins to discuss scheduling the remaining items. Maria says she could come back on a Tuesday morning in two weeks — that is her next day off — but she is not sure her mother can be left alone that long. The nurse asks whether Maria has anyone who could stay with her mother, and Maria says her daughter might be able to but works unpredictable hours. The nurse documents the barriers: rotating work schedule, caregiver responsibilities, cost concerns, and limited health literacy requiring plain-language communication and teach-back at every encounter.

### Stage 3 — Follow-up visit, 16 days later (Tuesday morning).
Maria returns for her follow-up visit. She reports she spoke with the financial counselor by phone and learned that her colonoscopy will be fully covered as a screening procedure. She has scheduled it for the following month on a day her daughter confirmed she can stay with Maria's mother. She completed her fasting labs at a walk-in lab over the weekend. Results are now in the chart: fasting glucose 104 mg/dL, total cholesterol 212 mg/dL, LDL 134 mg/dL, HDL 52 mg/dL, triglycerides 130 mg/dL. The provider reviews these and notes they do not meet thresholds for pharmacologic intervention at this time; lifestyle counseling is planned. Today the nurse is to administer the first dose of the recombinant zoster vaccine, schedule the cervical cancer screening (Pap with HPV co-testing), and complete a second round of teach-back on the screening plan.

During teach-back, the nurse asks Maria to explain in her own words what will happen at the colonoscopy and why she is having it. Maria says, "They put a camera inside to look at my intestines to see if there's anything growing in there before it turns into cancer. I'm doing it because my mom had colon cancer, so I'm at higher risk." The nurse then asks Maria to explain what she should do if she notices any new symptoms before the colonoscopy — such as blood in her stool, a change in bowel habits, or unexplained weight loss. Maria pauses and says, "I guess I'd wait until the colonoscopy to mention it?"

The nurse recognizes this as an incomplete understanding and a critical safety gap. The nurse explains that if Maria develops any of those symptoms before the scheduled colonoscopy, she should call the clinic right away rather than waiting, because those symptoms would change the visit from a screening to a diagnostic workup and might need to be evaluated sooner. Maria says, "Oh, so if something is actually wrong, I shouldn't just wait for the appointment — I should call you." The nurse confirms this is correct and repeats the teach-back.

Vitals today: T 36.7 °C, HR 72, BP 122/76, RR 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Weight 74 kg. The nurse administers the first dose of the recombinant zoster vaccine in the right deltoid. Maria tolerates it well. The nurse schedules the Pap with HPV co-testing for the same day as the colonoscopy prep teaching visit next month, to consolidate appointments and reduce the number of days Maria must take off work. The nurse also schedules the second dose of the zoster vaccine for two to six months after today, per the vaccine series protocol, and documents a reminder in the chart. Maria says, "I actually feel good about this. I was so scared to come in, but you explained everything so I could understand it. I'm going to bring my sister next time — she hasn't been to a doctor in years either."

## KEY DECISION POINTS

1. **Situation:** At Stage 1, during the intake interview, the nurse reviews Maria's responses on the intake forms, observes her behavior with the clinic pamphlet, and receives the Newest Vital Sign score. Among the available cues — the blank intake sections with "not sure what this means," the score of 1 out of 6 on the health-literacy screening tool, the slow reading with the pamphlet held at arm's length and then set down, the avoidance of eye contact, the statement about nodding along at a previous visit, the normal vital signs, the non-fasting glucose of 118 mg/dL, and the mild right shoulder tenderness — the nurse must identify which findings are relevant to planning the approach for this visit.
   - **Correct action:** The blank intake sections, the health-literacy score of 1 out of 6, the behavior with the pamphlet, and the statement about not understanding a previous provider are all significant cues indicating limited health literacy that will shape every communication in this visit. The avoidance of eye contact and fidgeting are cues of discomfort in the clinical setting. The normal vital signs, the non-fasting glucose of 118, and the shoulder tenderness are baseline data that do not drive the immediate communication plan.
   - **Why it is correct:** Recognizing limited health literacy early determines the nurse's entire communication strategy — plain language, teach-back, visual aids, and pacing. Missing these cues leads to information delivery the patient cannot use, which undermines every downstream screening decision. The pamphlet behavior may also suggest a vision concern worth exploring, but in the context of the other literacy cues, it is part of the same cluster. The normal vitals and the non-fasting glucose are relevant to the medical record but do not change how the nurse communicates.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Recognize cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

2. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse has identified that Maria is overdue for cervical cancer screening, breast cancer screening, and colorectal cancer screening, and is also due for influenza and zoster vaccines. The nurse must determine which of these is the highest priority screening to address, given Maria's age, her family history of a first-degree relative with colorectal cancer diagnosed at age 58, and the clinic's standing preventive-care protocol.
   - **Correct action:** Colorectal cancer screening (colonoscopy) is the highest priority screening. Per the clinic's protocol, Maria — with a first-degree relative diagnosed with colorectal cancer before age 60 — should have begun colonoscopy screening at age 40 or 10 years before the relative's diagnosis, whichever is earlier (meaning age 40, since 10 years before age 58 is age 48, and age 40 is earlier). She is now 52 and has never been screened, making her 12 years overdue for a screening with a strong evidence-based indication tied to her personal risk. The other screenings are overdue by a shorter interval or do not carry the same family-history risk. The influenza vaccine is an immediate operational action that can be completed today under standing orders without scheduling or preparation, but it is not the highest clinical priority screening or central risk hypothesis.
   - **Why it is correct:** Prioritization among multiple overdue preventive needs requires weighing the degree of delay, the patient's individual risk factors, and the strength of the indication. Maria's family history of colorectal cancer places her in a higher-risk category. Being 12 years past the recommended start date for a risk-stratified screening takes priority over screenings that are overdue by a shorter interval. Distinguishing immediate operational tasks (influenza vaccine today) from the highest-priority screening risk prevents the error of equating "fastest task" with "highest clinical priority."
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Prioritize hypotheses.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

3. **Situation:** At Stage 1, Maria expresses fear about colorectal cancer ("I don't want to know if I have it") and a practical barrier ("I can't take a day off work"). She crosses her arms and looks toward the door. The nurse must determine the best communication approach at this moment.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should first acknowledge Maria's fear and the practical barrier without judgment — reflecting her feelings ("It sounds like your mother's experience with colon cancer was very frightening, and it makes sense that you'd feel worried") — before providing any health information or screening education. Attempting to educate or persuade before addressing the emotional response will not be effective because Maria is in a defensive posture and is not ready to receive information. After the emotional acknowledgment, the nurse can gently reframe screening as a way to find problems early when they are most treatable, which is a different situation from her mother's experience. The work-schedule barrier should be validated as real and addressed with concrete problem-solving later, not dismissed.
   - **Why it is correct:** Therapeutic communication principles require the nurse to address the affective barrier before the cognitive one. Fear and avoidance are powerful obstacles to preventive care, and a patient who feels judged or rushed will disengage. Acknowledging the emotion builds trust and opens the door to education. Jumping to facts or reassurance ("It's just a screening, don't worry") minimizes the patient's experience and is a communication barrier. The practical barrier is equally real and must be treated as legitimate, not as an excuse.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Generate solutions.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

4. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse has used plain-language teaching to explain the difference between screening and diagnostic testing. Maria provides a teach-back response: "So you're saying I need the Pap smear because I might have cancer down there, and the mammogram because I might have breast cancer, and the colonoscopy because my mom had it. And the influenza shot is so I don't get the flu. And my blood sugar is fine so I don't need to worry about anything else." The nurse must evaluate whether this teach-back demonstrates adequate understanding.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse identifies that Maria correctly matches each screening to the right organ system and understands the family-history relevance of the colonoscopy. However, she has a partial misunderstanding: she frames every screening as "because I might have cancer," showing she does not grasp that screening is performed on asymptomatic people to detect problems early, not because cancer is suspected. She also has major omissions: she does not mention the recombinant zoster vaccine or the need to follow up on the fasting labs (lipid panel and fasting glucose).
   - **Why it is correct:** Teach-back is a diagnostic tool for identifying specific gaps in understanding. The most clinically important gap here is the conflation of screening with diagnostic testing, because it drives the client's fear ("I might have cancer") and may increase avoidance. Identifying these specific gaps allows the nurse to perform targeted re-teaching (such as using the smoke-detector analogy) and address the omissions rather than repeating the entire education session.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Analyze cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

5. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse must plan the sequence and logistics of the remaining preventive-care items — colonoscopy scheduling, cervical cancer screening, zoster vaccine series, and the return fasting labs — around Maria's identified barriers: a rotating work schedule with limited days off, caregiver responsibilities for her mother, cost concerns, and limited health literacy. The nurse must determine the best approach to consolidating and sequencing these items.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should consolidate appointments to minimize the number of separate visits, schedule around Maria's confirmed days off, connect her with the financial counselor before the colonoscopy to address cost concerns and insurance coverage questions, and ensure that each visit includes plain-language teaching with teach-back. Specifically: fasting labs can be completed at a walk-in lab on a day convenient to Maria; the zoster vaccine first dose can be given at the next clinic visit; the Pap with HPV co-testing can be scheduled on the same day as the colonoscopy preparation teaching visit next month; and the second zoster dose is scheduled within the recommended interval. The caregiver barrier should be addressed by helping Maria identify support for her mother on appointment days.
   - **Why it is correct:** Adherence to preventive care in patients with multiple practical barriers depends on reducing the burden of each encounter. Consolidating visits respects the patient's time and work constraints. Addressing cost concerns proactively — before the patient faces an unexpected bill — prevents a financial surprise that could erode trust and future adherence. Connecting the patient with the financial counselor is a concrete, actionable step within the nurse's scope. Maintaining plain-language communication and teach-back at every encounter is essential given the documented health-literacy level.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Take action.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

6. **Situation:** At Stage 3, during the second round of teach-back, Maria correctly explains the purpose and procedure of the colonoscopy. However, when asked what she should do if she notices new symptoms — blood in her stool, a change in bowel habits, or unexplained weight loss — before the scheduled colonoscopy, she says, "I guess I'd wait until the colonoscopy to mention it?" The nurse must evaluate whether Maria's overall understanding is adequate for safe follow-through.
   - **Correct action:** Maria's teach-back is partially correct but contains a critical safety gap. She understands the purpose of the screening colonoscopy but does not understand that new symptoms before the scheduled procedure would require earlier evaluation — she would wait rather than calling the clinic. This is an incomplete teach-back that requires correction. The nurse should clarify that new symptoms such as rectal bleeding, persistent change in bowel habits, or unexplained weight loss are reasons to contact the clinic promptly rather than waiting for the scheduled screening, because those symptoms would change the clinical situation from screening to diagnostic evaluation and might require a more urgent timeline. After providing this clarification, the nurse should repeat the teach-back to confirm Maria now understands the distinction.
   - **Why it is correct:** Evaluating teach-back requires the nurse to assess not just what the patient got right but what is missing or incorrect. A patient who would delay reporting new, potentially alarming symptoms because she believes the upcoming screening will address them is at risk for a delayed diagnosis. The distinction between "I feel fine and this is a routine check" and "I have new symptoms that need prompt attention" is clinically important and directly tied to the screening-versus-diagnostic concept the nurse has been teaching. Recognizing the gap and correcting it — without shaming the patient — is the evaluate-outcomes step of the clinical-judgment process applied to the teaching intervention.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Evaluate outcomes.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

## COMMON NURSING ERRORS

- **(Decision point 1) Focusing on the non-fasting glucose of 118 mg/dL as the most important finding and initiating diabetes-related teaching, while overlooking the health-literacy cues.** The non-fasting glucose is mildly elevated but does not meet diagnostic criteria for any condition on a non-fasting, single value; fasting labs are already ordered. Meanwhile, the health-literacy indicators are the findings that shape the entire visit approach. Treating a single non-fasting glucose as the priority misidentifies the most actionable cue.
- **(Decision point 1) Interpreting Maria's avoidance of eye contact and fidgeting as disinterest or noncompliance rather than recognizing them as cues of discomfort and possible embarrassment in the clinical setting.** This misinterpretation could lead the nurse to adopt a more directive or confrontational communication style, which would increase Maria's defensiveness.
- **(Decision point 2) Prioritizing the cervical cancer screening over the colorectal cancer screening because the Pap smear is "more overdue" by calendar time alone, without weighing the family-history risk factor that makes the colonoscopy the higher-priority item.** Calendar time since last screening is one factor, but risk stratification — Maria's first-degree relative with colorectal cancer — elevates the colonoscopy above a routine-interval Pap.
- **(Decision point 2) Treating all overdue items as equally urgent and attempting to schedule everything at once without prioritizing, which overwhelms the patient and increases the likelihood that she will not follow through on any of them.**
- **(Decision point 3) Responding to Maria's fear by immediately providing factual information about colonoscopy safety statistics or colorectal cancer survival rates, without first acknowledging her emotional response.** This is a common error in which the nurse prioritizes education over therapeutic communication. Facts delivered to a patient in a defensive posture are unlikely to be heard or retained, and the patient may disengage entirely.
- **(Decision point 3) Dismissing Maria's work-schedule concern as an excuse or telling her that her health should come first, which is judgmental and fails to recognize that employment and financial stability are legitimate competing priorities that must be problem-solved, not overridden.**
- **(Decision point 4) Judging Maria's teach-back as inadequate because she did not use medical terminology such as "asymptomatic" or "clinical indication."** Teach-back is assessed by whether the patient conveys the correct concept in her own words, not by whether she uses professional language. Requiring medical vocabulary from a patient with limited health literacy is an inappropriate standard.
- **(Decision point 5) Scheduling all appointments on separate days without considering consolidation, which multiplies the number of days Maria must take off work and arrange care for her mother.** This approach treats the scheduling as a clerical task rather than a nursing intervention that directly affects adherence.
- **(Decision point 5) Failing to connect Maria with the financial counselor and instead providing only vague reassurance that "insurance usually covers it."** Maria has already asked a specific and sophisticated question about whether coverage changes if a polyp is found. She needs a specific answer from someone who can review her plan, not a generalization.
- **(Decision point 6) Accepting Maria's teach-back as fully adequate because her explanation of the colonoscopy's purpose was correct, without probing further to discover the gap in her understanding about when to seek care for new symptoms.** Partial teach-back that covers the "what" but misses the "what if" leaves the patient without a critical safety instruction.
- **(Decision point 6) Correcting Maria's misunderstanding in a way that frightens her — for example, listing alarming symptoms and emphasizing cancer risk — which could reactivate the fear and avoidance that the nurse spent the visit addressing.** The correction should be matter-of-fact and empowering: "If you notice anything new, just call us so we can help you figure out the right next step."

## EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

- Recognize cues of limited health literacy — including behavioral indicators, screening-tool results, and patient statements — and adapt the communication plan accordingly.
- Prioritize among multiple overdue preventive screenings by weighing individual risk factors, degree of delay, and evidence-based guidelines rather than treating all screenings as equally urgent.
- Apply therapeutic communication techniques — specifically empathic acknowledgment and nonjudgmental validation — to address fear, embarrassment, and avoidance before initiating health teaching.
- Evaluate teach-back responses for both accuracy and completeness, identifying partial understanding that requires targeted follow-up teaching.
- Plan preventive-care follow-up that addresses real-world barriers — work schedule, caregiver responsibilities, cost, and health literacy — as nursing interventions that directly affect adherence and outcomes.

## BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after the nurse has addressed Maria's emotional and practical barriers, administered the influenza and first zoster vaccines, confirmed the colonoscopy is scheduled with financial coverage clarified, and completed two rounds of teach-back — the full picture of Maria's preventive-care needs and adherence plan has resolved.

- **Most likely condition:** Limited health literacy with delayed preventive-care engagement and unresolved barriers to screening follow-through. Maria's central clinical situation is not a disease state but a health-promotion gap: a patient whose limited health literacy, fear rooted in family experience, and practical life constraints have resulted in years of missed preventive care, requiring a structured, patient-centered plan to close the gap safely.
- **Plausible competing conditions:**
  1. *Noncompliance or disengagement from care* — wrong here: Maria's behavior is not willful refusal; it is driven by identifiable and addressable barriers (limited health literacy, fear, work and caregiver constraints, cost concerns), and she has demonstrated engagement and follow-through once those barriers were addressed. Labeling her as noncompliant would lead to a directive rather than collaborative approach and would miss the underlying causes.
  2. *Undiagnosed chronic disease requiring immediate medical management* — wrong here: the fasting glucose of 104 mg/dL and the lipid panel do not meet thresholds for pharmacologic intervention; the vital signs are normal; there are no acute symptoms. While lifestyle counseling is appropriate, the visit's priority is the preventive-care plan, not disease management.
- **Two priority actions:**
  1. Complete the teach-back correction regarding when to contact the clinic for new symptoms before the scheduled colonoscopy, and confirm Maria's understanding with a repeated teach-back — this closes the critical safety gap identified at Stage 3.
  2. Ensure all scheduled follow-up appointments — colonoscopy, Pap with HPV co-testing, and second zoster vaccine dose — are documented with confirmed dates, caregiver coverage for Maria's mother, and financial counselor contact completed, so that the preventive-care plan is concrete and actionable rather than aspirational.
- **Two wrong actions:**
  1. Accepting the Stage 3 teach-back as complete without correcting the gap about when to seek care for new symptoms — this leaves Maria believing she should wait for the colonoscopy even if she develops rectal bleeding or other warning signs, which could delay evaluation of a potentially serious finding.
  2. Adding additional screenings or teaching topics to this visit rather than consolidating the existing plan — overwhelming a patient with limited health literacy by introducing new information at the end of an already information-dense visit risks cognitive overload and undermines retention of the critical safety instruction just delivered.
- **Two parameters to monitor:**
  1. Maria's follow-through with the scheduled colonoscopy and Pap/HPV co-testing appointments — whether she attends the appointments is the most direct measure of whether the nurse's interventions (therapeutic communication, barrier reduction, financial counseling, plain-language teaching) achieved their intended outcome.
  2. Accuracy and completeness of teach-back at subsequent visits — ongoing assessment of Maria's understanding at each encounter confirms whether the health-literacy accommodations are effective and whether she retains the screening-versus-diagnostic distinction and the instruction to call for new symptoms.
- **Two irrelevant parameters:**
  1. Maria's comfort level with or use of professional medical terminology — the goal of plain-language teaching is comprehension of concepts, not acquisition of professional vocabulary; a patient who explains the colonoscopy's purpose accurately in everyday language has met the standard, and monitoring her use of medical terms would apply the wrong metric.
  2. Fasting glucose level at future visits as the primary outcome metric — while it is part of routine health maintenance, it does not evaluate whether the preventive-screening and health-literacy interventions are working; tracking it as the priority parameter confuses chronic-disease surveillance with the case's actual focus.
