**CASE TITLE**
Discharge medication reconciliation discrepancy involving duplicate anticoagulation therapy.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 71-year-old woman with a history of permanent atrial fibrillation, hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease stage 3a (baseline creatinine 1.6 mg/dL, eGFR 48 mL/min), osteoarthritis, and a recent left total knee arthroplasty performed four days ago is being prepared for discharge from the orthopedic surgical unit. Her home medication list, documented at admission, includes warfarin 5 mg daily (for atrial fibrillation, managed by her cardiologist), lisinopril 20 mg daily, metformin 500 mg twice daily, amlodipine 5 mg daily, and acetaminophen 650 mg as needed for joint pain. During the hospitalization, warfarin was held perioperatively and she was bridged with enoxaparin 1 mg/kg subcutaneously every 12 hours. Warfarin was restarted on postoperative day 2. Her INR this morning is 1.8. The orthopedic surgeon has entered discharge orders, and the discharge prescription list has been generated by the resident covering the service today.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The nurse receives the printed discharge medication list and the patient's belongings have been gathered. The patient is alert, pleasant, and eager to go home. Her daughter is present and will be the primary caregiver for the first two weeks. The patient states she "just wants to get her pills sorted out so she can leave." The nurse begins the discharge medication reconciliation process by comparing three documents side by side: the home medication list from admission, the inpatient medication administration record, and the new discharge prescription list.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline discharge-day assessment: T 36.8 °C, HR 78 (irregularly irregular), BP 134/76, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Surgical incision left knee is clean, dry, intact with staples in place, mild surrounding ecchymosis, no erythema or drainage. Pedal pulses palpable bilaterally. Mild pitting edema left lower extremity consistent with postoperative status. No signs of bleeding — gums normal, no petechiae, no melena reported, no hematuria. Patient is weight-bearing as tolerated with a rolling walker. She is oriented to person, place, time, and situation. She wears reading glasses and reports she can read medication labels with them on.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Discharge-day labs: INR 1.8, PT 21.4 seconds, aPTT 34 seconds, hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL (admission 12.4 g/dL, postoperative day 1 was 10.2 g/dL, now stable), platelets 198,000/µL, creatinine 1.7 mg/dL, eGFR 44 mL/min, BUN 28 mg/dL, potassium 4.6 mEq/L, blood glucose fasting 142 mg/dL, HbA1c 7.2% (drawn during admission).

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — The nurse reviews the discharge prescription list generated by the covering resident and identifies the following medications ordered for discharge: warfarin 5 mg daily, enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneously daily for 14 days (listed as "VTE prophylaxis post-TKA"), lisinopril 20 mg daily, metformin 1000 mg twice daily, amlodipine 5 mg daily, oxycodone 5 mg every 6 hours as needed for pain, and acetaminophen 650 mg every 6 hours as needed. The nurse notes two immediate concerns upon comparison with the home medication list and inpatient MAR. First, both warfarin and enoxaparin appear on the discharge list simultaneously — the patient would be going home on two anticoagulants. Second, the metformin dose on the discharge list is 1000 mg twice daily, but the home medication list and the admission reconciliation both document metformin 500 mg twice daily; the inpatient MAR also administered 500 mg twice daily throughout the stay. No order or documentation exists for a metformin dose increase. The nurse holds the discharge process and does not send the prescriptions to the pharmacy.

**Stage 2** — The nurse contacts the covering orthopedic resident to clarify both discrepancies. The resident states that the enoxaparin was "left on from the inpatient orders" and was not intentionally prescribed for discharge alongside warfarin; the resident agrees it should be removed. Regarding the metformin, the resident is uncertain whether the dose was intentionally changed and says, "I just pulled it from the system — maybe it auto-populated from a previous encounter." The nurse asks the resident to place a hold on the discharge prescriptions pending clarification. The nurse then contacts the inpatient clinical pharmacist for a formal reconciliation review. The pharmacist confirms that the enoxaparin-plus-warfarin duplication is a high-risk error — the patient's INR is already 1.8 and she has stage 3a CKD with an eGFR of 44, which increases bleeding risk with enoxaparin. The pharmacist also confirms that the metformin dose in the patient's verified home medication list and her outpatient pharmacy records is 500 mg twice daily; the 1000 mg dose appears to have auto-populated from a hospitalist's medication list during a prior admission two years ago when a dose increase was trialed and subsequently reversed by her primary care provider. The pharmacist recommends correcting both entries and documents the reconciliation in the electronic health record. The resident enters corrected orders: warfarin 5 mg daily (continue home dose, follow-up INR in 3 days with cardiologist), enoxaparin removed, metformin 500 mg twice daily.

**Stage 3** — With corrected discharge prescriptions now verified, the nurse proceeds with discharge teaching. The patient and her daughter are present. The nurse reviews each medication, its purpose, dose, and timing. When discussing warfarin, the patient's daughter asks, "Mom was getting shots in her belly here — shouldn't she keep getting those at home too?" The nurse explains that the enoxaparin injections were used temporarily while the warfarin was restarting and that taking both at home would create a dangerous bleeding risk, especially given her kidney function. The nurse reviews bleeding precautions: signs to report (unusual bruising, blood in urine or stool, prolonged bleeding from cuts, severe headache, dizziness), dietary consistency with vitamin K–containing foods, the importance of the INR check in 3 days, and not to start any new over-the-counter medications — especially NSAIDs — without checking with her provider. The nurse also clarifies the metformin dose, showing the patient her home pill bottle (which the daughter brought) labeled 500 mg, and confirms it matches the corrected discharge prescription. The patient verbalizes understanding of each medication, the daughter takes notes, and both confirm the follow-up appointments: orthopedic surgeon in 10 days for staple removal, cardiologist in 3 days for INR check, and primary care provider in 2 weeks. The nurse documents the reconciliation process, the discrepancies identified, the provider and pharmacist communications, the corrected orders, and the patient/family education with return demonstration of understanding.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse is comparing the home medication list, inpatient MAR, and discharge prescription list. Multiple data points conflict. The nurse must identify which discrepancies represent potential patient safety threats versus minor clerical differences. *Correct action:* The nurse identifies the simultaneous prescribing of warfarin and enoxaparin as a high-risk duplicate anticoagulation error and the unexplained doubling of the metformin dose as an unverified dose change. Both are flagged before any prescriptions are transmitted to the outpatient pharmacy. *Why it is correct:* Duplicate anticoagulation dramatically increases hemorrhagic risk, particularly in an elderly patient with CKD and a rising INR. An unverified dose doubling of metformin in a patient with CKD stage 3a could precipitate lactic acidosis or hypoglycemia. Catching these before discharge prevents the errors from reaching the patient. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must determine the relative urgency of the two discrepancies and decide what to do before contacting the provider. *Correct action:* The nurse prioritizes the duplicate anticoagulation as the more immediately dangerous discrepancy because it involves a high-alert medication class with potential for life-threatening hemorrhage, and holds the entire discharge prescription transmission pending clarification of both issues. *Why it is correct:* Anticoagulants are classified as high-alert medications by the Institute for Safe Medication Practices. The combination of two anticoagulants in a 71-year-old with CKD and a subtherapeutic-but-rising INR creates imminent bleeding risk that exceeds the risk posed by the metformin dose error. Holding transmission prevents either error from being dispensed. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the covering resident is unfamiliar with the patient's medication history and cannot confirm whether the metformin dose change was intentional. The nurse must decide the next appropriate action. *Correct action:* The nurse involves the inpatient clinical pharmacist to perform a formal medication reconciliation, including verification against the outpatient pharmacy dispensing records, rather than accepting the resident's uncertainty or independently changing the order. *Why it is correct:* The nurse does not have prescriptive authority to change the dose and cannot verify the accuracy of the auto-populated entry independently. The clinical pharmacist has access to dispensing records and medication reconciliation expertise and can provide an authoritative verification. This is an interdisciplinary safety process, not a task the nurse or an uncertain resident should resolve alone. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the pharmacist has confirmed both errors and the resident has entered corrected orders. The nurse must verify that the corrected discharge list is now accurate and complete before proceeding. *Correct action:* The nurse performs a final three-way comparison — corrected discharge prescriptions against the verified home medication list and the pharmacist's reconciliation documentation — confirming that warfarin 5 mg daily is present without enoxaparin, metformin is 500 mg twice daily, and all other medications match. The nurse also confirms that the follow-up INR order in 3 days is included in the discharge instructions. *Why it is correct:* A corrected order does not guarantee a correct discharge list; auto-population errors, partial saves, and residual incorrect entries are common in electronic health records. The final verification closes the loop on the reconciliation process. The INR follow-up is essential because the patient's INR is 1.8 and still rising toward therapeutic range, requiring monitoring. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient's daughter asks why the enoxaparin injections are not continuing at home, creating a teaching moment that also serves as a safety verification. The nurse must provide an accurate, patient-centered explanation. *Correct action:* The nurse explains that enoxaparin was a temporary bridge used while warfarin was not yet at therapeutic levels, that warfarin is now restarting and her INR is being monitored, and that taking both simultaneously would create a dangerous risk of bleeding — made worse by her reduced kidney function, which slows enoxaparin clearance. The nurse teaches specific bleeding precautions and confirms the patient and daughter can verbalize when to seek emergency care. *Why it is correct:* The daughter's question reveals a knowledge gap that, if unaddressed, could lead the family to seek enoxaparin from another provider or to perceive the discharge plan as incomplete. Connecting the explanation to the patient's specific renal status individualizes the teaching and reinforces why this is not a one-size-fits-all decision. Teach-back confirms comprehension. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* Across all three stages, the nurse must ensure that the identification of discrepancies, all provider and pharmacist communications, the corrected orders, and the patient education are documented. *Correct action:* The nurse documents each step in the electronic health record: the specific discrepancies found (duplicate anticoagulant, unverified metformin dose change), the time and content of communication with the resident and pharmacist, the pharmacist's verification findings, the corrected orders with the resident's name and time, the discharge teaching provided, and the patient's and daughter's verbalized understanding. *Why it is correct:* Documentation creates a legal record of the safety intervention, supports continuity for the next providers (cardiologist for INR follow-up, primary care for medication management), and satisfies regulatory and accreditation standards for medication reconciliation. Incomplete documentation could allow the error to resurface at a future admission or transition of care. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse notices the enoxaparin on the discharge list but assumes it is intentional because it was administered during the inpatient stay, reasoning that "the doctor must want it continued." This is wrong because the inpatient bridging indication no longer applies once warfarin is restarted and the INR is rising; failing to question the duplication allows a high-alert medication error to reach the patient.

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse identifies the metformin dose discrepancy but dismisses it as unimportant because "it's just metformin, not a high-risk drug." This is wrong because an unexplained dose doubling in a patient with CKD stage 3a and an eGFR of 44 increases the risk of lactic acidosis, and any unverified dose change violates safe medication reconciliation principles regardless of the drug class.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse addresses the metformin discrepancy first and sends the anticoagulant prescriptions to the pharmacy while waiting for the metformin clarification, reasoning that the anticoagulants were "already being given in-house." This is wrong because it allows the higher-risk error — duplicate anticoagulation — to be dispensed to the patient while the lower-risk issue is being resolved. Priority must go to the high-alert medication.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse accepts the resident's statement that the enoxaparin "should come off" and independently removes it from the discharge list without a formal order change or pharmacist verification, reasoning that it saves time. This is wrong because the nurse does not have authority to discontinue a prescribed medication without a provider order, and bypassing the pharmacist reconciliation process eliminates a critical safety checkpoint that also caught the metformin auto-population error.

Attached to Decision Point 4: After the resident enters corrected orders, the nurse assumes the electronic system has updated correctly and proceeds directly to discharge teaching without re-verifying the final prescription list. This is wrong because electronic health record systems can retain cached or partially updated medication lists, and a final verification is the last opportunity to catch a persistent error before the patient leaves the facility.

Attached to Decision Point 5: During discharge teaching, the nurse tells the patient and daughter to "just take the medications as listed" without explaining the rationale for removing the enoxaparin or addressing the daughter's question in clinical terms. This is wrong because it leaves the family without understanding of why the change was made, which could lead them to seek enoxaparin independently, question the plan's completeness, or fail to recognize bleeding signs as an emergency. Effective teach-back requires the patient and caregiver to understand the reasoning, not just comply with a list.

Attached to Decision Point 6: The nurse verbally communicates the discrepancies to the resident and pharmacist but does not document the reconciliation process, the specific errors found, or the corrective actions taken, reasoning that "the corrected orders speak for themselves." This is wrong because without documentation, there is no record that a safety event occurred, no audit trail for quality improvement, and no communication to downstream providers about the patient's vulnerability to auto-population errors in future transitions of care.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Identify high-risk medication discrepancies during discharge reconciliation by systematically comparing the home medication list, inpatient MAR, and discharge prescriptions.
- Prioritize duplicate high-alert medication errors over other discrepancies based on the severity and immediacy of potential patient harm.
- Coordinate interdisciplinary communication among the nurse, prescriber, and pharmacist to resolve medication reconciliation conflicts within the nurse's scope of practice.
- Provide individualized, patient-centered discharge medication teaching that addresses the patient's and caregiver's specific questions and incorporates teach-back to verify understanding.
- Document the complete medication reconciliation process, including discrepancies identified, communications with providers, corrective actions, and patient education outcomes.
