**CASE TITLE**
Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction following ABO-incompatible red blood cell transfusion.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old woman with a history of chronic kidney disease stage 3b, hypertension, and type 2 diabetes is admitted to a medical-surgical unit for symptomatic anemia. She presented to the emergency department with progressive fatigue, exertional dyspnea, and pallor over the past two weeks. Her admission hemoglobin is 6.4 g/dL, and the provider orders transfusion of two units of packed red blood cells. She has blood type A-positive. Because the pretransfusion specimen was mislabeled at collection, the compatibility record was generated from another patient’s type. At the bedside, the patient identifiers on the transfusion record and product paperwork appear to match this patient, but the product is actually ABO-incompatible with her true blood type. The unit is hung and infusion is started at the ordered rate of 2 mL/min for the first 15 minutes per facility protocol. The patient has a peripheral 20-gauge IV in her left forearm. She has no history of prior transfusion reactions. Current medications include lisinopril 10 mg daily (held on admission due to hypotension risk), metformin 500 mg twice daily, held during acute hospitalization per inpatient protocol, and amlodipine 5 mg daily.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The nurse completes the pre-transfusion assessment and baseline vital signs, verifies the patient's identity with two identifiers, and initiates the transfusion at 1420. The patient is resting comfortably in bed, alert and oriented, and reports feeling "just tired." She denies pain, shortness of breath, or itching. The unit appears to be infusing without difficulty.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Pre-transfusion baseline: T 36.8 °C, HR 92, BP 108/68, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Skin warm and dry, pale but without diaphoresis or flushing. Lungs clear bilaterally. Abdomen soft, non-tender. No peripheral edema. IV site without redness, swelling, or tenderness. Patient is calm and conversational. Urine output via bedside commode has been approximately 40 mL/hr since admission, urine straw-colored.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Admission labs: hemoglobin 6.4 g/dL, hematocrit 19.2%, WBC 7,800/µL, platelets 218,000/µL, BUN 32 mg/dL, creatinine 1.8 mg/dL (eGFR 38), potassium 4.6 mEq/L, sodium 139 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, total bilirubin 0.9 mg/dL, direct bilirubin 0.2 mg/dL, LDH 190 U/L, INR 1.0, aPTT 28 seconds, fibrinogen 310 mg/dL, haptoglobin 95 mg/dL. Urinalysis: yellow, clear, specific gravity 1.018, no blood, no protein.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 12 minutes after transfusion initiation (1432): The patient presses the call light. She reports sudden onset of low back pain that she describes as a deep ache across both flanks, and she feels "something is wrong — I feel like something terrible is about to happen." She is shivering visibly and reports feeling cold despite warm blankets. Her face is flushed. Vital signs: T 38.6 °C (up from 36.8 °C), HR 118 (up from 92), BP 96/58 (down from 108/68), RR 24 (up from 18), SpO₂ 95% on room air. Skin is flushed across the chest and face, warm, with early diaphoresis. She reports mild nausea. The IV site is unchanged — no redness, no swelling, no streaking. Approximately 25 mL of the unit has infused. The nurse notes that the urine in the bedside commode from the patient's most recent void, which occurred just moments ago, appears dark reddish-brown.

**Stage 2** — 30 minutes after the transfusion was stopped and normal saline bolus initiated (approximately 1505): The provider has been notified and is at the bedside. The patient is increasingly restless and anxious, reporting worsening flank pain and now chest tightness. She is tachypneic and her speech is fragmented. Vital signs: T 39.1 °C, HR 132, BP 82/50, RR 28, SpO₂ 93% on 4 L nasal cannula (applied after Stage 1). Skin is mottled across the extremities, with cool fingers and toes despite truncal flushing. A Foley catheter has been inserted per provider order; the initial drainage is 20 mL of dark port-wine-colored urine. Over the next 30 minutes, urine output is only 10 mL. The nurse observes new oozing from the peripheral IV insertion site and from a venipuncture site used for the earlier blood draw. No frank bleeding elsewhere, but petechiae are appearing across the anterior chest. Stat labs drawn at this time return: hemoglobin 5.8 g/dL, hematocrit 17.4%, platelets 104,000/µL (down from 218,000), potassium 5.4 mEq/L (up from 4.6), creatinine 2.6 mg/dL (up from 1.8), total bilirubin 4.2 mg/dL (up from 0.9), direct bilirubin 0.6 mg/dL, LDH 1,840 U/L (up from 190), INR 1.8 (up from 1.0), aPTT 52 seconds (up from 28), fibrinogen 140 mg/dL (down from 310), haptoglobin less than 10 mg/dL (down from 95), free plasma hemoglobin elevated. The blood bank has been notified and requests the discontinued unit with attached tubing, a post-reaction blood sample from the patient (drawn from the opposite arm), and the original pre-transfusion specimen for repeat type and crossmatch.

**Stage 3** — 2 hours after the transfusion was stopped (approximately 1620): The patient has been transferred to the intensive care unit. She has received a total of 1,500 mL of normal saline IV. A central venous catheter has been placed. The provider has ordered a vasopressor infusion (norepinephrine, titrated to maintain MAP above 65 mmHg per ICU protocol). The blood bank has confirmed the ABO incompatibility: the patient is type A-positive and the transfused unit was type B-positive. Current vital signs: T 38.4 °C, HR 120, BP 88/56 on norepinephrine at 0.08 mcg/kg/min, RR 24 on 6 L nasal cannula, SpO₂ 94%. Urine output over the past hour is 8 mL — dark amber, hemoglobin-positive on dipstick. Repeat labs: hemoglobin 5.2 g/dL, platelets 78,000/µL, potassium 5.9 mEq/L, creatinine 3.4 mg/dL, total bilirubin 6.1 mg/dL, LDH 2,600 U/L, INR 2.2, aPTT 68 seconds, fibrinogen 88 mg/dL. The patient is drowsy but arousable, oriented to person only. Peripheral pulses are thready. Mottling has extended to the knees and elbows. Oozing continues from catheter insertion sites. The ICU team is evaluating for emergent dialysis given the worsening hyperkalemia and oliguric acute kidney injury, and the blood bank is preparing ABO-compatible RBCs and plasma-containing components as needed; cryoprecipitate is being requested per component protocol for hypofibrinogenemia.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, 12 minutes into the transfusion, the patient reports sudden flank pain, a sense of impending doom, and visible rigors. Her temperature has risen 1.8 °C, heart rate has increased by 26 beats per minute, blood pressure has dropped from 108/68 to 96/58, and the urine in the commode is dark reddish-brown. The IV site is normal. Identify which of these findings are significant cues requiring immediate nursing action. *Correct action:* The significant cues are the acute onset of bilateral flank/back pain during a transfusion, the subjective sense of impending doom, the rigors with a temperature spike from 36.8 °C to 38.6 °C, the tachycardia (92 → 118), the hypotension (108/68 → 96/58), and the dark reddish-brown urine (hemoglobinuria). The unchanged IV site is a normal finding and does not explain the systemic findings. Together these findings constitute an acute transfusion reaction. *Why it is correct:* The temporal relationship — symptom onset within minutes of transfusion initiation — combined with the specific cluster of flank pain, hemodynamic instability, fever, rigors, and hemoglobinuria points to an acute reaction to the transfused blood. The sense of impending doom is a classic early symptom of acute hemolytic transfusion reaction and is not a psychiatric finding to be dismissed. The normal IV site rules out infiltration or phlebitis as the cause of the patient's distress. Each of these cues individually might have alternative explanations, but their simultaneous onset during an active transfusion makes the cluster unmistakable. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse has recognized the cue cluster. Now the nurse must determine what type of transfusion reaction is occurring. The findings include high fever with rigors, bilateral flank pain, hypotension, a sense of impending doom, and dark reddish-brown urine — all within 12 minutes of initiation. *Correct action:* Classify this presentation as most consistent with an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. The hemoglobinuria (dark reddish-brown urine), the bilateral flank pain (flank/back pain is associated with acute intravascular hemolysis and renal involvement; hemoglobinuria reflects free hemoglobin filtered through the kidneys), the rapid-onset fever with rigors, the hypotension, and the sense of impending doom together distinguish a hemolytic reaction from other transfusion reaction types. A febrile non-hemolytic reaction would present with fever and possibly rigors but would not produce hemoglobinuria, flank pain, or hemodynamic instability. An allergic reaction would present with urticaria, pruritus, and possibly anaphylaxis (bronchospasm, angioedema) but not flank pain or hemoglobinuria. Transfusion-associated circulatory overload would present with dyspnea, crackles, jugular venous distension, and hypertension — the opposite hemodynamic picture. *Why it is correct:* Acute hemolytic transfusion reactions result from ABO-incompatible red cells being destroyed by the recipient's preformed antibodies, triggering massive intravascular hemolysis. Free hemoglobin is released into the plasma and filtered by the kidneys, producing hemoglobinuria and causing renal tubular injury that manifests as flank pain. The complement cascade and cytokine release cause fever, rigors, hypotension, and the characteristic sense of impending doom. No other transfusion reaction type produces this specific combination. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse has identified an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. Multiple threats are emerging simultaneously: the patient is febrile, hypotensive, tachycardic, in pain, and producing hemoglobin-laden urine. Determine which threat is the most immediately life-threatening and must drive the priority response. *Correct action:* The cardiovascular collapse — the acute hypotension (96/58, down from 108/68) with tachycardia (118) — is the most immediately life-threatening element. The hemolytic reaction triggers a massive inflammatory and complement-mediated cascade that can rapidly progress to distributive shock, disseminated intravascular coagulation, and multiorgan failure. While the renal injury (hemoglobinuria) and the fever are serious, the immediate threat to survival is loss of adequate perfusion. The priority is to stop the source of the reaction and restore circulatory volume. *Why it is correct:* Acute hemolytic transfusion reactions can be fatal within minutes to hours, and the mechanism of death is cardiovascular collapse from the inflammatory cascade, compounded by DIC. Stopping the transfusion halts the ongoing antigen exposure that is driving the cascade. Maintaining vascular access and supporting perfusion with isotonic crystalloid addresses the immediate hemodynamic threat. Renal protection (maintaining urine output) is critical but secondary to maintaining circulation — the kidneys cannot be protected if perfusion is not restored. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must determine the complete set of immediate actions required to respond to this acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. *Correct action:* The immediate response includes the following actions, which are performed essentially simultaneously or in rapid succession as a coordinated set: stop the transfusion immediately by clamping the blood tubing (do not flush the remaining blood in the line into the patient); keep the IV line open by disconnecting the blood tubing and connecting new tubing with 0.9% normal saline to maintain venous access and support intravascular volume; stay with the patient and have another staff member notify the provider and the blood bank; monitor vital signs continuously; save the blood unit with its attached tubing and labels — do not discard any component; and prepare to collect a post-reaction blood sample and urine specimen per facility transfusion reaction protocol. *Why it is correct:* Stopping the transfusion immediately halts further infusion of incompatible red cells, limiting the volume of antigen exposure and the severity of the hemolytic cascade. The IV line must be maintained — not removed — because the patient is hypotensive and will need aggressive fluid resuscitation and possibly vasopressors; new tubing is connected rather than flushing the existing line because the blood remaining in the tubing is the incompatible product. Normal saline is the standard compatible fluid for blood administration sets. For the transfusion access line, use 0.9% normal saline with new tubing per transfusion-reaction protocol. Do not flush residual blood through the original tubing. Avoid teaching lactated Ringer's as the immediate line-maintaining fluid for a blood product reaction; additional resuscitation fluids should follow provider order and facility policy. The blood unit, tubing, and attached labels must be preserved and returned to the blood bank along with a fresh patient blood sample so the blood bank can investigate the incompatibility, repeat the type and crossmatch, and confirm the error. These actions are a coordinated set, not a rigid sequence — the critical point is that stopping the transfusion and maintaining the line happen first and essentially together, while notification and specimen collection follow immediately. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient's condition has worsened despite stopping the transfusion and initiating normal saline. She is now hypotensive at 82/50, tachycardic at 132, with mottled extremities, oliguria (10 mL in 30 minutes of dark port-wine urine), and new signs of coagulopathy — oozing from puncture sites and petechiae. Labs confirm hemolysis (haptoglobin less than 10, LDH 1,840, bilirubin 4.2) and early DIC (platelets 104,000, INR 1.8, aPTT 52, fibrinogen 140). The nurse must determine the priority nursing action at this moment. *Correct action:* The priority action is to escalate immediately: activate rapid response/ICU transfer or escalate to a higher level of care with the provider who is already at the bedside, given the patient is progressing toward hemodynamic collapse with signs of DIC and acute kidney injury despite initial fluid resuscitation. Concurrently, increase the rate of normal saline infusion per order or standing protocol to support perfusion, continue strict intake-and-output monitoring with hourly urine output measurement via the Foley catheter, and ensure that the blood bank has received the discontinued unit, tubing, and post-reaction blood sample so that ABO-compatible products can be prepared if transfusion of other blood components (such as fresh frozen plasma or cryoprecipitate for the DIC) becomes necessary. *Why it is correct:* The clinical trajectory — worsening hypotension, new coagulopathy, and falling urine output — indicates that the initial response, while correct, has not arrested the cascade. The patient is developing multiorgan dysfunction (cardiovascular, renal, hematologic) and needs ICU-level monitoring, vasopressor support, possible dialysis, and blood component therapy for DIC. The nurse's role at this point is to recognize the deterioration, escalate care, support perfusion, and ensure the blood bank investigation is underway so that compatible products are available. Delaying escalation to complete documentation, collect additional specimens, or administer antipyretics would be a dangerous misallocation of time. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient is in the ICU on vasopressor support with confirmed ABO incompatibility. She has received 1,500 mL of normal saline, norepinephrine is infusing, and the team is evaluating for dialysis and blood component therapy. The nurse must determine which findings would indicate that the patient's condition is stabilizing versus continuing to deteriorate. *Correct action:* The findings that would indicate stabilization are: improving urine output (increasing volume and lightening color from dark port-wine toward amber or yellow, indicating decreasing free hemoglobin in the urine), stabilizing or improving blood pressure with decreasing vasopressor requirements (ability to wean norepinephrine while maintaining MAP above 65), improving or stabilizing coagulation parameters (rising fibrinogen, falling INR and aPTT, stabilizing platelet count — indicating the DIC is not progressing), stabilizing or falling potassium (the current 5.9 is dangerous and trending upward), and improving level of consciousness (currently drowsy and oriented to person only). Conversely, the findings at Stage 3 that indicate continued deterioration are: urine output of only 8 mL in the past hour with persistent hemoglobinuria, rising creatinine (now 3.4, up from 1.8 at baseline), worsening coagulopathy (fibrinogen 88, INR 2.2, aPTT 68, platelets 78,000 — all worse than Stage 2), rising potassium (5.9), and declining mental status. These findings confirm the patient is still deteriorating and support the need for emergent dialysis and blood component replacement. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes after an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction requires tracking the three organ systems most threatened by the hemolytic cascade: cardiovascular (blood pressure, vasopressor requirements, perfusion as reflected by mental status and skin mottling), renal (urine output, creatinine, potassium, urine color), and hematologic (coagulation studies and platelet count as markers of DIC). A single improving parameter — such as a slight decrease in temperature — does not indicate overall stabilization if the renal and coagulation trajectories are still worsening. The nurse must synthesize the full picture across organ systems to accurately evaluate the patient's trajectory. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Dismissing the patient's sense of impending doom as anxiety related to the transfusion or to her hospitalization, and attributing the back pain to positioning in bed. In the context of an active transfusion, the sudden onset of impending doom combined with flank pain, fever, and hemodynamic changes is a hallmark of acute hemolytic reaction, not a psychogenic complaint. Failing to recognize these as significant cues delays the critical first action of stopping the transfusion.

- *(Decision 1)* Focusing on the normal IV site as reassuring and concluding that the transfusion is proceeding without complication. The absence of a local site reaction does not rule out a systemic transfusion reaction. Acute hemolytic reactions are systemic events driven by antigen-antibody interaction in the bloodstream, not at the infusion site.

- *(Decision 2)* Classifying the reaction as a febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction because fever and rigors are present, and managing it by slowing the transfusion rate and administering acetaminophen. A febrile non-hemolytic reaction does not produce hemoglobinuria, flank pain, or hypotension. Slowing the rate rather than stopping the transfusion in the presence of these findings allows continued infusion of incompatible blood and worsens the hemolytic cascade. The presence of hemoglobinuria is the critical distinguishing feature that rules out a simple febrile reaction.

- *(Decision 2)* Classifying the reaction as an allergic reaction and administering diphenhydramine. Allergic transfusion reactions present with urticaria, pruritus, and in severe cases anaphylaxis with bronchospasm and angioedema. This patient has no urticaria, no pruritus, and no respiratory symptoms consistent with allergy. The flank pain and hemoglobinuria are not features of allergic reactions.

- *(Decision 3)* Prioritizing the fever as the most urgent problem and focusing on antipyretic administration and cooling measures. While the fever is part of the reaction, it is not the immediate threat to life — the cardiovascular collapse and the ongoing hemolytic cascade are. Treating the fever without stopping the transfusion and addressing the hemodynamic instability is a dangerous misallocation of priority.

- *(Decision 4)* Stopping the transfusion but removing the IV catheter entirely, losing venous access in a patient who is hypotensive and will need aggressive fluid resuscitation and possibly vasopressors. The IV line must be maintained; only the blood tubing is disconnected and replaced with new tubing and normal saline.

- *(Decision 4)* Flushing the remaining blood in the existing tubing into the patient before switching to saline, reasoning that the small volume remaining in the line is negligible. Any additional volume of incompatible blood worsens the reaction. The tubing must be clamped and disconnected without flushing, and the blood unit with its attached tubing must be preserved for the blood bank investigation.

- *(Decision 4)* Discarding the blood unit or detaching the tubing from the bag after stopping the transfusion. The unit, all attached tubing, and all labels must be returned intact to the blood bank. They are essential for the investigation — the blood bank will re-crossmatch the unit against the patient's sample to confirm the incompatibility and identify the point of error.

- *(Decision 5)* Delaying escalation to the provider or rapid response team in order to complete the transfusion reaction documentation form or to collect all required specimens first. Documentation and specimen collection are important but must not delay notification when the patient is hemodynamically deteriorating. The nurse should call for help immediately and delegate or complete the paperwork concurrently.

- *(Decision 5)* Administering a fluid bolus of lactated Ringer's instead of normal saline. For the transfusion access line, use 0.9% normal saline with new tubing per transfusion-reaction protocol. Do not flush residual blood through the original tubing. Avoid teaching LR as the immediate line-maintaining fluid for a blood product reaction; additional resuscitation fluids should follow provider order and facility policy.

- *(Decision 6)* Interpreting a slight decrease in temperature (39.1 → 38.4 °C) as evidence that the patient is improving, without evaluating the renal, hematologic, and hemodynamic trajectories. The temperature change is modest and does not reflect the status of the three organ systems most threatened by the hemolytic cascade. The worsening urine output, rising creatinine and potassium, falling fibrinogen, and rising INR and aPTT all indicate continued deterioration despite the marginal temperature improvement.

- *(Decision 6)* Failing to recognize the significance of the falling fibrinogen (310 → 140 → 88 mg/dL) and rising INR/aPTT as markers of evolving DIC, and instead attributing the oozing from puncture sites to "difficult sticks" or the patient's chronic kidney disease. DIC is a known and life-threatening complication of acute hemolytic transfusion reactions, driven by the release of tissue factor and activation of the coagulation cascade by the hemolytic process. The coagulation trend — consumption of fibrinogen and platelets with prolongation of clotting times — is the laboratory signature of DIC and demands urgent intervention with blood component therapy.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the early clinical cue cluster of an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction — fever, rigors, flank pain, hypotension, hemoglobinuria, and a sense of impending doom — and distinguish it from other transfusion reaction types (febrile non-hemolytic, allergic, transfusion-associated circulatory overload).
- Analyze the pathophysiologic basis of the clinical findings in acute intravascular hemolysis, including the significance of hemoglobinuria, the mechanism of flank pain, and the laboratory markers of hemolysis (falling haptoglobin, rising LDH and bilirubin, elevated free plasma hemoglobin).
- Prioritize cardiovascular collapse as the immediate life-threatening consequence of an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction and identify the hemolytic cascade as the driver of multiorgan dysfunction.
- Execute the correct immediate nursing response to a suspected acute hemolytic transfusion reaction: stop the transfusion, maintain IV access with normal saline via new tubing, notify the provider and blood bank, preserve the blood unit and tubing, and collect post-reaction specimens per facility protocol.
- Evaluate the patient's trajectory across cardiovascular, renal, and hematologic parameters to determine whether the patient is stabilizing or continuing to deteriorate, and recognize the laboratory pattern of evolving disseminated intravascular coagulation.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the transfusion has been stopped and initial fluid resuscitation begun, with the patient progressing toward hemodynamic collapse, oliguric acute kidney injury, and laboratory-confirmed DIC.

- *Most likely condition:* Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction due to ABO incompatibility. The onset of fever, rigors, bilateral flank pain, hypotension, a sense of impending doom, and hemoglobinuria within 12 minutes of transfusion initiation, followed by progressive hemodynamic collapse, oliguric acute kidney injury with port-wine urine, and laboratory evidence of intravascular hemolysis (haptoglobin less than 10, LDH 1,840, bilirubin 4.2) with evolving DIC (platelets falling, fibrinogen falling, INR and aPTT rising), is unambiguously an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Anaphylactic transfusion reaction — wrong here: anaphylaxis presents with urticaria, bronchospasm, angioedema, and upper or lower airway compromise; this patient has no urticaria, no pruritus, no wheezing, and no angioedema, and anaphylaxis does not produce hemoglobinuria, flank pain, or the laboratory hemolysis pattern (collapsed haptoglobin, markedly elevated LDH). (2) Septic transfusion reaction from bacterial contamination of the blood unit — wrong here: while bacterial contamination can cause fever, rigors, and hypotension during transfusion, it does not produce hemoglobinuria, the specific bilateral flank pain of renal hemoglobin deposition, or the rapid laboratory pattern of intravascular hemolysis with falling haptoglobin; additionally, the confirmed ABO incompatibility from the blood bank investigation establishes the mechanism.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Ensure the blood bank has received the discontinued unit with attached tubing and a post-reaction blood sample from the patient so that the incompatibility is confirmed and ABO-compatible blood products (fresh frozen plasma, cryoprecipitate, compatible packed red blood cells if needed) can be prepared — the patient's worsening DIC and severe anemia will require component therapy, and no further products can be safely issued until the blood bank completes its investigation. (2) Notify the provider (or activate the rapid response team) that the patient is progressing toward hemodynamic collapse with signs of DIC and oliguric acute kidney injury despite initial fluid resuscitation, and that transfer to a higher level of care for vasopressor support, possible dialysis, and blood component therapy is needed immediately.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Administering acetaminophen and slowing the transfusion rate rather than stopping it, treating the reaction as a febrile non-hemolytic reaction — wrong because the hemoglobinuria, flank pain, and hemodynamic instability distinguish this from a simple febrile reaction, and continuing any infusion of incompatible blood worsens the hemolytic cascade and increases the risk of death. (2) Removing the peripheral IV and planning to obtain new access later, reasoning that the contaminated line should be completely removed — wrong because the patient is hypotensive and deteriorating, and loss of venous access in a patient who needs aggressive fluid resuscitation and will likely need vasopressors and blood component therapy is dangerous; the correct action is to disconnect the blood tubing, connect new tubing with normal saline, and maintain the existing IV access.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Urine output and urine color via the Foley catheter — urine output directly reflects renal perfusion and the degree of ongoing hemoglobin-mediated tubular injury; increasing output and lightening color (from port-wine toward amber) would indicate improving renal perfusion and decreasing free hemoglobin burden, while persistent oliguria with dark urine indicates continued renal injury and supports the need for dialysis. (2) Coagulation studies, specifically fibrinogen level and platelet count — these are the most sensitive markers of whether the DIC is progressing or stabilizing; a rising fibrinogen and stabilizing platelet count would indicate the consumptive coagulopathy is abating, while continued decline (fibrinogen already 140 and falling, platelets already 104,000 and falling) confirms ongoing consumption and the need for component replacement therapy.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Temperature trend alone — temperature trend alone is insufficient to judge stabilization. Fever helped identify the reaction, but improvement in temperature does not outweigh worsening urine output, potassium, creatinine, vasopressor needs, platelets, fibrinogen, INR, and aPTT. (2) The peripheral IV insertion site — the site has been normal throughout and is not relevant to evaluating the systemic hemolytic reaction; monitoring it for redness or swelling, while part of routine IV care, does not provide information about the patient's trajectory through the hemolytic crisis.
