**CASE TITLE**
Advance directive clarification and code status escalation in metastatic lung cancer with acute respiratory decline.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Mr. Harold Jeffries is a 55-year-old man admitted five days ago to a medical-surgical unit with progressive dyspnea and right-sided pleural effusion secondary to metastatic non-small cell lung cancer (stage IV, diagnosed eight months ago with known metastases to liver, right adrenal gland, and thoracic spine). He underwent a therapeutic thoracentesis on hospital day 2 with 1,800 mL of exudative fluid removed, producing temporary symptom relief. He is receiving palliative carboplatin-based chemotherapy — his most recent cycle was three weeks ago. Current orders include supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula titrated to maintain SpO₂ above 92%, dexamethasone 4 mg IV every 6 hours for tumor-related inflammation, pantoprazole 40 mg IV daily, morphine sulfate 2 mg IV every 4 hours as needed for dyspnea or pain, ondansetron 4 mg IV every 8 hours as needed, a regular diet with oral nutritional supplements, venous thromboembolism prophylaxis with enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneous daily, and daily chest X-ray. His attending physician is a pulmonologist who is the primary service provider but is scheduled to be in the operating room (bronchoscopy suite) from 0730 until approximately 1400 today. The on-call covering provider is a hospitalist physician who has not previously met Mr. Jeffries. The medical record contains no advance directive, no Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST) form, no healthcare power of attorney documentation, and no documented goals-of-care conversation. The code status field in the electronic health record reads "Full Code" — the default setting applied at admission. Mr. Jeffries is divorced and has two adult children. His daughter, Keisha, is listed as the emergency contact and visited yesterday evening. His son, Darnell, lives out of state and has not visited during this admission.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At 0700 during the start-of-shift assessment, the nurse enters the room and finds Mr. Jeffries awake, sitting upright in bed with the head of bed elevated to 45 degrees. He is alert, oriented, and calm. He initiates conversation spontaneously. After the nurse introduces herself and begins the assessment, Mr. Jeffries says, unprompted: "I've been thinking about this all night. I don't want to be kept alive by machines. If something happens, I don't want them to put a tube down my throat. I've seen what that looks like and I don't want it. I just want to be comfortable." He is clear, coherent, and deliberate in his statement. He makes eye contact. He adds: "I haven't told my kids yet. I don't know how to bring it up. But I know what I want."

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (0700): Temperature 37.1 °C, heart rate 92 and regular, blood pressure 118/72, respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 93% on 3 L nasal cannula. Alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. Conversational, makes decisions, answers questions appropriately — the nurse assesses him as having full decision-making capacity at this time. Respirations mildly labored with use of accessory muscles at rest; diminished breath sounds over the right lower and middle lung fields; scattered expiratory wheezes in left lung. No stridor. Skin warm, pale, with mild diaphoresis on the forehead. Peripheral capillary refill 3 seconds. Bilateral lower extremity trace edema. He is thin and appears fatigued but is in no acute distress at rest. Pain rated 3/10, described as right-sided chest wall ache. He can speak in full sentences without pausing for breath. The findings to track across stages are respiratory rate, SpO₂, work of breathing, level of consciousness, and ability to speak in full sentences — all of which will deteriorate.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn at 0530): WBC 3,800/µL (leukopenic — expected post-chemotherapy), hemoglobin 10.4 g/dL, platelets 112,000/µL, sodium 136 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, albumin 2.6 g/dL, LDH 680 U/L (elevated — tumor burden marker, trajectory relevant), arterial blood gas not ordered at baseline (but will become relevant). Morning chest X-ray has been ordered but not yet performed. The hemoglobin, platelet count, and LDH are the values whose trajectory matters: a falling platelet count or rising LDH would signal disease progression or treatment complication; the low albumin contextualizes the risk of reaccumulating pleural effusion.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0700 to 0900 (initial disclosure, nurse's immediate response). After Mr. Jeffries makes his statement, the nurse sits down, maintains eye contact, and listens without interrupting. She uses open-ended questions to explore his understanding: "Can you tell me more about what you mean by 'kept alive by machines'?" He clarifies that he does not want endotracheal intubation, mechanical ventilation, or cardiopulmonary resuscitation. He states he would accept oxygen by mask and IV medications for comfort. He says he has not discussed this with his children or with his physician. He says he does not have a written advance directive or a healthcare power of attorney. The nurse thanks him for sharing his wishes and explains that she takes his words seriously, that she will document what he has told her, and that she will work to make sure his care team is aware. She explains, honestly, that his wishes cannot become a medical order (such as a DNAR/DNI order) until a provider writes that order after a conversation with him, and that his attending is currently in a procedure but she will reach out to the covering provider. She asks whether he would like to speak with a social worker or chaplain about advance directive completion, and he says yes to social work but declines chaplain services. Between 0715 and 0830, the nurse takes the following actions: (a) documents Mr. Jeffries's verbatim statements in a nursing narrative note titled "Patient-Expressed End-of-Life Wishes," including her assessment that the patient is alert, oriented, and demonstrates decision-making capacity (able to understand the situation, appreciate its consequences, reason about options, and communicate a choice); (b) pages the attending pulmonologist's office and receives a callback from the office nurse at 0745 who states the physician is scrubbing for a case and will not be available until after 1400 — the nurse requests that the attending be informed that a goals-of-care conversation is needed today and documents the communication; (c) calls the on-call hospitalist covering the pulmonology service, Dr. Reeves, who answers at 0800 — the nurse communicates the patient's expressed wishes using SBAR format, requests that Dr. Reeves come evaluate the patient and discuss code status, and Dr. Reeves responds that he will "try to get up there after I finish morning rounds, probably around 1100"; the nurse documents this conversation verbatim, including the time and the provider's stated plan; (d) places a social work consult for advance directive assistance; (e) notifies the charge nurse of the situation. At 0900, Mr. Jeffries's condition is unchanged: SpO₂ 93% on 3 L, respiratory rate 20, heart rate 90, comfortable at rest. He is eating breakfast. No provider has yet come to the bedside. The code status remains Full Code in the medical record.

**Stage 2** — 0930 to 1100 (patient deterioration begins). At 0930 the portable chest X-ray results are available and show a large recurrent right-sided pleural effusion with near-complete opacification of the right hemithorax, leftward mediastinal shift, and a new small left pleural effusion not previously seen. The nurse reviews the image and recognizes the significance. At 0945, Mr. Jeffries becomes noticeably more dyspneic. Reassessment: temperature 37.3 °C, heart rate 110, blood pressure 108/68, respiratory rate 28, SpO₂ 88% on 3 L nasal cannula. He is sitting bolt upright, using accessory muscles prominently, speaking in 3- to 4-word phrases only. He appears anxious. Skin diaphoretic and pale. The nurse immediately increases supplemental oxygen to 6 L via simple face mask per the existing oxygen titration order (titrate to SpO₂ above 92%). SpO₂ improves to 90% but does not reach the target. The nurse pages Dr. Reeves again at 0950, communicating the clinical change: "Mr. Jeffries is acutely more dyspneic, SpO₂ 88% on 3 L improved to only 90% on 6 L face mask, respiratory rate 28, CXR showing large recurrent right effusion with mediastinal shift and new left effusion. I am concerned about acute respiratory failure. He also remains Full Code and has expressed wishes against intubation. I need you to come evaluate him now." Dr. Reeves states he is with another acutely ill patient and will come "as soon as I can." The nurse documents this exchange. At 1015, the nurse administers morphine 2 mg IV per the existing PRN order for dyspnea, which produces mild subjective relief. At 1030, reassessment: SpO₂ 89% on 6 L simple face mask, respiratory rate 30, heart rate 118, blood pressure 102/64. Mr. Jeffries is increasingly fatigued. He says between breaths: "Please... don't let them... put a tube in me." The nurse switches to a non-rebreather mask at 15 L per minute (within the scope of the oxygen titration order — titrate to maintain SpO₂ above 92%). SpO₂ rises to 91%. The nurse recognizes that the patient is approaching the threshold for a rapid response and that the code status issue is now clinically urgent — if he arrests or requires emergent airway management, the default Full Code status would mandate interventions that directly contradict his clearly expressed wishes. She pages Dr. Reeves a third time and reaches his voicemail. She documents the page and the non-response.

**Stage 3** — 1100 to 1230 (rapid response, code status resolution). At 1100, the nurse activates the rapid response team (RRT). Her rationale: the patient meets rapid response criteria (SpO₂ persistently below 92% despite maximal supplemental oxygen, respiratory rate above 28, and clinical trajectory suggesting imminent respiratory failure), and the covering provider has not responded to the most recent page. The RRT arrives within 4 minutes. The team includes an ICU nurse, a respiratory therapist, and the rapid response physician (a critical care fellow, Dr. Anand). The nurse provides a concise handoff: "This is Mr. Jeffries, 55 years old, metastatic lung cancer, admitted with pleural effusion. He has been worsening over the last 90 minutes — SpO₂ now 91% on non-rebreather, respiratory rate 30, progressive dyspnea. CXR shows large recurrent right effusion with mediastinal shift and new left effusion. He is currently Full Code in the chart, but he has told me clearly and repeatedly today that he does not want intubation or CPR. He has decision-making capacity. No advance directive is on file. I have paged the covering hospitalist three times and he has not arrived. I have documented all of his expressed wishes and my escalation attempts." Dr. Anand examines Mr. Jeffries, who at 1105 is responsive but fatigued: SpO₂ 90% on non-rebreather, respiratory rate 32, heart rate 122, blood pressure 98/60, speaking in single words only but still responsive to questions. Dr. Anand speaks directly to Mr. Jeffries: "Mr. Jeffries, your breathing is getting worse. We may need to talk about a breathing tube. Can you tell me what you would want?" Mr. Jeffries responds: "No tube. No machines. Comfortable." Dr. Anand assesses his capacity, documents it, and enters a DNAR/DNI order with the clinical notation: "Patient has decision-making capacity, has clearly and consistently refused intubation and CPR, consistent with expressed wishes documented in nursing note at 0715. Goals of care discussed at bedside. Patient desires comfort-focused measures. Will continue supplemental oxygen, morphine for dyspnea, and supportive care. Attending notified via page." Dr. Anand also orders an arterial blood gas: pH 7.31, PaCO₂ 48 mmHg, PaO₂ 58 mmHg, HCO₃ 24 mEq/L, lactate 1.8 mmol/L — consistent with acute hypercapnic and hypoxemic respiratory failure. He orders morphine 1–2 mg IV every 2 hours PRN dyspnea (replaces the every-4-hour order), continues the non-rebreather, and places a palliative care consult. The social worker arrives at 1145 and assists Mr. Jeffries in completing a state-specific advance directive form while he retains capacity, designating his daughter Keisha as healthcare power of attorney. The nurse calls Keisha to inform her of the clinical change and the advance directive completion, documenting the call. By 1230, the attending pulmonologist, Dr. Pham, calls the unit after receiving the pages. The nurse provides an update. Dr. Pham validates the DNAR/DNI order, agrees with the palliative care consult, and states she will come evaluate Mr. Jeffries after her cases are complete to discuss whether a repeat thoracentesis for palliation of dyspnea is appropriate. At 1230, Mr. Jeffries is resting with SpO₂ 91% on non-rebreather, respiratory rate 26, heart rate 108, BP 100/62. He received morphine 2 mg IV at 1200 and reports his dyspnea is "bearable." The advance directive and DNAR/DNI order are now in the medical record. The code status is updated in the electronic health record and a purple DNAR wristband is placed per hospital policy.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *The situation / question:* At 0700 (Stage 1), Mr. Jeffries tells the nurse he does not want to be "kept alive by machines." The nurse must decide what her immediate obligations are — specifically, what she must do with this information before leaving the room and within the first 30 minutes. *Correct action:* The nurse must (a) actively listen and explore the patient's wishes using open-ended questions to ensure she accurately understands what he is and is not refusing, (b) assess and document that the patient has decision-making capacity (alert, oriented, can understand, appreciate, reason, and communicate), (c) explain honestly that she cannot change his code status — that requires a provider order — but that she will advocate for a timely provider conversation, (d) document his verbatim statements and her capacity assessment in the medical record immediately, and (e) begin the escalation process by contacting the attending and the covering provider. She should not make promises about timeline but should commit to action. *Why it is correct:* The nurse is the patient's advocate and the witness to his expressed wishes. Documentation of verbatim statements creates a contemporaneous legal and clinical record that protects the patient's autonomy if he later loses capacity. The capacity assessment is within RN scope (screening-level) and provides the clinical foundation for the provider's subsequent formal evaluation. The nurse cannot write a DNAR order, but she can and must initiate the process. Failing to document or escalate is an advocacy failure that puts the patient at risk of receiving unwanted interventions. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — the patient's statement and the nurse's baseline assessment provide all necessary data.

2. *The situation / question:* At 0800 (Stage 1), the nurse has paged the attending (unavailable) and reached the covering hospitalist, Dr. Reeves, who says he will come "around 1100." The code status remains Full Code. The nurse must decide whether this response is adequate or whether further action is needed now. *Correct action:* The nurse should accept the response with clear documentation (including time, provider name, and the provider's stated plan), inform the charge nurse, and continue to monitor the patient — but she must also identify this as an unresolved safety risk and plan to re-escalate if the patient's condition changes or the provider does not arrive as stated. She should document her ongoing concern that the code status does not reflect the patient's expressed wishes and set a mental or literal reminder to follow up at 1100 if Dr. Reeves has not arrived. At this point, the patient is stable, so a three-hour wait is not ideal but is not yet dangerous. The nurse cannot compel the provider to come sooner when no acute deterioration exists. *Why it is correct:* The chain of command requires progressively urgent escalation proportional to the clinical risk. At 0800, the patient is stable (SpO₂ 93%, no acute distress), so the risk of an unwanted code is present but not imminent. The appropriate nursing action is documentation, charge nurse notification, and planned follow-up — not yet escalation to the supervisor or administrator. However, this calculus changes if the patient deteriorates, which is exactly what happens in Stage 2. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues (weighing the urgency of the code status gap against the patient's current stability). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — requires the patient's stable assessment data and the provider's response to the page.

3. *The situation / question:* At 0945 (Stage 2), Mr. Jeffries acutely decompensates — SpO₂ drops to 88%, respiratory rate rises to 28, he is speaking in short phrases only. The nurse has titrated oxygen per the existing order and paged Dr. Reeves again. The code status is still Full Code. The nurse must decide what she can and cannot do independently regarding the code status in this evolving situation. *Correct action:* The nurse cannot independently change the code status or write a DNAR/DNI order — this is a provider-only action. What the nurse must do is (a) continue to escalate by re-paging the provider and, if no response, activating the chain of command (charge nurse → nursing supervisor → administrator on call), (b) continue to treat the patient within the scope of existing orders (oxygen titration, PRN morphine for dyspnea), (c) ensure her documentation of the patient's repeated wishes is prominent and current so that any responding provider has immediate access to it, and (d) prepare to activate the rapid response team if the patient meets RRT criteria, because the RRT brings a provider to the bedside who has the authority to evaluate capacity and enter the DNAR/DNI order. The nurse must not assume that because the patient said he doesn't want intubation, she can unilaterally change his code status or write a DNAR/DNI order — this remains a provider-only action. However, she must distinguish between an unexpected cardiac arrest with no order (where full code is the legal default) and a conscious, capacitated patient actively refusing intubation at the bedside. For the latter, the nurse must immediately communicate the refusal to the responding team, escalate urgently, and ensure a provider evaluates capacity and enters the appropriate orders to honor the patient's right to refuse life-sustaining interventions. *Why it is correct:* This is the core tension of the case. The nurse's ethical and legal obligation is to advocate for the patient's expressed wishes and right to refuse care; her limitation is that she cannot independently write the order. These are in conflict. The resolution is not for the nurse to unilaterally act on one side but to escalate with increasing urgency until a provider resolves the conflict by entering an order that aligns with the patient's wishes. The rapid response system is the appropriate safety net because it guarantees a provider at the bedside. Documentation protects the patient by ensuring any provider who arrives is immediately aware of his expressed wishes. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses (the code status discrepancy has become clinically urgent because deterioration makes a code event plausible). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — requires the deterioration data to shift the urgency calculus.

4. *The situation / question:* At 1100 (Stage 3), the nurse activates the rapid response team. She must decide what information to include in the RRT handoff to ensure the code status issue is addressed alongside the clinical emergency. *Correct action:* The nurse must include in the handoff not only the clinical data (vital signs, SpO₂ trajectory, CXR findings, interventions attempted) but also, with equal prominence, the unresolved code status: the patient's clearly expressed and documented wishes against intubation and CPR, his assessed capacity, the absence of an advance directive, the current Full Code default, and her escalation history (three pages to the covering provider, attending unavailable). This information is clinically essential because it directly determines what the RRT can and should do — specifically, whether to prepare for intubation or to focus on comfort measures. *Why it is correct:* The RRT arrives prepared to intervene aggressively by default. If the nurse fails to communicate the patient's wishes and escalation history, the team may intubate a patient who has clearly and repeatedly refused it, causing direct harm and violating his autonomy. The nurse is the only person in the room who has witnessed the patient's statements throughout the day. Her handoff is the mechanism by which the patient's voice reaches the decision-makers. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3 — the RRT activation and the accumulation of prior escalation attempts make this the decision point.

5. *The situation / question:* At 1130 (Stage 3), the DNAR/DNI order has been entered, the advance directive has been completed with social work, and the patient is receiving comfort-focused care. The nurse must now evaluate whether the advocacy process was effective and identify any remaining gaps. *Correct action:* The nurse evaluates: (a) the DNAR/DNI order is present and correctly entered in the electronic health record, (b) the code status is visually confirmed (wristband, door sign per policy), (c) the advance directive naming Keisha as healthcare power of attorney is scanned into the medical record, (d) the daughter has been notified of both the clinical deterioration and the advance directive, (e) the palliative care consult has been placed, (f) the attending has been reached and has validated the plan, and (g) comfort measures (morphine for dyspnea, non-rebreather, positioning) are effective — the patient reports his dyspnea is "bearable." The nurse also identifies a systems-level gap: the three-hour delay between the first page to the covering provider and the code status resolution represents a period of vulnerability that should be communicated via an incident report or quality-improvement channel so that the institution can review its process for urgent goals-of-care discussions when the primary provider is unavailable. *Why it is correct:* Evaluation is not only about the individual patient outcome but about recognizing system failures that could harm future patients. The nurse's documentation of the escalation timeline is both a legal record and a quality-improvement data source. The nurse's advocacy responsibility extends beyond getting the immediate order — it includes confirming the order is implemented correctly, the family is informed, and the ongoing care plan reflects the patient's values. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3 — requires the resolution of the code status and the full escalation record.

6. *The situation / question:* Throughout the case, the nurse must decide what, how, and when to document each element of the patient's expressed wishes and the nurse's escalation efforts. *Correct action:* The nurse documents (a) the patient's verbatim statements at the time they are made (0700), including the context (unprompted, during morning assessment), (b) her assessment of decision-making capacity using the four criteria (understand, appreciate, reason, communicate), (c) every communication with a provider — the time, the provider's name, what was communicated, and the provider's verbatim response including stated plan, (d) every follow-up attempt and non-response, (e) the patient's repeated statements as they occur through the day (1030: "Please don't let them put a tube in me"), and (f) the resolution (DNAR/DNI order entered, by whom, at what time, after what assessment). Documentation is factual, objective, and contemporaneous — not retrospective, not interpretive, and not editorial. *Why it is correct:* Documentation serves three functions simultaneously: clinical (ensures any provider who opens the chart knows the patient's wishes), legal (creates a contemporaneous record that the nurse advocated and escalated appropriately), and ethical (gives the patient's voice permanence in the medical record so it survives any change in his ability to speak for himself). Late, vague, or opinion-based documentation undermines all three. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions (documentation is the operational tool that enables advocacy). *When it becomes answerable:* Spans all stages — but the full picture of what must be documented only becomes complete at Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse acknowledges Mr. Jeffries's statement with empathy ("I understand") but does not document his words or initiate escalation, planning instead to "mention it to the doctor when he rounds." This is wrong because verbal acknowledgment without documentation and action is not advocacy — the patient's wishes exist only in the nurse's memory, are inaccessible to other providers, and will be lost if the nurse becomes unavailable. Delay in escalation prolongs the period during which a code event would trigger unwanted interventions.

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse tells Mr. Jeffries, "I'll make sure no one intubates you, don't worry." This is wrong because the nurse cannot guarantee this outcome — she does not have the authority to write a DNAR/DNI order, and making a promise she cannot keep is both misleading and outside her scope. If the patient arrests before an order is placed, the code team is legally obligated to follow the Full Code status. The nurse should instead explain what she will do (document, escalate, advocate) and what the process requires (a provider order).

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse accepts Dr. Reeves's "around 1100" without documenting the conversation or informing the charge nurse, reasoning that the provider said he would come and it would be disrespectful to escalate further. This is wrong because undocumented communication cannot be verified, and the charge nurse needs to be aware of unresolved code status discrepancies on the unit for safety planning purposes. Respect for the provider does not override the nurse's duty to create a transparent record and activate the chain of command if needed.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse assumes that because Full Code is in the chart, she is obligated to physically assist the team in forcing intubation on a conscious, capacitated patient who is actively refusing at the bedside. This is wrong because a capacitated patient has the right to refuse any medical intervention, even life-sustaining ones. While the nurse cannot independently write a DNAR/DNI order, her role is not to force an unwanted procedure. The correct action is to immediately communicate the active refusal to the responding team, escalate urgently, continue treating the patient within the scope of existing orders, and ensure a provider evaluates capacity and enters the appropriate orders to honor the patient's refusal.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse withholds the PRN morphine because she is worried it will cause respiratory depression in a patient who is already hypoxemic, reasoning that giving a respiratory depressant would hasten death. This is wrong because morphine at the ordered dose (2 mg IV) is the standard of care for dyspnea in advanced cancer and acts primarily by reducing the sensation of breathlessness and sympathetic drive, not by suppressing the respiratory center at this dose. Withholding it causes the patient unnecessary suffering. The principle of double effect supports the use of appropriate symptom management even when a secondary effect (mild respiratory depression) is foreseeable, as long as the intent is symptom relief, not hastening death.

Attached to Decision Point 4: During the RRT handoff, the nurse focuses exclusively on the clinical emergency (vital signs, oxygen requirements, CXR findings) and does not mention the patient's expressed wishes or the unresolved code status, assuming the RRT will check the chart. This is wrong because the RRT is operating under time pressure and will default to the charted code status (Full Code) without proactive communication. The nurse is the only team member who has spent the day at the bedside and has witnessed the patient's statements. Omitting this from the handoff directly increases the likelihood of unwanted intubation.

Attached to Decision Point 5: After the DNAR/DNI order is entered, the nurse considers the issue resolved and does not verify that the order is correctly reflected in the electronic health record, does not place the wristband, and does not notify the daughter. This is wrong because an order that is entered but not implemented in the systems that bedside staff and code teams reference (visual identifiers, EHR code status field) may be missed during a subsequent emergency. Family notification is both an ethical obligation and a practical one — Keisha is now the healthcare power of attorney and must be aware of the clinical situation and the care plan.

Attached to Decision Point 6: The nurse documents at the end of the shift a single retrospective note summarizing the day's events from memory rather than creating contemporaneous entries throughout the shift. This is wrong because retrospective documentation is less reliable, may omit critical details or times, is more vulnerable to legal challenge, and fails to make the patient's wishes visible in real time to other providers who may access the chart during the shift. Contemporaneous documentation — at 0715 when the statement is made, at 0800 when Dr. Reeves responds, at 0950 when the second page occurs — is the professional and legal standard.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

1. Recognize a patient's verbal expression of end-of-life care preferences as a clinical cue requiring immediate nursing action — documentation, capacity screening, and provider escalation — even when no advance directive exists and no provider is immediately available.

2. Distinguish between actions within RN scope (documenting patient statements, assessing decision-making capacity at a screening level, escalating to providers, administering ordered comfort medications) and actions requiring a provider order (entering a DNAR/DNI order, changing code status) in the context of code status clarification.

3. Apply the chain of command appropriately and proportionally, escalating with increasing urgency as the clinical situation changes from stable to deteriorating, including activation of the rapid response team as a mechanism to bring a provider to the bedside when the covering provider is unresponsive.

4. Construct accurate, contemporaneous, and objective documentation of patient-expressed wishes, capacity assessments, and provider communications that serves clinical, legal, and ethical functions simultaneously.

5. Evaluate the effectiveness of the advocacy process after code status resolution by verifying that the order is implemented in all relevant systems, the healthcare proxy is notified, comfort measures are effective, and system-level gaps are reported through appropriate quality-improvement channels.
