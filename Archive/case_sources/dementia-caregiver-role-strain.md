# Caregiver role strain in home management of moderate Alzheimer's dementia

## PATIENT BACKGROUND
Margaret Tierney is a 78-year-old woman with a three-year history of Alzheimer's dementia, currently at a moderate stage. She lives with her 54-year-old daughter, Diane, who is her sole caregiver. Margaret was diagnosed by her neurologist and has been followed in a memory-care clinic every six months. Her other medical history includes hypertension managed with amlodipine 5 mg daily and lisinopril 10 mg daily, osteoarthritis of both knees, and a remote left hip fracture repaired five years ago. She has no history of falls since the hip repair. Margaret's husband died two years ago; Diane moved in at that time and has been managing all of her mother's care — medications, meals, bathing, toileting assistance, and nighttime supervision — while working full-time as an office manager. There is one other adult child, a son who lives out of state and contributes financially but is not involved in day-to-day care. Margaret has not been evaluated by her memory-care clinic in nine months because Diane cancelled the last appointment, stating she could not get time off work. A home-health nurse is making an initial visit today, ordered by Margaret's primary care provider after Diane called the office reporting that her mother's confusion and nighttime wandering have worsened over the past two months and she "can't keep doing this alone."

## INITIAL PRESENTATION
The home-health nurse arrives at 10:00 AM on a Tuesday. Diane answers the door in work clothes, appearing fatigued, with dark circles under her eyes. She states she took a half-day off work for this visit and needs to leave by noon. Diane states that her neighbor will stay with Margaret from noon until Diane returns from work, but that this is an informal favor and not a reliable long-term arrangement. She says, "Mom was up four times last night. She tried to go outside at 2 AM — I found her at the front door in her nightgown. I've started putting a chair against her bedroom door at night because I can't stay awake anymore. I know that's probably not right, but I don't know what else to do." Diane's voice is strained but not hostile; she becomes tearful when describing how she has not slept more than four consecutive hours in weeks. She adds that she has lost twelve pounds in the past two months because she forgets to eat, and that she called in sick to work three times last month. She says, "I promised Dad I'd never put her in a home."

Margaret is seated in a recliner in the living room. She is wearing a housecoat that is stained and has a faint odor of urine. She smiles at the nurse and says, "Are you here to see my husband?" She does not appear distressed. A half-eaten bowl of oatmeal is on the side table; Diane says Margaret ate "a few bites" this morning and refused the rest. Diane reports that Margaret has been refusing meals more often and has lost about eight pounds over the past three months. Pill bottles are visible on the kitchen counter — tops are off two of them, and a Wednesday-slot pill organizer is open, but today is Tuesday.

## ASSESSMENT FINDINGS
Margaret: T 36.8 °C, HR 76 and regular, BP 148/88, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Weight 58 kg (documented weight nine months ago at last clinic visit: 61.6 kg, confirming approximately 3.6 kg loss). She is alert but oriented only to her first name; she does not know the date, the season, or her address. She follows simple one-step commands ("squeeze my hand") but cannot follow two-step commands. Speech is fluent but with frequent word-finding pauses and occasional confabulation. She calls the nurse by her late husband's name twice. Skin is intact with no bruising, lacerations, or pressure areas. Bilateral knee crepitus with mild swelling; gait is slow and wide-based with a slight forward lean but she ambulates independently for short distances in the home. Grip strength is symmetric and adequate. Oral mucosa is slightly dry. Abdomen is soft, non-tender, with bowel sounds present. Perineal skin is intact but mildly erythematous, consistent with urine contact; Diane states Margaret has been incontinent of urine two to three times daily and wears pull-up briefs during the day but not at night because "she pulls them off." No fecal incontinence reported.

Diane: the nurse observes that Diane's hands tremble slightly when she opens the pill bottles. She reports headaches "almost every day," difficulty concentrating at work, and feeling "angry and then guilty" when Margaret asks the same question repeatedly. She denies any thoughts of harming herself or her mother. She states she has not seen her own primary care provider in over a year and has no current prescriptions for herself. She has not attended any caregiver support group and states she does not know what respite care is.

Home environment: the home is a single-story ranch. Grab bars are installed in the bathroom from the prior hip surgery. Night-lights are present in the hallway. The front door has a standard deadbolt that Margaret can unlock. The stove knobs are not covered or disabled. Throw rugs are present in the kitchen and hallway. Cleaning supplies are in an unlocked cabinet under the kitchen sink. A wooden chair is visible propped under the doorknob of a bedroom door at the end of the hall.

## LABORATORY DATA
Labs drawn at the primary care office one week ago (results in the chart): sodium 141 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, BUN 24 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose (fasting) 102 mg/dL, albumin 3.1 g/dL (reference 3.5–5.0), prealbumin 14 mg/dL (reference 17–36), TSH 2.8 mIU/L, hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, hematocrit 35%, WBC 6,200/µL, platelets 210,000/µL, urinalysis: trace leukocytes, no nitrites, specific gravity 1.028.

## CLINICAL COURSE

### Stage 1 — During the initial home visit (10:00–11:30 AM):
While the nurse is completing the assessment, Margaret stands from the recliner without warning, walks toward the kitchen, and turns on a stove burner. Diane rushes to turn it off and says, "She does this — she thinks she's making coffee. Last week she left the burner on for twenty minutes before I noticed." Margaret becomes mildly agitated when redirected away from the stove, pulling her arm from Diane's grasp and saying loudly, "Don't touch me! I want my coffee!" Diane responds by raising her voice: "Mom, stop it! You can't use the stove!" Margaret's agitation escalates — she begins pacing, wringing her hands, and repeating, "Where is Frank? I want Frank." Diane sits down at the kitchen table, puts her head in her hands, and says quietly, "I can't do this anymore. I'm failing her." The nurse observes that Diane does not strike, shove, or physically restrain Margaret at any point; her frustration is verbal and self-directed. Margaret's vital signs during the agitation episode: HR 92, BP 156/94, RR 20. She calms after approximately ten minutes when the nurse offers her a cup of decaffeinated tea and sits with her, speaking in a slow, calm tone. Margaret accepts the tea, sits down, and begins humming. Vital signs after calming: HR 78, BP 144/86, RR 16.

### Stage 2 — Later the same visit (11:30 AM–12:00 PM):
With Margaret calm and seated, the nurse speaks privately with Diane in the kitchen. Diane discloses additional information: she has been crushing Margaret's amlodipine and lisinopril tablets and mixing them into applesauce because Margaret spits out pills, but she is unsure whether Margaret swallows all of the applesauce. She estimates Margaret gets "most" of her medications "most days" but admits she missed giving them entirely two days last week because she overslept. She also reveals that Margaret fell in the bathroom three days ago — she found her on the floor next to the toilet, with no visible injury, and helped her up but did not report it to the provider. Diane states she did not report it because "I didn't want them to think I'm not taking care of her." She also mentions that her brother called last weekend and told her she "just needs to try harder," which made her feel worse. The nurse reviews the pill organizer and confirms that Tuesday's compartment is still full (today's medications have not been given), and Wednesday's compartment is empty (suggesting yesterday's pills may have been taken a day early or the organizer was filled incorrectly). Diane looks at the organizer and says, "I must have mixed up the days. I can't keep track anymore."

The nurse also learns that Diane has been giving Margaret diphenhydramine 25 mg at bedtime "to help her sleep," purchased over the counter, for the past three weeks. Diane states it "worked the first few nights but now it doesn't seem to help, and Mom seems more confused in the morning."

### Stage 3 — Follow-up home visit, five days later:
The nurse returns for a scheduled follow-up. Since the first visit, the nurse coordinated a referral to social work, provided Diane with contact information for the local Area Agency on Aging and a caregiver support group, and contacted the primary care provider about the diphenhydramine use, the unreported fall, the medication administration difficulties, the weight loss, and the blood pressure readings. The provider discontinued the diphenhydramine and ordered a pharmacy consultation for alternative medication formulations. The provider also placed a referral for physical therapy for fall-risk assessment and a dietary consultation for the weight loss.

At this visit, Diane reports she called the Area Agency on Aging and learned about an adult day program three miles from her home that operates Monday through Friday, 8 AM to 4 PM. She has not yet enrolled Margaret but states she is "thinking about it." She attended one online caregiver support group session two nights ago and says, "It helped to hear other people going through the same thing. I cried the whole time, but I felt less alone." She has removed the chair from Margaret's bedroom door. She purchased a door alarm for the front door, which she installed herself; it chimes when the door opens. She has not yet addressed the stove, the throw rugs, or the cleaning supplies. Margaret's medications have been given daily since the first visit; Diane is now using a phone alarm as a reminder. The pharmacy delivered amlodipine in an oral liquid formulation, which Margaret accepts in juice. The lisinopril is still being crushed in applesauce per the pharmacist's confirmation that the tablet is crushable.

Margaret: she appears slightly more alert than at the first visit. Diane reports Margaret slept through the night twice in the five days since diphenhydramine was stopped, and her morning confusion has lessened on those days. She is still incontinent of urine two to three times daily. She ate approximately 50% of meals over the past two days, up from the 25–30% Diane estimated previously. Weight today: 57.6 kg (down 0.4 kg from five days ago, but the trend is too short to interpret definitively). Vital signs: T 36.9 °C, HR 74, BP 138/82, RR 16, SpO₂ 98%. Perineal erythema is unchanged. No new falls. Diane reports Margaret attempted to open the front door once at night; the alarm woke Diane, who redirected her back to bed without incident.

Diane: she reports sleeping five to six hours on the nights Margaret slept through, and four hours on the other nights. She states her headaches have decreased from daily to "a few times this week." She has not yet seen her own primary care provider but says she made an appointment for the following week. She still becomes tearful when discussing her father's wish that Margaret not be placed in a facility, but she acknowledges, "Maybe Dad didn't know it would get this hard."

## KEY DECISION POINTS

1. **Situation:** At Stage 1, during the initial home visit, the nurse must identify which findings from the assessment of both Margaret and Diane represent significant cues requiring nursing attention. The data set includes Margaret's orientation deficits, the urine-stained clothing, the weight loss, the slightly dry oral mucosa, the mildly erythematous perineal skin, the elevated blood pressure, the wide-based gait, the intact skin without bruising, the symmetric grip strength, the normal temperature, and the adequate oxygen saturation; and Diane's fatigue, weight loss, trembling hands, reported sleep deprivation, missed work, tearfulness, the chair against the bedroom door, the open pill bottles, the wrong-day pill organizer, the unlocked front door, the uncovered stove knobs, the throw rugs, and the unlocked cleaning supplies.
   - **Correct action:** The significant cues are Margaret's orientation only to first name, the urine-stained clothing with perineal erythema, the weight loss (3.6 kg over nine months) with low albumin and prealbumin, the dry oral mucosa, the elevated blood pressure (148/88, and the medications may not be administered reliably), and the wide-based gait in the context of osteoarthritis, prior hip fracture history, and environmental trip hazards; and Diane's sleep deprivation, twelve-pound weight loss, trembling hands, missed work days, tearfulness, the chair barricading the bedroom door, the medication organizer error, and the environmental hazards (unlocked front door, uncovered stove, throw rugs, unlocked cleaning supplies). The intact skin without bruising, the symmetric grip strength, the normal temperature, and the adequate oxygen saturation are normal or expected findings that do not signal an acute concern.
   - **Why it is correct:** Recognizing the full cluster — nutritional decline, unreliable medication administration, incontinence-related skin risk, environmental safety hazards, and caregiver exhaustion — rather than focusing on any single finding is essential because the safety of the home care arrangement depends on both the patient's functional status and the caregiver's capacity. The intact skin, absence of bruising, normal temperature, symmetric grip strength, and adequate oxygen saturation lower concern for acute inflicted injury or acute infection, but they do not by themselves make the home situation safe or rule out other forms of abuse/neglect.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Recognize cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

2. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse must analyze the pattern of findings to distinguish caregiver role strain from elder abuse or neglect. Diane is using a chair to barricade Margaret's door, raising her voice during the agitation episode, and has not reported a fall or kept the clinic appointment. Margaret has weight loss, urine-stained clothing, and perineal erythema.
   - **Correct action:** The evidence supports caregiver role strain rather than intentional physical abuse. The nurse should avoid accusing Diane or assuming malice, but must still document the unsafe conditions, notify the provider and supervisor/social work, and follow agency/state policy for Adult Protective Services (APS) reporting if Margaret cannot be kept safe or if neglect is suspected. The pattern shows an overwhelmed, unsupported caregiver using unsafe but well-intentioned strategies, rather than intentional abuse or malice. The chair against the door is a desperate attempt to prevent nighttime wandering elopement, not punitive confinement; Diane's verbal frustration is self-directed ("I'm failing her"), not threatening toward Margaret; the missed appointment and unreported fall (disclosed at Stage 2) reflect Diane's fear of judgment and her inability to manage logistics, not concealment of harm; Margaret's skin is intact without bruising or injury patterns suggestive of inflicted trauma; and Diane is tearful and guilt-ridden, not dismissive or hostile. The unsafe strategies (door barricade, diphenhydramine for sleep, unreliable medication administration) are dangerous and must be corrected, but they arise from exhaustion and lack of knowledge.
   - **Why it is correct:** Differentiating role strain from abuse determines the nursing approach: role strain calls for nonjudgmental support, education, resource mobilization, and safety planning; suspected abuse or neglect would require mandatory reporting and a different intervention pathway. The distinction rests on the totality of the behavioral and physical evidence, not on any single finding in isolation.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Analyze cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

3. **Situation:** At Stage 2, after learning about the diphenhydramine use, the unreported fall, the medication administration errors, and the environmental hazards, the nurse must determine which safety risk is the most immediate priority to address before leaving the home today. The competing risks include nighttime wandering with elopement potential (front door is unlockable), unsecured stove access, the fall risk (wide-based gait, throw rugs, recent bathroom fall), the diphenhydramine use worsening confusion and fall risk, the unreliable antihypertensive administration, and the nutritional decline.
   - **Correct action:** The most immediate priority is ensuring Margaret is not left in an unsafe environment today. This includes replacing the bedroom-door barricade with a nonrestrictive door alarm/elopement alert strategy, immediately preventing unsupervised stove access (such as removing stove knobs, using knob covers, or disconnecting the appliance when not supervised), and helping Diane orient the neighbor who is staying with Margaret starting at noon on what to monitor today (stove safety, responding to the door alarm, emergency contacts, and when to call 911/Diane). While nighttime elopement is the highest-risk event, stove access represents an active, same-day fire and burn risk that must also be controlled before Diane leaves or Margaret is left unsupervised. The diphenhydramine use is a closely linked priority because it worsens confusion and falls/elopement risk, requiring provider communication today. The other environmental hazards (throw rugs, cleaning supplies) and medical issues (nutritional decline, blood pressure reliability) are important but subacute compared to these immediate physical safety concerns.
   - **Why it is correct:** Prioritization follows the principle of immediate threat to life and safety. Elopement from the home at night is the scenario most likely to result in serious harm or death in the near term, and it is happening now without an adequate safeguard. Unsecured stove access represents an active, immediate fire hazard. Addressing both before the caregiver leaves or Margaret is left unsupervised is critical. The diphenhydramine is compounding the risk by increasing confusion and sedation-related fall risk, making it the linked next priority.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Prioritize hypotheses.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

4. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse must develop a comprehensive home safety and support plan to present to Diane and coordinate with the care team.
   - **Correct action:** The plan includes multiple components, organized by domain. For wandering and elopement: remove the chair barricade immediately and replace it with a door alarm or door-contact sensor on the front door (and any other exterior door), discuss additional strategies such as a medical identification bracelet, and register Margaret with a wandering-response program if available locally. For stove safety: recommend stove-knob covers or disconnecting the stove when not in supervised use. For fall prevention: remove throw rugs, ensure adequate lighting, refer to physical therapy for gait and balance assessment, and ensure the grab bars are appropriately placed for current toileting needs. For hazardous materials: secure cleaning supplies in a locked cabinet. For medication management: contact the provider and pharmacist about liquid or alternative formulations for medications Margaret cannot swallow as tablets, establish a consistent administration routine with reminders (phone alarm, written schedule), and clarify the pill organizer system. For the diphenhydramine: contact the provider to report the over-the-counter use and recommend discontinuation, as anticholinergic medications worsen confusion in dementia and increase fall risk. For nutrition: refer to a dietary consultation, suggest small frequent meals of preferred foods, and monitor weight. For incontinence: establish a prompted toileting schedule (every two hours during waking hours), use absorbent briefs at night, and apply a moisture barrier to the perineal skin. For caregiver support: orient the neighbor today on immediate safety monitoring rules (preventing stove access, responding to the door alarm, using emergency contact numbers), refer to social work for assessment of community resources, provide information about respite care and adult day programs, provide contact information for a caregiver support group, and strongly encourage Diane to see her own primary care provider. For the unreported fall: document it, notify the provider, and educate Diane that reporting falls is essential for adjusting the care plan, not a reason for judgment.
   - **Why it is correct:** A safe and sustainable home care plan must address the patient's safety risks and the caregiver's capacity simultaneously. No single intervention is sufficient; the plan must be multi-domain because the risks are multi-domain. Framing the plan as support rather than criticism is essential to maintaining the therapeutic relationship with Diane and ensuring she follows through.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Generate solutions.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

5. **Situation:** At Stage 1, during the agitation episode when Margaret becomes upset after being redirected from the stove and Diane raises her voice in frustration and then says "I can't do this anymore. I'm failing her," the nurse must choose the most appropriate therapeutic communication response.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should first ensure Margaret's immediate safety (she is away from the stove and the burner is off), then address Diane with a nonjudgmental, empathic response that validates her feelings and opens the door to problem-solving — for example, acknowledging the difficulty of what Diane is experiencing, normalizing caregiver exhaustion, and expressing that asking for help is a sign of strength rather than failure. The nurse should not lecture Diane about raising her voice, should not immediately launch into a list of resources (which would bypass the emotional moment), and should not minimize the difficulty by offering false reassurance ("It'll get easier").
   - **Why it is correct:** Therapeutic communication with an overwhelmed caregiver requires validation before education. Diane's statement is an expression of grief, guilt, and exhaustion — responding with judgment or premature problem-solving would damage trust and reduce the likelihood that Diane will accept help. The nurse's role at this moment is to establish the therapeutic relationship that will make the rest of the safety planning possible.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Take action.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

6. **Situation:** At Stage 3, the nurse must evaluate whether the home care plan is becoming safer and more sustainable, or whether the situation remains unsafe and requires escalation (such as a higher level of home support, a formal capacity evaluation, or consideration of alternative living arrangements).
   - **Correct action:** The plan is showing partial improvement but is not yet adequate. Positive indicators: Diane removed the chair barricade and installed a door alarm, medications are being given daily with a phone alarm and the liquid formulation, diphenhydramine has been stopped with some improvement in Margaret's nighttime sleep and morning alertness, Diane attended a support group session and contacted the Area Agency on Aging, and Diane made an appointment with her own provider. Remaining concerns: the stove is still unsecured, throw rugs are still present, cleaning supplies are still unlocked, Margaret is still losing weight (though the trend is too short to interpret definitively), incontinence management has not changed, the adult day program enrollment has not occurred, Diane is still the sole daily caregiver with no respite in place, and Diane's own health has not yet been evaluated. The plan is moving in the right direction but requires continued follow-up, completion of the outstanding safety modifications, enrollment in the adult day program or arrangement of other respite, and ongoing monitoring of Margaret's weight, nutritional intake, skin integrity, and functional status. The situation does not currently require emergent removal or immediate facility placement, but the nurse should continue documentation, supervisor/social-work communication, and agency/state policy-guided reporting assessment if safety gaps persist or Diane’s capacity declines.
   - **Why it is correct:** Evaluating outcomes in caregiver role strain requires assessing both the patient's safety and the caregiver's trajectory. Partial improvement is expected — behavior change takes time, and Diane is demonstrating willingness and effort. The nurse's role is to reinforce progress, identify the gaps that remain, set specific follow-up goals with timelines, and maintain the threshold for escalation if the plan stalls.

## COMMON NURSING ERRORS

- Focusing exclusively on Margaret's clinical findings (the blood pressure, the weight loss, the incontinence) while overlooking Diane's cues of exhaustion, weight loss, trembling, and sleep deprivation — missing that the caregiver's deterioration is itself a critical finding that determines the safety of the entire care arrangement.
- Treating the normal findings (intact skin, no bruising, symmetric grip strength, normal temperature, adequate oxygen saturation) as reassuring enough to minimize the overall concern — while these findings lower concern for acute inflicted physical injury or acute infection, they do not by themselves make the home situation safe or negate the pattern of nutritional decline, medication mismanagement, and environmental hazards.
- Accusing Diane of intentional abuse or neglect based only on the chair barricade and raised voice, without assessing the full pattern, ensuring immediate safety, and following agency reporting/escalation policy. This misclassifies the situation and bypasses validation of caregiver role strain, while damaging the therapeutic relationship; the evidence supports role strain rather than intentional harm, though safety documentation and appropriate policy-guided reporting must still be followed.
- Prioritizing subacute concerns like nutritional decline or blood pressure management over the immediate safety threats of elopement and unsupervised stove access—the weight loss and unreliable antihypertensive administration are important, but Margaret turning on burners and wandering out of the house represent immediate life-threatening physical safety concerns.
- Prioritizing the medication organizer error as the top concern because it involves a "medication error" — the organizer mix-up reflects Diane's cognitive overload and must be addressed, but it is not the most immediate threat compared to nighttime elopement and the anticholinergic burden of diphenhydramine.
- Recommending that Diane "just get more help" without providing specific, actionable resources — vague advice is not a nursing intervention. The plan must include concrete referrals (social work, Area Agency on Aging, adult day program, caregiver support group) with contact information and follow-up.
- Advising Diane to continue the diphenhydramine at a lower dose rather than contacting the provider for discontinuation — anticholinergic medications are associated with worsened cognitive impairment and increased fall risk in older adults with dementia; dose reduction does not eliminate the risk, and the nurse should advocate for discontinuation and provider evaluation of the sleep disturbance.
- Responding to Diane's "I'm failing her" by immediately listing community resources or correcting her stove-safety practices — this bypasses the emotional moment and communicates that the nurse is more interested in the checklist than in Diane's distress, reducing trust and engagement.
- Telling Diane "You're doing a great job" as reassurance — this is false reassurance that dismisses the real difficulties Diane is experiencing and may make her feel unheard; therapeutic communication validates the feeling without minimizing or inflating.
- Concluding that the home situation is now safe because Diane installed a door alarm and is giving medications daily — multiple hazards remain unaddressed (unsecured stove, throw rugs, no respite care, ongoing weight loss), and a single visit's improvement does not establish a sustainable trajectory. Premature closure of the case would leave Margaret at continued risk.
- Concluding that the home situation is unsalvageable and recommending immediate facility placement — Diane is making measurable progress, is engaged with resources, and is demonstrating willingness to change. The situation warrants continued support and monitoring with clear benchmarks, not immediate removal of the patient from a caregiver who is actively improving.

## EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

- Recognize the full cue cluster of caregiver role strain — including the caregiver's own physical and emotional deterioration — as a critical determinant of patient safety in home dementia care.
- Distinguish caregiver role strain with unsafe compensatory strategies from intentional elder abuse or neglect, using the totality of behavioral, physical, and contextual evidence.
- Apply therapeutic communication principles with an overwhelmed caregiver, prioritizing validation and trust-building before education and resource provision.
- Develop a multi-domain home safety plan addressing wandering and elopement, environmental hazards, medication management, nutrition, incontinence care, and caregiver support, with specific and actionable referrals.
- Evaluate the trajectory of a home care plan by assessing both patient safety indicators and caregiver capacity, identifying partial progress, remaining gaps, and thresholds for escalation.

## BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the nurse has completed the full assessment of both Margaret and Diane, learned about the diphenhydramine use, the unreported fall, and the medication administration difficulties, and observed the agitation episode and Diane's emotional response.

- **Most likely condition:** Caregiver role strain. Diane is an unsupported sole caregiver for a mother with moderate dementia, exhibiting sleep deprivation, significant weight loss, functional impairment at work, emotional distress with guilt and tearfulness, and resorting to unsafe strategies (door barricade, over-the-counter anticholinergic sedation, unreported fall, missed medical appointments) driven by exhaustion and lack of knowledge rather than malice.
- **Plausible competing conditions:**
  1. *Intentional elder abuse or willful neglect as the primary explanation* — less likely here: the physical examination shows no bruising, lacerations, or injury patterns; Diane's frustration is self-directed and she expresses guilt rather than hostility or indifference; the unsafe strategies (chair barricade, diphenhydramine) are misguided attempts to manage real problems, not punitive acts; and Diane voluntarily requested the home-health visit, which is inconsistent with a pattern of concealment.
  2. *Caregiver burnout requiring immediate removal of the patient from the home* — wrong at this stage: Diane is distressed but not incapacitated, she denies thoughts of harming herself or Margaret, she is receptive to help, and the home hazards are modifiable with education and resources; the situation warrants intensive support and monitoring, not emergent placement.
- **Two priority actions:**
  1. Mitigate immediate home safety hazards today by replacing the bedroom-door barricade with a door alarm and disabling unsupervised stove access (removing stove knobs or using covers) before the caregiver leaves for work.
  2. Contact the primary care provider to report the over-the-counter diphenhydramine use and advocate for its discontinuation, as it is worsening Margaret's confusion and compounding the fall and wandering risk.
- **Two wrong actions:**
  1. Accusing Diane of intentional abuse based only on the chair barricade and raised voice, without assessing the full pattern, ensuring immediate safety, and following agency reporting/escalation policy.
  2. Immediately listing community resources and safety corrections during Diane's emotional moment ("I'm failing her") without first validating her distress — this bypasses the therapeutic relationship, reduces Diane's trust and engagement, and makes it less likely she will follow through on the plan.
- **Two parameters to monitor:**
  1. Completion of the specific home safety modifications (door alarm in place, stove secured, throw rugs removed, cleaning supplies locked) — these are the concrete, observable indicators that the immediate environmental risks are being mitigated.
  2. Diane's self-reported sleep, appetite, and engagement with support resources (caregiver support group attendance, adult day program enrollment, her own primary care appointment) — the caregiver's trajectory directly determines whether the home care arrangement is sustainable.
- **Two irrelevant parameters:**
  1. Margaret's oxygen saturation in isolation — there is no respiratory compromise or acute respiratory problem driving the care plan, and monitoring SpO₂ does not help evaluate caregiver role strain or home safety modifications.
  2. Margaret's score on a formal cognitive screening tool (such as the MMSE or MoCA) — while relevant for baseline staging, cognitive scores are not the primary short-interval outcome to monitor, as her moderate dementia is a chronic condition and will not change over the short term; the focus must be on home safety and caregiver coping.
