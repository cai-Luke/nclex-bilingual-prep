**CASE TITLE**
Vancomycin infusion reaction and nephrotoxicity in MRSA bacteremia with impaired renal function.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Mr. Gerald Toomey is a 67-year-old man admitted to a medical-surgical unit from the emergency department with methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) bacteremia confirmed by two sets of blood cultures drawn in the ED, both growing gram-positive cocci in clusters with preliminary MRSA identification. The suspected source is an infected arteriovenous fistula in his left forearm, placed six months ago for planned hemodialysis initiation; the fistula site is erythematous, warm, indurated, and draining purulent material. His past medical history includes stage 3b chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 1.6 mg/dL, baseline creatinine clearance estimated at 44 mL/min by Cockcroft-Gault), type 2 diabetes managed with insulin glargine 18 units at bedtime and a correction-dose sliding scale, hypertension managed with lisinopril 10 mg daily and carvedilol 12.5 mg twice daily, obesity (BMI 33), and bilateral sensorineural hearing loss for which he wears hearing aids. He has no documented drug allergies. The infectious disease service has been consulted and has ordered vancomycin with pharmacy-guided dosing. Pharmacy has calculated a loading dose of vancomycin 25 mg/kg IV (actual body weight 98 kg, loading dose rounded to 2,000 mg) to be infused over at least 2 hours, followed by a maintenance dose of 1,250 mg IV every 12 hours, with a vancomycin trough to be drawn before the fourth maintenance dose. Additional orders include normal saline at 75 mL/hr for hydration, blood cultures to be repeated in 48 hours, a transthoracic echocardiogram to rule out endocarditis, vascular surgery consult for the infected fistula, fingerstick blood glucose every 6 hours with sliding-scale insulin coverage, continuation of home medications, strict intake and output monitoring, and daily basic metabolic panel including creatinine. No pre-medications (antihistamines or antipyretics) have been ordered before the first vancomycin infusion.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The nurse receives Mr. Toomey at 1400 on admission day. He is alert, oriented, and conversational. He reports feeling "achey and feverish" for three days with progressive redness around his AV fistula site. He appears fatigued but is in no acute distress. He states he has never received vancomycin before. The pharmacy has sent the loading dose to the unit, and the nurse is preparing to initiate the infusion.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (1400, pre-infusion): Temperature 38.6 °C, heart rate 96 and regular, blood pressure 142/84, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. Skin warm, dry, and intact except at the left forearm AV fistula site, which shows a 4 cm area of erythema, induration, and tenderness with a small amount of purulent drainage at the cannulation site. Thrill palpable and bruit audible over the fistula. No rashes, urticaria, or angioedema anywhere on the body at baseline. Heart sounds regular without murmur. Lungs clear bilaterally. Abdomen soft, nontender, obese. Bilateral lower extremity 1+ pitting edema to the mid-shin (chronic per patient). Peripheral IV in the right antecubital fossa, 18-gauge, flushing without difficulty. Patient reports baseline mild bilateral hearing difficulty managed with hearing aids; he can follow conversation at normal volume with hearing aids in place. The nurse establishes a pre-infusion baseline for the findings that will change: skin color and condition (no flushing, no rash), heart rate (96), blood pressure (142/84), subjective symptoms (no pruritus, no chest tightness), hearing acuity (functional with hearing aids), and urine output (will begin tracking with infusion).

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn in ED at 1200): WBC 14,800/µL with 82% neutrophils and 9% bands (left shift), hemoglobin 11.2 g/dL, platelets 228,000/µL, sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 4.6 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L, BUN 32 mg/dL, creatinine 1.8 mg/dL (elevated above his baseline of 1.6 — likely pre-renal component from sepsis and volume depletion), eGFR 38 mL/min, glucose 198 mg/dL, lactate 2.2 mmol/L (mildly elevated), procalcitonin 4.8 ng/mL (elevated, consistent with bacterial infection), albumin 2.8 g/dL. Blood cultures pending. Urinalysis shows trace protein, no casts, no bacteria. The creatinine trajectory from baseline 1.6 to admission 1.8 is the critical value to follow — further rises will signal vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity superimposed on pre-existing CKD. The WBC and lactate trajectories will reflect treatment response.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Admission day, 1430 to 1600 (first vancomycin infusion). The nurse verifies the vancomycin order (2,000 mg IV loading dose over a minimum of 2 hours), confirms patient identity and absence of documented allergies, establishes baseline vital signs, and initiates the infusion at 1430 via an infusion pump at a rate calculated to deliver the dose over 120 minutes. The nurse plans to reassess vital signs at 15 minutes, 30 minutes, and then every 30 minutes during the infusion per unit protocol. At 1445 (15 minutes into the infusion, approximately 250 mg delivered), the nurse returns to check on the patient and finds the following changes: Mr. Toomey reports sudden onset of intense itching on his face, neck, and upper chest. He says his skin "feels like it's on fire." He denies shortness of breath, throat tightness, tongue swelling, or difficulty swallowing. Assessment: the face, neck, and upper trunk are diffusely erythematous and flushed, with a well-demarcated boundary at approximately the nipple line — the lower trunk and extremities are unaffected. Scattered raised, pruritic, blanchable wheals are visible across the anterior chest. Heart rate 118 (up from 96), blood pressure 108/62 (down from 142/84), respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 96%. No stridor, no wheezing, no angioedema of the lips or tongue, no urticaria below the waist. The patient is anxious but alert and breathing comfortably. He says: "Am I allergic to this? Should we stop it?" The nurse must rapidly distinguish this presentation from anaphylaxis and determine the correct immediate action.

The clinical reasoning: the distribution (face, neck, upper trunk with a clear inferior boundary), the temporal relationship (within 15 minutes of infusion start at a standard rate), the absence of airway compromise, angioedema, bronchospasm, or generalized urticaria, and the concurrent tachycardia with mild hypotension are classic for vancomycin flushing syndrome (historically called "red man syndrome"), a non–IgE-mediated histamine-release reaction caused by rapid infusion, not a true allergy. The nurse stops the infusion pump immediately, keeps the IV line patent with normal saline, places the patient in a supine position with legs slightly elevated, obtains a full set of vital signs, and notifies the provider. She reassures the patient that this appears to be a rate-related reaction, not an allergy, and that the medication is still needed and can likely be continued at a slower rate after pre-medication. The provider orders diphenhydramine 25 mg IV and acetaminophen 650 mg PO. The nurse administers both. After 30 minutes, the flushing begins to recede, the pruritus diminishes, heart rate recovers to 100, and blood pressure rises to 128/76. The provider orders the infusion to be restarted at half the original rate (extending the remaining dose over approximately 3 hours instead of the remaining 1.75 hours). The nurse restarts the infusion at the reduced rate at 1530. She monitors vital signs every 15 minutes for the first 30 minutes and then every 30 minutes. The infusion completes at 1830 without recurrence of symptoms. Final vital signs: temperature 38.2 °C, heart rate 94, blood pressure 132/78, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 97%. The flushing and wheals have fully resolved. The nurse documents the reaction, the interventions, and the outcome, and communicates to pharmacy and the provider that future vancomycin infusions should include diphenhydramine 25 mg IV premedication 30 minutes before the infusion and a minimum infusion rate of no faster than 1,000 mg per hour (i.e., 10 mg/min or slower). She does not document this as an allergy in the medical record — she documents it as an adverse drug reaction (infusion-related flushing syndrome). Urine output for the first 8 hours of admission: 240 mL (30 mL/hr).

**Stage 2** — Hospital day 3 (approximately 42 hours after the loading dose). Vital signs: temperature 37.8 °C (trending down), heart rate 88, blood pressure 134/78, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. The patient received three maintenance doses of vancomycin 1,250 mg IV every 12 hours (at 2000 on day 1, 0800 on day 2, and 2000 on day 2) without recurrence of flushing syndrome, each pre-medicated with diphenhydramine and infused over at least 2 hours. The vancomycin trough was drawn at 0730 (30 minutes before the fourth maintenance dose due at 0800), and the result returns at 0830: vancomycin level 22.4 mcg/mL. While 2020 consensus guidelines recommend AUC/MIC monitoring (target 400-600) rather than trough-only targets to minimize nephrotoxicity, the institutional trough-surrogate protocol flags 22.4 mcg/mL as supratherapeutic (surrogate target 15–20 mcg/mL); this increases the risk of nephrotoxicity, particularly in a patient with pre-existing CKD. The clinical pharmacist reviews the level and contacts the nurse at 0830 with the following recommendation, which the pharmacist is also communicating to the infectious disease physician: reduce the maintenance dose to 1,000 mg IV every 12 hours, recheck the trough before the third dose at the new regimen, and increase monitoring for nephrotoxicity (daily creatinine, strict I&O) and ototoxicity (baseline and daily assessment of hearing acuity and tinnitus). Morning labs (day 3): WBC 12,200/µL (improving), creatinine 1.7 mg/dL (slightly improved from admission 1.8 with hydration), BUN 28 mg/dL, potassium 4.4 mEq/L, lactate 1.4 mmol/L (improving), glucose 164 mg/dL. Urine output for the previous 24 hours: 1,440 mL (60 mL/hr average — adequate).

The nurse's role at this stage: she receives the pharmacist's recommendation and understands its rationale — the supratherapeutic trough combined with impaired renal clearance increases drug accumulation, which is the primary mechanism of vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity (acute tubular injury) and ototoxicity (cochlear hair cell damage, often irreversible). She confirms that the dose reduction has been ordered by the provider before administering the next dose. She does not administer the scheduled 1,250 mg dose — she holds it until the new 1,000 mg order is verified in the system. She initiates structured ototoxicity monitoring: she asks Mr. Toomey to remove his hearing aids and assesses his ability to hear a whispered voice at 2 feet in each ear (the bedside whisper test), asks about new tinnitus, dizziness, or changes in hearing, and documents the baseline findings for comparison. He reports no new tinnitus, no dizziness, and his hearing is unchanged from admission. She documents the trough level, the pharmacist's recommendation, the provider's new order, the held dose, and the ototoxicity screen. Repeat blood cultures drawn at 48 hours are pending.

**Stage 3** — Hospital day 4 (approximately 76 hours after the loading dose). Vital signs at 0600: temperature 37.4 °C, heart rate 82, blood pressure 148/88, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. The patient has received vancomycin 1,000 mg IV every 12 hours since the dose adjustment. The repeat trough (drawn before the morning dose) returns at 0730: vancomycin trough 18.6 mcg/mL (within the institutional surrogate target range of 15–20). However, the morning basic metabolic panel reveals a significant change: creatinine 2.4 mg/dL (up from 1.7 on day 2 and 1.8 on admission; baseline 1.6). BUN 44 mg/dL (up from 28). Potassium 5.1 mEq/L (up from 4.4). Bicarbonate 19 mEq/L (down from 22). eGFR 26 mL/min (down from 38 at admission). The nurse reviews the overnight intake and output record: over the preceding 12 hours, urine output has fallen to 180 mL total (15 mL/hr — oliguria by definition). The patient reports that he noticed he has been urinating much less and that "it looks darker." He also reports new, persistent bilateral ringing in his ears that started "sometime during the night" and was not present before. He denies dizziness or hearing loss beyond his baseline. Assessment of the AV fistula site shows improving erythema and reduced drainage — the local infection is responding. Repeat blood cultures from day 2 show no growth at 48 hours (a positive sign for clearing bacteremia). The echocardiogram from day 2 showed no vegetations (endocarditis ruled out). Lower extremity edema has increased to 2+ bilaterally.

The nurse recognizes the constellation: rising creatinine with oliguria in the setting of vancomycin therapy indicates probable vancomycin-induced acute kidney injury (nephrotoxicity). The new tinnitus suggests possible early ototoxicity. The rising potassium and falling bicarbonate reflect declining renal function. The nurse takes the following immediate actions: (a) holds the scheduled 0800 vancomycin dose — she does not administer it despite the trough being within target range, because the creatinine rise and oliguria indicate that renal clearance is deteriorating and further dosing will worsen toxicity; (b) notifies the infectious disease provider and the covering provider simultaneously, reporting the creatinine trajectory (1.6 → 1.8 → 1.7 → 2.4), the oliguria (180 mL/12 hr), the rising potassium (5.1), the new tinnitus, and the held dose; (c) contacts the clinical pharmacist to report the creatinine change and tinnitus, as pharmacy will need to recalculate dosing or recommend an alternative antibiotic; (d) assesses urine color and documents it (dark amber, concentrated); (e) ensures the IV normal saline is infusing at the ordered rate and checks for any nephrotoxic co-medications (she notes lisinopril is still ordered — an ACE inhibitor that reduces GFR and should be evaluated for temporary discontinuation by the provider); (f) rechecks potassium with a point-of-care iStat to confirm the lab value (returns 5.0, confirmed); (g) obtains a 12-lead ECG to evaluate for hyperkalemic changes (ECG shows normal sinus rhythm at 82 bpm, no peaked T waves, no QRS widening — reassuring but requiring ongoing monitoring); (h) performs a bedside hearing assessment — whisper test shows decreased acuity bilaterally compared to the day 2 baseline, and the patient confirms bilateral high-pitched tinnitus is constant and new. The nurse documents all findings, held medications, and provider notifications with times and responses. The infectious disease provider returns the call at 0815 and orders: hold vancomycin, obtain a stat vancomycin level (result 24.1 mcg/mL — the drug is accumulating despite the lower dose due to impaired clearance), switch to daptomycin 6 mg/kg IV every 48 hours (renally dosed) as an alternative MRSA bactericidal agent, hold lisinopril, obtain a renal ultrasound to rule out obstruction, and add nephrology consult. The nurse verifies and carries out the new orders, continues strict I&O monitoring with hourly urine output documentation, and reassesses the tinnitus at the end of the shift (still present but unchanged in character).

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *The situation / question:* At Stage 1, fifteen minutes into the vancomycin loading dose infusion, the patient develops upper-body flushing, pruritus, erythematous wheals on the chest, tachycardia (118 bpm), and hypotension (108/62). The nurse must determine whether this is anaphylaxis or vancomycin flushing syndrome and choose the correct immediate action. *Correct action:* The nurse recognizes the presentation as vancomycin flushing syndrome based on the characteristic distribution pattern (face, neck, and upper trunk with a clear lower boundary, sparing the lower body), the absence of anaphylaxis features (no airway compromise, no angioedema, no bronchospasm, no generalized urticaria), and the temporal relationship to the infusion rate. She stops the infusion immediately, keeps the IV patent with normal saline, positions the patient supine with legs elevated, obtains vital signs, and notifies the provider. She does not administer epinephrine. *Why it is correct:* Vancomycin flushing syndrome is a non–IgE-mediated reaction caused by direct mast cell degranulation triggered by rapid infusion. It is rate-dependent, not dose-dependent, and is managed by stopping the infusion, treating symptoms with antihistamines, and restarting at a slower rate — not by discontinuing the drug permanently. Anaphylaxis, by contrast, is IgE-mediated, involves airway and circulatory compromise, produces generalized (not regionalized) urticaria, and requires epinephrine. Misidentifying this reaction as allergy would result in discontinuation of vancomycin, the drug of choice for MRSA bacteremia, forcing the use of a potentially less effective alternative. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — the infusion reaction data is needed.

2. *The situation / question:* After the flushing syndrome resolves at Stage 1, the nurse must decide how to document the event in the medical record — specifically, whether to enter it as a drug allergy or as an adverse drug reaction, and what instructions to communicate for future infusions. *Correct action:* The nurse documents the event as an adverse drug reaction (vancomycin infusion-related flushing syndrome), not as a drug allergy. She specifies the reaction details, the interventions, and the outcome. She communicates to pharmacy and the care team that future vancomycin infusions require pre-medication with diphenhydramine 25 mg IV 30 minutes before infusion and a maximum infusion rate of 10 mg/min (or 1,000 mg/hr). *Why it is correct:* Mislabeling the reaction as a "vancomycin allergy" in the medical record would create a permanent barrier to the patient receiving vancomycin in the future, which could be life-threatening in subsequent MRSA infections. The distinction between an infusion-rate reaction (manageable with rate adjustment and pre-medication) and a true allergy (contraindicating future use) has long-term consequences for the patient's care. Accurate documentation is a patient-safety intervention. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — requires the reaction data and the clinical reasoning about the mechanism.

3. *The situation / question:* At Stage 2, the vancomycin trough returns at 22.4 mcg/mL (supratherapeutic; target 15–20). The pharmacist recommends a dose reduction. The next scheduled dose is due in 30 minutes. The nurse must decide whether to administer the scheduled 1,250 mg dose or hold it until the new order is entered. *Correct action:* The nurse holds the scheduled 1,250 mg dose. She does not administer a dose that she knows is based on a regimen producing supratherapeutic levels while a dose reduction is being processed. She contacts the provider to confirm the new dose order, verifies the 1,000 mg dose is entered in the system, and administers the reduced dose only after the order is active. *Why it is correct:* Administering a dose at the higher regimen when the trough is already supratherapeutic would further increase drug accumulation, amplifying the risk of nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity in a patient with impaired renal clearance. The nurse exercises clinical judgment by integrating the lab result with the pharmacist's recommendation — this is not "holding without an order" (which would be outside scope) but rather recognizing that the existing order is being actively revised and that administering the dose during this transition would cause harm. She communicates with the provider to obtain the corrected order before proceeding. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — requires the trough level and the pharmacist's recommendation.

4. *The situation / question:* At Stage 2, the pharmacist recommends monitoring for ototoxicity. The nurse must determine what ototoxicity screening within RN scope involves and how to establish a meaningful baseline given that the patient has pre-existing bilateral hearing loss. *Correct action:* The nurse performs a bedside hearing assessment (whisper test at 2 feet, each ear separately, with hearing aids removed) and asks specifically about new tinnitus, dizziness, or subjective hearing changes. She documents these findings as a dated baseline. She recognizes that the pre-existing hearing loss makes this patient higher-risk for clinically significant ototoxicity because he has less auditory reserve, and that any change from this documented baseline — not from normal hearing — is the threshold for concern. She plans to repeat this assessment daily and before each shift change. *Why it is correct:* Vancomycin ototoxicity manifests as high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss and tinnitus, which may be irreversible. The bedside whisper test and tinnitus inquiry are within RN scope and provide a serial, reproducible comparison. The key clinical principle is that the baseline must be individualized to this patient's pre-existing hearing status — a patient who already has hearing loss will cross the functional threshold of disability sooner than a patient with normal hearing, making early detection more critical. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — the ototoxicity monitoring recommendation and the patient's hearing history are both needed.

5. *The situation / question:* At Stage 3, the morning labs reveal creatinine 2.4 mg/dL (up from 1.7 on day 2), potassium 5.1 mEq/L, oliguria (15 mL/hr over 12 hours), and the patient reports new bilateral tinnitus. The vancomycin trough is 18.6 mcg/mL — within the target range. The next vancomycin dose is due at 0800. The nurse must decide what to do. *Correct action:* The nurse holds the 0800 vancomycin dose, notifies the infectious disease provider and the clinical pharmacist immediately, reports the creatinine trajectory, oliguria, rising potassium, new tinnitus, and the trough level in a structured format, and requests urgent evaluation for vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity. She does not administer the dose despite the trough being within the target range. *Why it is correct:* A trough level within the target range does not indicate safety when end-organ toxicity is clinically manifest. The creatinine rise of 0.7 mg/dL over 48 hours (a greater than 40% increase from the day 2 value), combined with oliguria, represents acute kidney injury likely caused by vancomycin-mediated direct tubular toxicity — a known dose- and duration-dependent complication exacerbated by pre-existing CKD, concurrent volume shifts, and the prior supratherapeutic trough. Administering another dose into a patient whose kidneys are failing to clear the drug would produce further accumulation, worsening both the nephrotoxicity and the emerging ototoxicity. The nurse's clinical judgment integrates the lab value (trough), the clinical signs (oliguria, tinnitus), and the trend data (creatinine trajectory) rather than relying on a single parameter. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3 — requires the creatinine trajectory across all stages, the urine output data, and the new tinnitus finding.

6. *The situation / question:* At Stage 3, after the provider has ordered vancomycin to be held and switched to daptomycin, the nurse reviews the medication administration record and notes that lisinopril 10 mg daily is still active and the morning dose has not yet been given. The nurse must decide whether to administer the lisinopril as scheduled. *Correct action:* The nurse holds the lisinopril and notifies the provider, requesting an order to discontinue or hold it. She does not administer it. *Why it is correct:* ACE inhibitors reduce efferent arteriolar tone and decrease glomerular filtration pressure, further lowering GFR in a patient who is already in acute kidney injury with rising creatinine and oliguria. In the setting of acute kidney injury, ACE inhibitors are routinely held to protect remaining renal function. Additionally, the patient's potassium is 5.1 mEq/L, and ACE inhibitors promote potassium retention, increasing the risk of clinically dangerous hyperkalemia. The nurse integrates the pharmacological knowledge (ACE inhibitor mechanism), the clinical context (AKI, hyperkalemia risk), and the safety principle (hold nephrotoxic and potassium-retaining agents in AKI) to make a sound clinical decision. She does not independently discontinue the medication — she holds and clarifies with the provider. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes (reassessing the safety of the current medication regimen in light of the changed clinical picture). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3 — requires the creatinine and potassium data from day 4 and the context of acute kidney injury.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse interprets the flushing, pruritus, and hypotension as anaphylaxis and administers intramuscular epinephrine. This is wrong because the reaction pattern (upper-body distribution, absence of airway compromise, absence of generalized urticaria) is inconsistent with anaphylaxis. Epinephrine in a 67-year-old with hypertension and tachycardia could precipitate a hypertensive crisis, myocardial ischemia, or arrhythmia. The appropriate first-line intervention is stopping the infusion and administering an antihistamine, not epinephrine.

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse stops the infusion and refuses to restart it, insisting the patient is "allergic to vancomycin" and requesting an alternative antibiotic. This is wrong because vancomycin flushing syndrome is not an allergy — it is rate-dependent and manageable. Discontinuing vancomycin for a non-allergic reaction forces a switch to a potentially less optimal agent for MRSA bacteremia (e.g., daptomycin, which cannot treat pneumonia if the source were pulmonary) and creates a permanent mislabel in the medical record.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse documents the event as a "vancomycin allergy — anaphylaxis" in the allergy field of the electronic health record. This is wrong because it permanently restricts future vancomycin use across all healthcare encounters, is clinically inaccurate, and could deprive the patient of a critical antibiotic in a future life-threatening MRSA infection. The correct documentation is "adverse drug reaction — vancomycin infusion-related flushing syndrome," with details of management and instructions for future infusions.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse administers the scheduled 1,250 mg dose at the original regimen because "the order hasn't been changed yet" and she does not want to practice outside her scope by holding a medication without an explicit hold order. This is wrong because administering a medication that the nurse has evidence is producing supratherapeutic and potentially toxic levels — while a dose reduction is actively being processed — is not adherence to scope; it is failure to exercise clinical judgment. The nurse's scope includes holding a medication and seeking clarification when patient safety data (the trough level, the pharmacist's recommendation) indicates that the current order may cause harm.

Attached to Decision Point 4: The nurse charts "hearing within normal limits" without performing a specific assessment and without accounting for the patient's pre-existing bilateral hearing loss. This is wrong because this patient's hearing is not within normal limits at baseline; he wears hearing aids for pre-existing sensorineural loss. Documenting "within normal limits" creates an inaccurate baseline that would mask any subsequent vancomycin-related decline. The assessment must be specific, individualized, and repeatable.

Attached to Decision Point 5: The nurse sees that the vancomycin trough is 18.6 mcg/mL (within the 15–20 surrogate target range) and administers the 0800 dose, reasoning that the level is therapeutic and the dose should continue as ordered. This is wrong because the trough value is only one piece of the clinical picture. The creatinine rise, oliguria, and new tinnitus are clinical evidence of end-organ toxicity that supersede the trough value in determining safety. A "therapeutic" trough in the setting of declining renal function means the drug is accumulating because the kidneys can no longer clear it at the previous rate — the next trough will be even higher. The nurse must integrate all available data, not defer to a single lab parameter.

Attached to Decision Point 5: The nurse notifies only the covering hospitalist and does not contact the infectious disease provider or the pharmacist, reasoning that the hospitalist can handle it. This is wrong because vancomycin dosing in MRSA bacteremia is an infectious disease and pharmacy co-managed decision. The ID provider needs to determine the alternative antibiotic, and the pharmacist needs to recalculate dosing for the patient's current renal function. Notifying only one discipline delays the coordinated response needed for a safe antibiotic transition.

Attached to Decision Point 6: The nurse administers the lisinopril because "it's a home medication and there's no order to hold it." This is wrong because the clinical picture has changed materially since admission. The patient is in acute kidney injury with rising potassium, and the ACE inhibitor will worsen both. The nurse has a professional obligation to hold and clarify a medication when the clinical context makes it potentially harmful, even if no explicit hold order exists. Administering a nephrotoxic, potassium-retaining medication during AKI with potassium 5.1 mEq/L is unsafe.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

1. Differentiate vancomycin flushing syndrome from anaphylaxis based on clinical presentation pattern, mechanism, and temporal relationship to infusion rate, and implement the correct nursing response for each (rate reduction and antihistamine versus epinephrine and discontinuation).

2. Document a vancomycin infusion reaction accurately as an adverse drug reaction rather than a drug allergy, understanding the long-term patient-safety consequences of mislabeling.

3. Interpret a supratherapeutic vancomycin trough level in the context of renal impairment, apply the hold-and-clarify principle before administering the next dose, and collaborate with pharmacy in the dose-adjustment process.

4. Perform and document individualized ototoxicity screening at the bedside, establishing a patient-specific baseline that accounts for pre-existing hearing deficits, and recognize new tinnitus as an early ototoxicity warning sign requiring escalation.

5. Integrate multiple data streams — creatinine trajectory, urine output trend, trough level, new symptoms — to recognize vancomycin-induced nephrotoxicity, hold the offending agent, identify co-administered nephrotoxic medications, and escalate to the interdisciplinary team (provider, pharmacy, nephrology) with a structured, complete report.ed, complete report.
