**CASE TITLE**
Intimate partner violence disclosure during routine prenatal care.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 28-year-old woman, gravida 2 para 1, at 26 weeks gestation presents for a scheduled prenatal visit at an outpatient obstetric clinic. Her pregnancy has been uncomplicated to date. Her first child is a 3-year-old son who lives with her and her partner. She has no significant past medical history. She takes a prenatal vitamin daily and has no known drug allergies. Her prenatal labs at 12 weeks were unremarkable: blood type A positive, antibody screen negative, rubella immune, HIV negative, hepatitis B surface antigen negative, RPR nonreactive, GBS status pending at 36 weeks. Her anatomy scan at 20 weeks was normal. She has attended all scheduled prenatal appointments, though she has arrived late to the last two visits and cancelled one appointment, rescheduling it a week later.

Her partner accompanies her to every visit and typically answers questions directed at the patient. The clinic's standard protocol includes routine intimate partner violence (IPV) screening at the initial prenatal visit, at 28 weeks, and at the postpartum visit, using a validated screening tool. The protocol specifies: "Screening must be conducted privately — the patient must be separated from any accompanying person, including partners, family members, and children over age 3, before screening questions are asked. If the patient discloses abuse, the nurse documents using the patient's own words, provides information about community resources including the National Domestic Violence Hotline, develops a preliminary safety plan with the patient, and notifies the provider. The nurse does not contact law enforcement or the partner without the patient's explicit consent unless mandatory reporting criteria are met (such as suspected child abuse or neglect, injury by a weapon, or vulnerable adult abuse). Mandatory reporting of IPV against a competent adult varies significantly by jurisdiction; this clinic follows state law which does not mandate reporting adult IPV without the patient's consent unless one of the aforementioned specific triggers is present."

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient arrives for her 26-week visit with her partner, who is seated beside her in the exam room. She is quiet and avoids eye contact with the nurse. She is wearing a long-sleeved shirt despite warm weather. When the nurse asks how she has been feeling, her partner answers, "She's been fine — just tired. The baby's been kicking a lot." The patient nods but does not elaborate. The nurse notes that the 28-week IPV screening is approaching and that the clinic protocol permits screening at the current visit if the opportunity arises during a private moment. The nurse needs to arrange a private encounter with the patient.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 36.8 °C, HR 82, BP 118/74, RR 16, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Weight 68.2 kg, up 1.4 kg from the visit two weeks ago, consistent with expected gestational weight gain. Fundal height 26 cm, appropriate for gestational age. Fetal heart tones 148 bpm by Doppler, regular. No vaginal bleeding or leaking of fluid reported. Mild bilateral ankle edema, symmetric, non-pitting. The patient winces when the nurse takes her blood pressure on her left arm. When the sleeve is raised for the cuff, the nurse observes a cluster of bruises on the left upper arm and inner forearm in varying stages of healing — some yellow-green, some deep purple. The patient quickly pulls her sleeve down and says, "I bumped into the door frame — I'm so clumsy lately."

**LABORATORY DATA**
Urine dipstick: protein trace, glucose negative, leukocyte esterase negative, nitrites negative. Hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL. Hematocrit 34%. Platelet count 218,000/µL. One-hour glucose challenge test (50 g glucola): pending, drawn today. Blood type and antibody screen: no new antibodies. All values within expected range for 26 weeks gestation.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — During the visit, the nurse uses a standard clinical workflow to create a private moment: she asks the partner to step to the waiting area while the patient provides a urine sample and undergoes the physical examination, explaining that this is routine clinic procedure. Once alone with the patient, the nurse conducts the IPV screening. She begins with a normalizing statement: "We ask all of our pregnant patients these questions because we know that safety at home can affect the health of both the mother and the baby." She uses the clinic's validated screening tool. The patient is initially guarded, giving one-word answers. When asked, "Has anyone at home hit, kicked, or physically hurt you in the past year?" the patient's eyes fill with tears. After a pause, she says quietly, "He doesn't mean to. It only happens when he's been drinking. He grabbed my arm last week — that's what the bruises are from. He pushed me into the wall a few weeks ago. He's never hurt our son. He's a good father." She then becomes anxious and says, "Please don't tell him I said anything. He'll know I told. It will get worse." She asks the nurse to promise not to tell anyone.

**Stage 2** — The nurse has documented the disclosure using the patient's own words and has notified the provider per protocol. The provider has seen the patient and confirmed no acute injury requiring emergency intervention. The nurse now sits with the patient to discuss safety. The patient reveals additional information: her partner controls the household finances, monitors her phone, and has threatened to take their son if she leaves. She states she is not ready to leave the relationship. She says, "He's been stressed about money. Once the baby comes, things will get better." She asks the nurse, "Do you think I'm a bad mother?" She reports that her partner has never directly harmed their 3-year-old son but that the child has witnessed the partner pushing and grabbing her on at least two occasions. She denies any access to weapons in the home. She has no independent transportation. She has a sister who lives approximately 30 minutes away but has not told her about the abuse. The patient states she does not want to call any hotline today but is willing to take a resource card "if it's small enough to hide."

**Stage 3** — Before the visit ends, the nurse works with the patient on a preliminary safety plan. The patient identifies her sister as a potential emergency contact and agrees to memorize the National Domestic Violence Hotline number rather than storing it in her phone, which her partner monitors. She agrees to pack a small bag with essential documents (her ID, insurance card, her son's birth certificate) and keep it at her sister's home. She asks the nurse to schedule her next prenatal appointment at a time when her partner is at work so she can come alone, and to call her only on a secondary phone number she provides. The nurse explicitly asks the patient what contact method is safe before sending any portal messages, discharge instructions, or resource links, recognizing that electronic communications are often monitored by abusers. The nurse provides a resource card designed to look like a routine clinic card. The patient asks, "Will this be in my medical chart? Can he see it?" The nurse must address the documentation and confidentiality question. At the end of the visit, the partner returns to the exam room and asks, "Everything okay? What took so long?" The patient looks at the nurse.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the initial presentation, the nurse observes behavioral and physical cues of IPV. The nurse must determine the appropriate next step before any screening can occur. *Correct action:* Arrange a private encounter with the patient by separating her from her partner using a routine, non-suspicious clinical rationale. Do not screen for IPV in the partner's presence. Do not confront the partner. *Why it is correct:* Screening for IPV in the presence of the suspected abuser is unsafe and ineffective. Using a routine clinical workflow avoids alerting the partner and protects the patient. The nurse's role is to create the conditions for safe disclosure, not to confront. *Skill:* Recognize cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient discloses physical abuse but immediately asks the nurse to promise not to tell anyone. *Correct action:* Do not promise absolute confidentiality. Explain that the nurse must document the encounter and involve the provider in ensuring her safety, but reassure the patient that the information will be shared only with the clinical team and will not be disclosed to her partner. Clarify that under state law, there is no universal mandatory reporting of IPV against a competent adult to law enforcement; however, mandatory reporting obligations apply if specific triggers like suspected child abuse/neglect are present. *Why it is correct:* Promising absolute confidentiality is dishonest. Transparency about the limits of confidentiality preserves trust while meeting professional obligations. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient reveals that her 3-year-old son has witnessed the partner pushing and grabbing her. *Correct action:* Recognize that a child witnessing domestic violence may constitute a reportable concern under child abuse and neglect statutes in many jurisdictions. The nurse must consult with the provider and, if required by the state law regarding child welfare, initiate a report to child protective services. This must be communicated to the patient transparently as an action taken to support safety, not a punitive measure. *Why it is correct:* Nurses are mandated reporters for suspected child abuse and neglect in all US jurisdictions. While adult IPV alone is rarely a mandatory report without consent, child welfare is a distinct trigger. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient states she is not ready to leave the relationship. *Correct action:* Respect the patient's autonomy and her decision not to leave at this time. Affirm that leaving is her decision to make when she is ready. Provide information about available resources without requiring her to use them immediately. *Why it is correct:* A patient experiencing IPV is the expert on her own level of danger. Premature departure without a safety plan increases risk. Pressuring a patient to leave may cause her to disengage from prenatal care. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient asks whether the disclosure will appear in her medical chart and whether her partner can access it, and safety regarding electronic communication arises. *Correct action:* Explain that the disclosure is documented for continuity of care and generally cannot be released to her partner without her authorization, but also assess whether he has portal access, shared-device access, proxy access, insurance/EOB access, or access to her phone before sending any electronic messages or resources. *Why it is correct:* Omission of IPV documentation compromises clinical safety planning. However, abusers frequently use shared portal logins, phone monitoring, or forced HIPAA releases to control victims. The nurse must address these specific technological vulnerabilities as part of modern safety planning. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At the end of Stage 3, the partner returns and asks, "Everything okay? What took so long?" *Correct action:* Provide a truthful but non-revealing response that normalizes the length of the visit — for example, "We had some routine lab work and screening to go over today, and we discussed some things about the third trimester." Do not lie about a medical finding, but do not disclose the IPV screening. *Why it is correct:* The nurse has a duty to protect the patient's safety, which means not alerting the partner. A response that fabricates a medical problem may create new complications. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Conducting the IPV screening with the partner present in the room or confronting the partner about the bruises.
- *(Decision 2)* Promising the patient absolute confidentiality, or falsely believing that all domestic violence must be automatically reported to police regardless of the adult patient's consent.
- *(Decision 3)* Deciding independently that because the child has not been directly struck, there is no reportable concern regarding child welfare.
- *(Decision 4)* Pressuring the patient to leave the relationship immediately, or judging her choice to stay.
- *(Decision 5)* Sending standard educational links or visit summaries to the patient's phone or patient portal without verifying that these communication channels are safe from the abuser's monitoring.
- *(Decision 6)* Fabricating a medical complication to explain the length of the visit, or displaying nonverbal anxiety.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Implement a safe IPV screening process by separating the patient from the accompanying person using routine clinical workflow.
- Respond to an IPV disclosure balancing empathy, honesty about confidentiality limits, and respect for patient autonomy.
- Distinguish between the lack of universal mandatory reporting for adult IPV versus the specific mandatory reporting obligations triggered when child safety is compromised.
- Develop a patient-centered safety plan that accounts for electronic/portal communication vulnerabilities.
- Protect the patient's safety through appropriate documentation practices and safety-preserving communication in the presence of the suspected abuser.
