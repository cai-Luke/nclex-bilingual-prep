

**CASE TITLE**
Warfarin initiation with enoxaparin bridge therapy at discharge following acute deep vein thrombosis.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 63-year-old man is being prepared for discharge from a medical-surgical unit after a five-day hospitalization for an acute left lower-extremity proximal deep vein thrombosis (DVT) extending from the popliteal to the common femoral vein, confirmed by duplex ultrasonography on admission day. His medical history includes obesity (BMI 34), type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 1,000 mg twice daily, hypertension treated with amlodipine 10 mg daily, and osteoarthritis for which he has been taking over-the-counter ibuprofen 400 mg as needed, typically three to four times per week. He has no history of prior venous thromboembolism, no known bleeding disorders, and no prior exposure to anticoagulants. He lives with his wife, manages his own medications, and works as a long-haul truck driver. He was started on a therapeutic enoxaparin dose of 1 mg/kg subcutaneously every 12 hours on admission day, and warfarin 5 mg daily was initiated on the evening of hospital day 2. His renal function is normal (eGFR 88 mL/min/1.73 m²). The facility's specific discharge protocol calls for continuing the enoxaparin bridge at home until the INR is therapeutic (2.0–3.0) on two consecutive measurements at least 24 hours apart, which is stricter than the standard minimum of five days of overlap with an INR ≥2.0 for at least 24 hours.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
On hospital day 5, the morning of anticipated discharge, the nurse enters the room to begin discharge teaching. The patient is sitting in a chair, dressed in street clothes, and states he is eager to go home. His wife is present and has a list of written questions.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (hospital day 5 morning): T 36.9 °C, HR 74, BP 134/78, RR 14, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Alert, oriented, in no acute distress. Left lower extremity: mild residual edema from mid-calf to ankle, no erythema or warmth, pedal pulse palpable, sensation intact, compression stocking in place. No bruising at enoxaparin injection sites (bilateral abdomen). No petechiae, no gum bleeding, no melena reported. Ambulating independently with mild discomfort in the left calf.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Hospital day 2 (warfarin start): INR 1.0, aPTT 29 sec, platelets 212,000/µL, Hgb 14.8 g/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL.
Hospital day 4: INR 1.6, platelets 198,000/µL, Hgb 14.5 g/dL.
Hospital day 5 (morning of planned discharge): INR 1.9, platelets 195,000/µL, Hgb 14.3 g/dL.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Hospital day 5, morning. The INR result of 1.9 returns while the nurse is reviewing the discharge medication list with the patient and his wife. The patient's wife asks, "The doctor said his blood needs to be between 2 and 3. It's 1.9 — is that close enough to stop the shots?" The patient adds that he took two ibuprofen tablets this morning for knee pain and asks whether he can keep taking ibuprofen at home since "it's just over the counter." The discharge medication reconciliation list generated by pharmacy includes warfarin 5 mg daily, enoxaparin 1 mg/kg every 12 hours, metformin 1,000 mg twice daily, amlodipine 10 mg daily, and ibuprofen 400 mg as needed. The patient also mentions that his wife has been reading online and bought a large bag of frozen spinach and kale to "build his blood back up" because she heard he might be anemic from the blood thinner.

**Stage 2** — Hospital day 5, early afternoon. The nurse has notified the provider about the ibuprofen on the medication reconciliation list and the patient's reported use this morning. The provider discontinues ibuprofen and writes for acetaminophen 650 mg every 6 hours as needed for pain, maximum 3 grams per day given the patient's age. The provider confirms that the INR of 1.9 does not meet the therapeutic threshold and that the enoxaparin bridge must continue at home; warfarin will continue at 5 mg daily, and the first outpatient INR is ordered for two days post-discharge at the anticoagulation clinic. During the teaching session, the patient asks how to give himself the enoxaparin injections at home — he has watched the nurses do it but has never self-administered. His wife volunteers to give the injections. The nurse begins a return demonstration. During the demonstration, the wife pinches a skin fold on the patient's abdomen and prepares to aspirate before injecting. The patient asks, "How will I know if something is going wrong with the blood thinner — like if I'm bleeding inside?"

**Stage 3** — Hospital day 5, late afternoon, one hour before discharge. The patient has successfully demonstrated subcutaneous injection technique with his wife. The nurse is completing the final discharge review. The patient mentions that he is scheduled to return to work driving his truck in four days and asks whether that is safe. He also asks whether he should "eat a lot of green vegetables to stay healthy" or avoid them. His wife pulls out a printed list of "foods to avoid on warfarin" she found online, which includes all leafy greens, broccoli, green tea, avocado, and soy products, and says, "I guess we just cut all of these out." The patient's final set of vitals before discharge: T 37.0 °C, HR 72, BP 130/76, RR 14, SpO₂ 98%. No new bruising. Left leg edema unchanged. The anticoagulation clinic appointment is confirmed for two days post-discharge, and the patient has a printed appointment card. The nurse reviews the signs and symptoms requiring emergency contact versus next-day clinic contact.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the wife asks whether the INR of 1.9 is "close enough" to discontinue the enoxaparin bridge. The nurse must determine whether the enoxaparin can be stopped. *Correct action:* Explain that the INR of 1.9 is subtherapeutic — it has not reached the target range of 2.0–3.0 — and that the enoxaparin bridge must continue. Additionally, while the national standard requires at least five days of overlap and an INR ≥2.0 for at least 24 hours, the facility's specific bridge protocol requires two consecutive therapeutic INR values at least 24 hours apart before enoxaparin is discontinued. The patient has had less than five days of overlap so far (warfarin first dose was given the evening of hospital day 2; by hospital day 5 morning he has received three doses). *Why:* Warfarin initially depletes protein C and protein S (natural anticoagulants with shorter half-lives) before it fully suppresses the procoagulant factors II, VII, IX, and X. During this early window, the patient is paradoxically hypercoagulable despite a rising INR. The bridge with enoxaparin provides reliable anticoagulation until warfarin achieves a stable therapeutic effect. Stopping the bridge prematurely exposes the patient to thrombus extension or embolization. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the discharge medication reconciliation list includes ibuprofen 400 mg as needed, and the patient reports taking two tablets this morning. The nurse must evaluate whether ibuprofen is appropriate for this patient. *Correct action:* Identify ibuprofen as a significant safety concern in a patient on warfarin and enoxaparin, hold the discharge reconciliation, and notify the provider to request an alternative analgesic. Do not discharge the patient with ibuprofen on the medication list. *Why:* NSAIDs inhibit platelet aggregation via COX-1 blockade and independently increase the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding. Combined with warfarin and enoxaparin, ibuprofen substantially raises the risk of a major bleeding event. This is not a dose-dependent caution — it is a clinically significant drug interaction that warrants removal and substitution. The patient's routine use (three to four times weekly) makes this a high-priority catch, not an incidental finding. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, during the injection return demonstration, the wife aspirates before injecting the enoxaparin subcutaneously. The nurse must evaluate the technique. *Correct action:* Gently correct the technique — aspiration is not performed with subcutaneous enoxaparin injections. The correct technique is to insert the needle at a 90-degree angle into a pinched abdominal skin fold, inject slowly, and not aspirate before or after injection. The prefilled syringe's air bubble should not be expelled before injection; it follows the medication to clear the needle and reduce bruising. *Why:* Aspiration with subcutaneous low-molecular-weight heparin injections increases local tissue trauma and bruising. Manufacturer guidelines and evidence-based practice standards specify no aspiration for subcutaneous enoxaparin. Because the wife will be administering injections at home without nursing supervision, correcting this technique error before discharge is essential to prevent injection-site complications and ensure the patient receives the full dose. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes (evaluating the adequacy of the return demonstration). *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient asks how he will know if he is "bleeding inside." The nurse must provide bleeding-precaution education that distinguishes emergent from non-emergent signs. *Correct action:* Teach the patient to recognize and categorize bleeding signs by urgency. Signs requiring immediate emergency care (call 911): sudden severe headache with confusion or vision changes, vomiting blood or coffee-ground emesis, large-volume bright red rectal bleeding or black tarry stools, coughing up blood, uncontrolled bleeding from any site, signs of stroke (sudden weakness, slurred speech). Signs requiring same-day contact with the anticoagulation clinic: new or worsening bruising disproportionate to injury, blood in urine, nosebleeds lasting longer than ten minutes despite pressure, bleeding gums that do not stop, heavy menstrual bleeding (if applicable to the patient's household members who may also need to recognize signs). General precautions: use a soft-bristle toothbrush, use an electric razor, avoid contact sports, wear shoes to prevent foot injuries, apply prolonged pressure to any cut. *Why:* Patients on dual anticoagulation (warfarin plus enoxaparin bridge) are at elevated bleeding risk. Distinguishing emergent from reportable bleeding empowers the patient to seek the right level of care at the right time — neither delaying a life-threatening hemorrhage nor flooding the emergency department for minor bruising. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the wife presents a list of "foods to avoid on warfarin" and proposes eliminating all vitamin K–containing foods from the diet, while simultaneously planning to increase spinach and kale intake to prevent anemia. The nurse must address both the dietary misconception and the vitamin K interaction. *Correct action:* Clarify that the goal is not to eliminate vitamin K–rich foods but to maintain a consistent daily intake of vitamin K so that the warfarin dose can be calibrated to the patient's usual diet. Abrupt large increases in vitamin K intake (such as adding daily spinach and kale smoothies) will decrease the INR and reduce warfarin's effectiveness, potentially allowing clot progression. Abrupt elimination of all vitamin K foods could cause the INR to rise unpredictably, increasing bleeding risk. The patient should eat a normal, balanced diet and keep his green-vegetable intake roughly the same from week to week. Additionally, clarify that warfarin does not cause anemia through nutrient depletion — his hemoglobin is stable at 14.3 g/dL — and that the spinach-and-kale plan is based on a misunderstanding. If the patient wants to change his diet significantly, he should inform the anticoagulation clinic so the warfarin dose can be adjusted accordingly. *Why:* Warfarin inhibits vitamin K–dependent clotting factor synthesis. Vitamin K intake directly opposes warfarin's mechanism. Wide swings in vitamin K intake cause INR instability, which is the primary modifiable risk factor for both bleeding and clotting complications on warfarin. Consistency, not avoidance, is the evidence-based dietary guidance. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient states he plans to return to work as a long-haul truck driver in four days. The nurse must evaluate whether this plan raises safety concerns that require provider notification. *Correct action:* Identify this as a safety concern requiring provider and anticoagulation-clinic input before clearance. The patient is on dual anticoagulation with a subtherapeutic INR, meaning his regimen is still being titrated. Long-haul truck driving involves prolonged immobility (a risk factor for the DVT that prompted this admission), limited access to emergency medical care, and potential impairment from anticoagulant-related bleeding events while operating a commercial vehicle. The nurse should advise the patient not to return to driving until cleared by his provider and should document the occupational concern in the discharge communication to the anticoagulation clinic and the primary care provider. *Why:* Prolonged sitting was likely a contributing factor to the initial DVT. Returning to extended immobility before therapeutic anticoagulation is established increases the risk of thrombus extension or pulmonary embolism. Additionally, a bleeding event while driving a commercial vehicle poses a danger to the patient and the public. This is a discharge-coordination and safety issue that the nurse is responsible for identifying and escalating. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Telling the patient the INR of 1.9 is "almost there" and implying the enoxaparin can be stopped soon based on a single value approaching 2.0. This ignores the facility protocol for two consecutive therapeutic values and the standard minimum five-day overlap, and could lead the patient to self-discontinue the bridge prematurely at home.

- *(Decision 1)* Assuming that because the INR is trending upward (1.0 → 1.6 → 1.9), the bridge is working and the exact threshold does not matter. The trend is reassuring but does not change the clinical criteria for bridge discontinuation.

- *(Decision 2)* Overlooking the ibuprofen on the medication reconciliation list because it is an over-the-counter medication and not a "prescribed" drug, or assuming the patient knows not to take it with blood thinners. OTC medications are a leading source of drug interactions, and the reconciliation list explicitly includes it — the nurse is the last safety check before discharge.

- *(Decision 2)* Advising the patient to simply "take less ibuprofen" or to take it with food as a sufficient risk-reduction strategy. Dose reduction and gastroprotection do not eliminate the platelet-inhibition interaction with anticoagulants; the drug class itself is the problem in this context.

- *(Decision 3)* Not observing the return demonstration and assuming verbal understanding is sufficient for a psychomotor skill. The aspiration error would not have been caught without direct observation, and the wife will be performing the injections unsupervised.

- *(Decision 3)* Correcting the aspiration error but failing to assess other technique elements — angle of insertion, site rotation, not rubbing the site after injection, and not expelling the air bubble — resulting in an incomplete competency evaluation.

- *(Decision 4)* Providing a generic list of bleeding signs without differentiating which require emergency action versus clinic follow-up. This either causes the patient to treat all bleeding as an emergency (leading to unnecessary ED visits and possible self-discontinuation of anticoagulants out of fear) or to underreact to a serious hemorrhage because "they said to call the clinic."

- *(Decision 5)* Instructing the patient to avoid all vitamin K–containing foods entirely, reinforcing the wife's online list. This creates INR instability and nutritional deficiency and is the most common dietary misconception in warfarin education.

- *(Decision 5)* Failing to address the wife's plan to increase spinach and kale for "anemia prevention," allowing a large dietary vitamin K increase that would directly antagonize the warfarin and lower the INR, potentially leading to clot extension while the patient believes he is doing something healthy.

- *(Decision 6)* Not recognizing the occupational history as clinically relevant to the discharge plan, treating the return-to-work question as a social or administrative matter outside the nurse's scope. Identifying prolonged immobility as a modifiable DVT risk factor and flagging it for provider input is squarely within nursing assessment and advocacy.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Apply the clinical criteria for safe discontinuation of enoxaparin bridge therapy during warfarin initiation, including INR thresholds and minimum overlap duration.
- Identify clinically significant drug interactions between NSAIDs and anticoagulants during medication reconciliation and escalate appropriately.
- Evaluate the adequacy of a patient or caregiver return demonstration for subcutaneous injection technique and correct errors before discharge.
- Provide structured bleeding-precaution education that differentiates emergent from non-emergent signs and supports appropriate patient self-triage.
- Teach evidence-based dietary guidance for warfarin therapy emphasizing vitamin K consistency rather than elimination.
