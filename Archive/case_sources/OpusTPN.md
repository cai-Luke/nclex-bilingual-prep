**CASE TITLE**
Severe chemotherapy-induced mucositis with total parenteral nutrition dependence and central line complications.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old woman with acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is on day 12 of induction chemotherapy (cytarabine and daunorubicin, "7+3" regimen) on an inpatient oncology unit. She has a triple-lumen peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC) in her right basilic vein placed on admission. Her medical history includes well-controlled type 2 diabetes (metformin, held during admission; sliding-scale insulin in use), mild chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 1.3 mg/dL), and a 30-pack-year smoking history. She is profoundly neutropenic (absolute neutrophil count less than 100/µL) and has been on neutropenic precautions. Five days ago she developed progressive oral and esophageal pain, was unable to swallow liquids, and was started on total parenteral nutrition (TPN) via her PICC line at 85 mL/hr, a dedicated lumen. Current orders include TPN with standard amino acids, dextrose, lipids, electrolytes, and multivitamins; morphine patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) for mucositis pain; fluconazole 400 mg IV daily for antifungal prophylaxis; cefepime 2 g IV every 8 hours for febrile neutropenia coverage; ice chips for comfort as tolerated; chlorhexidine-free sodium bicarbonate mouth rinses every 4 hours; strict intake and output; daily weights; and capillary blood glucose monitoring every 6 hours with sliding-scale insulin coverage.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At the start of the night shift the nurse receives handoff that the patient has been increasingly miserable over the past 24 hours. She is rating her mouth and throat pain 9 out of 10 despite PCA use, is refusing all oral care attempts, drooling into a basin, and has not voided in the past 5 hours. The day nurse reports the TPN bag was changed 2 hours ago and that the patient "spiked a temp" at the end of day shift but the provider was not yet notified. The patient appears flushed and is curled on her side.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline assessment at start of night shift: Temperature 38.9 °C (102.0 °F) orally (elevated from 37.2 °C twelve hours earlier), heart rate 112 and regular, blood pressure 96/58 (down from 118/72 earlier in the day), respiratory rate 22, SpO₂ 95% on room air. Weight is 64.2 kg, up 1.8 kg from admission weight of 62.4 kg. The patient is alert but appears fatigued and in significant distress from pain. Oral examination reveals confluent, deep ulcerations across the buccal mucosa, tongue, and soft palate with white-yellow pseudomembranes and areas of bleeding — consistent with World Health Organization grade IV mucositis. Lips are cracked and bleeding. Thick, ropy saliva pools in the oropharynx. The patient gags and winces when attempting to open her mouth fully. Skin is warm, flushed, and dry. The right upper arm PICC dressing is intact but the insertion site shows new erythema extending approximately 2 cm from the insertion point, with mild tenderness on palpation; no purulent drainage is visible, but the area was not erythematous at the previous dressing assessment 48 hours ago. Abdomen is soft, mildly distended, with hypoactive bowel sounds. No stool in 3 days. Peripheral edema is trace bilateral in the lower extremities. Urine output has been 120 mL over the last 8 hours (approximately 15 mL/hr), decreased from 40–50 mL/hr the day prior.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn 6 hours prior to the nurse's shift: WBC 0.3 × 10³/µL (ANC less than 100/µL), hemoglobin 7.8 g/dL, platelets 18,000/µL, BUN 32 mg/dL, creatinine 1.6 mg/dL (up from baseline 1.3), sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 3.2 mEq/L (low), chloride 101 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L, magnesium 1.4 mg/dL (low), phosphorus 2.8 mg/dL, calcium 8.0 mg/dL, albumin 2.1 g/dL (low), prealbumin 8 mg/dL (low, marking nutrition risk and inflammation alongside elevated CRP), blood glucose 268 mg/dL (elevated; was 312 mg/dL four hours before that), AST 42 U/L, ALT 38 U/L, total bilirubin 1.0 mg/dL, triglycerides 310 mg/dL (elevated), lactate 2.8 mmol/L (mildly elevated), C-reactive protein 14.2 mg/dL (elevated). Blood cultures were drawn from the PICC and peripherally at the time of the temperature spike but results are pending.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 4 hours into the night shift: The nurse notifies the provider of the new fever (38.9 °C), hypotension (96/58), tachycardia (112), oliguria, the PICC site erythema, and the lab abnormalities. The provider orders a 500 mL normal saline bolus (given cautiously given the patient's fluid retention), repeat blood cultures from the PICC and a peripheral site, a stat basic metabolic panel, lactate, and blood glucose. The provider also orders potassium chloride 40 mEq IV over 4 hours and magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 2 hours to correct electrolyte deficits before rechecking levels. The nurse administers the fluid bolus and electrolyte replacements. Two hours later, repeat vitals show temperature 39.2 °C (103.6 °F), heart rate 118, blood pressure 92/54 (worsening despite the bolus), respiratory rate 24, SpO₂ 94% on room air. Urine output is only 30 mL over the 2 hours following the bolus. The stat labs return: potassium 3.0 mEq/L (still low despite replacement), magnesium 1.3 mg/dL (unchanged), creatinine 1.9 mg/dL (rising), blood glucose 324 mg/dL (rising; the TPN dextrose load is outpacing the sliding-scale coverage), lactate 3.6 mmol/L (rising). The nurse notes the TPN infusion rate has not been adjusted and the blood glucose trend is upward. The patient is now shivering with rigors. The PICC site erythema has not changed. The nurse recognizes the clinical picture is worsening and urgently re-contacts the provider.

**Stage 2** — 4 to 8 hours into the shift: The provider orders transfer to a step-down unit for closer hemodynamic monitoring. A second 500 mL normal saline bolus is ordered, along with a continuous insulin infusion at 2 units/hr with hourly blood glucose checks per hospital protocol, given that the blood glucose is now persistently above 300 mg/dL and sliding scale is inadequate. The TPN rate is reduced to 60 mL/hr to decrease the dextrose load. The provider adds vancomycin 1 g IV (for empiric gram-positive coverage given the PICC site change and concern for catheter-related bloodstream infection, or CRBSI) and orders the interventional radiology team to be on standby for possible PICC removal and source-control planning. The microbiology lab calls with a preliminary blood culture report: the PICC-drawn culture is flagging positive for gram-positive cocci in clusters at 8 hours of incubation; the peripheral culture drawn simultaneously has not yet flagged. The early flagging of the PICC culture before the peripheral culture is highly concerning for CRBSI, though not definitive until paired cultures grow the same organism with >2-hour differential time to positivity. Repeat vitals after interventions: temperature 39.4 °C, heart rate 122, blood pressure 100/62 (slightly improved with the second bolus), respiratory rate 22, SpO₂ 95% on 2 L nasal cannula (now requiring supplemental oxygen). Blood glucose is 245 mg/dL on the insulin drip (trending down). Urine output improves slightly to 25 mL/hr. Oral assessment is unchanged — the mucositis is confluent and the patient has not performed mouth rinses in over 6 hours because of pain; the PCA morphine demand history shows she has been pressing the button at maximum frequency. The nurse faces critical decisions about line management, glycemic control monitoring, oral care facilitation, and pain reassessment.

**Stage 3** — 12 to 16 hours after the start of the shift (into the following day shift): The PICC-drawn blood culture is confirmed as coagulase-negative Staphylococcus (later speciated as Staphylococcus epidermidis), with the PICC culture turning positive more than 2 hours before the peripheral culture, confirming CRBSI. The provider orders PICC removal. Because urgent central access is required to continue TPN and maintain glycemic stability, a new triple-lumen PICC is placed at a new site in the left arm under ID guidance with repeat cultures planned, and TPN is resumed at 60 mL/hr through the new line. Vancomycin is continued; cefepime is continued for neutropenic fever coverage. After 8 hours on the insulin drip, blood glucose has stabilized at 180–200 mg/dL. Repeat labs: potassium 3.8 mEq/L (improving after aggressive replacement), magnesium 1.8 mg/dL (improving), creatinine 1.7 mg/dL (plateau, slight improvement), lactate 1.9 mmol/L (trending down), triglycerides 280 mg/dL (still elevated but decreasing with the reduced TPN rate). Temperature is 38.1 °C (trending down from peak), heart rate 98, blood pressure 110/68, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 97% on room air (supplemental oxygen weaned). Urine output has improved to 35–40 mL/hr. The patient reports mouth pain is now 6 out of 10 after the PCA dose was optimized and a scheduled IV acetaminophen was added; she is able to tolerate gentle sodium bicarbonate rinses again with coaching. The new PICC site is clean, dry, and without erythema. The nurse evaluates these trends and determines whether the patient is improving, identifies ongoing monitoring priorities, and plans for nutritional reassessment as the mucositis trajectory changes.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must synthesize the new fever, worsening hypotension despite a fluid bolus, rising lactate, oliguria, rising creatinine, and the new PICC site erythema into a clinical picture. What is the most likely emerging complication, and which cue is most critical to communicate to the provider? *Correct action:* The nurse should recognize and communicate that the constellation of findings — fever in a neutropenic patient, hemodynamic instability unresponsive to fluids, rising lactate, and new PICC insertion site erythema — is most consistent with sepsis, likely originating from a catheter-related bloodstream infection. The single most critical cue to emphasize is the persistent hemodynamic deterioration (worsening hypotension and tachycardia) despite the fluid bolus, as it signals progression toward septic shock. *Why it is correct:* In profound neutropenia the patient lacks an adequate inflammatory response, so even subtle signs of infection (low-grade site erythema without frank purulence) carry high significance. Persistent hypotension after a fluid challenge in the context of rising lactate meets systemic inflammatory response and early sepsis criteria and demands escalation. The PICC is the most probable source given new site changes and the indwelling device. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues and analyze cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1, after the post-bolus reassessment data are available.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the blood glucose is 324 mg/dL and rising while the patient is receiving TPN with a high dextrose concentration, and the sliding-scale insulin is not controlling it. What should the nurse do? *Correct action:* The nurse should notify the provider that the blood glucose trend is worsening despite sliding-scale coverage and that the TPN dextrose load may be exceeding the patient's insulin capacity, especially in the setting of physiologic stress (sepsis, steroids if applicable) and her diabetes history. The nurse should anticipate an order for a continuous insulin infusion and a possible reduction in the TPN infusion rate, and should not independently stop or adjust the TPN rate without an order. *Why it is correct:* TPN-associated hyperglycemia is common and is exacerbated by sepsis-related insulin resistance and the patient's underlying diabetes. Persistent hyperglycemia above 300 mg/dL worsens infection outcomes and immune function in an already immunocompromised patient. Abruptly discontinuing TPN carries a risk of rebound hypoglycemia (because endogenous insulin secretion has upregulated in response to the continuous dextrose load) and removes the patient's sole source of nutrition. The nurse should follow institutional protocol, which typically involves notifying the provider, increasing glucose monitoring, adjusting insulin, and providing a dextrose-containing IV fluid if TPN is interrupted. The correct approach is rate reduction plus an insulin drip, not immediate TPN cessation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1, once the rising glucose trend and ineffective sliding scale are evident.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the PICC-drawn blood culture flags positive for gram-positive cocci in clusters while the peripheral culture has not yet flagged. How should the nurse interpret this finding, and what nursing actions are appropriate? *Correct action:* The nurse should recognize that a differential time to positivity — the PICC culture flagging more than 2 hours before the peripheral culture — is strongly suggestive of a catheter-related bloodstream infection rather than a contaminant. The nurse should document and communicate this finding to the provider, anticipate an order for PICC removal, ensure that a plan is in place for alternative access and glycemic management before removal (since TPN cannot be infused peripherally at this concentration), and verify that vancomycin (empiric gram-positive coverage) is infusing. The nurse should not independently remove the PICC. *Why it is correct:* Differential time to positivity (the central line culture turning positive >2 hours before the peripheral culture with the same organism) is the standard method of definitively diagnosing CRBSI when paired cultures are drawn. The early flagging is highly concerning and warrants immediate empiric action. Gram-positive cocci in clusters are consistent with Staphylococcus species, the most common CRBSI pathogen. In a neutropenic patient, source control (line removal) is critical. However, because TPN is the patient's only nutrition source and requires central access, removing the line without planning alternative access or a glucose plan would interrupt essential therapy and risk hypoglycemia. The nurse's role is to coordinate the sequence: coordinate with the provider for alternative access or a glucose plan, empiric antibiotics, and source-control, then remove the infected line. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues and take action. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2, once the preliminary culture results and differential positivity data are available.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient has not performed oral care (sodium bicarbonate rinses) in over 6 hours because of severe pain, and the mucositis is confluent with bleeding and pseudomembranes. What is the priority nursing intervention regarding oral care? *Correct action:* The nurse should coordinate pain management so that oral care can be performed — specifically, timing a PCA bolus or requesting an order for a topical oral analgesic (such as viscous lidocaine rinse or "magic mouthwash" if ordered) 15 to 20 minutes before attempting gentle oral care. The nurse should then assist the patient with atraumatic sodium bicarbonate rinses, using an ultra-soft toothbrush if tolerated, or a moist gauze/foam swab if bleeding or unable to brush, given the thrombocytopenia (platelets 18,000/µL) and bleeding risk. The nurse should not skip oral care entirely, but must not imply care should be forced after refusal; instead, use analgesia to facilitate comfort and cooperation. *Why it is correct:* Oral care is a critical nursing intervention in mucositis management; it reduces secondary infection risk (bacterial and fungal superinfection of ulcerated mucosa), maintains mucosal integrity, and promotes comfort. In a neutropenic patient, the disrupted mucosal barrier is a primary portal of entry for systemic infection. Abandoning oral care worsens infection risk. However, the severely thrombocytopenic state (platelets below 20,000/µL) requires atraumatic technique to prevent hemorrhage. Coordinating analgesia before care is an evidence-based nursing strategy to encourage compliance without forcing interventions. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions and take action. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2, once the pain severity, oral assessment, and platelet count are all considered together.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the infected PICC has been removed and a new one placed, antibiotics are infusing, TPN is resumed at a reduced rate on the new line, the insulin drip has stabilized blood glucose around 180–200 mg/dL, lactate is trending down, and vitals are improving. Which set of findings confirms the patient is clinically improving, and which parameter still requires close monitoring and possible intervention? *Correct action:* The nurse should identify the following constellation as evidence of improvement: downtrending temperature (38.1 °C from a peak of 39.4 °C), heart rate returning toward baseline (98 from 122), blood pressure normalizing (110/68), lactate decreasing (1.9 from 3.6 mmol/L), improving urine output (35–40 mL/hr from 15 mL/hr), and blood glucose stabilizing. However, the nurse should flag the triglyceride level (280 mg/dL, still elevated) as a parameter requiring ongoing monitoring — sustained hypertriglyceridemia above 400 mg/dL from lipid-containing TPN can cause pancreatitis, and the level should be rechecked within 24 hours; the lipid component of TPN may need to be reduced or held if it continues to rise. Additionally, creatinine has plateaued (1.7 mg/dL) and has not yet returned to baseline, so renal function monitoring must continue. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating response to treatment requires the nurse to identify trends rather than single values. The lactate trend is the most sensitive marker of improving tissue perfusion. The triglyceride concern is specific to TPN-dependent patients — lipid overload syndrome is a known complication, and the nurse must monitor for it because the patient cannot simply be transitioned to enteral nutrition while grade IV mucositis persists. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3, once the post-intervention labs and vital sign trends are available.

6. *Situation:* Across the entire case, the nurse must decide whether TPN can be safely discontinued in favor of oral or enteral nutrition at any point. At Stage 3, the patient can tolerate gentle mouth rinses but is not swallowing liquids. Is it appropriate to discontinue TPN? *Correct action:* No. TPN must be continued. The patient has grade IV mucositis with confluent ulceration from the oral cavity through the esophagus, cannot swallow liquids, and is at high nutritional risk with systemic inflammation (albumin 2.1, prealbumin 8, elevated CRP). Transitioning to enteral nutrition (oral or tube feeding) requires evidence that the mucosa can tolerate intake — the patient must be able to swallow thin liquids without aspiration or significant pain, and the mucosal integrity must begin to recover (downgrading from grade IV). Tolerating mouth rinses is not equivalent to tolerating nutritional intake. The nurse should document the oral assessment, communicate the current mucositis grade, and anticipate that TPN will continue until mucosal recovery is sufficient for an enteral nutrition trial, typically coinciding with neutrophil recovery (ANC above 500/µL). *Why it is correct:* Premature discontinuation of TPN in a patient with grade IV mucositis would result in complete loss of nutritional support, worsening malnutrition, impaired wound healing, and further immunocompromise. The gut mucosa regenerates as the neutrophil count recovers, and the transition from TPN to enteral feeding should be gradual and guided by clinical swallowing assessment. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses (nutrition delivery method). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3, once the oral assessment and laboratory nutritional markers are available together.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

For Decision Point 1: Attributing the fever and hemodynamic changes solely to the mucositis pain and dehydration, without considering the PICC site as an infection source. This is a dangerous error because it delays recognition of CRBSI and sepsis in a neutropenic patient. Mucositis alone does not cause hypotension with rising lactate; the source must be investigated.

For Decision Point 1: Failing to report the PICC site erythema because there is no purulent drainage. In neutropenic patients, the absence of purulence does not rule out infection — the patient lacks adequate neutrophils to mount a purulent response. Subtle erythema at a line site in a neutropenic, febrile patient is a significant cue.

For Decision Point 2: Independently stopping the TPN infusion to control the blood glucose. Abrupt TPN discontinuation carries a risk of rebound hypoglycemia because of the sudden removal of the dextrose source while insulin levels remain elevated. If TPN must be interrupted, the nurse should follow institutional protocol, which typically includes notifying the provider, frequent glucose monitoring, adjusting insulin, and infusing a dextrose-containing IV fluid (like 10% dextrose) at the same rate. The nurse should never stop TPN without a provider order and a glucose management plan.

For Decision Point 2: Assuming the hyperglycemia is adequately managed by the sliding scale and not reporting the upward trend. Sliding-scale insulin is reactive, not proactive, and is insufficient for managing continuous dextrose infusion in a septic, diabetic patient. Failure to escalate allows worsening hyperglycemia, which impairs neutrophil function (even in a neutropenic patient, macrophage and residual immune function are glucose-sensitive) and worsens outcomes.

For Decision Point 3: Interpreting the gram-positive cocci as a likely contaminant and taking no action. While coagulase-negative Staphylococcus can be a contaminant, the differential time to positivity and the clinical context (febrile neutropenia, PICC site erythema, sepsis physiology) make CRBSI the most likely diagnosis. Dismissing the result delays source control.

For Decision Point 4: Using a standard toothbrush for oral care because "the mouth needs thorough cleaning." With platelets at 18,000/µL, mechanical trauma from a regular toothbrush can cause significant oral hemorrhage. Use analgesia before care, bland rinses, an ultra-soft toothbrush if tolerated, or a moist gauze/foam swab if bleeding or unable to brush. While skipping oral care entirely increases the risk of secondary infection, care should not be forced after refusal; it must be facilitated through adequate pain management.

For Decision Point 5: Focusing only on the temperature trending down and declaring the patient improved, while ignoring the elevated triglycerides and the plateaued creatinine. TPN-specific complications (hypertriglyceridemia, hepatic steatosis) require ongoing surveillance. An incomplete evaluation misses evolving complications.

For Decision Point 6: Attempting to transition the patient to clear liquids because she tolerated mouth rinses, interpreting rinse tolerance as readiness for oral intake. Swishing and spitting a small volume of sodium bicarbonate solution is fundamentally different from swallowing nutritional volumes through a severely ulcerated esophagus. Premature oral trials risk aspiration, pain crisis, esophageal bleeding, and caloric deficit.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

1. Recognize and analyze cues of catheter-related bloodstream infection in a neutropenic patient, including subtle line-site changes, differential blood culture positivity, and sepsis indicators, and distinguish them from expected mucositis-related findings.

2. Apply safe TPN management principles, including blood glucose monitoring and insulin titration during continuous parenteral nutrition, prevention of rebound hypoglycemia during TPN interruption, and surveillance for lipid-related complications such as hypertriglyceridemia.

3. Prioritize and coordinate evidence-based oral care in the setting of severe mucositis and thrombocytopenia, integrating pain management timing with atraumatic technique to maintain mucosal defense without causing hemorrhage.

4. Sequence nursing actions during clinical deterioration in an immunocompromised, TPN-dependent patient, including escalation of hemodynamic concerns, coordination of infected line removal with preservation of central venous access, and electrolyte replacement priorities.

5. Evaluate clinical response to treatment across multiple parameters — hemodynamic trends, lactate clearance, glycemic control, nutritional markers, and infection resolution — and identify which findings require continued monitoring versus those that confirm improvement.
