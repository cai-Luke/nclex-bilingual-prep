**CASE TITLE**
Multiple HIPAA disclosure events during a single shift: breach identification, mitigation, and reporting.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old woman, Mrs. Elaine Torrance, is admitted to a medical-surgical unit following an open reduction and internal fixation of a left tibial plateau fracture sustained in a workplace fall. She works as a warehouse supervisor for a regional logistics company. Her medical history includes type 2 diabetes managed with metformin and glipizide, hypertension treated with lisinopril, and mild chronic kidney disease (eGFR 52 mL/min). She is postoperative day one. Current orders include enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneous daily for DVT prophylaxis, cefazolin 1 g IV every 8 hours, hydromorphone 0.5 mg IV every 4 hours as needed for pain, a regular diet, physical therapy evaluation, and wound care with dressing changes every 12 hours. She is alert, oriented, and participates in her care. Her emergency contact is listed as her adult daughter, Megan Torrance, who visited yesterday evening. Mrs. Torrance has not designated any other individuals as authorized to receive her health information. The facility's Notice of Privacy Practices was provided and signed at admission. The unit uses bedside shift report with the door closed per facility policy, and the facility has a written HIPAA breach-response policy that requires the nurse who discovers or witnesses a potential breach to immediately mitigate the exposure, notify the charge nurse, complete an incident report through the facility's electronic reporting system, and ensure same-shift notification to the unit manager and the facility Privacy Officer according to the reporting chain. The policy explicitly states that the incident report is a quality-improvement document and is not to be referenced or documented in the patient's medical record.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The day-shift nurse, RN Jordan, arrives at 0700 for a 12-hour shift and receives handoff on Mrs. Torrance and four other patients. Mrs. Torrance is resting comfortably, reports pain at 4 out of 10, and her surgical dressing is clean, dry, and intact. The shift begins uneventfully.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline at 0715: T 37.2 °C, HR 78, BP 134/82, RR 14, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Left lower extremity is elevated on two pillows, surgical dressing over the proximal tibia is clean and dry with no strike-through, and distal neurovascular status is intact. The patient is alert, oriented, and her mood is pleasant. She mentions she is worried about how long she will be off work and whether her employer will find out the details of her medical condition.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn at 0600: Hgb 11.4 g/dL, creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (eGFR 52), glucose 168 mg/dL. Remaining basic metabolic panel and complete blood count are within normal limits.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1 — 0730 to 1030: A cascade of disclosure events unfolds.**

At 0745, the unit phone rings. A male caller identifies himself as "Robert Torrance, Elaine's husband" and asks for her latest lab results and when she will be discharged. RN Jordan checks the chart and sees no individual named Robert Torrance listed as an authorized contact; only the daughter Megan is listed. The caller becomes insistent, saying he has been married to the patient for thirty years and has a right to know. RN Jordan has not spoken with Mrs. Torrance yet this morning about whether she wishes to authorize anyone else to receive information.

At 0830, RN Jordan is charting at the nurses' station and notices that RN Casey, a nurse assigned to a completely different pod on the unit and who has no care responsibility for Mrs. Torrance, has Mrs. Torrance's electronic health record open on a workstation. RN Casey is scrolling through the operative report and the medication administration record. When RN Jordan asks, RN Casey says, "I just wanted to see what hardware they used — I'm thinking about going into ortho and I like to read the surgical notes when we get interesting cases." RN Casey has no assignment, consultation, or quality-improvement role related to Mrs. Torrance's care.

At 0945, a fax arrives at the nurses' station from Mrs. Torrance's employer, the logistics company. The cover sheet is on company letterhead and is addressed to the "Medical Records Department." It requests a copy of Mrs. Torrance's admission records, operative report, and anticipated return-to-work date, stating that the information is needed "for workers' compensation processing." There is no signed patient authorization form accompanying the fax. The fax was routed to the unit fax machine rather than to the Health Information Management (HIM) department.

At 1015, a second-semester nursing student assigned to the unit for a clinical rotation is performing a dressing change on Mrs. Torrance's surgical wound under the supervision of a clinical instructor. The student uses a personal smartphone to photograph the wound, stating it will be used for a clinical-course presentation. In the photograph, Mrs. Torrance's hospital identification wristband — bearing her full name, date of birth, and medical record number — is clearly visible. Mrs. Torrance did not give written consent for the photograph, and the student's clinical program has a policy prohibiting identifiable patient images on personal devices.

At 1025, RN Jordan is walking past the hallway outside Room 412 and overhears two nurses conducting a late transfer report in the corridor. They are discussing another patient's HIV status and medication regimen by name, and a family member of a neighboring patient is standing within earshot, clearly able to hear the conversation.

Vitals at 1030: T 37.3 °C, HR 82, BP 130/78, RR 14, SpO₂ 97%. Mrs. Torrance's clinical status is unchanged. Pain is 3 out of 10. The surgical wound appears clean with mild serous drainage on the inner gauze layer. No signs of infection.

**Stage 2 — 1030 to 1400: Mitigation, reporting, and an evolving situation.**

As these events unfold, RN Jordan takes immediate action to mitigate each exposure in real time, with formal reporting and follow-up continuing into the afternoon. After speaking with Mrs. Torrance about the morning phone call, the nurse learns that Mrs. Torrance and Robert Torrance are legally separated, and Mrs. Torrance explicitly states she does not want Robert to receive any information about her hospitalization. She says, "He's the last person I want knowing my business." RN Jordan documents Mrs. Torrance's verbal statement restricting disclosure to Robert Torrance and confirms that only Megan Torrance is authorized to receive information.

RN Jordan approaches RN Casey about the chart access. RN Casey is dismissive: "I didn't do anything with the information — I was just looking. It's not like I told anyone." RN Casey does not acknowledge the access as problematic.

RN Jordan contacts the charge nurse, who confirms that RN Casey's access constitutes an unauthorized access of protected health information because RN Casey had no treatment, payment, or healthcare-operations reason to view the record. The charge nurse instructs RN Jordan to complete an incident report and states she will notify the unit manager and the Privacy Officer.

Regarding the employer fax, RN Jordan does not release any records. The fax is secured at the nurses' station and not forwarded. RN Jordan contacts HIM to report the misdirected fax and to confirm the facility's process: records may be released to an employer for workers' compensation purposes only with a valid, signed patient authorization or a specific state workers' compensation statute provision, and the request must be processed through HIM, not the nursing unit.

RN Jordan locates the nursing student and the clinical instructor. The student confirms the photograph is still on the personal phone and has not been uploaded, shared, or transmitted. The clinical instructor was unaware the student had taken the photo. RN Jordan instructs the student to delete the photograph immediately in the presence of the instructor and RN Jordan. The nurse confirms immediate deletion from the device (including the "recently deleted" folder) and reports the event to the charge nurse and Privacy Officer, who will investigate potential cloud syncing. The clinical instructor acknowledges the violation of the clinical program's policy and states she will file a report with the nursing program.

RN Jordan addresses the hallway report conversation by approaching the two night-shift nurses, informing them that their conversation was audible to a visitor, and asking them to move to a private area. The nurses move into an empty room to complete their call.

By 1200, RN Jordan has completed the facility's electronic incident reporting process, documenting both RN Casey's unauthorized chart access and the student's unauthorized photograph, ensuring the Privacy Officer is notified of all suspected privacy events. The report for the electronic access includes the time, the specific records viewed, and the steps taken. RN Jordan does not document the incident reports or the breaches in Mrs. Torrance's medical record.

Vitals at 1400: T 37.1 °C, HR 76, BP 128/76, RR 14, SpO₂ 98%. Mrs. Torrance is tolerating a regular diet, ambulated to the bathroom with a walker and physical therapy assistance, and reports pain at 3 out of 10. Wound dressing is clean and dry after the morning change.

**Stage 3 — 1400 to 1900: Follow-through, evaluation, and system safeguards.**

At 1430, the unit manager and the Privacy Officer meet with RN Jordan and the charge nurse. The Privacy Officer confirms that RN Casey's access will be investigated through the facility's formal breach-determination process. The electronic health record's audit trail has captured the exact time, duration, and specific screens RN Casey accessed. The Privacy Officer states that if the investigation confirms the access was unauthorized and not for treatment, payment, or operations, the facility will determine whether individual notification to Mrs. Torrance is required under the breach notification rule, based on a risk assessment of whether the access compromised the privacy of the information. The Privacy Officer also confirms that the nursing student's photograph, because it was deleted before transmission and contained identifiable information, will be reviewed as a potential breach as well, and the clinical affiliation agreement with the nursing program will be referenced.

At 1500, HIM contacts RN Jordan to confirm that the employer's fax request has been received by HIM and that no records will be released without a signed authorization from Mrs. Torrance or a valid legal basis. HIM will contact the employer to inform them of the required process.

At 1530, Mrs. Torrance asks RN Jordan, "Did something happen with my chart? A friend of mine works here and she mentioned that someone was looking at my records who shouldn't have been." RN Jordan acknowledges that the facility is aware of a concern and that the Privacy Officer is handling it, without disclosing details of the investigation or the identity of the staff member involved. RN Jordan reassures Mrs. Torrance that her privacy is being taken seriously and that she will be contacted by the Privacy Officer if notification is warranted.

At 1700, the charge nurse confirms that the following system safeguards are being reviewed or implemented: the unit fax machine will have signage reminding staff to redirect misdirected records requests to HIM; the clinical instructor will reinforce the prohibition on identifiable patient photographs on personal devices at the next pre-clinical conference; the unit will conduct a brief in-service on minimum-necessary access and the facility's break-the-glass audit process for electronic records; and bedside shift report and phone report will be reviewed at the next staff meeting with emphasis on conducting all patient-related conversations in private spaces.

Vitals at 1900: T 37.0 °C, HR 74, BP 126/74, RR 14, SpO₂ 98%. Mrs. Torrance is comfortable, pain 2 out of 10, wound is clean, and she is progressing well postoperatively. The clinical situation is stable. The shift ends with the incident report filed, the Privacy Officer investigation initiated, no records released to the employer, the identifiable photograph deleted, and the hallway conversation interrupted.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* Across Stage 1, multiple events occur in rapid succession — the phone caller requesting results, RN Casey accessing the chart without a care role, the employer fax requesting records without authorization, the nursing student photographing the wound with the identifiable wristband visible, and the hallway conversation overheard by a visitor. The nurse must identify which of these events represent actual or potential violations of patient privacy and which, if any, are permissible. *Correct action:* RN Casey accessing the chart without a treatment/payment/operations purpose and the nursing student photographing an identifiable patient image on a personal device without consent are active privacy violations. The phone caller requesting information without being listed as an authorized contact and the employer fax requesting records without a signed patient authorization are significant risks for improper disclosure if acted upon. The hallway conversation overheard by a visitor is also a privacy risk — it involves another patient's HIV status and name spoken in a public area. None of these five events is permissible as it occurred. *Why it is correct:* Protected health information may be disclosed only to individuals the patient has authorized, to those with a treatment/payment/operations need, or under specific legal exceptions. The caller is not authorized. RN Casey has no care role. The employer has no signed authorization. The student created an identifiable record on a personal device without consent. The hallway conversation failed to use reasonable safeguards to limit incidental disclosure of another patient's sensitive information. Recognizing all five as problematic — rather than dismissing any as harmless — is the foundational step. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* Having identified the five events, the nurse must classify each one according to whether it is clearly impermissible, potentially permissible under specific conditions, or an incidental disclosure that may or may not constitute a violation. *Correct action:* RN Casey's chart access is clearly impermissible — there is no treatment, payment, or operations justification, and curiosity is not a permitted purpose. The nursing student's identifiable photograph is clearly impermissible — it was taken on a personal device without patient consent and violates both HIPAA and the clinical program's policy. The employer's fax request is impermissible as submitted — records cannot be released without a signed patient authorization or a valid legal basis, and the request bypassed HIM. The phone caller's request is impermissible unless the nurse can verify the caller's identity and confirm the patient has authorized that individual to receive information; as it stands, the caller is not listed and the patient has not been consulted. The hallway conversation is an incidental disclosure that crossed into impermissible territory because reasonable safeguards (private location, lowered voices, closed doors) were not used, and the information disclosed — another patient's HIV status by name — is particularly sensitive. *Why it is correct:* HIPAA's minimum-necessary standard and the distinction between permissible disclosures (treatment/payment/operations, patient authorization, legal exceptions) and impermissible ones (curiosity, unauthorized access, release without authorization) govern the classification. Incidental disclosures are not violations when reasonable safeguards are in place, but the hallway conversation lacked those safeguards. The employer request is not automatically permissible under workers' compensation without proper authorization or a specific statutory provision processed through HIM. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* With multiple events requiring action, the nurse must evaluate the severity and nature of each event. Which event is the clearest completed, non-incidental unauthorized access requiring formal breach-response investigation? *Correct action:* RN Casey's unauthorized chart access. *Why it is correct:* RN Casey is a workforce member who accessed protected health information without any treatment, payment, or operations purpose. This is a completed unauthorized access — the information has already been viewed. Unlike the phone caller (where no information was disclosed because the nurse had not yet released anything), the employer fax (where no records have been sent), or the hallway conversation (which involved a different patient and has already ended), RN Casey's access is a confirmed exposure of Mrs. Torrance's protected health information by a facility workforce member, which clearly triggers the facility's formal breach-response policy. The nursing student's photograph is also a serious and active breach, but RN Casey's access involved a staff member with ongoing system access who purposefully bypassed role-based minimum-necessary standards to view the full medical record (operative report, medication administration record), representing a broad and logged exposure that demands immediate institutional investigation and audit-trail preservation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1 (the access is witnessed at 0830).

4. *Situation:* The nurse must determine the full set of appropriate actions to address all five events. *Correct action:* For the phone caller — do not release any information; speak with Mrs. Torrance to determine whether she wishes to authorize Robert Torrance or anyone else; document her stated restriction. For RN Casey's chart access — ask RN Casey to exit the record immediately, notify the charge nurse, complete an incident report per facility policy, and support notification of the unit manager and Privacy Officer; do not document the incident report in the patient's medical record. For the employer fax — do not release any records from the unit; secure the fax; redirect the request to HIM, which will process it only with a valid signed authorization or applicable legal basis. For the nursing student's photograph — instruct the student to delete the photograph immediately in the presence of the nurse and the clinical instructor; confirm immediate deletion from the device (including recently deleted folders); ensure the event is reported so the Privacy Officer can investigate potential cloud syncing; and ensure the clinical instructor reports the violation to the nursing program. For the hallway conversation — interrupt the conversation, direct the nurses to a private area, and remind them that patient-identifiable information must be discussed only in private settings with reasonable safeguards. *Why it is correct:* Each action applies the minimum-necessary principle and the facility's breach-response obligations. The nurse's role is to mitigate the exposure (stop ongoing disclosure, delete identifiable images, secure misdirected documents), report through the proper channel (incident report, charge nurse, Privacy Officer — not the medical record), and prevent further unauthorized access. The incident report is a quality-improvement/risk-management document kept separate from the clinical record; the medical record should contain only patient-care documentation and should not reference the incident report. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2 (the full picture of what has occurred and the patient's stated wishes are needed to determine the complete action set).

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, having identified RN Casey's unauthorized access as the priority breach and knowing the full set of needed actions, the nurse must choose the first action to take. *Correct action:* Ask RN Casey to close the record/log out immediately, then notify the charge nurse and follow the breach-reporting chain. *Why it is correct:* The facility's breach-response policy directs the discovering nurse to immediately mitigate the exposure and notify the charge nurse. For an active chart access, mitigation means immediately stopping the exposure by having the unauthorized user exit the record. This is not a disciplinary action, but rather immediate containment. Once contained, notifying the charge nurse is the next step in the defined chain (charge nurse → incident report → unit manager → Privacy Officer). The nurse should not confront RN Casey in a disciplinary capacity (that is a management function), should not attempt to file the incident report before notifying the charge nurse, and should not delay reporting to address the less urgent events first. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse evaluates whether the breach has been appropriately contained, reported, and whether recurrence-prevention measures are in place. *Correct action:* The breach is contained and properly reported when the following are confirmed: the incident reporting process is complete and has not been documented in the patient's medical record; the Privacy Officer has been notified and has initiated the formal breach-determination process using the EHR audit trail; no protected health information was released to the unauthorized phone caller or the employer; the identifiable photograph has been deleted, reported to the Privacy Officer, and the clinical program notified; the hallway conversation was interrupted and moved to a private area; and system safeguards — fax-machine signage, in-service on minimum-necessary access, reinforcement of private-space reporting, and clinical-program policy reinforcement — are being implemented to prevent recurrence. *Why it is correct:* Effective breach response requires mitigation (stopping the exposure), reporting (incident report through the proper system, not in the medical record), investigation (Privacy Officer using the audit trail), notification determination (risk assessment for whether the patient must be formally notified), and prevention (system-level changes to reduce future risk). The nurse evaluates all five components. The critical teaching point about documentation is that the incident report is a privileged quality-improvement/risk-management document — documenting it in the medical record would compromise that protection and is a policy violation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Dismissing RN Casey's chart access as harmless because "she didn't do anything with the information" and failing to recognize it as a privacy violation. Viewing protected health information without a treatment, payment, or operations purpose is itself the violation, regardless of whether the information is further disclosed. A nurse who accepts the "just looking" rationale misses the most significant breach event of the shift.

- *(Decision 1)* Treating the hallway conversation as acceptable because it involved a different patient and was "almost over anyway," rather than recognizing it as a privacy risk requiring intervention. The fact that it involves another patient does not relieve the witnessing nurse of the obligation to act — reasonable safeguards were not used, and a visitor overheard identifiable, sensitive health information.

- *(Decision 2)* Classifying the employer's fax request as permissible because it mentions workers' compensation, assuming that workers' compensation automatically permits release without patient authorization. Workers' compensation provisions vary by state and require specific legal basis or a signed authorization processed through HIM — the nurse on the unit does not independently release records based on an employer's assertion.

- *(Decision 2)* Classifying the phone caller's request as permissible after the caller states he is the patient's husband, without verifying authorization with the patient. Marital status alone does not confer automatic right to receive protected health information; the patient must authorize disclosure, and in this case the patient explicitly does not want the caller to receive information.

- *(Decision 3)* Prioritizing the nursing student's photograph over RN Casey's chart access as the most urgent breach. While the photograph is a serious violation, it is contained on a single device that has not been transmitted, and it can be immediately deleted. RN Casey's access involved a workforce member with ongoing electronic access to the full medical record, creating a broader and less easily reversible exposure that triggers the facility's formal breach-response chain.

- *(Decision 4)* Releasing limited information to the phone caller under the assumption that confirming the patient is hospitalized is harmless. Even confirming or denying a patient's presence in the facility is a disclosure of protected health information and requires the patient's authorization unless the patient has opted into the facility directory.

- *(Decision 5)* Completing the incident report first and then notifying the charge nurse afterward, reversing the facility's policy sequence. The policy directs immediate mitigation (stopping the access), followed by notification of the charge nurse as the first reporting step, then the incident report. Alternatively, confronting RN Casey in a disciplinary manner rather than containing the exposure and reporting through the chain — discipline is a management function, not the discovering nurse's role.

- *(Decision 6)* Documenting in Mrs. Torrance's medical record that an incident report was filed regarding the privacy breach. The incident report is a quality-improvement/risk-management document; referencing it in the clinical chart compromises its protected status and violates facility policy. The nurse should document only the clinical facts of the patient's care, not the existence or content of the incident report.

- *(Decision 6)* Considering the breach resolved once the incident report is filed, without confirming that the Privacy Officer has been notified, that system safeguards are being implemented, or that the other four events have been fully addressed. Filing the report is one step in a multi-component response, not the endpoint.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize multiple types of HIPAA privacy violations and risks — unauthorized workforce access, disclosure to unverified callers, release of records without authorization, creation of identifiable images on personal devices, and failure to use reasonable safeguards during verbal communication — as they occur in real-time clinical practice.
- Classify disclosure events as permissible, impermissible, or conditionally permissible by applying the minimum-necessary standard and the treatment/payment/operations framework.
- Prioritize the most urgent reportable breach among multiple simultaneous privacy events based on the scope of information exposed, the reversibility of the exposure, and the ongoing risk of further access.
- Implement the facility's breach-response policy — mitigate, notify the charge nurse, file an incident report, and notify the Privacy Officer — without documenting the incident report in the patient's medical record.
- Evaluate breach containment by confirming that all exposures have been mitigated, reporting has been completed through the proper channel, and system-level safeguards are in place to prevent recurrence.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after all five disclosure events have been identified, classified, and addressed, and the formal breach-response process is underway.

- *Most likely condition:* Unauthorized access of protected health information constituting a reportable breach under the facility's HIPAA breach-response policy. RN Casey, a workforce member with no treatment, payment, or operations role in Mrs. Torrance's care, accessed the full electronic health record out of personal curiosity, which is a completed, non-incidental, impermissible access of protected health information.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Incidental disclosure not requiring formal reporting — wrong here: RN Casey's access was deliberate and purposeful, not an inadvertent or incidental exposure that occurred despite reasonable safeguards; curiosity-driven access to the full operative report and medication record is not incidental. (2) Permissible access for healthcare operations or education — wrong here: RN Casey cited personal interest in orthopedic surgery, not a quality-improvement project, peer review, training assignment, or any other healthcare-operations function; the access had no institutional purpose and does not meet the operations exception.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Ask RN Casey to exit the record immediately to mitigate further exposure, then notify the charge nurse of the unauthorized chart access to initiate the facility's breach-response chain — this is the first step in the defined policy sequence and activates the reporting pathway to the unit manager and Privacy Officer. (2) Complete the facility's electronic incident report documenting the unauthorized access, including the time, the specific records viewed, and the mitigation steps taken — this creates the formal record that the Privacy Officer needs to conduct the breach-determination risk assessment and preserves the audit trail.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Documenting in Mrs. Torrance's medical record that an incident report was filed regarding the breach — this compromises the privileged, quality-improvement status of the incident report and violates facility policy; the medical record contains only clinical care documentation, never references to incident reports. (2) Accepting RN Casey's explanation that "I didn't do anything with the information" and deciding not to report the access — viewing protected health information without a permitted purpose is itself the violation regardless of whether the information is further disclosed; failing to report allows the breach to go unaddressed and permits continued unauthorized access.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Confirmation that the Privacy Officer has initiated the formal breach-determination process and that the EHR audit trail documenting RN Casey's access has been preserved — this verifies that the institutional response is proceeding and that the evidence needed for the risk assessment and any required patient notification is intact. (2) Implementation of system-level safeguards — the in-service on minimum-necessary access, the fax-redirection signage, the reinforcement of private-space reporting, and the clinical-program policy review — which confirms that recurrence-prevention measures are in place and the response extends beyond the individual incident to the systemic vulnerabilities it exposed.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Mrs. Torrance's postoperative clinical status (vital signs, wound healing, pain level) — her medical condition is stable and progressing well; monitoring it does not evaluate whether the privacy breach has been contained, reported, or prevented from recurring. (2) Whether the phone caller (Robert Torrance) calls back — the nurse has already confirmed the patient's restriction and documented it; tracking the caller's future behavior does not evaluate the effectiveness of the breach response for the identified violations, and the restriction is already in place regardless of whether he calls again.
