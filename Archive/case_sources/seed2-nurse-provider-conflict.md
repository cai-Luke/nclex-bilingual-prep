**CASE TITLE**
Interprofessional conflict resolution: nurse–provider disagreement over a questionable potassium replacement order.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 71-year-old woman is on a medical-surgical unit, post-operative day two following an open cholecystectomy complicated by bile leak requiring surgical drain placement. Her medical history includes hypertension, type 2 diabetes managed with metformin (held perioperatively), and stage 3b chronic kidney disease with a baseline creatinine of 1.3 mg/dL and an estimated GFR of 42–44 mL/min. She has been receiving maintenance IV fluids (0.9% normal saline at 75 mL/hr) and has a single 20-gauge peripheral IV in her left forearm. Her surgical drain output has been moderate — approximately 200 mL of bile-tinged fluid over the past 12 hours. She has been tolerating clear liquids and is expected to advance her diet tomorrow. Her primary surgical attending is off service for the night; a second-year covering resident is managing overnight calls for the surgical service. The unit uses a standardized electrolyte replacement protocol that specifies: for peripheral IV potassium chloride replacement, the maximum concentration is 10 mEq per 100 mL and the maximum rate is 10 mEq per hour; central venous access is required for concentrations or rates above that threshold. The protocol also directs the nurse to verify renal function (most recent creatinine and eGFR) before administering any potassium replacement and to hold and clarify any order that does not conform to the protocol parameters.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The night-shift nurse receives the patient in handoff at 1900. The day-shift nurse reports that the patient has been stable but had some nausea earlier in the shift, ate very little, and had a potassium level drawn at 1600 that came back at 3.1 mEq/L. The day nurse called the covering resident about the low potassium before leaving but had not yet received a callback. The patient is alert, oriented, and in no acute distress. She reports mild incisional soreness and some generalized fatigue but denies palpitations, muscle cramps, or weakness.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Vital signs at 1900: T 37.1 °C, HR 82 and regular, BP 134/78, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Cardiac monitor shows normal sinus rhythm without ectopy. Lungs clear bilaterally. Abdomen soft, mildly tender around the incision with intact staples and a functioning surgical drain. Bowel sounds present in all four quadrants. Left forearm peripheral IV site without redness, swelling, or tenderness; IV fluid infusing without difficulty. Pedal pulses palpable bilaterally, no peripheral edema. The patient is calm and conversational, asking when she can go home.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Results drawn at 1600: sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 3.1 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 28 mg/dL, creatinine 1.6 mg/dL (up from baseline 1.3), eGFR 34 mL/min, magnesium 1.9 mg/dL, phosphorus 3.4 mg/dL, glucose 142 mg/dL. CBC: WBC 9,200/µL, Hgb 10.8 g/dL, platelets 188,000/µL. Most recent basic metabolic panel from post-operative day one had shown potassium 3.8 mEq/L and creatinine 1.4 mg/dL.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 1945: The covering resident returns the call. The nurse reports the potassium of 3.1 mEq/L and mentions the patient's baseline CKD and the current creatinine of 1.6 with an eGFR of 34. The resident, sounding hurried, orders: "Give potassium chloride 40 mEq in 100 mL normal saline IV, run it over one hour through the peripheral line." The nurse pauses — the unit's electrolyte replacement protocol caps peripheral potassium at 10 mEq per hour and 10 mEq per 100 mL, and the patient's renal function is below baseline. The nurse says, "I want to double-check — our protocol has a peripheral max of 10 mEq per hour. This order would be 40 mEq over one hour. Could we adjust the rate or split the dose?" The resident responds with audible irritation: "Her potassium is 3.1 — she needs it replaced. I've done this a hundred times. Just run it." The resident does not address the renal function concern or the protocol discrepancy. The nurse has not yet entered or administered the order.

**Stage 2** — 2000: The nurse does not administer the potassium. She verifies the protocol and labs, notifies the charge nurse, and then documents the communication/escalation and outcome factually. She explains the situation to the charge nurse: the order exceeds the unit protocol for peripheral IV potassium replacement, the patient has worsening renal function (eGFR 34, down from baseline), and the resident dismissed her concern without providing a clinical rationale for overriding the protocol. The charge nurse agrees the order is outside protocol parameters and contacts the surgical attending's on-call backup — the chief resident — per the unit's chain-of-command policy. Meanwhile, the patient remains on the cardiac monitor. Her rhythm is unchanged — normal sinus without ectopy. Vital signs at 2000: HR 80, BP 130/76, RR 16, SpO₂ 97%. She reports no new symptoms. The potassium has not been administered.

**Stage 3** — 2030: The chief resident reviews the situation by phone, thanks the nurse for catching the discrepancy, and enters a revised order: potassium chloride 10 mEq in 100 mL normal saline IV, infuse over one hour via the peripheral line, repeat times four doses with a serum potassium recheck after the second dose. The chief resident also orders a basic metabolic panel to be redrawn at 0600. The covering resident calls the unit back shortly afterward; his tone is less irritated. He acknowledges the protocol and says he was managing multiple pages and did not look up the unit's peripheral potassium parameters before ordering. He asks the nurse to let him know the repeat potassium result. The nurse administers the first 10 mEq dose at 2045. At 2245, after the second dose has infused, a stat potassium (drawn from the opposite arm, not from the KCl infusion line) returns at 3.4 mEq/L. The patient's cardiac rhythm remains normal sinus without ectopy. She denies any IV site discomfort, palpitations, or muscle symptoms. The nurse continues with the third dose per the revised order and documents the entire sequence — the original order, the protocol concern, the chain-of-command escalation, the revised order, the doses administered, and the follow-up communication with the covering resident.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, when the covering resident orders potassium chloride 40 mEq in 100 mL normal saline IV over one hour through a peripheral line, the nurse must identify which elements of the order and the patient's clinical data together create a safety concern. The relevant elements include the ordered rate and concentration relative to the unit's stated electrolyte replacement protocol, the patient's peripheral-only IV access, and the patient's current renal function. Other elements — the patient's stable cardiac rhythm, her mild incisional tenderness, her request about discharge timing — are present in the clinical picture but are not part of the safety concern with this order. *Correct action:* The nurse identifies the following as the elements that together make the order unsafe to carry out as written: the rate of 40 mEq over one hour exceeds the unit protocol maximum of 10 mEq per hour for peripheral IV potassium; the concentration of 40 mEq in 100 mL exceeds the protocol maximum of 10 mEq per 100 mL for peripheral infusion; the patient has only peripheral IV access with no central line; and the patient's creatinine has risen to 1.6 with an eGFR of 34, meaning potassium clearance is impaired and aggressive replacement carries additional risk. The stable cardiac rhythm, the incisional tenderness, and the discharge question are not elements of the safety concern. *Why it is correct:* The unit protocol exists precisely to prevent peripheral vein irritation, phlebitis, and dangerously rapid potassium administration. The patient's declining renal function compounding the risk because impaired clearance means administered potassium will not be excreted at a normal rate, increasing the chance of iatrogenic hyperkalemia. Recognizing that the order conflicts with an explicit, stated protocol — and that the patient's own renal data adds a second layer of risk — is the essential first step before any communication or escalation can occur. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, after the nurse raises the concern and the resident responds with irritation and insists the nurse "just run it," the nurse must determine what is actually driving this conflict — whether it is a genuine patient-safety issue, a personality clash, a scope-of-practice disagreement, or a difference in clinical preference where either approach would be acceptable. *Correct action:* The conflict is driven by a genuine patient-safety issue: the order as written violates the unit's electrolyte replacement protocol, and the patient's renal impairment adds clinical risk that the resident did not address. The resident's irritation and dismissiveness are the emotional surface of the interaction, but they are not the cause of the conflict — the cause is that the nurse has identified a concrete, protocol-based safety concern and the provider has not offered a clinical rationale for overriding the protocol. This is not a personality clash (the nurse's initial communication was professional and specific), not a scope disagreement (the nurse is not refusing to follow a legitimate order but is questioning one that falls outside established safety parameters), and not a matter of equivalent clinical preference (the protocol sets a clear boundary, and the order exceeds it). *Why it is correct:* Correctly analyzing the root cause of the disagreement prevents the nurse from being deflected by the emotional dynamics of the interaction. If the nurse misreads the situation as a personality conflict, she may try to smooth the relationship rather than protect the patient. If she misreads it as a scope issue, she may defer to the physician's authority and administer the order. Identifying it as a safety issue — grounded in a specific protocol violation and a specific patient risk factor — keeps the focus on the patient and provides the factual basis for any escalation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must decide what takes priority: maintaining a comfortable working relationship with the covering resident, following the physician's order because the patient does need potassium replacement, or protecting the patient from the specific risks of the order as written. *Correct action:* Patient safety takes priority over hierarchical deference and over the urgency of replacing the potassium. The patient does need potassium replacement — her level of 3.1 mEq/L is clinically significant — but the need for replacement does not override the need to replace it safely. The nurse's obligation is to ensure the potassium is administered within safe parameters, not to choose between replacing it and not replacing it. The professional relationship with the resident matters and should be preserved where possible, but it cannot take precedence over a protocol-based safety concern. *Why it is correct:* The ethical principle of nonmaleficence and the nurse's professional accountability under the state nurse practice act require the nurse to refuse to carry out an order she has identified as unsafe, regardless of the prescriber's insistence. The patient's hypokalemia is real and warrants treatment, but the correct prioritization is that the potassium must be replaced at a safe rate and concentration — not that it must be replaced immediately at any rate. Framing the priority this way also prevents the false dichotomy of "give it as ordered" versus "refuse and do nothing" — the nurse is advocating for a modified, safe order, not for withholding treatment. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 1 and into Stage 2, the nurse must determine the full set of appropriate actions to resolve this conflict safely and professionally. Multiple steps are involved, and some commonly considered responses are inappropriate. *Correct action:* The appropriate actions, taken together, are: (a) use assertive, structured safety communication — the CUS framework ("I am Concerned about this order, I am Uncomfortable administering it as written, this is a Safety issue") or an equivalent graded-assertiveness tool — to clearly restate the concern to the resident with specific reference to the protocol and the patient's renal function; (b) request a modified order or an authorized policy-consistent override through the correct escalation/pharmacy/attending pathway — a resident's verbal rationale alone is not sufficient for the bedside nurse to knowingly violate a stated facility protocol; (c) if the resident continues to insist, withhold the order (do not administer it) and escalate through the chain of command by notifying the charge nurse, who can contact the next level of authority; (d) document the original order, the nurse's concern, the communication with the resident, the escalation, and the outcome. The following actions are inappropriate: administering the potassium as ordered despite the identified safety concern (this violates the nurse's professional accountability); refusing the order and walking away without escalating or ensuring the patient receives safe replacement (this abandons the patient's need for treatment); filing an incident report as the first step instead of communicating directly (an incident/safety report may be appropriate after the patient is safe, depending on policy, but it does not replace direct safety communication and chain-of-command escalation for the immediate clinical issue); or asking a colleague to administer the order instead (this transfers the safety risk rather than resolving it). *Why it is correct:* Graded assertiveness — starting with direct, respectful communication and escalating only when the concern is not resolved — is the professional standard for interprofessional conflict over patient safety. It preserves the collaborative relationship when possible while ensuring the patient is protected. Withholding an unsafe order is within the RN's scope and obligation. Documentation creates a factual record that protects the patient, the nurse, and the institution. The inappropriate actions each fail in a specific way: blind compliance prioritizes hierarchy over safety; refusal without escalation prioritizes the nurse's discomfort over the patient's need; relying solely on an incident report without direct communication does not resolve the immediate clinical need; and delegation of the unsafe order to another nurse does not make the order safe. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2 (the full resolution pathway, including the charge nurse involvement and chain-of-command escalation, is visible only at Stage 2).

5. *Situation:* At Stage 1, immediately after the resident insists "just run it" and the nurse has recognized the safety concern, the nurse must choose the single first action to take. *Correct action:* The correct action is to use CUS/two-challenge language to explicitly frame the concern as a safety issue; if the provider still does not acknowledge the safety issue, the nurse must then escalate through the chain of command. The nurse does not administer the potassium, does not hang up and immediately call the charge nurse without formally stating the safety concern, and does not simply document and wait. *Why it is correct:* Direct, assertive communication with the ordering provider is always the first step in a safety disagreement. Since the nurse's initial soft clarification was dismissed, she must issue a clear second challenge using a structured framework like CUS. Escalation through the chain of command is the correct next step if this explicit safety communication fails, but jumping to the charge nurse without formally stating the safety concern bypasses the collaborative process and may damage the working relationship unnecessarily. At the same time, the nurse does not administer the order while the concern is unresolved. The CUS framework (or equivalent graded-assertiveness language) gives the communication structure and clarity, making it harder for the concern to be dismissed as vague discomfort. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after the chain-of-command escalation has resulted in a revised order, the potassium is being administered at the protocol-appropriate rate, and the covering resident has called back with a less adversarial tone, the nurse must evaluate whether the conflict has been resolved safely and whether the outcome is adequate. *Correct action:* The resolution is adequate. The indicators of successful resolution are: the unsafe order was not carried out; a revised order consistent with the unit protocol was obtained through the chain of command; the patient received the needed potassium replacement at a safe rate; the repeat potassium (3.4 mEq/L after two doses) shows the replacement is effective; the patient experienced no adverse effects (no IV site complications, no cardiac rhythm changes, no symptoms); the covering resident acknowledged the protocol and communicated constructively in the follow-up call, indicating the professional relationship was preserved rather than ruptured; and the nurse documented the entire sequence. If the covering resident had remained hostile, had retaliated, or had refused to communicate further, the resolution would be clinically adequate (the patient was protected) but the interprofessional component would require further follow-up with the nurse manager or a formal debrief — but that is not the case here. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating the outcome of a conflict-resolution process requires assessing both dimensions: patient safety (was the unsafe order corrected? did the patient receive appropriate treatment? was harm avoided?) and professional collaboration (was the relationship preserved? is the communication channel functional going forward?). A resolution that protects the patient but permanently damages the working relationship is incomplete; a resolution that smooths the relationship but allows the unsafe order to stand is a failure. Here, both dimensions are met. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Focusing on the patient's stable cardiac rhythm and concluding that the order is probably fine because the patient is not currently symptomatic from the hypokalemia — this mistakes the absence of acute cardiac symptoms for the absence of risk from the order itself. The safety concern is about the rate and concentration of the replacement, not about the patient's current rhythm. A nurse who reasons "she's in normal sinus, so fast replacement won't hurt" has confused the indication for treatment with the safety of the delivery method.

- *(Decision point 1)* Identifying the patient's discharge question or her mild incisional tenderness as elements of the safety concern — these are present in the clinical picture but are entirely unrelated to the potassium order. Including them reflects an inability to distinguish relevant from irrelevant cues.

- *(Decision point 2)* Interpreting the conflict as a personality clash or an ego issue — "the resident is just having a bad night" — and deciding to smooth it over by accommodating the order or waiting until the resident is in a better mood. This misidentifies the root cause and leads to either unsafe compliance or dangerous delay. The resident's irritation is a communication barrier, not the cause of the disagreement.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing the urgency of potassium replacement over the safety of the delivery method — reasoning that a potassium of 3.1 is dangerously low and the patient "needs it now," so the rate concern is secondary. While 3.1 mEq/L requires correction, the patient is hemodynamically stable and in normal sinus rhythm; there is time to obtain a safe order. Administering potassium at an unsafe rate to correct hypokalemia faster creates a new and potentially more dangerous problem (iatrogenic hyperkalemia in a patient with impaired renal clearance, peripheral vein injury, or cardiac irritability from rapid infusion).

- *(Decision point 4)* Refusing the order and documenting the refusal but not escalating through the chain of command and not ensuring the patient receives a revised, safe replacement order — this protects the nurse legally but abandons the patient's clinical need. The patient still has a potassium of 3.1 and needs replacement; the nurse's obligation is to advocate for a safe order, not simply to decline the unsafe one.

- *(Decision point 4)* Asking another nurse to administer the order — this does not resolve the safety concern; it transfers the risk to a colleague and to the patient. The order is still outside protocol regardless of who hangs the infusion.

- *(Decision point 5)* Immediately calling the charge nurse or the chief resident as the very first action, without first giving the covering resident a clear, structured restatement of the concern — this skips the direct-communication step and escalates prematurely. While escalation is appropriate and necessary if direct communication fails, bypassing the ordering provider without a genuine attempt at assertive dialogue damages collaboration and may be perceived as going behind the resident's back. The correct sequence is direct assertive communication first, then escalation if the concern is not resolved.

- *(Decision point 5)* Administering the potassium as ordered while simultaneously calling the charge nurse to report the concern — this is not a safe compromise. Once the nurse has identified the order as outside protocol and potentially harmful, administering it "while escalating" exposes the patient to the very risk the nurse identified. The order must be withheld until clarified.

- *(Decision point 6)* Concluding that the conflict is resolved solely because the patient received the potassium and the repeat level improved, without considering whether the interprofessional communication was restored — this evaluates only the clinical outcome and ignores the relational dimension. If the covering resident remained hostile or retaliatory, the nurse would need to follow up with the nurse manager, because an unresolved interprofessional rupture creates ongoing safety risk for future patients on the same shift.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Identify the specific elements of a medication order and a patient's clinical data that together constitute a protocol-based safety concern warranting the nurse to hold and clarify the order.
- Distinguish a genuine patient-safety conflict from a personality clash, a scope-of-practice disagreement, or a difference in clinical preference, and explain why the root-cause analysis matters for choosing the correct response.
- Apply graded assertiveness and structured safety communication (such as the CUS framework) as the first step in an interprofessional disagreement over a patient-safety concern, before escalating through the chain of command.
- Demonstrate the complete chain-of-command escalation process — direct communication, charge nurse involvement, next-level authority contact, documentation — and distinguish it from inappropriate responses such as blind compliance, refusal without escalation, or punitive reporting.
- Evaluate the outcome of a conflict-resolution process across both dimensions: patient safety (was the unsafe order corrected and safe treatment provided?) and professional collaboration (was the working relationship preserved or repaired?).

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after the chain-of-command escalation has produced a revised, protocol-compliant potassium replacement order, the patient is receiving safe treatment, and the covering resident has communicated constructively in follow-up.

- *Most likely condition:* An unsafe medication order creating an interprofessional safety conflict requiring assertive communication and chain-of-command escalation. The order for peripheral IV potassium chloride at a rate and concentration exceeding the unit's electrolyte replacement protocol, given to a patient with worsening renal function, is the safety trigger; the resident's dismissal of the nurse's concern without clinical rationale is the conflict that demands a structured resolution process.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) A personality clash or communication-style difference — wrong here because the nurse's concern is grounded in a specific, documented protocol violation and a concrete patient risk factor (declining eGFR), not in interpersonal friction; the resident's irritation is a barrier to resolution, not the cause of the disagreement. (2) A legitimate difference in clinical judgment where either approach is acceptable — wrong here because the unit protocol sets an explicit ceiling for peripheral potassium administration, and the resident did not provide a clinical rationale for overriding it; this is not a gray-area judgment call but a clear protocol discrepancy compounded by the patient's impaired renal clearance.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Use structured assertive safety communication — CUS or equivalent graded-assertiveness language — to clearly restate the protocol-based concern to the ordering provider, giving the resident a direct opportunity to respond before escalating. (2) When direct communication does not resolve the concern, escalate through the chain of command by notifying the charge nurse, who contacts the next level of authority, while withholding the order and documenting the sequence.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Administering the potassium as ordered despite the identified protocol violation — this prioritizes hierarchical compliance over patient safety and exposes the patient to the risks of rapid, concentrated peripheral potassium infusion with impaired renal clearance; the nurse is professionally accountable for carrying out an order she has identified as unsafe. (2) Refusing the order and documenting the refusal without escalating or ensuring the patient receives a revised safe order — this protects the nurse from liability but abandons the patient's clinical need for potassium replacement; advocacy requires pursuing a safe alternative, not simply declining the unsafe one.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Revised order is protocol-compliant and original unsafe order is not administered — this confirms that the patient-safety dimension of the conflict is resolved and the patient receives the treatment she needs within safe parameters. (2) Potassium trend, rhythm, and IV site tolerance during replacement — this evaluates whether the safe replacement method is effective and tolerated by the patient, which is the immediate clinical goal.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Discharge timing question or mild incisional soreness — while these are part of the patient's experience, they are not the focus of resolving the unsafe medication order. (2) The resident's workload or number of pages — while this context may explain the resident's irritation, it is irrelevant to the objective safety parameters of the order.
