CASE TITLE
Acute graft-versus-host disease following allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation.

PATIENT BACKGROUND
A 52-year-old woman with a history of acute myeloid leukemia in first complete remission underwent a matched unrelated donor allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplant twenty-six days ago. Her conditioning regimen was myeloablative with busulfan and cyclophosphamide. Her graft-versus-host disease prophylaxis consists of tacrolimus administered as a continuous intravenous infusion, titrated to a target trough of 10 to 15 ng/mL, and methotrexate, which was completed per protocol on days one, three, six, and eleven post-transplant. She achieved neutrophil engraftment on day fourteen. She has a triple-lumen central venous catheter in the right internal jugular vein. Current orders include tacrolimus continuous infusion, acyclovir for viral prophylaxis, fluconazole for antifungal prophylaxis, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole on weekends for Pneumocystis prophylaxis, and a platelet transfusion threshold of ten thousand per microliter. She is on a regular diet, ambulating on the unit, and has been tolerating oral intake well until the current shift. She is readmitted to the bone marrow transplant unit from the outpatient transplant clinic after reporting new symptoms over the past forty-eight hours.

INITIAL PRESENTATION
The patient presents to the transplant unit stating that she has developed progressively worsening watery diarrhea over the past two days, now occurring eight to ten times daily, with crampy abdominal pain that is diffuse and rated five out of ten. She also notes a new rash on her palms, the soles of her feet, and her ears that appeared yesterday and has been spreading to her upper chest and back. She reports decreased appetite and mild nausea but no vomiting. She denies fever, cough, or dysuria. She appears fatigued and mildly uncomfortable but is alert, oriented, and conversational.

ASSESSMENT FINDINGS
Baseline vital signs on admission to the unit: temperature 37.3 degrees Celsius, heart rate 96 beats per minute, blood pressure 108/68 millimeters of mercury, respiratory rate 18 breaths per minute, oxygen saturation 97 percent on room air. Weight is 64.2 kilograms, which is down 2.1 kilograms from her clinic visit five days ago. Skin assessment reveals a diffuse erythematous maculopapular rash on the palms bilaterally, the soles of the feet, the pinnae of both ears, and scattered across the upper chest and posterior shoulders, estimated to cover approximately 30 percent of body surface area. The rash blanches with pressure and is non-tender. No bullae, desquamation, or mucosal involvement is noted. Oral mucosa is moist but slightly erythematous. Abdomen is soft, mildly distended, with diffuse tenderness to palpation, hyperactive bowel sounds in all four quadrants. No guarding or rebound. Stool is greenish-watery without visible blood. Central line site is clean, dry, and without erythema or drainage. Peripheral edema is absent. Heart rate, blood pressure, stool volume, rash extent, and oral mucosa are the findings that will change meaningfully across the three stages.

LABORATORY DATA
Baseline labs drawn at readmission: white blood cell count 3,800 per microliter with an absolute neutrophil count of 2,100 per microliter, hemoglobin 9.8 grams per deciliter, platelets 42,000 per microliter. Sodium 138 milliequivalents per liter, potassium 3.4 milliequivalents per liter, chloride 104 milliequivalents per liter, bicarbonate 22 milliequivalents per liter, blood urea nitrogen 28 milligrams per deciliter, creatinine 1.0 milligrams per deciliter, glucose 118 milligrams per deciliter, magnesium 1.6 milligrams per deciliter, phosphorus 3.2 milligrams per deciliter, albumin 2.8 grams per deciliter, total bilirubin 1.8 milligrams per deciliter, direct bilirubin 1.2 milligrams per deciliter, alanine aminotransferase 62 units per liter, aspartate aminotransferase 58 units per liter, alkaline phosphatase 148 units per liter, lactate dehydrogenase 310 units per liter. Tacrolimus trough level is 8.2 nanograms per milliliter, which is below the target range of 10 to 15 nanograms per milliliter. C-reactive protein 42 milligrams per liter. Stool studies sent include Clostridioides difficile toxin polymerase chain reaction, stool culture, cytomegalovirus polymerase chain reaction from blood, and adenovirus polymerase chain reaction. Stool volume over the first eight-hour shift is measured at 620 milliliters. The values whose trajectory across the three stages is clinically decisive are potassium, magnesium, total and direct bilirubin, liver transaminases, albumin, tacrolimus trough, stool volume, and creatinine.

CLINICAL COURSE

Stage 1 — twelve hours after readmission: Stool studies return negative for Clostridioides difficile toxin, stool culture shows no enteric pathogens, blood cytomegalovirus polymerase chain reaction is undetectable, and adenovirus polymerase chain reaction is negative. The transplant team clinically diagnoses acute graft-versus-host disease involving the skin (stage 2, approximately 30 percent body surface area rash), the gastrointestinal tract (stage 2, diarrhea volume greater than one liter per day), and the liver (stage 1, bilirubin 1.8 to 2.0 milligrams per deciliter), grading the overall disease as grade II acute graft-versus-host disease. The provider orders methylprednisolone 2 milligrams per kilogram per day intravenously divided into two doses, continuation of tacrolimus with an increase in the infusion rate to achieve therapeutic trough levels, a skin biopsy from a representative rash area, strict intake and output monitoring with stool volume charting every shift, daily weights, and a consult with the dietitian for a low-residue diet. Vital signs at this point: temperature 37.6 degrees Celsius, heart rate 102, blood pressure 104/64, respiratory rate 18, oxygen saturation 97 percent. Repeat potassium is 3.1 milliequivalents per liter, magnesium 1.4 milligrams per deciliter. Stool output over the preceding twenty-four hours totals 1,480 milliliters. The rash has not changed significantly. The patient reports continued cramping and now has seven episodes of diarrhea in eight hours. Oral intake has decreased further. Intravenous fluids are initiated at 125 milliliters per hour of lactated Ringer solution per provider order.

Stage 2 — forty-eight hours after readmission, thirty-six hours into corticosteroid therapy: The patient's diarrhea has worsened despite methylprednisolone. Stool output over the past twenty-four hours is 2,200 milliliters, now with visible blood streaking in the stool. The rash has spread to the trunk and proximal extremities, now involving approximately 50 percent of body surface area. The patient reports worsening abdominal cramping, rated seven out of ten, and has intermittent nausea with one episode of non-bloody emesis. Stool output over the past twenty-four hours has decreased to 900 milliliters with no further visible blood. The rash has stabilized and begun to fade on the upper chest and ears, now covering approximately 40 percent of body surface area, with no bullae or desquamation. Abdominal cramping has decreased to three out of ten. Vital signs: temperature 37.2 degrees Celsius, heart rate 92, blood pressure 110/70, respiratory rate 16, oxygen saturation 98 percent on room air. Urine output over the past eight hours is 320 milliliters. She is more alert and reports improved comfort. Repeat labs: potassium 3.8 milliequivalents per liter, magnesium 1.8 milligrams per deciliter, total bilirubin 2.4 milligrams per deciliter, direct bilirubin 1.6 milligrams per deciliter, alanine aminotransferase 72 units per liter, creatinine 1.2 milligrams per deciliter, blood urea nitrogen 26 milligrams per deciliter, albumin 2.4 grams per deciliter, tacrolimus trough 12.1 nanograms per milliliter, glucose 162 milligrams per deciliter, hemoglobin 8.8 grams per deciliter post-transfusion. Blood cultures drawn at the time of fever in Stage 2 have returned with no growth at forty-eight hours. The team discusses a slow oral diet advancement beginning with clear liquids and ongoing close monitoring. The nurse must now evaluate which parameters confirm the patient is responding to therapy and which findings still require vigilance.

KEY DECISION POINTS

1. Situation: At Stage 1, after infectious stool studies and viral polymerase chain reactions return negative and the tacrolimus trough is found to be subtherapeutic, the nurse must analyze the cluster of findings — new maculopapular rash on acral surfaces, high-volume watery diarrhea, mildly elevated bilirubin with rising liver enzymes, and onset at day twenty-six post-transplant — and determine whether these findings collectively support the diagnosis of acute graft-versus-host disease or suggest another etiology such as infection, drug reaction, or engraftment syndrome. Correct action: The nurse recognizes that the constellation of rash predominantly on palms, soles, and ears, combined with voluminous watery diarrhea and cholestatic liver enzyme elevation, arising in the third to fourth week after allogeneic transplant with a subtherapeutic immunosuppressant level, is the classic triad of acute graft-versus-host disease involving skin, gastrointestinal tract, and liver. The negative infectious workup further supports this conclusion. Why it is correct: Acute graft-versus-host disease characteristically manifests between days fourteen and one hundred post-transplant when donor T-lymphocytes mount an immune response against host tissues. The three classic target organs are skin, gastrointestinal tract, and liver. The acral distribution of the rash (palms, soles, ears) is a hallmark presentation that distinguishes it from most drug eruptions, which tend to be truncal. The subtherapeutic tacrolimus level of 8.2 nanograms per milliliter indicates inadequate immunosuppression, which is a known precipitant for graft-versus-host disease flares. Negative infectious studies are essential because cytomegalovirus colitis, Clostridioides difficile infection, and adenovirus can mimic or coexist with gastrointestinal graft-versus-host disease. Clinical-judgment skill: Analyze cues. Answerable from: Stage 1, because the infectious workup results and the subtherapeutic tacrolimus level are not available at initial presentation.

2. Situation: At Stage 1, the nurse notes the potassium of 3.1 milliequivalents per liter and magnesium of 1.4 milligrams per deciliter in the context of high-volume diarrhea. Before high-dose corticosteroids are administered, the nurse must identify the most urgent concern related to these electrolyte values and determine whether to intervene or simply document and monitor. Correct action: The nurse recognizes that the hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia in the setting of ongoing large-volume diarrheal losses represent an urgent safety concern that must be communicated to the provider immediately. The nurse should anticipate orders for intravenous electrolyte replacement, understand that magnesium must be repleted before or concurrently with potassium because hypomagnesemia causes renal potassium wasting and makes hypokalemia refractory to correction, and recognize that the initiation of high-dose corticosteroids will further exacerbate both hypokalemia and hyperglycemia. Why it is correct: Profuse secretory diarrhea in gastrointestinal graft-versus-host disease causes massive losses of potassium, magnesium, and bicarbonate. Hypokalemia below 3.0 milliequivalents per liter places the patient at risk for cardiac arrhythmias, ileus, and respiratory muscle weakness. Magnesium is a cofactor for the sodium-potassium ATPase pump; when magnesium is depleted, the kidneys cannot retain potassium regardless of supplementation. High-dose corticosteroids promote renal potassium excretion and elevate blood glucose, compounding these derangements. Clinical-judgment skill: Recognize cues. Answerable from: Stage 1, when the repeat electrolyte values and corticosteroid order become available.

COMMON NURSING ERRORS

Attached to Decision Point 1: Attributing the diarrhea to Clostridioides difficile infection and delaying recognition of graft-versus-host disease until stool studies return. While infection must be ruled out, the nurse who anchors on a single infectious cause and fails to consider the full clinical picture — acral rash distribution, timing post-transplant, subtherapeutic immunosuppression, liver involvement — misses the opportunity to escalate care. Clostridioides difficile diarrhea does not explain the rash or the cholestatic liver pattern.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Misidentifying the rash as a drug allergy and recommending discontinuation of prophylactic medications. The maculopapular rash of acute graft-versus-host disease can superficially resemble a drug eruption, but its acral predominance, timing relative to transplant, and association with gastrointestinal and hepatic findings distinguish it. Stopping essential prophylactic antimicrobials such as acyclovir or fluconazole based on a misdiagnosis could expose the patient to life-threatening opportunistic infections.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Dismissing the potassium of 3.1 milliequivalents per liter as only mildly low and planning to address it with dietary potassium or oral supplements. In a patient with high-volume diarrhea who is about to receive high-dose corticosteroids, oral supplementation is insufficient and unreliable, and the trajectory is predictably downward. Intravenous replacement is needed, and the downward trend must be communicated urgently.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Prioritizing the supratherapeutic tacrolimus level or the blood glucose over the hemodynamic instability. While both are clinically important, reducing the tacrolimus rate and administering insulin are not rescue interventions. The tachycardia, hypotension, and oliguria signal impending hypovolemic shock, which threatens all organ perfusion and must be addressed first.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Independently holding or discontinuing the tacrolimus infusion entirely because the trough is supratherapeutic. While reducing the dose is appropriate per order, abruptly stopping calcineurin inhibitor therapy in a patient with active, escalating graft-versus-host disease could trigger an immune flare with potentially fatal consequences. The nurse should reduce per order and clarify with the provider, not unilaterally discontinue.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Administering the red blood cell transfusion before initiating potassium replacement, reasoning that the hemoglobin of 7.9 grams per deciliter is more dangerous. While transfusion is indicated, the potassium of 2.8 milliequivalents per liter with ongoing losses poses a more immediate cardiac arrhythmia risk. Additionally, stored red blood cells can release potassium, but in this context the patient's profound total body potassium depletion makes this a negligible concern compared to the need for aggressive repletion.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the clinical improvement as evidence that immunosuppressive therapy can be rapidly tapered. Premature corticosteroid taper is one of the most common errors in graft-versus-host disease management and frequently leads to disease flare. The nurse should understand that tapering decisions are made by the transplant team over weeks, guided by sustained response, and should not advocate for or anticipate rapid dose reduction based on early improvement.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Failing to monitor for infection because the patient appears to be improving. The combination of high-dose corticosteroids and ruxolitinib creates profound immunosuppression. The negative blood cultures at forty-eight hours are reassuring but do not eliminate ongoing risk. Corticosteroids blunt the febrile response, so the nurse must maintain a high index of suspicion for infection even in an afebrile patient and report subtle changes such as new confusion, tachycardia without other explanation, or new skin lesions promptly.

EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

Recognize the classic triad of acute graft-versus-host disease — skin rash with acral predilection, secretory diarrhea, and cholestatic hepatic injury — in the context of the post-transplant timeline and differentiate it from infectious mimics by integrating stool study results, viral markers, and the immunosuppression level.

Analyze the relationship between subtherapeutic or supratherapeutic calcineurin inhibitor levels and clinical events in the transplant patient, including the pharmacokinetic impact of hepatic graft-versus-host disease on tacrolimus metabolism and the nephrotoxic consequences of drug accumulation.

Prioritize competing clinical problems in a deteriorating transplant patient by applying airway-breathing-circulation principles, recognizing that hemodynamic instability from volume depletion takes precedence over electrolyte correction, which takes precedence over supportive measures such as transfusion and nutritional support.

Sequence multiple simultaneous nursing interventions safely in a complex clinical scenario, including intravenous fluid resuscitation, cardiac-monitored electrolyte replacement, immunosuppressant dose adjustment, blood glucose management, and transfusion therapy.

Evaluate the response to graft-versus-host disease treatment using organ-specific markers — stool volume and character, rash extent and morphology, bilirubin trend — while simultaneously identifying new iatrogenic risks introduced by the treatment itself, particularly the heightened infection vulnerability created by combined immunosuppressive therapy.
