**CASE TITLE**
Refeeding syndrome during parenteral nutrition initiation in a malnourished post-operative client.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 62-year-old woman is admitted to the surgical unit following an open Roux-en-Y revision for a chronic gastrojejunal anastomotic stricture that had rendered her unable to tolerate oral or enteral intake for approximately five weeks before surgery. Her pre-operative weight is 46 kg (BMI 17.1); she has lost 11 kg over the preceding two months. Her medical history includes type 2 diabetes managed with metformin (held perioperatively), chronic gastroesophageal reflux disease, and a remote cholecystectomy. She has no history of cancer, chemotherapy, or radiation. She does not drink alcohol. She arrived to the surgical unit from the PACU six hours ago after an uncomplicated four-hour procedure.

Current orders: peripheral parenteral nutrition (PPN) via a 20-gauge peripheral IV in the left forearm at 40 mL/hr, initiated in the PACU as a bridge while a peripherally inserted central catheter (PICC) is placed the following morning for central total parenteral nutrition (TPN). The PPN formulation is a low-osmolarity amino-acid/dextrose solution with lipids. Additional orders include famotidine 20 mg IV every 12 hours, subcutaneous heparin 5,000 units every 8 hours for DVT prophylaxis, acetaminophen 1,000 mg IV every 6 hours as needed for pain, and point-of-care blood glucose monitoring every 6 hours. The surgical team has written: "Advance to central TPN via PICC once line placement is confirmed; pharmacy will compound. Start at goal rate per nutrition consult. Monitor basic metabolic panel plus magnesium and phosphorus every 12 hours for the first 48 hours, then daily. Replace electrolytes per unit protocol: potassium chloride 20 mEq IV over 1 hour for K 3.0–3.4 mEq/L, 40 mEq IV over 2 hours for K < 3.0; sodium phosphate 15 mmol IV over 2 hours for phosphorus 1.5–2.0 mg/dL, 30 mmol IV over 4 hours for phosphorus < 1.5; magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 2 hours for Mg < 1.5 mg/dL."

The nutrition consult documents her as high risk for refeeding syndrome given BMI < 18.5, negligible oral intake for more than five weeks, and pre-existing electrolyte depletion. The consult recommends initiating TPN at no more than 10 kcal/kg/day and advancing slowly over 5–7 days, with aggressive electrolyte monitoring and repletion. Thiamine 200 mg IV daily for 5–7 days is ordered, with the first dose having been administered prior to the initiation of the dextrose-containing PPN in the PACU.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At the start of the night shift, approximately eight hours post-operatively, the nurse assumes care. The patient is drowsy but arousable and oriented to person, place, and time. She reports mild incisional pain rated 3 out of 10 and mild nausea. She has had no oral intake. The PPN is infusing at the ordered rate through the peripheral IV, which is without redness, swelling, or tenderness. She has a Foley catheter draining clear yellow urine.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline vital signs: T 36.8 °C (oral), HR 92, BP 108/64, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Weight 46 kg. She appears thin with temporal wasting and loose skin over the upper arms. Mucous membranes are dry. Abdomen has a midline surgical dressing that is clean, dry, and intact with a Jackson-Pratt drain containing 40 mL of serosanguineous fluid. Bowel sounds are absent. Peripheral IV site in the left forearm is without erythema or induration; PPN infusing freely. Skin turgor is decreased over the dorsum of the hand. Pedal pulses are palpable bilaterally; no peripheral edema. Neurological exam is grossly intact; deep tendon reflexes are 2+ and symmetric. Point-of-care glucose at this assessment is 142 mg/dL. Foley output over the first eight post-operative hours totals 280 mL (approximately 35 mL/hr).

**LABORATORY DATA**
Immediate post-operative labs (drawn in the PACU, approximately six hours before the nurse's first assessment):
Sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 3.5 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.8 mg/dL, glucose 156 mg/dL, calcium 8.4 mg/dL, phosphorus 2.8 mg/dL, magnesium 1.8 mg/dL, albumin 2.1 g/dL, prealbumin 8 mg/dL, WBC 9,200/µL, hemoglobin 11.0 g/dL, platelets 210,000/µL. Liver function tests: AST 28 U/L, ALT 22 U/L, total bilirubin 0.6 mg/dL.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 18 hours post-operatively (approximately 10 hours after the nurse's initial assessment). The PICC line was placed in the right upper arm that morning; chest X-ray confirms tip position in the lower superior vena cava. The PPN is discontinued and central TPN is initiated at the rate recommended by the nutrition consult (10 kcal/kg/day). Thiamine 200 mg IV has been given. The 12-hour repeat electrolyte panel is now resulted:

Sodium 137 mEq/L, potassium 3.1 mEq/L (down from 3.5), chloride 101 mEq/L, bicarbonate 23 mEq/L, BUN 16 mg/dL, creatinine 0.8 mg/dL, glucose 198 mg/dL, phosphorus 1.8 mg/dL (down from 2.8), magnesium 1.4 mg/dL (down from 1.8).

Vital signs: T 37.1 °C, HR 104, BP 104/60, RR 18, SpO₂ 97% on room air. The patient reports feeling "shaky" and mildly short of breath. She has new generalized weakness, paresthesias, and increasing fatigue. Foley output over the preceding 8 hours is 200 mL (25 mL/hr). The PICC dressing is clean, dry, and intact; the insertion site is without erythema or drainage. Point-of-care glucose is 204 mg/dL.

**Stage 2** — 30 hours post-operatively (12 hours after Stage 1). The nurse had administered potassium chloride 20 mEq IV over 1 hour, sodium phosphate 15 mmol IV over 2 hours, and magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 2 hours per the unit electrolyte replacement protocol after the Stage 1 labs. The provider was notified of the falling electrolytes and the glucose of 204 mg/dL; an order for regular insulin per sliding scale was added, and the nutrition team was asked to review the TPN advancement plan.

Repeat labs now result: potassium 2.9 mEq/L (further decline despite repletion), phosphorus 1.3 mg/dL (further decline despite repletion), magnesium 1.3 mg/dL (further decline despite repletion), glucose 226 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL.

Vital signs: T 38.2 °C, HR 118, BP 98/58, RR 22, SpO₂ 95% on room air. The patient is now lethargic, oriented only to person, and has proximal muscle weakness — she cannot lift her arms above her shoulders. She reports palpitations and chest tightness. Continuous telemetry, placed after Stage 1, shows sinus tachycardia with frequent premature ventricular contractions (PVCs) and a prolonged QT interval. Respiratory effort is shallow with decreased breath sounds at the bases bilaterally. She denies rigors, and her extremities are warm and well-perfused. The PICC insertion site now has a 1.5 cm area of erythema and tenderness surrounding the insertion point, with a small amount of purulent drainage expressed on palpation. The PICC dressing was last changed 24 hours ago per protocol.

**Stage 3** — 42 hours post-operatively (12 hours after Stage 2). The provider was notified urgently at Stage 2 of the continued electrolyte decline despite repletion, the new cardiac rhythm changes, the respiratory compromise, and the PICC site findings. Orders received: reduce TPN rate by 50% (do not discontinue abruptly), administer aggressive electrolyte repletion at the higher-tier protocol doses (potassium 40 mEq IV over 2 hours, sodium phosphate 30 mmol IV over 4 hours, magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 2 hours — repeat magnesium in 4 hours if still < 1.5), obtain blood cultures from the PICC and a peripheral site before starting vancomycin 1 g IV, obtain a 12-lead ECG, and increase glucose monitoring to every 4 hours with insulin coverage.

After these interventions are carried out over the next 12 hours, repeat labs show: potassium 3.4 mEq/L (rising), phosphorus 1.9 mg/dL (rising), magnesium 1.6 mg/dL (rising), glucose 168 mg/dL (improving), creatinine 0.9 mg/dL. Vital signs: T 37.6 °C, HR 96, BP 110/66, RR 18, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Telemetry shows sinus rhythm without PVCs; QT interval has shortened. The patient is more alert, oriented to person and place, and can lift her arms above her shoulders. The PICC site erythema has not expanded; cultures are pending. The TPN is still infusing at the reduced rate. The nutrition team plans to hold at the reduced rate for 48 hours, then advance by 5 kcal/kg/day increments with continued electrolyte monitoring every 12 hours.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the 12-hour repeat electrolyte panel has resulted alongside new clinical findings. The nurse reviews the full set of data — the electrolyte changes from baseline, the glucose level, the vital signs, the new generalized weakness and paresthesias, the decreased urine output, and the unchanged PICC site — and must identify which findings are significant cues requiring nursing attention. *Correct action:* The significant cues are the falling phosphorus (2.8 → 1.8 mg/dL), falling potassium (3.5 → 3.1 mEq/L), falling magnesium (1.8 → 1.4 mg/dL), rising glucose (156 → 204 mg/dL), new tachycardia (HR 92 → 104), new generalized weakness and paresthesias, the patient's subjective shakiness and mild dyspnea, and the declining urine output (35 → 25 mL/hr). The unchanged PICC site appearance and the stable temperature (37.1 °C) are not significant cues at this stage. *Why it is correct:* The simultaneous decline in phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium in a severely malnourished patient within hours of initiating parenteral nutrition is the hallmark electrolyte pattern of refeeding syndrome — intracellular shift of these ions driven by insulin release in response to carbohydrate delivery after prolonged starvation. The weakness and paresthesias are early neuromuscular manifestations of hypophosphatemia and hypomagnesemia. The rising glucose reflects the carbohydrate load exceeding the malnourished patient's impaired glucose tolerance. The tachycardia may reflect both the electrolyte derangement and the hyperglycemia-related osmotic effect. The clean PICC site and near-normal temperature do not signal a line complication at this point. Recognizing the full cue cluster — and not being distracted by the benign findings — is the first step in identifying the evolving emergency. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse has identified the falling electrolytes and the rising glucose. The nurse must now determine what clinical condition these cues, taken together with the patient's history of prolonged malnutrition and recent initiation of parenteral nutrition, most strongly indicate. *Correct action:* The cue pattern — simultaneous decline in phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium occurring within hours of initiating caloric delivery in a patient with severe protein-calorie malnutrition — is consistent with refeeding syndrome. The hyperglycemia is a concurrent complication of parenteral nutrition but does not explain the electrolyte triad. The clean PICC site and absence of fever make a central-line–associated bloodstream infection unlikely at this stage. *Why it is correct:* Refeeding syndrome occurs when carbohydrate delivery triggers insulin secretion in a patient whose total body stores of phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium are depleted from prolonged starvation. Insulin drives these ions intracellularly, producing acute extracellular depletion. The hallmark is hypophosphatemia, often accompanied by hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia, within the first 24–72 hours of refeeding. The glucose elevation is a separate PN complication — the malnourished liver has impaired glycogen metabolism and reduced insulin sensitivity — but it does not cause the electrolyte triad. Analyzing the cues as a unified syndrome rather than three isolated lab abnormalities directs the nurse toward the correct management framework. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the electrolytes have continued to fall despite initial repletion, the patient now has cardiac rhythm changes (frequent PVCs, prolonged QT), respiratory compromise, altered mental status, and proximal muscle weakness. Simultaneously, the PICC site shows new erythema and purulent drainage, and the temperature is 38.2 °C. The nurse must determine which problem is the most immediately life-threatening and demands priority intervention. *Correct action:* The refeeding-driven electrolyte derangement — specifically the worsening hypophosphatemia (1.3 mg/dL), hypokalemia (3.0 mEq/L), and hypomagnesemia (1.3 mg/dL) with active cardiac dysrhythmia (PVCs, prolonged QT) and respiratory compromise — is the most immediately life-threatening problem. The PICC site findings raise concern for a suspected catheter-related bloodstream infection (CRBSI) and must be addressed, but the infection is not yet producing hemodynamic instability attributable to sepsis (the tachycardia and hypotension are more consistent with the electrolyte-driven cardiac irritability and the volume status of a malnourished patient, and she is warm/perfused without rigors). The cardiac rhythm changes from the electrolyte derangement carry the most immediate risk of a fatal arrhythmia. *Why it is correct:* Severe hypophosphatemia (< 1.5 mg/dL) causes diaphragmatic weakness and respiratory failure; combined hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia prolong the QT interval and predispose to torsades de pointes and ventricular tachycardia. The PVCs on telemetry confirm that the arrhythmia risk is already active. While the PICC site findings require blood cultures and empiric antibiotics for possible CRBSI, the infection has not yet produced septic shock, and the electrolyte-driven cardiac and respiratory compromise is the proximate threat to life. Prioritizing the refeeding electrolyte emergency over the developing line infection directs the nurse to the interventions that prevent cardiac arrest and respiratory failure in the next minutes to hours. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must determine the appropriate set of interventions to address the worsening refeeding syndrome, the hyperglycemia, and the PICC site findings, and must understand which actions are safe and which would be dangerous. *Correct action:* The management plan includes: (a) notify the provider urgently of the continued electrolyte decline despite repletion, the cardiac rhythm changes, and the PICC site findings; (b) anticipate an order to reduce the TPN infusion rate — not discontinue it abruptly — to slow the carbohydrate-driven intracellular electrolyte shift; (c) administer aggressive electrolyte repletion at the higher-tier protocol doses with cardiac monitoring during potassium and magnesium infusions; (d) obtain blood cultures from the PICC line and a peripheral site before initiating any ordered antibiotic; (e) increase glucose monitoring frequency and administer insulin per sliding scale for the hyperglycemia; (f) maintain continuous telemetry and monitor respiratory status closely. Do not independently stop a high-dextrose PN infusion without a provider order and a glucose-monitoring/dextrose backup plan. In this case, reduce the PN rate as ordered because it slows the carbohydrate-driven electrolyte shift while avoiding an unmanaged interruption. *Why it is correct:* Reducing the caloric delivery rate decreases the insulin surge that is driving the intracellular electrolyte shift, which is the root mechanism of refeeding syndrome. Aggressive repletion at higher doses is needed because the initial standard-tier doses were insufficient — the ongoing insulin-driven shift is consuming the repleted electrolytes faster than they can equilibrate. Blood cultures before antibiotics preserve diagnostic accuracy for the suspected CLABSI. The prohibition against abrupt independent TPN discontinuation is a fundamental nursing safety principle: an unmanaged interruption of a high-dextrose infusion can produce rebound hypoglycemia, so any cessation must be coordinated with glucose monitoring and potential dextrose replacement. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the provider has been notified and orders have been received to reduce the TPN rate by 50%, administer higher-tier electrolyte repletion, obtain blood cultures and start vancomycin, obtain a 12-lead ECG, and increase glucose monitoring. The nurse must determine the correct sequence for carrying out these actions. *Correct action:* Reduce the PN rate immediately, maintain telemetry/obtain the 12-lead ECG, and begin urgent magnesium/potassium/phosphate repletion as soon as available. Draw paired PICC/peripheral blood cultures before administering vancomycin; antibiotics follow cultures. These actions should be delegated/parallelized where possible and the infection workup should not delay electrolyte correction. Increase glucose monitoring to every 4 hours and administer insulin per sliding scale. *Why it is correct:* Reducing the TPN rate addresses the root driver of the refeeding electrolyte shift and must not be delayed. The ECG documents the baseline rhythm before electrolyte repletion alters it. Electrolyte repletion is the definitive treatment for the cardiac and respiratory compromise and must begin immediately. Blood cultures must precede antibiotic administration to avoid sterilizing the cultures and losing the ability to identify the organism. The vancomycin, while important for suspected CRBSI, addresses a developing infection that is not yet the proximate life threat — it follows the cultures and does not take priority over the anti-arrhythmic electrolyte repletion. Glucose monitoring and insulin coverage are ongoing and do not delay the acute interventions. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, 12 hours after the interventions were carried out, the nurse reviews the repeat labs, vital signs, telemetry, and clinical assessment to determine whether the patient is improving, stable, or deteriorating. *Correct action:* The rising electrolytes (potassium 3.0 → 3.4, phosphorus 1.3 → 1.9, magnesium 1.3 → 1.6), the resolution of PVCs and shortening of the QT interval on telemetry, the improved level of consciousness (oriented to person and place, able to lift arms), the normalizing respiratory rate and oxygen saturation, and the decreasing glucose (226 → 168) all confirm that the refeeding syndrome is responding to the rate reduction and aggressive repletion. The electrolytes are trending upward but have not yet normalized — continued monitoring every 12 hours and readiness for additional repletion doses are required. The plan to hold the TPN at the reduced rate for 48 hours before cautiously advancing is appropriate. The PICC site erythema has not expanded, and the patient is on empiric vancomycin; culture results will guide further antibiotic decisions. The nurse should not interpret the improving trajectory as a signal to resume the original TPN rate or to discontinue monitoring. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes in refeeding syndrome requires tracking the specific electrolytes that were depleted (phosphorus, potassium, magnesium), the cardiac rhythm that was compromised (QT interval, PVCs), and the clinical manifestations that reflected organ dysfunction (mental status, respiratory effort, muscle strength). Improvement in all of these domains confirms that the interventions are working. However, refeeding syndrome can recur if caloric delivery is advanced too quickly, so the evaluation must also confirm that the rate of TPN advancement is conservative and that monitoring frequency is maintained. A single set of improving labs does not mean the crisis is over — the trajectory must be sustained. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Focusing on the glucose of 204 mg/dL as the most significant new finding and overlooking the simultaneous decline in phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium. Hyperglycemia in a patient receiving parenteral nutrition is expected and manageable with insulin coverage; the electrolyte triad is the more dangerous development because it signals refeeding syndrome with risk of cardiac arrest and respiratory failure. A nurse who fixates on the glucose may delay recognition of the refeeding pattern.

- *(Decision point 1)* Treating the mild temperature of 37.1 °C or the unchanged PICC site as a significant cue at Stage 1, diverting attention from the electrolyte changes. At this stage, neither finding is abnormal enough to warrant priority attention, and pursuing a line-infection workup would delay recognition of the refeeding electrolyte emergency.

- *(Decision point 2)* Interpreting the three falling electrolytes as three separate, unrelated problems — "low potassium, low phosphorus, low magnesium" — rather than analyzing them as a unified syndrome driven by a single mechanism (insulin-mediated intracellular shift during refeeding). This fragmented analysis leads to piecemeal management and misses the need to address the root cause by modifying the rate of caloric delivery.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing the PICC site erythema and purulent drainage over the electrolyte-driven cardiac dysrhythmia. While the PICC findings are concerning and must be addressed, the PVCs and prolonged QT from severe hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, and hypomagnesemia carry the more immediate risk of a lethal arrhythmia. A nurse who prioritizes the line infection may delay the electrolyte repletion that prevents cardiac arrest.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing the hyperglycemia of 226 mg/dL as the most dangerous problem at Stage 2. While hyperglycemia requires treatment, a glucose of 226 mg/dL is not immediately life-threatening in the same way that the electrolyte-driven cardiac rhythm changes are. Treating the glucose aggressively with insulin while neglecting the electrolyte repletion could worsen the hypokalemia, since insulin drives potassium intracellularly — compounding the very problem that is causing the dysrhythmia.

- *(Decision point 4)* Independently discontinuing the TPN abruptly in response to the worsening refeeding syndrome, reasoning that stopping the caloric source will stop the electrolyte shift. An unmanaged interruption of a high-dextrose infusion removes the dextrose supply while the pancreas continues to secrete insulin in response to the preceding glucose load, producing rebound hypoglycemia that can cause seizures, loss of consciousness, and death. The correct action is to reduce the rate as ordered, not stop the infusion independently without a backup dextrose plan.

- *(Decision point 5)* Starting vancomycin before drawing blood cultures. Once the antibiotic is infusing, blood cultures may yield false-negative results, and the ability to identify the causative organism and tailor antibiotic therapy is lost. Cultures must always be drawn before the first dose of a new antibiotic.

- *(Decision point 5)* Delaying the TPN rate reduction to complete the 12-lead ECG or draw cultures first. While both the ECG and cultures are important, reducing the TPN rate is a simple intervention (adjusting the pump) that immediately addresses the root driver of the refeeding electrolyte shift and should not be deferred for other tasks.

- *(Decision point 6)* Interpreting the improving electrolytes at Stage 3 as evidence that the refeeding syndrome has resolved and recommending or accepting an order to resume the original TPN rate. The electrolytes are trending upward but have not normalized, and the total body stores remain depleted. Premature advancement of the TPN rate will re-trigger the intracellular shift and cause the electrolytes to plummet again. The conservative advancement plan (hold for 48 hours, then increase by 5 kcal/kg/day) must be maintained.

- *(Decision point 6)* Failing to continue monitoring the PICC site and culture results because the refeeding syndrome is improving. The CLABSI workup is still in progress; culture results will determine whether the PICC must be removed and whether vancomycin should be continued, narrowed, or changed. Improvement in the refeeding syndrome does not resolve the infection concern.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the hallmark electrolyte pattern of refeeding syndrome — simultaneous decline in phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium — in a malnourished patient within hours of initiating parenteral nutrition, and distinguish it from isolated lab abnormalities and other PN complications.

- Analyze the mechanism of refeeding syndrome (insulin-mediated intracellular electrolyte shift triggered by carbohydrate delivery after prolonged starvation) and apply that understanding to guide management decisions, including the need to reduce caloric delivery rate rather than simply repleting electrolytes.

- Prioritize the life-threatening electrolyte-driven cardiac and respiratory complications of refeeding syndrome over concurrent but less immediately dangerous problems such as hyperglycemia and a developing catheter-related infection.

- Apply parenteral nutrition safety principles, including the prohibition against abrupt TPN discontinuation (taper to prevent rebound hypoglycemia), the need for blood cultures before antibiotic initiation, and the requirement for continuous cardiac monitoring during IV potassium and magnesium repletion.

- Evaluate the effectiveness of refeeding syndrome management by tracking the trajectory of the specific electrolytes, cardiac rhythm, and clinical manifestations that were deranged, while recognizing that improving trends do not justify premature advancement of caloric delivery.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the electrolytes have continued to fall despite initial repletion, cardiac rhythm changes have appeared, and the full clinical picture of refeeding syndrome has declared.

*Most likely condition:* Refeeding syndrome. The simultaneous progressive decline in phosphorus (2.8 → 1.8 → 1.3 mg/dL), potassium (3.5 → 3.1 → 3.0 mEq/L), and magnesium (1.8 → 1.4 → 1.3 mg/dL) occurring within 30 hours of initiating parenteral nutrition in a patient with severe protein-calorie malnutrition from five weeks of negligible oral intake, accompanied by cardiac dysrhythmia (PVCs, prolonged QT), respiratory compromise, proximal muscle weakness, and altered mental status, is unambiguously refeeding syndrome.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Suspected catheter-related bloodstream infection (CRBSI) — the PICC site erythema, purulent drainage, and temperature of 38.2 °C raise legitimate concern, but possible CRBSI does not explain the progressive electrolyte triad or the prolonged QT with PVCs; the hemodynamic changes (tachycardia, mild hypotension) are more consistent with the electrolyte-driven cardiac irritability and the volume status of a malnourished patient than with septic shock (the patient remains warm and well-perfused without rigors), and the infection findings appeared after the electrolyte decline was already established. (2) Parenteral-nutrition–associated hyperglycemia as the primary problem — the glucose has risen to 226 mg/dL, but hyperglycemia alone does not produce the phosphorus-potassium-magnesium triad, the PVCs, or the prolonged QT; it is a concurrent PN complication, not the dominant condition driving the cardiac and respiratory deterioration.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Reduce the TPN infusion rate by 50% per order to decrease the carbohydrate-driven insulin surge that is the root mechanism of the intracellular electrolyte shift. (2) Administer aggressive electrolyte repletion at the higher-tier protocol doses (potassium 40 mEq IV over 2 hours, sodium phosphate 30 mmol IV over 4 hours, magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 2 hours) with continuous telemetry monitoring to correct the life-threatening deficits causing the cardiac dysrhythmia and respiratory compromise.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Independently discontinuing the TPN abruptly without an order or dextrose backup plan — an unmanaged interruption removes the dextrose source while insulin secretion continues, producing rebound hypoglycemia that can cause seizures and loss of consciousness; the correct action is to reduce the rate as ordered. (2) Administering aggressive insulin coverage for the glucose of 226 mg/dL as the first intervention — insulin drives potassium intracellularly and would worsen the hypokalemia that is already producing PVCs and a prolonged QT, compounding the most immediately dangerous derangement rather than correcting it.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Serum phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium levels on repeat labs every 12 hours — an upward trend in all three confirms that the rate reduction and repletion are overcoming the intracellular shift and that refeeding syndrome is resolving. (2) Cardiac rhythm on continuous telemetry, specifically resolution of PVCs and shortening of the QT interval — these are the direct markers of whether the electrolyte-driven cardiac toxicity is abating and the immediate arrhythmia risk is decreasing.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Serum glucose alone — while glucose monitoring is part of PN management, the glucose level does not track resolution of the refeeding electrolyte crisis; a nurse who focuses on normalizing the glucose as the primary outcome measure is monitoring the wrong complication. (2) White blood cell count — the WBC may be relevant to the CLABSI workup, but it does not evaluate whether the refeeding syndrome is improving or worsening; tracking the WBC as the primary parameter conflates the developing infection with the dominant electrolyte emergency.
