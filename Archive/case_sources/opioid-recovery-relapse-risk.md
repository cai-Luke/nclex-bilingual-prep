# Opioid use disorder in early recovery: relapse risk, pain management, and safety assessment after orthopedic injury

## PATIENT BACKGROUND
Marcus Delaney is a 38-year-old man with a history of opioid use disorder (OUD) who has been in recovery for approximately five months. He was stabilized on buprenorphine/naloxone (Suboxone) 16 mg/4 mg sublingual daily through an outpatient medication-assisted treatment (MAT) program and has been attending weekly group counseling. He works as a warehouse associate. His recovery supports include a sponsor through Narcotics Anonymous, a stable relationship with his girlfriend, and his mother, who lives nearby. He has a history of two prior relapses, the most recent eight months ago, which involved IV heroin use and resulted in a nonfatal overdose reversed with naloxone by paramedics. He has no psychiatric history other than a remote episode of depression during active use. He does not drink alcohol. He takes no other medications. His girlfriend was trained in naloxone administration and keeps a naloxone kit at home.

He presents to the emergency department after a fall from a loading dock at work, sustaining a closed, displaced fracture of the left distal radius. Orthopedics has evaluated him and plans closed reduction with procedural sedation in the ED, followed by casting and outpatient follow-up. The ED provider has placed orders for ketorolac 15 mg IV once now, ondansetron 4 mg IV PRN nausea, and has written "hold buprenorphine/naloxone — will discuss pain plan with patient and MAT prescriber." The provider has also ordered acetaminophen 1,000 mg PO every 6 hours scheduled and a nerve block consultation for post-reduction analgesia. No full opioid agonist analgesics have been ordered. The nurse is assigned to Marcus at the start of the ED visit.

## INITIAL PRESENTATION
Marcus is sitting upright on the stretcher, cradling his left arm in a temporary splint. He is alert, oriented, and cooperative but visibly anxious. He tells the nurse, "I need you to know I'm in recovery — I don't want anything that's going to mess that up. But this pain is really bad and I'm scared they're either going to give me something that sets me off, or they're going to let me sit here and suffer because of my history." He rates his pain 8 out of 10. He appears tearful and is gripping the side rail with his right hand. He adds, "My girlfriend doesn't even know I'm here yet. She's going to freak out — last time I was in an ED it was because I overdosed."

## ASSESSMENT FINDINGS
Vital signs: T 36.8 °C, HR 102, BP 148/92, RR 18, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Pain rated 8/10, localized to the left wrist and forearm, worsened with any movement. Left distal forearm is visibly deformed with moderate swelling; radial pulse 2+ and symmetric; fingers warm, capillary refill under 2 seconds, sensation intact to light touch in all digits, able to flex and extend fingers with pain. No open wound. Pupils equal, round, 4 mm, reactive. Skin warm and dry. No diaphoresis, no tremor, no piloerection, no rhinorrhea, no yawning. Mucous membranes moist. Bowel sounds present. Gait not assessed due to arm injury. He is fidgeting, shifting position frequently, and repeatedly asking when the doctor will return. He denies nausea, vomiting, chest pain, shortness of breath, and dizziness. He denies any substance use in the past five months. He reports he took his buprenorphine/naloxone dose this morning as usual. Last meal was six hours ago.

## LABORATORY DATA
CBC: WBC 9,200/µL, Hgb 14.8 g/dL, Hct 43%, platelets 238,000/µL. BMP: sodium 140 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, CO₂ 24 mEq/L, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 118 mg/dL. Hepatic panel: AST 28 U/L, ALT 32 U/L, alkaline phosphatase 68 U/L, total bilirubin 0.8 mg/dL. Urine drug screen: positive for buprenorphine (consistent with prescribed medication), negative for opiates, benzodiazepines, amphetamines, cocaine, cannabinoids, and methadone. Alcohol level: 0 mg/dL. PT/INR: 12.4 sec / 1.0.

## CLINICAL COURSE

### Stage 1 — ED arrival through pre-procedure preparation (0–2 hours)
Marcus receives ketorolac 15 mg IV and acetaminophen 1,000 mg PO. After 45 minutes his pain decreases from 8/10 to 6/10. Vitals: HR 94, BP 138/86, RR 16, SpO₂ 99%. He is somewhat calmer but still anxious. He asks the nurse, "Are they going to put me under for this? What are they going to use? I looked it up on my phone and it says they use fentanyl for sedation — I can't have fentanyl. I'll be right back where I started." He then says, more quietly, "Sometimes I think it would be easier if I just didn't have to deal with any of this anymore." The nurse notes this statement. When asked to clarify, he says, "I don't mean like that — I'm not going to hurt myself. I just mean recovery is exhausting. Every time something goes wrong I feel like I'm one bad day away from using again." He reports he has not contacted his girlfriend or his sponsor yet. He says he does not want to "bother" them. The regional anesthesia team arrives and discusses performing an ultrasound-guided brachial plexus nerve block for procedural and post-procedural pain control, which would avoid systemic opioid use. Marcus agrees but remains visibly tense. The ED provider confirms the plan: nerve block for analgesia, propofol for brief procedural sedation during the closed reduction, and continued multimodal non-opioid pain management afterward. The provider has not yet reached Marcus's MAT prescriber regarding the buprenorphine hold. The nurse recognizes that this hold needs clarification before a dose is missed, as abrupt discontinuation of MAT can increase OUD recurrence/relapse risk.

### Stage 2 — Post-procedure through ED observation (2–6 hours)
The closed reduction is performed successfully under propofol sedation with the nerve block in place. Marcus wakes from procedural sedation within minutes, oriented and without nausea. A short-arm cast is applied. Post-procedure vitals: HR 82, BP 126/78, RR 14, SpO₂ 99%. Pain is 2/10 with the nerve block active. He is told the nerve block will wear off in 12–18 hours and that he will need a pain management plan for when it does. The ED provider has now spoken with the MAT prescriber, who recommends resuming buprenorphine/naloxone at the current dose tonight and notes that buprenorphine itself provides analgesia at the mu-opioid receptor and, combined with scheduled acetaminophen, ibuprofen 400 mg PO every 6 hours (to begin at the next scheduled NSAID time to avoid overlapping with the IV ketorolac dose), and ice/elevation, should provide adequate post-fracture pain control. The MAT prescriber also recommends a follow-up MAT appointment within 48 hours. The ED provider writes discharge orders accordingly: resume buprenorphine/naloxone 16 mg/4 mg sublingual daily tonight, acetaminophen 1,000 mg PO every 6 hours, ibuprofen 400 mg PO every 6 hours with food (starting at the next scheduled NSAID interval to avoid overlap with ketorolac), ice and elevation, orthopedic follow-up in 5 days, MAT follow-up within 48 hours, and return precautions for cast complications.

Marcus's girlfriend, Tanya, arrives during this period. She is upset and tearful. She says to the nurse in the hallway, outside Marcus's hearing, "Is he using again? Please just tell me the truth. I can't go through another overdose." She then says, "He's been so moody the last few weeks — not sleeping, snapping at me. I found him sitting in his car in the driveway at 2 AM last week and he wouldn't tell me what he was doing. I'm scared." Marcus, when the nurse returns to his room, says, "She's out there talking about me, isn't she? She doesn't trust me. Nobody does. What's the point of staying clean if everyone still treats you like a junkie?" His eyes are red. He is picking at the edge of his cast. He denies suicidal ideation when asked directly but says, "I just feel really alone right now."

### Stage 3 — Discharge preparation and teaching (6–8 hours)
Marcus is being prepared for discharge. The nerve block is beginning to wear off; he reports pain increasing to 5/10 in the left wrist. Vitals: HR 88, BP 132/82, RR 16, SpO₂ 99%. He took his evening buprenorphine/naloxone dose 30 minutes ago. He has taken acetaminophen and ibuprofen as scheduled. He is restless and says, "This is exactly the kind of thing that made me use before — being in pain, feeling like crap, not being able to work. I know what would make this pain go away in five minutes." He pauses and then says, "I'm not going to do it. But I'd be lying if I said I wasn't thinking about it." Tanya is in the room. She looks at the nurse with alarm. Marcus has not yet called his sponsor. He says his NA meeting is tomorrow evening but he is not sure he will go because "I can't drive with this cast and I don't want to ask anyone for a ride." He has not confirmed his MAT follow-up appointment. When the nurse reviews the discharge medications, he asks, "Is the ibuprofen going to be enough? What if the pain gets really bad tonight — what do I do? I don't want to end up in the ER again." He also asks, "If something did happen — if I did slip — does Tanya's naloxone kit still work? She got it like a year ago."

## KEY DECISION POINTS

1. **Situation:** At Stage 1, Marcus makes the statement, "Sometimes I think it would be easier if I just didn't have to deal with any of this anymore." He then clarifies that he does not mean self-harm but that recovery is exhausting and he feels one bad day from relapse. The nurse must determine which elements of his presentation at this moment are significant cues requiring nursing attention versus expected or benign findings. The relevant data set includes: his statement about things being easier, his clarification denying self-harm intent, his self-reported pain of 6/10 after analgesia, his elevated heart rate and blood pressure, his tearfulness and fidgeting, his report that he has not contacted his girlfriend or sponsor, his statement that he does not want to bother them, his anxiety about the sedation plan, and his urine drug screen results.
   - **Correct action:** The significant cues are the ambiguous statement about not wanting to deal with things (which requires further exploration regardless of his clarification), his social isolation — actively avoiding his two primary recovery supports and framing contact as "bothering" them — his expressed fear that any opioid exposure will trigger relapse, and his tearfulness and emotional distress beyond what the pain alone accounts for. The elevated heart rate and blood pressure are expected with acute pain and anxiety in this context and are not independently alarming given that they improved after analgesia. The urine drug screen confirming buprenorphine and negative for illicit substances is reassuring and consistent with his reported adherence — it is not a cue requiring action.
   - **Why it is correct:** In early recovery, self-isolation from support systems is a well-established relapse warning sign. The ambiguous statement, even with clarification, warrants documentation and continued monitoring because patients may minimize distress. Recognizing which findings cluster into a psychosocial risk pattern — and which are physiologically expected — is the foundation for all subsequent nursing decisions.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Recognize cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

2. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse must interpret the cluster of cues identified above — Marcus's self-isolation, his catastrophic framing ("one bad day away from using"), his ambiguous statement, and his emotional distress — in the context of his history of two prior relapses, his most recent relapse involving IV heroin and a nonfatal overdose, and his current acute stressor (pain, inability to work, fear of opioid exposure). The nurse must determine what these cues, taken together, most likely represent.
   - **Correct action:** The cue cluster is most consistent with acute relapse risk in the context of a major psychosocial and physical stressor in a patient with early recovery and a history of relapse under stress. It is not withdrawal — he has no objective withdrawal signs (no diaphoresis, tremor, piloerection, rhinorrhea, yawning, or mydriasis), and his reported daily adherence and UDS positive for buprenorphine support that he is not in withdrawal. It is not active suicidal ideation — he denied intent and plan when explored, and his distress is contextually appropriate — but the ambiguous statement places him in a category requiring ongoing safety monitoring rather than dismissal. It is not drug-seeking behavior — his anxiety about opioid exposure is protective, not appetitive; he is asking to avoid opioids, not requesting them. The dominant concern is that his coping resources are being overwhelmed by converging stressors while he is actively disconnecting from his support network.
   - **Why it is correct:** Differentiating relapse risk from withdrawal, overdose, suicidal crisis, and drug-seeking is essential because each requires a different nursing response. Mislabeling his distress leads to either unnecessary escalation (psychiatric hold for a patient who is not suicidal) or dangerous under-response (dismissing relapse risk as mere anxiety). The absence of withdrawal signs combined with buprenorphine adherence (and UDS positive for buprenorphine) rules out withdrawal. The protective nature of his opioid anxiety distinguishes this from drug-seeking.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Analyze cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

3. **Situation:** At Stage 2, Tanya reports concerning behavioral changes — mood instability, insomnia, sitting alone in the car at 2 AM — and asks the nurse directly whether Marcus is using again. Marcus, separately, expresses hopelessness about others' trust ("What's the point of staying clean if everyone still treats you like a junkie?"), reports feeling alone, and shows signs of shame and emotional withdrawal. The nurse must determine which concern takes priority at this moment: Tanya's need for information and support, Marcus's escalating emotional distress and deepening isolation, the discharge medication teaching that is pending, or contacting the MAT prescriber about the buprenorphine plan.
   - **Correct action:** The priority is Marcus's escalating emotional distress and deepening isolation. His statements have shifted from anxious-but-coping in Stage 1 to hopeless and withdrawn in Stage 2. He is now expressing purposelessness ("what's the point"), which represents a qualitative change from his earlier distress. This warrants a focused therapeutic interaction — using open-ended, nonjudgmental communication to explore his current emotional state, gently reassessing for suicidal ideation given the shift in tone, and supporting reconnection with his recovery network (sponsor, NA, MAT program). Tanya's concerns are valid, but the nurse must maintain Marcus's confidentiality and cannot confirm or deny his substance-use status, urine drug screen results, or medication plan without his explicit consent. The nurse should address Tanya by validating her fear in a supportive, general way without disclosing patient-specific info, then work to invite Marcus's consent to include Tanya in his discharge safety planning. Prioritizing Marcus's escalating emotional distress and support network engagement is the immediate need, while a shared, supportive conversation between Marcus and Tanya can be facilitated once Marcus agrees. The discharge teaching and MAT prescriber contact are important but not time-sensitive at this moment — the buprenorphine plan has already been communicated by the MAT prescriber, and discharge is still hours away.
   - **Why it is correct:** Psychosocial prioritization in substance use recovery follows the same airway-breathing-circulation logic as physiologic triage: the most immediately destabilizing threat is addressed first. Marcus's progressive emotional deterioration — from anxiety to hopelessness to purposelessness — is the trajectory that precedes relapse or self-harm. Addressing Tanya first, however well-intentioned, would rupture the therapeutic alliance at the moment it is most needed.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Prioritize hypotheses.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

4. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse needs to respond therapeutically to Marcus's statement, "What's the point of staying clean if everyone still treats you like a junkie?" and his report of feeling alone. The nurse must determine the appropriate therapeutic communication approach and the set of actions that address his immediate psychosocial needs.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should first reflect his emotion without judgment — acknowledging the pain of feeling distrusted and the exhaustion of recovery without minimizing it or offering false reassurance (e.g., "It sounds like you're feeling hurt that the people you care about still seem worried, even after everything you've done to stay in recovery. That's a heavy thing to carry on top of a broken arm."). The nurse should then gently explore his use of the word "point" — not as an accusatory suicide screening question but as a continuation of the therapeutic exchange (e.g., "When you say 'what's the point,' can you tell more about what you mean by that?"). Based on his response, the nurse should conduct a brief structured safety assessment if his language suggests any passive ideation, document the interaction and his responses, encourage him to contact his sponsor or another recovery support before discharge — offering concrete assistance such as "Would it help if I handed you your phone so you could text your sponsor while I step out for a few minutes?" — and normalize the difficulty of the moment without normalizing the isolation (e.g., "A lot of people in recovery find that stressful situations like this are the hardest part. Reaching out to your support people right now isn't bothering them — it's exactly what the plan is for."). The nurse should avoid platitudes ("You're doing great," "Just stay positive"), avoid arguing with his perception of others' distrust, and avoid disclosing Tanya's hallway conversation, which would violate his trust.
   - **Why it is correct:** Therapeutic communication in substance use recovery requires validating the emotional experience without reinforcing the cognitive distortion (that recovery is pointless). Reflection demonstrates that the nurse heard the feeling, not just the words. Exploring "what's the point" serves dual purposes — it is both a therapeutic deepening of the conversation and an indirect safety screen. Offering concrete, low-barrier assistance with contacting supports addresses the behavioral pattern (avoidance) rather than just advising him to do something he has already resisted.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Generate solutions.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

5. **Situation:** At Stage 3, Marcus openly acknowledges craving ("I know what would make this pain go away in five minutes") while simultaneously stating he does not intend to act on it. His pain is rising as the nerve block wears off. He has not contacted his sponsor, is uncertain about attending his NA meeting, has not confirmed his MAT follow-up, and is asking about the adequacy of his pain regimen and the viability of the naloxone kit. The nurse must carry out discharge teaching and safety planning.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse should address the converging discharge needs as an integrated safety and recovery plan rather than as separate checklist items. The actions, which are concurrent priorities rather than a strict sequence, include: (a) Validate his honesty about the craving — "Thank you for telling me that. Being honest about cravings is a strength, not a failure" — and reinforce that cravings are expected, time-limited, and do not mean relapse is inevitable. (b) Teach the multimodal pain plan concretely: acetaminophen and ibuprofen are taken on a scheduled basis, not PRN, to stay ahead of the pain (instructing them to begin the ibuprofen at the next scheduled NSAID interval to avoid overlap with the IV ketorolac, and emphasizing not to exceed the maximum daily dose of acetaminophen [4,000 mg/day] or use concurrent over-the-counter products containing acetaminophen); ice and elevation reduce swelling and pain; his buprenorphine provides partial mu-agonist analgesia in addition to its anti-craving effect. Explain that uncontrolled pain is itself a major relapse driver, and if pain becomes unmanageable, he should contact the MAT prescriber, orthopedics, or return to the ED for supervised clinical reassessment rather than self-medicating or using non-prescribed opioids; clarify that medically supervised opioid analgesia, if deemed necessary by providers, can be managed safely under close supervision and is not strictly forbidden. (c) Address the naloxone kit: he and Tanya should check the expiration date printed on the specific device/package. While many newer NARCAN 4 mg nasal spray products have longer labeled shelf lives (up to 4 years), this varies by product and lot; if expired, he should obtain a replacement through his MAT program, a pharmacy, or a community distribution program; however, in an emergency, any available naloxone should still be administered while calling 911 rather than withholding it due to the expiration date. Teach Tanya the signs of an opioid overdose — inability to awaken, slow/shallow/irregular or absent breathing, choking/gurgling/snoring sounds in a person who cannot be awakened, blue/gray/pale lips or nail beds, a limp body, and pinpoint pupils when present — and confirm she remembers the administration steps. (d) Assist with concrete recovery-support logistics: offer to help him call his sponsor now, before discharge; problem-solve transportation to the NA meeting (ride-share, phone meeting, a friend from the group); and confirm the MAT follow-up appointment by calling the clinic or helping him call while still in the ED. (e) Provide the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline number (988) and the SAMHSA National Helpline (1-800-662-4357) as resources he can use if he feels overwhelmed after discharge.
   - **Why it is correct:** Discharge from an acute care setting is a high-risk transition for patients in early recovery, particularly when pain is undertreated or when the patient has identified active craving and has weakened support connections. Integrating harm-reduction teaching (naloxone review), relapse-prevention planning (support contacts, meeting logistics), pain management education (scheduled multimodal regimen with clear escalation pathway), and safety resources into a single coherent conversation — rather than delivering them as disconnected instructions — increases the likelihood that the patient will use them.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Take action.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

6. **Situation:** At Stage 3, the nurse must determine which findings would indicate that the discharge plan is adequate and Marcus is safe to leave, versus which findings would require the nurse to escalate before discharge — notifying the provider, requesting a social work or behavioral health consultation, or in extreme circumstances initiating safety precautions.
   - **Correct action:** Indicators that the plan is adequate and discharge is appropriate include: Marcus has contacted at least one recovery support (sponsor, NA contact, or MAT program) before leaving; he can verbalize his pain management plan and knows whom to call if pain is uncontrolled; he and Tanya can demonstrate or verbalize naloxone use; he has a confirmed MAT follow-up appointment within 48 hours; he denies suicidal ideation on direct reassessment; and his affect has shifted from hopeless and withdrawn toward engaged and future-oriented, even if still anxious. Indicators that would require escalation before discharge include: new or emerging suicidal ideation (passive or active) on reassessment, a statement of intent to use opioids after discharge, refusal to contact any recovery support or attend any follow-up, or an inability to identify any reason to maintain recovery (complete loss of protective factors). The nurse should also escalate if Tanya reports that the behavioral changes she described — insomnia, 2 AM isolation, mood instability — have included any self-harm statements, weapon access, or giving away possessions, as these would reframe the risk profile.
   - **Why it is correct:** Evaluating outcomes in psychosocial care requires assessing both objective markers (confirmed appointments, demonstrated knowledge, stated plan) and qualitative markers (affect, engagement, future orientation, willingness to use supports). A patient who has moved from hopelessness to active problem-solving — even imperfectly — is on a different trajectory than one who remains withdrawn and purposeless. The nurse's role is not to guarantee that relapse will not occur but to confirm that the safety net is in place and that the patient is not leaving in acute crisis.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Evaluate outcomes.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

## COMMON NURSING ERRORS

- Treating Marcus's elevated heart rate and blood pressure as the primary cues requiring intervention — interpreting them as possible withdrawal or a cardiovascular event — rather than recognizing them as expected findings in a patient with acute pain and anxiety who has already shown improvement after analgesia. This error leads to unnecessary medical workup and delays attention to the psychosocial cues that are actually significant.
- Dismissing Marcus's statement about not wanting to "bother" his girlfriend and sponsor as a personality trait or politeness rather than recognizing self-isolation from recovery supports as a relapse warning sign. In early recovery, withdrawal from the support network is a behavioral cue, not a preference.
- Labeling Marcus's anxiety about opioid exposure as drug-seeking behavior — interpreting his repeated questions about the sedation plan and his insistence on avoiding fentanyl as manipulation or an attempt to control his care. This is the opposite of what is happening: his anxiety is protective and recovery-oriented. Mislabeling it as drug-seeking damages the therapeutic relationship and may lead to undertreating his pain or dismissing his concerns.
- Interpreting his emotional distress as opioid withdrawal because he is in a population with OUD, despite the complete absence of objective withdrawal signs and reported adherence with a urine drug screen positive for buprenorphine, which confirms exposure consistent with prescribed use but is not a therapeutic serum level. This error conflates the patient's history with his current physiology.
- Prioritizing Tanya's request for information over Marcus's emotional needs — going to Marcus and saying, "Your girlfriend is worried you're using again; are you?" This violates the therapeutic relationship, confirms Marcus's belief that he is not trusted, and may precipitate the very withdrawal and relapse risk the nurse is trying to prevent. Tanya's concerns are legitimate and should be addressed, but after engaging Marcus and with his knowledge and involvement. Also, violating Marcus's privacy rights by confirming his OUD diagnosis, UDS results, or MAT regimen to Tanya without his consent, rather than validating her anxiety generally and working to obtain Marcus's permission to involve her in safety planning.
- Prioritizing the discharge medication teaching over Marcus's emotional state because the teaching is a concrete, documentable task. Completing a checklist while the patient is in emotional crisis results in information that is neither heard nor retained and misses the window to intervene in the escalating psychosocial deterioration.
- Offering false reassurance — "You're doing amazing, five months is a long time, you should be proud" — instead of reflecting his actual emotional experience. While well-intentioned, this dismisses his pain, implies he should not feel the way he feels, and shuts down further disclosure. It is a social response, not a therapeutic one.
- Disclosing the content of Tanya's hallway conversation to Marcus — telling him what she said about the 2 AM car incident or her fear that he is using. This breaches the separate therapeutic interaction with Tanya and weaponizes her vulnerability. The nurse should encourage Tanya to share her concerns with Marcus directly, in a supported conversation if appropriate, but should not act as an intermediary delivering her fears.
- Telling Marcus that he should not be having cravings at five months of recovery, or responding to his craving disclosure with alarm or visible concern that signals judgment. Cravings are a normal, expected part of recovery and do not indicate failure. A nurse who reacts with surprise or worry reinforces the shame that drives concealment and isolation.
- Advising Marcus to dispose of the naloxone kit because "you won't need it if you stay clean" — framing harm-reduction tools as incompatible with recovery. Naloxone availability is a safety measure, not an endorsement of use. Removing it increases mortality risk without reducing relapse risk.
- Failing to clarify the buprenorphine hold order with the provider and MAT prescriber before a dose is missed — buprenorphine should not be routinely discontinued for acute pain management as abrupt interruption can cause withdrawal and significantly increase OUD relapse risk.
- Overlapping ibuprofen doses immediately with recent IV ketorolac administration, rather than waiting for the next scheduled NSAID interval, which increases the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding and renal impairment.
- Failing to instruct the patient on acetaminophen safety, including avoiding duplicate over-the-counter products containing acetaminophen to prevent accidental hepatotoxicity.
- Providing discharge teaching as a list of instructions without addressing the logistical barriers Marcus has identified — transportation to NA, uncertainty about the MAT appointment, concern about pain escalation overnight. Teaching that does not solve the stated barriers is unlikely to result in adherence.
- Discharging Marcus solely because he denies suicidal ideation on a single direct question, without assessing the broader safety picture — whether he has reconnected with supports, whether his affect has shifted, whether he has a concrete plan for the next 48 hours. A single "no" to a suicide screening question is necessary but not sufficient for evaluating psychosocial safety at discharge.
- Escalating to a psychiatric hold or refusing to discharge Marcus because he acknowledged craving. Craving with insight, stated intention not to act, and willingness to engage in safety planning is not a criterion for involuntary hold. Over-escalation is as harmful as under-escalation — it pathologizes normal recovery experiences and erodes trust in the healthcare system.

## EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

- Recognize psychosocial and behavioral cues of relapse risk in a patient with opioid use disorder in early recovery, distinguishing them from withdrawal, drug-seeking, and suicidal crisis.
- Apply nonjudgmental therapeutic communication techniques — reflection, open-ended exploration, validation without false reassurance — in response to emotionally loaded statements from a patient experiencing shame, hopelessness, and craving.
- Prioritize psychosocial nursing interventions when a patient's emotional trajectory is deteriorating, even when competing clinical tasks (medication teaching, discharge documentation) are pending.
- Integrate harm-reduction teaching (naloxone review, overdose recognition, escalation pathways for uncontrolled pain) with recovery-support planning (sponsor contact, meeting logistics, MAT follow-up) into a discharge safety plan.
- Evaluate readiness for safe discharge by assessing both objective markers (confirmed follow-up, verbalized plan, demonstrated knowledge) and qualitative markers (affect, engagement, willingness to use recovery supports), and identify criteria that would require escalation before discharge.

## BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, as the nerve block is wearing off, Marcus has openly acknowledged craving, and the nurse is finalizing the discharge plan.

- **Most likely condition:** Acute relapse risk in the context of converging physical and psychosocial stressors — uncontrolled pain, functional impairment, shame, self-isolation from recovery supports, and active craving — in a patient with early-recovery OUD and a history of relapse under stress. The patient is not in active relapse, withdrawal, or suicidal crisis, but he is at the highest-risk point in the case for decompensation after discharge.
- **Plausible competing conditions:**
  1. *Active opioid withdrawal* — wrong here: Marcus has no objective withdrawal signs (no diaphoresis, tremor, mydriasis, piloerection, rhinorrhea, yawning, or GI distress), his urine drug screen confirms buprenorphine exposure consistent with prescribed use, and he took his dose this evening; his distress is psychosocial and pain-related, not pharmacologic.
  2. *Acute suicidal crisis requiring psychiatric intervention* — wrong here: Marcus denied suicidal ideation on two separate direct assessments, his ambiguous statements were explored and contextualized as recovery fatigue and hopelessness rather than intent, and he retains protective factors (stated desire to stay clean, willingness to discuss craving openly, engagement with the nurse); his risk level warrants ongoing monitoring and safety planning, not involuntary hold or psychiatric emergency response.
- **Two priority actions:**
  1. Engage Marcus in concrete discharge safety planning — assist him in contacting his sponsor or another recovery support before he leaves the ED, confirm the MAT follow-up appointment, problem-solve transportation to his NA meeting, and provide crisis line numbers — so that he leaves with an activated support network rather than a list of phone numbers he has not called.
  2. Conduct harm-reduction and pain-management teaching with both Marcus and Tanya together — review the multimodal pain regimen and the specific escalation pathway if pain is uncontrolled (call MAT prescriber or return to ED), verify the printed expiration date on the specific naloxone kit, and confirm Tanya can recognize overdose signs (inability to awaken, slow/shallow/irregular or absent breathing, blue/gray/pale lips or nail beds, a limp body) and administer naloxone — so that the household has both a pain plan and a safety plan.
- **Two wrong actions:**
  1. Withholding discharge and requesting a psychiatric consultation or initiating a safety hold based on Marcus's craving disclosure — this over-escalates a normal recovery experience (craving with insight and stated intention not to act), pathologizes honest communication, and teaches the patient that disclosing cravings to healthcare providers results in loss of autonomy, making future disclosure less likely.
  2. Completing discharge paperwork and providing written instructions without addressing the logistical barriers Marcus has identified (no transportation to NA, unconfirmed MAT appointment, uncertainty about pain escalation overnight) — this treats discharge teaching as a documentation task rather than a safety intervention, and the unresolved barriers are the specific mechanisms through which relapse risk converts to actual relapse.
- **Two parameters to monitor:**
  1. Marcus's engagement with his recovery support network before and after discharge — whether he contacts his sponsor, confirms his MAT follow-up, and has a plan for his NA meeting — because reconnection with supports is the strongest modifiable predictor of whether the relapse risk identified in this visit will or will not progress to actual relapse.
  2. His pain trajectory over the next 24–48 hours as reported at MAT follow-up or by phone — because uncontrolled pain in a patient with OUD is both a suffering issue and a relapse driver, and the adequacy of the multimodal non-opioid regimen must be confirmed rather than assumed.
- **Two irrelevant parameters:**
  1. Repeat urine drug screening at the MAT follow-up — while UDS is a standard component of MAT monitoring, it is not the priority parameter for evaluating this ED discharge safety plan.
