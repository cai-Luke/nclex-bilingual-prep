**CASE TITLE**
Postpartum depression with intrusive thoughts of infant harm.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 29-year-old primipara, G1P1, delivered a healthy full-term male infant via uncomplicated vaginal delivery six weeks ago. She has no prior psychiatric history but reports a family history of major depressive disorder in her mother. She is breastfeeding exclusively. Her pregnancy was uncomplicated, and she had no history of mood disturbance during the prenatal period. She lives with her partner, who works full-time during the day. She has no history of substance use. She presented to her obstetrician's office for her six-week postpartum visit and was referred to the outpatient behavioral health clinic affiliated with the hospital after screening positive on the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) with a score of 19 out of 30. She denied suicidal ideation on the screening but endorsed the item about self-blame and the item about being anxious or worried for no good reason. She has been started on sertraline 50 mg daily by her obstetrician, which is compatible with breastfeeding, and referred for cognitive-behavioral therapy. She is seen today for her first behavioral health intake appointment, accompanied by her mother, who is visiting for the week.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
She arrives at the behavioral health clinic holding her infant in a car seat. She appears fatigued, with flat affect, minimal eye contact, and speaks in a low, monotone voice. She states she has not been sleeping well even when the baby sleeps, reports persistent feelings of inadequacy as a mother, and says she "can't stop the bad thoughts." When asked to elaborate, she becomes tearful and says she keeps imagining dropping the baby down the stairs, which horrifies her. She states she would never hurt her baby and that these thoughts make her feel like "a monster." She reports she has been avoiding carrying the baby near the staircase and has asked her partner to handle all diaper changes upstairs. She has been taking the sertraline for five days.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
General appearance: thin, fatigued-appearing woman, appropriately dressed but with unwashed hair, holding her hands tightly in her lap. Affect is flat with intermittent tearfulness. Speech is soft and slow but coherent and goal-directed. She is oriented to person, place, time, and situation. No psychomotor agitation. No evidence of disorganized or delusional thinking. She does not believe the thoughts are commands or voices — she identifies them as her own unwanted thoughts. She denies any desire or plan to act on the thoughts. She denies suicidal ideation, homicidal ideation, or intent to harm herself or the infant. She reports appetite has been poor — she has lost 4 kg beyond her expected postpartum weight loss. She rates her mood as 2 out of 10. She reports anhedonia: she used to enjoy reading and cooking but has no interest in either. She describes guilt about not feeling bonded to her infant and says she sometimes feels the baby would be "better off with someone else." Vital signs: T 36.8 °C, HR 78, BP 118/72, RR 16, SpO₂ 99% on room air. PHQ-9 completed in clinic: score 18 (moderately severe depression). Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS): no lifetime or recent suicidal ideation, no suicidal behavior.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Obtained at the six-week postpartum visit by the obstetrician: TSH 2.4 mIU/L (normal 0.4–4.0), CBC within normal limits, hemoglobin 12.1 g/dL, comprehensive metabolic panel unremarkable. These results make clinically significant thyroid dysfunction and anemia unlikely contributors to her mood symptoms.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Initial behavioral health intake (today, day 5 of sertraline). The nurse's immediate priority is conducting a structured safety and risk assessment. The nurse assesses for suicidal ideation, intent/plan, thoughts of harming the infant, psychosis, command hallucinations, ability to care for the infant, and support at home. Following this assessment, the patient discloses the intrusive thoughts about dropping the baby, her avoidance behaviors around the staircase, and her distress about the thoughts. She is tearful but engaged. Her mother, who is in the waiting room, tells the nurse privately that her daughter "hasn't been herself" and that she found her crying in the bathroom at 3 AM two nights ago, not because the baby was crying but because she "couldn't turn off her brain." The mother asks whether the intrusive thoughts mean her daughter is dangerous to the baby. The patient reports sleeping approximately 2–3 hours per night in fragmented intervals, even when the baby sleeps 4–5 hour stretches. She has not yet attended any therapy sessions. She reports taking the sertraline as prescribed but says she "doesn't feel any different yet." She denies any side effects from the medication. The nurse administers the EPDS again: score 20. The patient expresses fear that if she tells anyone about the thoughts, her baby will be taken away.

**Stage 2** — Two-week follow-up visit (day 19 of sertraline). The patient returns, this time without her mother, who has gone home. She is alone with the infant. She reports the sertraline "might be helping a little" — she is sleeping slightly better (3–4 hours per night) and her appetite has improved marginally. However, the intrusive thoughts have not diminished and have expanded: she now also imagines the baby choking, and she has begun checking the baby's breathing every 15–20 minutes throughout the night, even when the baby is sleeping soundly. She has started avoiding bathing the baby alone because she imagines the baby slipping underwater. She has not left the house alone with the baby since her mother departed four days ago. She has attended one telehealth CBT session and reports the therapist began discussing the concept of intrusive thoughts versus intent, which she found "a little helpful." She continues to deny suicidal ideation or intent to harm the infant. She appears more anxious than at the first visit — she is fidgeting, picking at her cuticles, and her speech is slightly pressured when discussing the thoughts. PHQ-9: 16 (still moderately severe, slight improvement). EPDS: 18. She reports her partner has been supportive but is becoming frustrated because she will not let him sleep — she wakes him to check the baby. She states, "I know it doesn't make sense, but I can't stop." She asks the nurse whether the medication dose should be increased. Vital signs: T 36.9 °C, HR 84, BP 122/76, RR 18, SpO₂ 99%.

**Stage 3** — Five-week follow-up visit (day 33 of sertraline, now titrated to 100 mg daily as of day 21 per provider order). The patient arrives with the infant in a stroller. She makes eye contact and greets the nurse by name. She reports sleeping 5–6 hours per night, eating regular meals, and has taken the baby for a walk outside twice this week. She states the intrusive thoughts still occur but are "quieter" and less frequent — once or twice a day rather than constantly. She no longer avoids the staircase, though she still holds the railing. She has attended three CBT sessions and reports that cognitive restructuring techniques — specifically labeling the thoughts as "just thoughts, not plans" — have been the most helpful intervention. She bathed the baby alone yesterday without significant distress. EPDS: 11 (decreased from 20 and 18). PHQ-9: 10 (moderate, improved from 18 and 16). She smiles briefly when describing the baby's new habit of grasping her finger. She reports her partner and she have established a nighttime schedule so each gets a longer sleep block. She asks how long she will need to stay on the medication and whether it is still safe for breastfeeding. She also asks whether she is at risk for this happening again if she has another baby. Vital signs: T 36.7 °C, HR 74, BP 116/70, RR 14, SpO₂ 99%.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient discloses intrusive thoughts about dropping the baby down the stairs. The nurse must classify these thoughts — are they consistent with the ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts of postpartum depression/anxiety, or do they suggest psychotic features requiring emergent intervention? *Correct action:* The first nursing action must be a structured risk assessment for psychosis, command hallucinations, and intent to harm. Once assessed, recognize these as ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts consistent with postpartum depression with anxious features, not postpartum psychosis. The patient is distressed by the thoughts, identifies them as unwanted and contrary to her wishes, has no command hallucinations or delusions, maintains reality testing, and has implemented avoidance behaviors to prevent the feared scenario — all hallmarks of intrusive thoughts rather than psychotic ideation. *Why:* Misclassifying intrusive thoughts as psychosis leads to unnecessary hospitalization and separation from the infant; failing to recognize actual psychosis is a safety emergency. The distinction must be made on the specific cues following a thorough safety assessment. *Skill:* Recognize cues, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient expresses fear that disclosing her thoughts will result in her baby being taken away. The mother separately asks whether the daughter is dangerous. The nurse must respond therapeutically to both the patient's fear and the mother's concern. *Correct action:* After establishing safety via the structured risk assessment, validate the patient's courage in disclosing the thoughts. Normalize ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts as a recognized feature of postpartum depression that does not indicate danger to the infant, and communicate that having these ego-dystonic thoughts alone, after a safety assessment showing no intent, psychosis, or unsafe care, does not automatically mean CPS will be contacted. For the mother, provide psychoeducation that intrusive thoughts in postpartum depression are distressing precisely because the mother does not want to harm the baby, distinguish this from psychosis, and enlist the mother as a support person. Document the assessment, the patient's denial of intent, and the safety plan. *Why:* Fear of disclosure is the primary barrier to treatment in postpartum depression with intrusive thoughts. The therapeutic response must be grounded in the clinical distinction already made via safety assessment — the patient's ego-dystonic presentation and intact reality testing support reassurance. If the nurse skips the safety assessment to prematurely reassure, the reassurance is clinically unfounded. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the intrusive thoughts have expanded (now including choking and drowning scenarios), avoidance behaviors have increased (not bathing the baby alone, not leaving the house alone with the baby), and compulsive checking has emerged. The patient's depression scores have improved only marginally. The nurse must analyze whether this trajectory represents expected early treatment response, inadequate treatment response requiring escalation, or clinical deterioration. *Correct action:* Analyze this as an inadequate treatment response with emerging obsessive-compulsive features that warrants provider notification for medication adjustment. The marginal improvement in depression scores and sleep after 19 days on sertraline 50 mg is below the optimal trajectory. The expanding avoidance and compulsive checking are functionally impairing. The nurse should communicate these findings to the prescribing provider, anticipate a dose increase, and reinforce continued engagement with CBT, while avoiding promising rapid or universal improvement. *Why:* Sertraline is first-line for postpartum depression and is effective for the obsessive-compulsive spectrum features that commonly co-occur, but the therapeutic dose range for these symptoms is often 100–200 mg daily. Waiting passively for the medication to "kick in" at the current dose delays relief. *Skill:* Analyze cues, prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient asks whether the medication dose should be increased. The nurse must respond within scope of practice. *Correct action:* Acknowledge the patient's observation that her symptoms have not adequately improved, validate that her question is appropriate, and inform her that the nurse will communicate her symptom report and the updated screening scores to the prescribing provider, who will determine whether a dose adjustment is indicated. The nurse should advocate for timely provider review given the functional impairment, but avoid guaranteeing a specific clinical timeline. *Why:* Dose titration is a prescriber decision. The nurse's role is to provide accurate, organized clinical data so the provider can make an informed decision. *Skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient shows improvement across multiple domains — mood, sleep, appetite, social functioning, bonding behaviors, reduced frequency and intensity of intrusive thoughts, decreased avoidance, and improved screening scores. She asks how long she will need to stay on the medication and whether it is safe to continue breastfeeding. The nurse must evaluate the response to treatment and provide appropriate anticipatory guidance. *Correct action:* Evaluate the trajectory as a positive treatment response, noting that recovery timelines vary and ongoing therapy is crucial. For the medication questions, reinforce that sertraline at the current dose is considered compatible with breastfeeding per current guidelines, and that the duration of treatment is a decision she will make with her prescribing provider, but that current recommendations generally support continuing antidepressant therapy for at least 6–12 months after symptom remission to reduce relapse risk. Regarding future pregnancies, inform her that a history of postpartum depression increases the risk of recurrence. *Why:* Premature discontinuation of antidepressant therapy is the most common cause of relapse in postpartum depression. Providing accurate anticipatory guidance supports informed decision-making and treatment adherence without overpromising. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision Point 1)* Interpreting the intrusive thoughts as evidence of psychotic thinking or imminent danger to the infant, leading to unnecessary emergency psychiatric referral. This is wrong because the patient's presentation is the clinical opposite of postpartum psychosis. Overreacting disrupts treatment and confirms the patient's fear that disclosure leads to punishment.
- *(Decision Point 1)* Dismissing the intrusive thoughts as "normal new-mom worries" and failing to document them or incorporate them into the clinical assessment. Minimizing the symptoms delays appropriate care.
- *(Decision Point 2)* Responding to the patient's fear of disclosure by providing premature or blanket reassurance without completing a thorough safety assessment. Therapeutic validation must follow, not replace, a structured safety assessment. Skipping the assessment makes the reassurance clinically unfounded.
- *(Decision Point 2)* Breaching the patient's confidentiality by sharing detailed clinical information with the mother without the patient's explicit consent.
- *(Decision Point 3)* Attributing the expanding intrusive thoughts and compulsive checking at Stage 2 to a side effect of sertraline, leading to a recommendation to discontinue the medication. The obsessive-compulsive features are symptoms of the underlying illness, not adverse effects of the SSRI.
- *(Decision Point 4)* Telling the patient independently that her dose should be increased, or conversely, telling her to "just give it more time" at the current dose without communicating the clinical picture to the prescribing provider.
- *(Decision Point 5)* Advising the patient to discontinue sertraline now that she is feeling better, or suggesting she stop breastfeeding because of the medication.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Differentiate ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts in postpartum depression from the ego-syntonic or delusional ideation of postpartum psychosis using specific clinical cues following a structured safety assessment.
- Apply therapeutic communication principles to address a postpartum patient's fear that disclosing intrusive thoughts will result in loss of custody, while maintaining a structured safety assessment.
- Analyze an inadequate early treatment response in postpartum depression — distinguishing undertreated symptoms from medication side effects — and advocate for timely provider communication without overpromising results.
- Practice within RN scope when a patient asks about medication dose changes, balancing patient advocacy with appropriate referral to the prescribing provider.
- Evaluate a positive treatment trajectory using validated screening tools, functional status, and bonding behaviors, and provide evidence-based anticipatory guidance about treatment duration, breastfeeding safety, and recurrence risk.
