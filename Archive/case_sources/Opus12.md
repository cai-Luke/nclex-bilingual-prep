**CASE TITLE**
Acute inpatient suicide risk identified during a medical-surgical admission for diabetic foot infection.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old man is admitted to a medical-surgical unit for IV antibiotic therapy and surgical consultation for a non-healing diabetic foot ulcer with cellulitis. His history includes type 2 diabetes mellitus (diagnosed 14 years ago, complicated by peripheral neuropathy and retinopathy), hypertension, major depressive disorder with two prior episodes (the most recent three years ago, treated with sertraline, which he discontinued on his own eight months ago), and a remote history of one suicide attempt by medication overdose at age 40, which required ICU admission. He lives alone; his wife died of pancreatic cancer 14 months ago. He is retired from a manufacturing job and has a 22-year-old daughter who lives in another state. Current inpatient orders include vancomycin 1.25 g IV every 12 hours, piperacillin-tazobactam 3.375 g IV every 8 hours, sliding-scale insulin, metformin 1000 mg PO twice daily, lisinopril 20 mg PO daily, a wound care consult, a surgical consult, and a standard fall-risk protocol. No psychiatric consult has been placed. The admission nursing assessment used the facility's standard depression screening (PHQ-2), which was scored 4 (positive on both items), but no further assessment was initiated at that time.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient is on hospital day 2. The day-shift nurse enters the room for morning assessment and medication administration. The patient is sitting on the edge of the bed, staring at the floor. He has not eaten breakfast. When greeted, he responds flatly, "What's the point." He does not make eye contact. His call light has not been used overnight, and the night-shift nurse documented that he slept poorly, declining a sleep aid, and was found awake and tearful at 0300. The bedside table holds an unopened get-well card and a framed photograph of a woman, face down.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline vitals: T 37.6 °C, HR 82, BP 138/86, RR 16, SpO₂ 96% on room air. He is alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. Speech is slow and monotone. Affect is flat. He avoids eye contact and answers questions with one- or two-word responses. He appears fatigued, with dark circles under his eyes. Left foot is wrapped; dressing is clean, dry, and intact with no strike-through. Pedal pulses are palpable but diminished on the left. Capillary refill left toes > 3 seconds. No acute cardiopulmonary distress. Abdomen soft, non-tender. He has a peripheral IV in the right forearm, site without erythema or swelling. The room contains standard medical-surgical equipment: an IV pole, a pulse oximetry cable, a blood pressure cuff, a rolling overbed table, and a plastic water pitcher. The bathroom door closes fully and has a standard lever handle. The patient's personal belongings include a leather belt, a phone charger cord, and a razor in a toiletry bag on the windowsill.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn on admission, hospital day 1): WBC 14,200/µL, Hgb 11.8 g/dL, platelets 210,000/µL, BUN 28 mg/dL, creatinine 1.3 mg/dL, eGFR 56 mL/min/1.73 m², sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.4 mEq/L, glucose 238 mg/dL, HbA1c 9.4%, albumin 2.9 g/dL, vancomycin trough pending (to be drawn before fourth dose), ESR 62 mm/hr, CRP 8.4 mg/dL, wound culture pending.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Hospital day 2, 0815. The nurse sits down at eye level and uses open-ended, direct language: "Sometimes when people are going through a lot, they think about ending their life. Are you having thoughts like that?" The patient is silent for several seconds, then says quietly, "I think about it. I think about it a lot. Since Marie died, I don't see why I'm still here. Now they're talking about maybe taking my foot. What's left?" When asked whether he has thought about how he would do it, he says, "I've got pills at home. I saved up. I figured when I got out of here…" He denies having any means in the hospital but adds, "There's always a way if you really want to." He denies a specific timeline but says, "I just don't want to wake up tomorrow." He rates his emotional pain as 9 out of 10. He says he has not spoken to his daughter in three weeks and states, "She's better off without me dragging her down." The nurse completes the facility's full suicide risk assessment tool (the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale, C-SSRS) per policy. The patient endorses active suicidal ideation with a method identified (stockpiled pills at home), intent without a specific date, and a history of a prior attempt. He denies current access to means in the hospital. C-SSRS screening indicates high risk. The nurse immediately notifies the charge nurse and the attending provider.

**Stage 2** — Hospital day 2, 0900–1030. The provider places a psychiatric consult and orders one-to-one (1:1) continuous observation. The nurse initiates the facility's suicide precautions protocol: the patient is placed on 1:1 continuous observation with a designated sitter; the room is swept for ligature risks and sharps — the belt, phone charger cord, razor, and plastic bag from personal belongings are removed with the patient's knowledge and placed in a locked area at the nurses' station; the bathroom door is adjusted so it cannot be locked from inside; the IV tubing and pulse oximetry cable are shortened and secured to minimize ligature length; the overbed table and IV pole are assessed and left in place as they are not readily convertible to weapons; the call light cord is replaced with a short-cord version per unit protocol. The patient becomes agitated during the room sweep, raising his voice: "You're treating me like a prisoner. I shouldn't have said anything." He stands abruptly, pulls at his IV, and moves toward the door. The sitter is present. Vitals at this time: HR 104, BP 152/94, RR 20, SpO₂ 96%. The nurse must de-escalate while maintaining safety.

**Stage 3** — Hospital day 2, 1400. The psychiatric consultation has been completed. The psychiatrist documents high acute suicide risk, recommends restarting an SSRI (sertraline 50 mg PO daily, with titration plan), continuation of 1:1 observation, daily psychiatric reassessment, and a social work consult for discharge safety planning including means restriction counseling (addressing the stockpiled pills at home). The patient is calmer now. He is tearful but cooperative. He tells the nurse, "I'm sorry I yelled. I'm just tired of everything." He asks whether his daughter has been called. The nurse checks the chart: no emergency contact has been notified, and the patient has not given permission to contact his daughter. He now says, "Maybe you should call her. I don't know what to say to her." Vitals: HR 78, BP 134/82, RR 16, SpO₂ 97%. He ate half of his lunch. The 1:1 sitter reports no further agitation or attempts to leave. The wound care consult note recommends continued IV antibiotics and a vascular surgery evaluation before any amputation decision. The vancomycin trough drawn this morning returned at 14.8 µg/mL (facility target 15–20 µg/mL for serious infection). The social worker arrives and begins discharge safety planning, asking the patient about firearms in the home (he denies any), the location of the stockpiled pills, and whether a trusted person can remove them before discharge. The patient identifies his daughter as the only person he would trust to do this.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the opening of Stage 1, the nurse observes behavioral cues — flat affect, psychomotor retardation, poor oral intake, the statement "What's the point," the overnight tearfulness, and the positive PHQ-2 from admission that was not followed up. The nurse must decide what to do next. *Correct action:* Ask the patient directly and explicitly about suicidal ideation using clear, non-euphemistic language, then complete the facility's full suicide risk assessment (C-SSRS). *Why it is correct:* A positive PHQ-2 triggers further assessment per evidence-based screening protocols. Behavioral warning signs (withdrawal, hopelessness statements, sleep disturbance, anhedonia) in a patient with known major depressive disorder, a prior suicide attempt, and recent major loss constitute converging cues that demand direct inquiry. Asking directly about suicide does not increase risk and is the standard of care; indirect or vague questioning may miss active ideation. The C-SSRS stratifies risk by distinguishing wish to die, ideation, ideation with method, ideation with intent, and ideation with plan and intent, which determines the level of intervention. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* After the C-SSRS is completed in Stage 1 and the patient is classified as high risk (active ideation, identified method, intent, prior attempt), the nurse must determine the priority actions and their correct sequence. *Correct action:* The nurse must not leave the patient alone from this moment forward — remain with the patient (or ensure another qualified staff member does) while simultaneously notifying the charge nurse and the attending provider and requesting a psychiatric consult and 1:1 continuous observation. Notification must be immediate, not deferred to the end of the medication pass or shift report. *Why it is correct:* A patient with active suicidal ideation, a method, intent, and a history of a prior attempt is at high acute risk. Continuous observation is the immediate safety intervention; it takes priority over completing other nursing tasks. The nurse cannot independently order a psychiatric consult or formal 1:1 observation — these require provider orders — so notification is the mechanism to obtain them. Leaving the patient alone, even briefly, to find the charge nurse or make a phone call creates an unmonitored interval during which the patient could act on suicidal intent using available means in the room. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse is conducting the environmental safety sweep. The room contains a belt, a phone charger cord, a razor, a plastic bag, IV tubing, a pulse oximetry cable, a bathroom door that locks, and a call light cord. The nurse must determine which items or features to address and how. *Correct action:* Remove the belt, phone charger cord, razor, and plastic bag (potential ligature and sharps risks). Disable the bathroom door lock. Replace the standard call light cord with a short-cord or breakaway version per unit protocol. Secure and shorten IV tubing and monitoring cables to reduce ligature potential. Assess remaining items (IV pole, overbed table) for risk but leave them if they are not convertible to means of self-harm in this context. *Why it is correct:* Ligature points and sharps are the primary environmental risks on a medical-surgical unit. Flexible cords and belts are the most common means of inpatient suicide by hanging; removing or shortening them is the single most effective environmental intervention. The bathroom is a high-risk location because it is the only space where a patient can be unobserved if the door locks. Medical equipment that is necessary for care (IV, monitoring) cannot simply be removed, so the intervention is to minimize the usable length. The sweep is systematic, not selective — every item is evaluated against its ligature, sharp, or weapon potential. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* During the environmental sweep in Stage 2, the patient becomes agitated, says "You're treating me like a prisoner. I shouldn't have said anything," pulls at his IV, and moves toward the door. The nurse must respond. *Correct action:* Use verbal de-escalation: acknowledge the patient's feelings without being dismissive ("I hear you — this feels intrusive, and I understand that"), validate that speaking up took courage, explain the purpose of the precautions in simple, non-punitive language ("These steps are to keep you safe while we get you more support"), maintain a calm and non-confrontational tone and posture, position oneself between the patient and the door without physically blocking or restraining, and direct the sitter to remain close. Do not use physical restraint, do not threaten consequences, and do not minimize the patient's distress. If the patient attempts to leave, inform him calmly that the team is concerned for his safety and that leaving against medical advice in the context of acute suicidal ideation may require the provider to evaluate for an involuntary hold, but do not physically prevent him from leaving unless there is an active order for involuntary detention under the applicable state statute. *Why it is correct:* Agitation in a suicidal patient who feels autonomy is being removed is a predictable response and a moment of heightened risk. Confrontational or coercive responses escalate agitation and damage therapeutic alliance, which is the primary protective factor the nurse can influence. De-escalation preserves the relationship while maintaining safety. Physical restraint is a last resort and requires a provider order; it is not indicated here because the patient is agitated but not violent. The nurse's legal and ethical obligation is to maintain safety, which at this moment means keeping the patient in a monitored environment through therapeutic engagement, not force. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action, evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient asks the nurse to call his daughter. He has not previously given permission to contact her, and she is listed as his emergency contact in the chart but has not been notified. The nurse must decide how to handle this request in the context of patient privacy, therapeutic communication, and discharge safety planning. *Correct action:* Obtain the patient's explicit verbal consent (documented in the chart) to contact his daughter, clarify with the patient what information he wants shared and what he does not, and facilitate the call — either by helping the patient call her himself if he is willing, or by making the call with the patient present and sharing only the information the patient has authorized. Coordinate with the social worker, who needs to discuss means restriction (removal of the stockpiled pills) with a trusted person before discharge, and the patient has identified his daughter as that person. Do not contact the daughter without the patient's permission, even though she is listed as an emergency contact, unless the treatment team determines that an imminent-danger exception to confidentiality applies and documents that determination. *Why it is correct:* HIPAA permits disclosure to an emergency contact when the patient is incapacitated or in imminent danger and the disclosure is in the patient's best interest, but the patient here is alert, competent, and now actively consenting — so the standard consent pathway applies and is preferable because it preserves autonomy and trust. The therapeutic value of reconnecting the patient with his daughter is significant: social connectedness is a modifiable protective factor against suicide. The means restriction conversation (removing the stockpiled pills from the home) is a critical component of discharge safety planning and requires a trusted person's involvement; the patient's willingness to involve his daughter is a positive prognostic sign and should be supported. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse reviews the overall clinical picture — the psychiatric plan (SSRI restarted, daily reassessment, 1:1 continued), the medical plan (IV antibiotics continuing, vascular surgery evaluation pending, vancomycin trough slightly below target), and the emerging discharge safety plan. The nurse must evaluate which findings indicate the patient's acute risk is stabilizing and which indicate ongoing or new concerns. *Correct action:* Signs of stabilization include the patient's willingness to engage (apologizing, eating, asking for his daughter to be called), cooperation with the sitter, hemodynamic return to near-baseline, and the initiation of psychiatric treatment. Ongoing concerns include the continued presence of suicidal ideation (he has not retracted it), the unresolved means at home (stockpiled pills not yet removed), the pending surgical decision (possible amputation, which could be a new stressor and risk amplifier), and the subtherapeutic vancomycin trough, which requires dose adjustment and continued monitoring of the infection that is driving the hospitalization. The nurse should not interpret the patient's calmer demeanor as resolution of suicide risk — risk reassessment is ongoing and must be repeated at defined intervals per policy and with any change in clinical status, including after the surgical consultation. *Why it is correct:* A calm period after an acute suicidal crisis does not reliably indicate reduced risk; in some cases, a patient who has decided to act becomes calmer because the decision itself reduces distress. Ongoing structured reassessment (repeat C-SSRS at defined intervals) is the standard of care. The pending amputation discussion is a foreseeable acute stressor that could re-escalate risk, and the nurse should communicate this concern to the psychiatric team proactively. The subtherapeutic vancomycin trough is a concurrent medical issue that must not be lost in the focus on psychiatric safety — the patient's foot infection is the reason for hospitalization, and undertreated infection could lead to clinical deterioration that compounds despair. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes, recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Using indirect or euphemistic language ("Are you feeling down?" or "You're not thinking of doing anything silly, are you?") instead of asking directly about suicidal thoughts. This approach often fails to elicit disclosure because the patient may not recognize vague language as an invitation to discuss suicidal ideation, or may take the cue that the nurse is uncomfortable and withhold. Direct, compassionate inquiry is the evidence-based standard.

- *(Decision point 1)* Attributing the patient's flat affect and withdrawal entirely to pain, poor sleep, or adjustment to hospitalization and not pursuing a suicide risk assessment. The converging cues — prior attempt, recent bereavement, discontinued antidepressant, positive PHQ-2, hopelessness statement — require direct follow-up regardless of whether a medical explanation for low mood also exists.

- *(Decision point 2)* Leaving the patient alone to go find the charge nurse or make a phone call after the patient discloses active suicidal ideation. This creates an unmonitored interval during a period of high acute risk. The correct action is to stay with the patient and use the call light, a nearby phone, or another staff member to relay the notification.

- *(Decision point 2)* Deciding that because the patient says he does not have means in the hospital, the risk is low enough to defer notification until after completing the medication pass. The patient's statement "There's always a way if you really want to" directly contradicts the assumption that absence of brought-in means equals safety. The environmental hazards identified in the room (belt, cords, razor) confirm that means are available.

- *(Decision point 3)* Removing only the most obvious items (the razor) and overlooking the belt, phone charger cord, and call light cord as ligature risks. Flexible cords and straps are the most common means of inpatient suicide by hanging; a systematic sweep must evaluate every item, not only those that look like weapons.

- *(Decision point 3)* Removing the IV and monitoring equipment entirely to eliminate ligature risk, thereby interrupting medically necessary antibiotic therapy. The correct intervention is to shorten and secure the tubing, not to discontinue treatment.

- *(Decision point 4)* Responding to the patient's agitation by physically blocking the doorway, threatening to place him in restraints, or telling him "You have to stay — it's for your own good" in a dismissive tone. These responses escalate agitation, damage trust, and may constitute false imprisonment if no legal authority for involuntary detention has been established. De-escalation through validation and calm explanation is the appropriate first response.

- *(Decision point 4)* Interpreting the patient's regret about disclosing ("I shouldn't have said anything") as evidence that he was not truly suicidal and downgrading the precautions. Retraction of disclosure under distress is a recognized pattern and does not reduce risk; it may increase it, because the patient may now be less likely to communicate future intent.

- *(Decision point 5)* Calling the daughter without the patient's permission on the grounds that she is listed as the emergency contact. Emergency contact designation does not automatically authorize disclosure of psychiatric information. The patient is alert and competent and is now willing to consent, so the consent pathway is both legally correct and therapeutically superior.

- *(Decision point 6)* Interpreting the patient's calmer demeanor, apology, and willingness to eat as evidence that the suicidal crisis has resolved, and recommending discontinuation of 1:1 observation. A single period of calm does not constitute resolution; structured reassessment at defined intervals is required, and the pending amputation discussion represents a foreseeable stressor that could re-escalate risk.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Recognize converging behavioral and historical cues that indicate a medical-surgical patient requires direct, explicit suicide risk assessment.
- Prioritize immediate patient safety (continuous observation, not leaving the patient alone) over routine nursing tasks when active suicidal ideation with intent is disclosed.
- Conduct a systematic environmental safety sweep on a medical-surgical unit, distinguishing removable hazards from medical equipment that must be modified in place.
- Apply verbal de-escalation techniques to maintain therapeutic alliance and safety when a suicidal patient becomes agitated during suicide precautions.
- Evaluate the adequacy of a discharge safety plan by integrating psychiatric, medical, and psychosocial factors, including means restriction and social support.
