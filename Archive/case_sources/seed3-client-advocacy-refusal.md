**CASE TITLE**
Client advocacy for a capacitated older adult refusing PEG tube placement against family pressure.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Margaret Ellison is an 82-year-old woman admitted to a medical-surgical unit from home with an exacerbation of New York Heart Association Class IV heart failure. Her history includes ischemic cardiomyopathy with an ejection fraction of 15% on last echocardiogram, chronic kidney disease stage 4 (baseline creatinine 2.8 mg/dL), type 2 diabetes managed with basal insulin, and progressive dysphagia evaluated by speech-language pathology over the past six months. She has had three hospitalizations in the past four months for fluid overload and was recently referred to palliative care as an outpatient, though the family canceled that appointment. She lives alone in a senior apartment with a home health aide four hours daily. Her two adult children — a son who lives locally and a daughter who lives out of state — are listed as emergency contacts. She has a completed advance directive on file naming her son as durable power of attorney for healthcare, to be activated only if she loses decisional capacity. Current orders include IV furosemide 40 mg every 12 hours, fluid restriction of 1,200 mL/day, a low-sodium cardiac diet with nectar-thick liquids per speech-language pathology recommendation, daily weights, and telemetry monitoring.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
Mrs. Ellison was brought to the emergency department by her son after he found her at home with worsening shortness of breath, bilateral lower extremity edema, and poor oral intake over the past five days. She reports she has been eating very little because swallowing has become effortful and uncomfortable, and she is "tired of fighting this." She is alert, conversational, and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. She tells the admitting nurse clearly: "I don't want any more tubes or machines. I've had a good life. I want to be comfortable."

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 92 and irregular, blood pressure 104/62, respiratory rate 22, SpO₂ 93% on 2 L nasal cannula. Weight 68.4 kg (up 4.2 kg from her documented dry weight of 64.2 kg). Bilateral crackles in the lower lung fields. Jugular venous distension visible at 45 degrees. Bilateral 3+ pitting edema to mid-shin. Skin warm and dry, no breakdown, Braden score 16. Abdomen soft, non-tender, with no organomegaly. She is thin with temporal wasting. Oral mucosa is moist. She manages small sips of nectar-thick water without overt coughing but takes prolonged time to initiate each swallow. Neurological exam is grossly intact: she follows commands, recalls her medication names, describes her medical history accurately, and explains her reasoning for declining further aggressive treatment in her own words. Mini-Cog score 4 out of 5 (normal clock draw, recalled 2 of 3 words). Mood is calm and reflective; she denies feeling depressed or hopeless, stating she feels "at peace."

**LABORATORY DATA**
BNP 2,840 pg/mL. Sodium 131 mEq/L. Potassium 5.1 mEq/L. BUN 48 mg/dL. Creatinine 3.2 mg/dL (baseline 2.8). eGFR 14 mL/min/1.73 m². Glucose 142 mg/dL. Albumin 2.6 g/dL. Hemoglobin 9.8 g/dL. WBC 6,200/µL. Platelets 188,000/µL. INR 1.1. Prealbumin 11 mg/dL.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Hospital day 1, afternoon. The attending physician discusses Mrs. Ellison's progressive dysphagia and poor nutritional status with the family. The physician recommends percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG) tube placement to maintain nutrition and hydration, noting her poor intake, progressive dysphagia, temporal wasting, and uncertainty regarding goals of care (her low albumin of 2.6 and prealbumin of 11 are also noted as context). Mrs. Ellison listens to the full explanation, asks two clarifying questions ("Will the tube cure my heart?" and "Will I still be able to eat anything by mouth?"), and then declines: "I understand what you're telling me. I don't want a feeding tube. I want to eat what I can and be comfortable." The physician documents: "Patient demonstrates understanding of risks and benefits of PEG placement, including that PEG does not necessarily eliminate aspiration risk, and continued weight loss without it. She articulates her values and reasoning clearly. Decision-making capacity intact. Patient declines PEG at this time." The son, David, approaches the nurse at the station afterward and says, "She doesn't know what she's saying. She's old and she's giving up. You need to talk some sense into her. I'm her power of attorney — I want the tube placed." The daughter, Karen, calls the unit and tells the nurse: "My brother and I agree. We want everything done. She's just scared. Can't you convince her?" The nurse reviews the chart and confirms the advance directive specifies the son's DPOAHC authority activates only upon a determination that Mrs. Ellison lacks decisional capacity.

Vitals at this time: HR 88, BP 108/64, RR 20, SpO₂ 94% on 2 L. Weight 67.8 kg (down 0.6 kg from admission). She is resting comfortably, tolerating small amounts of pureed food and nectar-thick liquids. Telemetry shows atrial fibrillation with controlled ventricular rate.

**Stage 2** — Hospital day 2, morning. David has spoken with the attending physician again, expressing frustration that "nobody is listening to the family." The physician tells the nurse privately: "The family is really upset. Maybe we should just schedule the PEG consult and see if she changes her mind once GI comes by. It might calm things down." The nurse re-assesses Mrs. Ellison, who is alert and oriented, calm, and reiterates her wishes without prompting: "I told the doctor yesterday. No tube. I want to go home and be comfortable. I've thought about this for months — this isn't a snap decision." She adds: "My children mean well, but this is my body and my life." The nurse asks if she has felt pressured or coerced, and she says, "David keeps coming in and telling me I'm being selfish. It upsets me, but it doesn't change my mind." She asks the nurse: "Will you make sure they respect what I want?"

Vitals: HR 90, BP 100/60, RR 20, SpO₂ 94% on 2 L. Weight 67.2 kg (continued diuresis). She ate approximately 30% of her pureed breakfast. BNP redrawn: 2,410 pg/mL. Creatinine 3.4 mg/dL. Potassium 5.3 mEq/L. Sodium 130 mEq/L.

**Stage 3** — Hospital day 2, late afternoon. The nurse has initiated several actions. A palliative care consult has been placed. The charge nurse has been informed of the ethical concern. The nurse has documented Mrs. Ellison's repeated, consistent refusal and her demonstration of capacity in the nursing notes, including direct quotes. A family meeting has been arranged for the following morning with the attending physician, the palliative care team, the social worker, and both adult children (Karen will attend by phone). David arrives on the unit and tells the nurse he has contacted a lawyer and "will get a court order" to have the PEG placed. Mrs. Ellison is resting. She tells the nurse she is glad someone is listening to her and asks about hospice options at home.

Vitals: HR 86, BP 102/58, RR 18, SpO₂ 95% on 2 L. Weight 66.8 kg. She tolerated small amounts of pureed lunch. No new symptoms. Telemetry unchanged. The palliative care nurse practitioner has completed an initial assessment and documents that Mrs. Ellison "demonstrates intact decisional capacity, consistent values-based reasoning, and an informed, voluntary refusal of PEG tube placement. Recommend goals-of-care meeting with family, comfort-focused plan of care, and hospice referral per patient's expressed wishes."

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, after the physician's discussion and Mrs. Ellison's refusal, the nurse reviews the chart. Among the available documentation — the physician's capacity assessment note, the advance directive naming the son as DPOAHC with activation only upon loss of capacity, Mrs. Ellison's stated wishes and clarifying questions, the Mini-Cog score, the son's claim of POA authority, the albumin and prealbumin values, and the speech-language pathology dysphagia evaluation — the nurse must identify which findings establish that Mrs. Ellison's refusal is the governing decision. *Correct:* The Mini-Cog score supports that there is no obvious cognitive-screening red flag, but it does not by itself establish decision-making capacity. The governing cues are the documented decision-specific capacity assessment, Mrs. Ellison's demonstrated understanding and reasoning, her consistent voluntary refusal, and the advance directive clause stating that surrogate authority activates only if she loses capacity. The albumin, prealbumin, and dysphagia evaluation are clinically relevant context for the PEG recommendation but do not bear on who holds decision-making authority. The son's claim of POA authority is a cue to recognize — specifically, to recognize that it does not apply here because the activation condition has not been met. *Why:* A capacitated adult's autonomous decision governs her own care. Surrogate decision-making authority under a DPOAHC is dormant while the principal retains capacity. Recognizing the specific documentation that establishes capacity — and distinguishing it from clinical data that supports the medical recommendation but not the authority question — is the foundational cue-recognition step. *Skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the son insists his DPOAHC status gives him authority to override his mother's refusal and order the PEG placed. The nurse must determine whose decision governs. *Correct:* Mrs. Ellison's decision governs. Her decisional capacity is documented and intact; therefore, she — not her surrogate — holds the legal and ethical authority to accept or refuse treatment. The DPOAHC grants the son authority to make decisions only if and when Mrs. Ellison is determined to lack capacity, which has not occurred. His emotional distress and disagreement with her choice do not constitute grounds to activate surrogate authority. *Why:* The ethical principle of autonomy holds that a competent adult has the right to refuse any treatment, including life-sustaining treatment, even when others disagree. Surrogate decision-making is a substitute mechanism that exists to protect patients who cannot decide for themselves; it does not function as a veto over a capacitated patient's own choices. The son's role as DPOAHC is legally meaningful but currently inactive. Analyzing these cues correctly prevents the nurse from deferring to family pressure under a misunderstanding of surrogate authority. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the physician suggests scheduling the PEG consult anyway to "calm things down," and the son is escalating pressure. The nurse must determine the priority ethical obligation. *Correct:* The priority is advocating for Mrs. Ellison's autonomous, informed, and voluntary refusal. This takes precedence over managing family distress, maintaining collegial harmony with the physician, or pursuing the nutritional intervention. *Why:* Client advocacy — amplifying and protecting the patient's own voice when it is at risk of being overridden — is the nurse's primary ethical obligation in this situation. Scheduling a PEG consult after the patient has clearly refused, for the purpose of appeasing the family or pressuring her to reconsider, raises an ethical concern. If Mrs. Ellison later requests more information or consents to an informational consult, that would be different. The family's distress is real and deserves compassionate attention, but it does not outweigh the patient's right to self-determination. Prioritizing advocacy here means the nurse does not passively comply with the physician's suggestion or defer to the family's emotional urgency. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must determine the full set of appropriate nursing actions to uphold Mrs. Ellison's wishes and address the family conflict. *Correct:* The appropriate actions include: (a) documenting Mrs. Ellison's repeated, consistent refusal with direct quotes and the nurse's own assessment of her capacity presentation (alert, oriented, calm, articulating values-based reasoning without coercion); (b) communicating directly with the physician that the patient has again declined and that scheduling a PEG consult over her refusal raises an ethical concern; (c) request or initiate a palliative care consult according to facility policy to support goals-of-care clarification and comfort planning; (d) notify the charge nurse/supervisor and facilitate a structured family meeting with the interdisciplinary team (with Mrs. Ellison's consent and participation if she wants and can tolerate it) to address the family's distress, clarify the legal status of the DPOAHC, and support the family in understanding Mrs. Ellison's rights; (e) escalate to ethics consultation and, if legal threats continue, risk management per facility policy; (f) confirming with Mrs. Ellison that her decision remains informed and voluntary and that she has not been coerced. Actions that are not appropriate include: attempting to persuade Mrs. Ellison to accept the PEG, deferring to the son's demand because he holds the DPOAHC document, scheduling the PEG consult without the patient's consent, or avoiding the conflict by not addressing the physician's suggestion. *Why:* Advocacy is not a single gesture but a coordinated set of actions — documentation, communication, interdisciplinary collaboration, and ongoing reassessment of voluntariness. Each action reinforces the patient's autonomy from a different angle: documentation creates the legal record, physician communication prevents an unauthorized consult, palliative care and social work bring expertise in goals-of-care conversations and family support, and the ethics service provides institutional backing when advocacy meets resistance. Coercing or repeatedly pressuring the patient violates autonomy. Deferring to the family violates the legal and ethical framework. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, after re-assessing Mrs. Ellison and confirming that she remains alert, oriented, informed, voluntary, and consistent in refusing PEG placement, the nurse must choose the first advocacy action in response to the physician's suggestion to schedule the PEG consult anyway. *Correct:* The nurse's first action is to communicate directly with the physician — respectfully, clearly, and without delay — that Mrs. Ellison has again declined the PEG, that her capacity is intact, and that scheduling a consult over her documented refusal raises an ethical concern that the nurse cannot support. The nurse should use structured communication (e.g., SBAR or CUS framework per facility practice): state the situation (patient has consistently refused), the background (capacity documented, DPOAHC not activated), the assessment (scheduling the consult without patient consent undermines autonomy), and the recommendation (hold the consult, proceed with palliative care referral and family meeting). *Why:* This is the immediate, direct advocacy action that addresses the proximate threat to the patient's autonomy — the physician's plan to schedule an intervention the patient has refused. It must come before the family meeting, before the ethics consult, and before further documentation, because if the PEG consult is placed now, the patient will be subjected to exactly the pressure she has asked to be protected from. The nurse is not diagnosing or prescribing; the nurse is exercising the professional obligation to question an action that conflicts with the patient's documented wishes. The other actions (palliative care consult, family meeting, ethics referral) are all appropriate and necessary but are not the first action because they take longer to arrange and do not address the immediate risk. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the palliative care team has assessed Mrs. Ellison, the family meeting is scheduled, and the nurse must evaluate whether the advocacy interventions have been effective. *Correct:* The outcomes that confirm effective advocacy are: Mrs. Ellison's wishes are documented consistently across nursing notes and the palliative care assessment; the PEG consult has not been placed over her refusal; a comfort-focused plan of care is being developed in alignment with her stated goals; she reports feeling heard and is asking about hospice options (indicating she perceives her autonomy as intact); and an interdisciplinary family meeting is arranged to address the family's concerns through appropriate channels rather than through pressure on the patient. The son's threat of legal action does not indicate advocacy failure — it indicates ongoing family distress that the meeting and social work support will address. *Why:* Effective advocacy is evaluated by whether the patient's autonomous decision is being honored in the plan of care, whether the patient perceives that her voice is being respected, and whether the systems are in place to sustain that protection (documentation, interdisciplinary involvement, ethics availability). The goal is not to eliminate family disagreement — that may persist — but to ensure that disagreement does not override the patient's rights. Mrs. Ellison's calm demeanor, her expressed gratitude, and her proactive inquiry about hospice are positive indicators that she feels safe and supported in her decision. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Focusing on the albumin of 2.6 and prealbumin of 11 as the most important findings — these support the medical rationale for PEG placement but do not address the authority question. A nurse who fixates on the nutritional data may conclude that the PEG is medically necessary and therefore should proceed, conflating medical indication with patient consent. The nutritional values are real and concerning, but they do not override a capacitated patient's refusal.

- *(Decision point 2)* Accepting the son's claim that his DPOAHC status gives him current authority to make decisions, reasoning that "he's the POA, so he decides." This is a common and dangerous misunderstanding. The DPOAHC is a contingent authority that activates only when the principal lacks capacity. Mrs. Ellison has documented capacity; therefore, the son has no decision-making authority at this time, regardless of his emotional conviction or the document he holds.

- *(Decision point 2)* Questioning Mrs. Ellison's capacity because she is 82, has advanced illness, and is making a decision the family disagrees with. Age, serious illness, and an unpopular choice are not evidence of incapacity. Her capacity has been formally assessed and documented. Conflating disagreement with incapacity is a form of ageism and paternalism that directly harms the patient.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing family satisfaction or conflict avoidance over patient autonomy — for example, reasoning that "keeping the family calm" is the most important goal, or that the nurse should "stay neutral" and let the physician and family work it out. Neutrality in the face of a threat to a patient's autonomous refusal is not neutral — it is a failure to advocate. The nurse's obligation is to the patient first.

- *(Decision point 4)* Attempting to persuade Mrs. Ellison to reconsider the PEG "for her family's sake" or "just to talk to the GI doctor." This undermines autonomy and fails to protect the patient's expressed wishes. The patient has made an informed, voluntary, and repeated decision. Re-presenting the option she has already declined, at the family's request, violates her autonomy and places the nurse in the role of agent for the family rather than advocate for the patient.

- *(Decision point 5)* Deferring the conversation with the physician and instead focusing on documenting the refusal or calling social work first. Documentation and social work involvement are both appropriate actions, but they do not stop the immediate threat — the physician's plan to schedule the PEG consult. If the nurse documents beautifully but does not speak up before the consult is placed, the patient's autonomy has already been violated. The direct communication must come first.

- *(Decision point 5)* Contacting the ethics committee as the first action instead of speaking directly with the physician. Ethics consultation is appropriate if the conflict persists after direct communication and the family meeting, but it is not the first step. The nurse has a professional obligation to address the concern directly with the provider before escalating to an institutional committee. Using the ethics committee as a first resort bypasses the collaborative relationship and delays the immediate advocacy.

- *(Decision point 6)* Interpreting the son's threat of legal action as evidence that the nurse's advocacy has failed or that the approach should change. The son's distress is understandable, but a legal threat from a family member does not alter a capacitated patient's right to refuse treatment. The appropriate response is to continue supporting Mrs. Ellison's decision, ensure thorough documentation, involve social work and the ethics service as needed, and allow the family meeting to proceed. Changing course because of a legal threat places family preference above the capacitated client's decision.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Identify the specific documentation and clinical findings that establish a patient's decisional capacity and distinguish them from findings that support a medical recommendation but do not bear on decision-making authority.
- Apply the principle of autonomy to determine that a capacitated patient's refusal governs care, even when family members holding durable power of attorney disagree.
- Prioritize client advocacy as the nurse's primary ethical obligation when a patient's autonomous decision is at risk of being overridden by family pressure or provider ambivalence.
- Generate a coordinated set of advocacy actions — direct communication with the provider, documentation, interdisciplinary referrals, and family meeting facilitation — and distinguish them from inappropriate actions such as coercion, deference to surrogate authority, or conflict avoidance.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of advocacy interventions by assessing whether the patient's wishes are reflected in the plan of care, the patient perceives her autonomy as intact, and institutional supports are in place to sustain the protection.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after Mrs. Ellison has consistently and repeatedly refused PEG placement with documented intact capacity, the physician's suggestion to schedule the consult anyway has been addressed, palliative care has assessed the patient and confirmed her capacity and wishes, and the interdisciplinary family meeting is arranged.

*Most likely condition:* A capacitated client's autonomous refusal of a recommended intervention (PEG tube) being challenged by family members who hold dormant surrogate authority. The ethical and legal framework is clear: Mrs. Ellison's decision governs.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) A surrogate decision-making situation in which the DPOAHC holder has active authority — wrong here because Mrs. Ellison has documented decisional capacity; the DPOAHC activation clause has not been triggered, and no provider has determined she lacks capacity. The son holds a valid document, but it is not operative. (2) A patient with impaired capacity making an uninformed refusal that requires further evaluation before it can be honored — wrong here because her capacity has been assessed by the attending physician and confirmed by the palliative care team; she asked clarifying questions, articulated her values, and her refusal has been consistent across multiple conversations over two days. Her Mini-Cog is normal. There is no clinical basis to question her capacity.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Communicate directly with the physician to ensure the PEG consult is not scheduled over the patient's documented refusal, using structured communication to frame the ethical concern clearly. (2) Facilitate the interdisciplinary family meeting with palliative care, social work, and the attending physician (with Mrs. Ellison's consent and participation if she wants and can tolerate it) to address the family's distress through appropriate channels, clarify the legal status of the DPOAHC, and support development of a comfort-focused plan aligned with Mrs. Ellison's goals.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Scheduling or allowing the PEG consult to proceed without the patient's consent or as a pressure tactic — this violates Mrs. Ellison's autonomy by subjecting her to an evaluation for a procedure she has clearly and repeatedly refused; the consult itself becomes a form of coercive pressure. (2) Deferring to the son's demand because he holds the DPOAHC document, reasoning that the nurse should "let the family and the doctor sort it out" — this abandons the advocacy obligation; the nurse who steps back when a capacitated patient's refusal is being challenged fails to protect the patient's expressed wishes.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Consistency of the plan of care with Mrs. Ellison's stated wishes — the care plan, physician orders, and interdisciplinary documentation should reflect a comfort-focused approach with no PEG consult placed over her refusal; any deviation signals that advocacy has not been sustained. (2) Mrs. Ellison's own perception of whether her wishes are being respected — her statements, her emotional state, and her willingness to engage in planning (such as her inquiry about hospice) indicate whether she feels her autonomy is intact or whether she is experiencing ongoing coercion.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) The albumin and prealbumin trends — while clinically relevant to her nutritional status and prognosis, they do not evaluate whether the advocacy intervention has succeeded; a declining albumin does not change the ethical analysis or indicate that the nurse should revisit the PEG discussion against the patient's wishes. (2) The family's satisfaction level — while the family's distress deserves compassionate attention, family satisfaction is not the metric for evaluating advocacy effectiveness; the family may remain dissatisfied even when the patient's rights are fully protected, and using family satisfaction as the outcome measure risks re-centering the family's preferences over the patient's autonomy.
