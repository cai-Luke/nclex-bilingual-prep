# Pressure injury prevention and safe mobility in a frail older adult after hip hemiarthroplasty

## PATIENT BACKGROUND
Margaret Tierney is an 82-year-old woman admitted to the orthopedic unit following a right hip hemiarthroplasty for a displaced femoral neck fracture sustained in a ground-level fall at home. She lives alone in a single-story apartment and was independent with activities of daily living before the fall, though her daughter reports she had been "slowing down" over the past several months — eating less, losing about seven pounds unintentionally, and napping frequently during the day. Her medical history includes osteoporosis, osteoarthritis of both knees, gastroesophageal reflux disease, mild cognitive impairment (Montreal Cognitive Assessment score 22 six months ago), chronic constipation, and well-controlled hypertension. She wears bilateral hearing aids and reading glasses.

Current medications: amlodipine 5 mg daily, omeprazole 20 mg daily, polyethylene glycol 17 g daily, acetaminophen 650 mg every six hours scheduled, hydromorphone 0.5 mg IV every four hours as needed for moderate-to-severe pain per provider order, ondansetron 4 mg IV every six hours as needed for nausea, enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneous daily for venous thromboembolism prophylaxis per facility postoperative protocol, and a clear-liquid diet advancing to regular as tolerated.

Postoperative orders include posterior hip precautions (no flexion beyond 90 degrees, no internal rotation, no adduction past midline), physical therapy evaluation on postoperative day one, incentive spirometry every two hours while awake, sequential compression devices when in bed, and an indwelling urinary catheter placed intraoperatively with an order to discontinue on postoperative day one per the facility's catheter-associated urinary tract infection prevention bundle.

The Braden Scale score on admission is 14 (moderate risk): sensory perception 3 (slightly limited — she is hard of hearing and occasionally confused when drowsy), moisture 3 (occasionally moist), activity 2 (chairfast at best for now), mobility 3 (slightly limited — can reposition with assistance), nutrition 2 (probably inadequate — poor recent intake, NPO perioperatively), and friction/shear 1 (requires moderate-to-maximum assistance with repositioning and slides against sheets during transfers).

She is drowsy but arousable, oriented to person and place but not to date. She reports right hip pain of 7 out of 10 at rest and says she is afraid to move because "everything hurts." She received hydromorphone 0.5 mg IV in the recovery room ninety minutes ago. She has not used the incentive spirometer since arrival on the unit. Her daughter is at the bedside and asks whether her mother can just sleep through the night without being disturbed.

## KEY DECISION POINTS

1. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse has just repositioned Mrs. Tierney and is reviewing the full set of findings from the initial assessment and the post-repositioning check. Which findings from the combined baseline and Stage 1 data are clinically significant cues requiring nursing attention, and which are expected, normal, or not immediately relevant?
   - **Correct action:** The significant cues are the 2 × 3 cm non-blanchable coccygeal erythema (a Stage 1 pressure injury already forming), the perineal moisture with gluteal-fold erythema (a moisture-associated skin-damage risk compounding the pressure risk), the pain level of 7/10 that is preventing her from participating in repositioning and mobility, the post-opioid sedation with respiratory rate of 12 and SpO₂ dipping to 94% (opioid effect requiring monitoring), the failure to use the incentive spirometer (pulmonary complication risk), and the poor oral intake, recent unintentional weight loss, dry mucous membranes, and low albumin/prealbumin as adverse-risk markers, raising concern for reduced tissue tolerance and delayed healing. The findings that are expected or not immediately significant include the mild right foot edema (expected postoperative swelling with intact neurovascular status), the hypoactive bowel sounds (expected after anesthesia), the temperature of 36.8 °C (normal), the hemoglobin drop from 11.8 to 10.4 g/dL (expected surgical blood loss, stable, not requiring intervention at this level), and the urine output of 350 mL over five hours (averaging 70 mL/hr, which is adequate and reassuring for a postoperative adult, not indicating oliguria).
   - **Why it is correct:** Recognizing the coccygeal erythema as a Stage 1 pressure injury — not merely "redness" — triggers the pressure-injury prevention protocol. The perineal moisture is an independent risk factor for skin breakdown that compounds the pressure load. The interplay between uncontrolled pain and immobility is the central driver of her pressure-injury and pulmonary risk: she will not move, cough, or use the spirometer while pain is 7/10, but the opioid that controls the pain is producing sedation that also suppresses respiratory effort and mobility. The nutritional markers (albumin 2.8, prealbumin 14) reflect systemic inflammation and adverse outcome risk that compromise tissue tolerance, rather than serving as direct indicators of body protein. Separating these actionable cues from the expected postoperative findings (mild edema, ileus-range bowel sounds, anticipated hemoglobin drop, and reassuring urine output) prevents both alarm fatigue and missed priorities.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Recognize cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1 (requires both the baseline assessment findings and the Stage 1 post-repositioning data).

2. **Situation:** At Stage 1, the nurse must interpret the cluster of findings — the existing coccygeal Stage 1 pressure injury, the Braden score of 14, the perineal moisture, the prominent bony prominences with thin habitus, the albumin of 2.8 g/dL and prealbumin of 14 mg/dL, the pain-driven immobility, and the poor oral intake — and determine what condition or risk they point to as a unified clinical picture.
   - **Correct action:** These findings together indicate that Mrs. Tierney is at high and escalating risk for progressive pressure injury, driven by the convergence of four independent risk domains: sustained pressure (immobility from pain, inability to reposition independently, already-present Stage 1 sacral injury), moisture (perineal dampness creating maceration risk), nutritional compromise (poor intake and recent weight loss raising nutrition risk, with low albumin and prealbumin serving as adverse-risk/inflammatory markers that reinforce concern but do not diagnose malnutrition by themselves), and friction/shear (she slides against sheets during repositioning and requires maximum assistance). The Braden score of 14 formally places her at moderate risk, and the already-present Stage 1 injury plus compounding factors make the trajectory clinically more concerning than the score alone suggests.
   - **Why it is correct:** Pressure injury develops from the interaction of pressure intensity and duration with tissue tolerance. Tissue tolerance is reduced by moisture (maceration weakens the epidermis), nutritional compromise (protein/energy deficits impair collagen synthesis and tissue resilience, with low visceral proteins signaling adverse systemic risk/inflammation), and shear (distorts blood flow in the microvasculature). Analyzing these as a converging cluster — rather than treating each finding in isolation — is what distinguishes a nurse who will intervene proactively from one who documents "skin intact" and moves on. The Stage 1 injury is evidence that tissue damage is already underway, not merely predicted.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Analyze cues.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 1.

3. **Situation:** At Stage 2, Mrs. Tierney has multiple competing care needs: she has urinary urgency with a bladder scan of 420 mL and is requesting catheter reinsertion, she had orthostatic hypotension and lightheadedness on her first standing attempt, her pain is 5/10 and undertreated (last IV dose five hours ago), her oral intake remains poor, and the coccygeal pressure injury persists. The nurse must determine which need takes priority right now.
   - **Correct action:** The immediate priority is safe toileting — facilitating voiding before the bladder volume increases further and before she attempts to get up urgently on her own. This means providing a bedside commode (raised seat, armrests, to maintain hip precautions), administering the available pain medication first to make the transfer tolerable, assisting with a supervised transfer using appropriate fall-prevention measures (non-skid footwear, gait belt, locked bed and commode wheels, two-person assist given the orthostatic episode), and monitoring for orthostatic symptoms during the transfer. Reinserting the catheter is not the priority action — the CAUTI prevention bundle directs against routine reinsertion, and she has the urge to void, which suggests bladder function is returning; the nursing goal is to facilitate voiding, not to bypass it. If she cannot void on the commode or has a high post-void residual, follow the facility urinary-retention algorithm, usually intermittent straight catheterization before indwelling catheter reinsertion unless criteria are met. The pain, nutrition, and pressure-injury concerns are all important but are not as time-sensitive as the 420 mL bladder with urgency in a patient who is a fall risk and may attempt to get up unassisted.
   - **Why it is correct:** A distended bladder with urgency in a patient who has demonstrated orthostatic hypotension and impaired mobility creates an acute fall risk — she may attempt to get to the bathroom independently, or she may experience incontinence that worsens perineal moisture and skin breakdown. Addressing the toileting need safely prevents both a fall and a moisture insult to already-compromised skin. Premedicating for pain before the transfer is part of the safe-toileting plan, not a separate competing priority. Catheter reinsertion would eliminate the urgency but introduces infection risk and removes the stimulus for bladder retraining; it is the wrong solution when the patient has the physiologic capacity to void. A bladder-scan algorithm guiding straight catheterization is the appropriate clinical backup plan for urinary retention.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Prioritize hypotheses.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

4. **Situation:** At Stage 2, the nurse must develop a plan that addresses Mrs. Tierney's interconnected problems — pain limiting mobility, orthostatic hypotension limiting safe ambulation, poor nutrition impairing tissue healing, ongoing pressure-injury risk, and the need for progressive mobilization to prevent complications. What combination of nursing interventions constitutes the appropriate care plan?
   - **Correct action:** The plan integrates five domains. For pain and mobility: premedicate with the available analgesic 20–30 minutes before planned physical therapy sessions and transfers; use multimodal non-pharmacologic comfort measures (repositioning with pillows maintaining hip precautions, ice to the surgical site per order, distraction, and ensuring hearing aids are in place so she can participate in coaching); coordinate with physical therapy to schedule mobility attempts when pain control is optimized. For orthostatic hypotension and fall prevention: dangle at the bedside before standing, rise slowly, monitor symptoms and vital signs, use a gait belt and appropriate assistive device, ensure the call light is within reach at all times, and maintain the bed in the lowest locked position. For nutrition: request a dietary/nutrition consult for calorie and protein supplementation given poor intake, weight loss, and low albumin/prealbumin (which are inflammatory/adverse-risk markers rather than direct measures of protein status); offer small, frequent meals and high-protein supplements between meals; track intake and output; ensure preferred foods are available to encourage eating. For pressure-injury prevention: continue the every-two-hour repositioning schedule with documentation of position and skin assessment at each turn; maintain the pressure-redistribution overlay; continue heel suspension with proper boot fit (checking that the heel is fully floated and the strap is not creating a new pressure point); apply barrier cream to the perineum after each episode of moisture; keep linens smooth and dry; and avoid elevating the head of bed above 30 degrees when supine to reduce sacral shear. For toileting: establish a prompted voiding schedule every two to three hours using the bedside commode to prevent urgency-driven unsafe transfers and to reduce incontinence-related moisture exposure.
   - **Why it is correct:** These problems are interdependent — pain drives immobility, immobility drives pressure injury, poor nutrition impairs tissue tolerance, moisture accelerates skin breakdown, and orthostatic hypotension makes every transfer a fall risk. A plan that addresses only one domain (e.g., pain alone) will fail because the other drivers remain active. The plan is entirely within RN scope: it coordinates existing orders, initiates nursing-driven interventions (repositioning, toileting schedule, barrier cream, nutrition tracking), and generates appropriate consults (dietary). Each element is tied to a specific assessment finding rather than being a generic checklist.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Generate solutions.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 2.

5. **Situation:** At Stage 3, the nursing assistant reports the new area of non-blanchable erythema on the posterior right heel where the heel-suspension boot strap was found to have shifted. The nurse must take action.
   - **Correct action:** The nurse immediately removes the boot, inspects the heel thoroughly (the 1.5 × 1 cm area of intact skin with non-blanchable erythema and warmth is a new Stage 1 pressure injury caused by the device), documents the finding including size, location, color, skin integrity, and warmth, and implements corrective offloading — either reapplying the boot with proper positioning and padding so the heel is fully suspended with no strap contact on the calcaneus, or switching to an alternative heel-offloading method such as a pillow suspension that elevates the calf and floats the heel completely off all surfaces. The nurse notifies the provider of the new Stage 1 pressure injury per facility wound-care protocol, reassesses the fit of the boot on the opposite foot to confirm it is not creating a similar problem, and adds the right heel to the every-two-hour skin-check documentation. The nurse also communicates with the nursing assistant about the importance of checking device position at every repositioning and ensuring the heel remains fully floated.
   - **Why it is correct:** A medical device is now the source of a new pressure injury — the boot that was placed to prevent heel breakdown has itself caused breakdown because it shifted and the strap became a pressure point. This is a recognized mechanism of device-related pressure injury. The correct action is not simply to reposition the boot but to evaluate whether the device is appropriate, ensure it is correctly fitted, and consider an alternative if it cannot be maintained in the correct position. Documenting the injury, notifying the provider, and adjusting the prevention plan are all required steps. Ignoring the finding or assuming the boot is inherently protective would allow the injury to progress.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Take action.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

6. **Situation:** At Stage 3, the nurse reviews the full trajectory of Mrs. Tierney's care over the first two postoperative days and evaluates whether the basic-care and mobility interventions have been effective. Which outcomes indicate the plan is working, which indicate it is not, and what modifications are needed?
   - **Correct action:** Indicators of effectiveness include the following: the coccygeal Stage 1 pressure injury is improving (decreased from 2 × 3 cm to 1 × 2 cm with lighter color, still non-blanchable but trending toward resolution), pain is better controlled on the oral regimen (3/10 at rest, 5/10 with activity — improved from 7/10 at baseline, while continuing geriatric safety monitoring for sedation, dizziness/falls, delirium, constipation, and hyponatremia with tramadol), orthostatic hypotension has resolved (standing BP 118/70 with only a modest heart-rate increase), mobility has progressed from bedfast to walking 15 feet with a walker, she is voiding successfully on the commode without catheter reinsertion, she has had a bowel movement, and oral intake has improved from near-zero to 50% of meals with approximately 900 mL fluid intake. Indicators that the plan is not fully effective include the following: the new right heel Stage 1 pressure injury (demonstrating a failure in the device-management component of the pressure-prevention plan), still-limited oral intake (eating only 50% of meals, with low albumin/prealbumin reinforcing the tissue-tolerance risk though not diagnosing malnutrition directly), the ongoing at-risk Braden score (15, which is mild risk), and fragmented sleep. (The hemoglobin drift to 9.8 g/dL is a parallel postoperative anemia concern rather than an indicator of pressure-injury prevention effectiveness). Modifications needed: correct the heel-offloading failure as described in decision point 5, follow up on the dietary consult to intensify protein and calorie supplementation, consider whether the repositioning schedule can be coordinated with other care activities to consolidate nighttime interruptions while still maintaining the every-two-hour turning requirement, and continue to advance mobility goals with physical therapy.
   - **Why it is correct:** Evaluating outcomes requires comparing current data against the baseline trajectory, not against a single threshold. The sacral improvement confirms that the repositioning, pressure-redistribution overlay, and moisture management are working for that site. The mobility and voiding progress confirm that the pain-management and safe-toileting plans are effective. However, the new heel injury is a sentinel event that signals a gap in the plan — the nurse must not dismiss it as minor simply because the sacral area is improving. The persistent nutritional compromise (poor intake, with low albumin/prealbumin indicating ongoing adverse outcome risk rather than serving as direct indicators of body protein) means tissue tolerance remains compromised and the risk of further breakdown continues. Evaluation is not a one-time judgment but a comparison of multiple trajectories, some improving and some not, with plan modification directed at the lagging domains.
   - **Clinical-judgment skill:** Evaluate outcomes.
   - **Answerable from:** Stage 3.

## COMMON NURSING ERRORS

- Treating the coccygeal non-blanchable erythema as a normal finding from lying on the operating table and not recognizing it as a Stage 1 pressure injury that requires immediate intervention. This error leads to delayed prevention measures and allows progression to deeper tissue damage.
- Focusing on the hemoglobin drop from 11.8 to 10.4 g/dL as the most urgent finding and initiating actions around possible hemorrhage, while overlooking the skin-integrity, pain, and immobility cues. The hemoglobin change is expected after surgical blood loss and is stable; treating it as the priority diverts attention from the actual evolving risks.
- Assessing each risk factor for pressure injury in isolation — noting the low albumin/prealbumin on the lab report, the moisture on the skin assessment, and the immobility on the activity evaluation — without synthesizing them into a unified, escalating risk picture (wherein poor intake raises nutrition risk, and low visceral proteins represent inflammatory/adverse-outcome risk). This fragmented analysis leads to piecemeal interventions (e.g., ordering a dietary tray but not addressing moisture or repositioning) rather than a coordinated prevention plan.
- Prioritizing pain management as the single most urgent need at Stage 2 and delaying toileting assistance, reasoning that pain must be controlled before any transfer can occur. While premedication is part of the plan, the 420 mL bladder with urgency is the time-critical issue — the patient may attempt to get up alone or become incontinent, creating immediate fall and skin-moisture risks. Pain medication can be given as part of preparing for the commode transfer, not as a reason to postpone it.
- Agreeing to reinsert the urinary catheter because the patient requests it and because it would eliminate the urgency and the moisture risk. This prioritizes short-term convenience over infection prevention and bladder retraining. The CAUTI prevention bundle directs against routine reinsertion when the patient has the urge to void and bladder function is returning; the correct approach is to facilitate voiding, not to bypass it. Catheterization should only be considered if she is unable to void or has high residual volumes, following the facility urinary-retention algorithm (typically straight catheterization first).
- Developing a care plan that addresses pain and mobility but omits the nutritional component, treating poor intake and low albumin/prealbumin as medical problems outside nursing scope. Nutritional support — tracking food/supplement intake against goals, requesting a dietary consult, and providing an environment conducive to eating — is a core nursing responsibility in pressure-injury prevention and postoperative recovery.
- Assuming the heel-suspension boot is inherently protective and simply repositioning it without inspecting the heel or evaluating whether the device design is contributing to the injury. Medical devices are a recognized cause of pressure injuries, and the correct response is to assess the skin under the device, evaluate the device fit, and consider alternatives — not to trust the device label.
- Documenting the heel redness as "skin intact, will continue to monitor" without notifying the provider, staging the injury, or modifying the offloading plan. This passive approach allows a preventable injury to progress.
- Concluding that the overall plan is effective because the sacral pressure injury is improving and the patient is mobilizing, without recognizing that the new heel injury represents a plan failure that requires modification. Evaluation must account for all sites and all domains, not just the ones that are trending favorably.
- Discontinuing the every-two-hour repositioning schedule because the patient is now getting out of bed to the commode and walking short distances, reasoning that increased activity eliminates the need for scheduled turns. The patient is still spending the majority of each 24-hour period in bed, the Braden score remains in the at-risk range, and nutrition risk persists because intake remains limited and tissue tolerance remains compromised — the repositioning schedule remains necessary.

## EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES

- Recognize and correctly stage early pressure injury (Stage 1: non-blanchable erythema on intact skin) and distinguish it from expected postoperative findings, including identifying device-related pressure injury.
- Analyze the convergence of multiple pressure-injury risk factors — immobility, moisture, nutrition risk (integrating poor intake, weight loss, and low albumin/prealbumin as adverse-risk markers rather than diagnostic tools), friction/shear — as an integrated clinical picture rather than isolated findings, using the Braden Scale as one component of a broader assessment.
- Prioritize competing basic-care needs (safe toileting with fall prevention, pain management, nutrition, pressure-injury prevention) in a frail postoperative older adult, balancing urgency against safety.
- Plan and implement a coordinated set of nursing interventions across pain management, mobility, skin care, nutrition, toileting, and sleep that addresses the interdependence of these domains within RN scope of practice.
- Evaluate the effectiveness of basic-care and pressure-injury prevention interventions by comparing outcomes across multiple domains over time, identifying both improvements and plan failures, and modifying the plan accordingly.

## BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, postoperative day two, after the full trajectory of the first 48 hours is visible — the sacral pressure injury is improving, mobility and voiding are progressing, but a new device-related heel pressure injury has appeared and nutritional status remains compromised.

- **Most likely condition:** Impaired skin integrity with evolving pressure injury risk driven by the convergence of immobility, poor intake/recent weight loss with adverse-risk visceral protein markers, moisture exposure, and device-related pressure — manifested by a Stage 1 sacral pressure injury that is responding to intervention and a new Stage 1 right heel pressure injury caused by a shifted heel-suspension boot.
- **Plausible competing conditions:**
  1. *Surgical-site infection causing the skin changes* — wrong here: the coccygeal and heel erythema are remote from the surgical incision, the incision itself is well-approximated with only serous drainage and no surrounding erythema or warmth, the patient is afebrile, and the WBC is normal; the skin findings are pressure-related, not infectious.
  2. *Deep vein thrombosis presenting as lower-extremity skin changes* — wrong: the heel erythema is localized to the exact point of device contact on the posterior calcaneus, not diffuse; there is no calf tenderness, no unilateral swelling beyond the expected mild postoperative right-foot edema, and sequential compression devices and enoxaparin prophylaxis are in place; the finding is a device-related pressure injury, not a vascular event.
- **Two priority actions:**
  1. Correct the right heel offloading immediately — remove the shifted boot, fully suspend the heel off all surfaces using a properly fitted device or pillow elevation of the calf, and verify the heel is completely floated at every repositioning.
  2. Intensify nutritional support by following up on the dietary consult, initiating high-protein oral supplements between meals, and setting a specific daily calorie and protein intake goal to support tissue healing — poor oral intake (eating only 50% of meals) raises nutrition risk, and the low albumin/prealbumin are adverse-risk markers reinforcing concern that Mrs. Tierney is not meeting the metabolic demands required to support tissue tolerance and healing.
- **Two wrong actions:**
  1. Simply repositioning the same heel-suspension boot without inspecting the heel, evaluating the strap placement, or considering an alternative offloading method — this treats the device as inherently safe and allows the same mechanism of injury to recur.
  2. Discontinuing the every-two-hour repositioning schedule because the patient is now getting out of bed several times a day — the patient still spends the majority of each 24-hour period in bed, the Braden score remains at risk, and nutrition risk persists because intake remains limited and tissue tolerance remains compromised; stopping scheduled turns would expose the improving sacral area to renewed sustained pressure.
- **Two parameters to monitor:**
  1. Serial skin assessments of the coccygeal and right heel areas — tracking the size, color, blanchability, and skin integrity of both Stage 1 injuries at every repositioning directly indicates whether the pressure-prevention interventions are effective.
  2. Meal and supplement intake versus dietitian protein/calorie goals — tracking her daily food and supplement consumption evaluates whether the nutritional support plan is meeting her metabolic demands to support healing.
- **Two nonpriority or less useful parameters to monitor:**
  1. Hemoglobin trend — while the hemoglobin has drifted from 10.4 to 9.8 g/dL, this reflects expected postoperative hemodilution and ongoing low-grade surgical blood loss, not a marker of pressure-injury resolution or nutritional recovery; tracking it does not evaluate the effectiveness of the basic-care interventions that are the focus of this synthesis.
  2. Incentive spirometry volumes — while pulmonary hygiene is an important postoperative nursing responsibility, spirometry performance does not evaluate whether the pressure-injury prevention plan or the nutritional support is working; it is a parallel concern, not the parameter that confirms the skin-integrity and mobility trajectory.
