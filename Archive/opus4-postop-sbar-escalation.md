

**CASE TITLE**
SBAR escalation for subtle postoperative deterioration following open cholecystectomy.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 72-year-old man is postoperative day one following an open cholecystectomy for acute cholecystitis with gangrenous changes noted intraoperatively. His past medical history includes type 2 diabetes mellitus managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily (held perioperatively), benign prostatic hyperplasia, mild chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 1.4 mg/dL), and well-controlled atrial fibrillation on apixaban 5 mg twice daily (last dose held 48 hours preoperatively, not yet restarted). He lives independently with his wife. Postoperative orders include IV normal saline at 100 mL/hr, hydromorphone 0.5 mg IV every 4 hours as needed for pain, cefazolin 1 g IV every 8 hours, subcutaneous heparin 5,000 units every 8 hours, fingerstick blood glucose every 6 hours with sliding-scale insulin coverage, incentive spirometry every hour while awake, ambulation twice daily, and a Jackson-Pratt drain to bulb suction from the gallbladder fossa. The surgical resident rounded at 0630 and documented the patient as progressing appropriately. The day-shift nurse assumes care at 0700.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At the 0700 assessment, the patient is resting in bed. He is pleasant, conversational, and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. He rates his incisional pain at 4/10, which he says is improved from last night. He reports mild nausea but no vomiting. He has not yet ambulated today. He slept in short intervals overnight and says he feels "a little foggy" but attributes it to the pain medication.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline vital signs at 0700: T 37.6 °C (oral), HR 82 (irregular, consistent with known atrial fibrillation), BP 134/78, RR 16, SpO₂ 95% on room air. Lungs have diminished breath sounds at the right base with no adventitious sounds. Abdomen is soft, mildly distended, tender around the right subcostal incision with an intact dry dressing and no visible drainage on the outer bandage. The Jackson-Pratt drain contains 40 mL of serosanguineous fluid collected over the past 8 hours. Bowel sounds are hypoactive. The patient has a Foley catheter in place; urine output over the past 8 hours is 320 mL (40 mL/hr average), urine is amber and clear. Peripheral IV in the left hand is patent without signs of infiltration. Skin is warm and dry. Capillary refill is 2 seconds in all extremities. Bilateral lower extremities show no edema, no calf tenderness, and no erythema. The patient moves all extremities and follows commands appropriately.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Morning labs drawn at 0600: WBC 13,800/µL, hemoglobin 11.2 g/dL, platelets 188,000/µL, sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, bicarbonate 24 mEq/L, BUN 24 mg/dL, creatinine 1.5 mg/dL, glucose 162 mg/dL, lactate 1.2 mmol/L, INR 1.1. Fingerstick blood glucose at 0600 was 162 mg/dL, covered with 2 units of regular insulin per sliding scale.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0700 to 1100. At 0900, the nurse assists the patient to sit at the edge of the bed for his first ambulation attempt. He becomes lightheaded and asks to lie back down. The nurse obtains vitals: T 37.9 °C, HR 94 (irregular), BP 118/72, RR 18, SpO₂ 94% on room air. The patient is still oriented to person, place, and time but is slower to respond to questions and twice asks the nurse what day it is after being told. He rates his pain at 5/10 and requests hydromorphone. The nurse checks the Jackson-Pratt drain: it now contains 65 mL since it was emptied at 0700 — the fluid has shifted from serosanguineous to darker, more sanguineous output. The Foley bag shows 60 mL of urine output over the past 2 hours (30 mL/hr). The right subcostal dressing has a small area of new serous staining, approximately 3 cm in diameter. The nurse administers the hydromorphone as requested. At 1100, the nurse reassesses: T 38.2 °C, HR 102 (irregular), BP 112/68, RR 20, SpO₂ 93% on room air. The patient is now intermittently confused, asking for his wife who is sitting beside him and asking whether he has had his surgery yet. He is drowsy between interactions. Foley output for the 1000–1100 hour is 20 mL. The Jackson-Pratt drain has accumulated an additional 45 mL of frankly sanguineous fluid in the past 2 hours. The dressing stain has expanded to 6 cm. Fingerstick glucose at 1100 is 198 mg/dL.

**Stage 2** — 1100 to 1300. The nurse recognizes a pattern of deterioration and prepares to contact the surgical resident using SBAR. The nurse organizes the following information: the vital sign trend from 0700 to 1100, the change in mental status from oriented and conversational to intermittently confused, the declining urine output, the increasing and darkening Jackson-Pratt output, the expanding dressing stain, and the low-grade fever trend. The nurse calls the surgical resident at 1115. The resident listens and states, "He just had surgery yesterday, some confusion and a low-grade fever are normal. Give him Tylenol and I'll check on him during afternoon rounds." The resident does not place new orders and does not come to assess the patient. At 1200, the nurse reassesses: T 38.5 °C, HR 110 (irregular), BP 104/62, RR 24, SpO₂ 92% on room air. The patient does not know where he is and is picking at his gown. Foley output for the past hour is 10 mL. The Jackson-Pratt drain has added another 55 mL of dark sanguineous fluid. The dressing stain is now 10 cm and has a brownish-red center. The nurse draws a stat lactate and CBC per standing postoperative protocol for suspected clinical deterioration: lactate 3.8 mmol/L, hemoglobin 9.1 g/dL (down from 11.2 this morning), WBC 16,400/µL.

**Stage 3** — 1300 to 1500. After invoking the chain of command, the attending surgeon arrives at 1315 and examines the patient. Orders are placed for a stat CT abdomen with IV contrast, type and crossmatch for 2 units packed red blood cells, a 500 mL normal saline bolus, broadening antibiotics to piperacillin-tazobactam 3.375 g IV, and transfer to the surgical intermediate care unit. The CT reveals a moderate hemoperitoneum with active extravasation near the cystic artery stump. The patient is taken to the operating room for re-exploration. Pre-transport vitals: T 38.4 °C, HR 116 (irregular), BP 98/58, RR 26, SpO₂ 93% on 4 L nasal cannula (applied by the nurse when SpO₂ dropped below 92%). Foley output for the past hour is 5 mL. The patient is responsive only to voice and cannot state his name. The nurse prepares the patient for emergent transport, verifies that the surgeon has initiated the appropriate consent/surrogate/emergency-consent process per policy (as the nurse cannot obtain surgical consent from a confused patient), verifies the blood products are available, and hands off to the OR nurse using SBAR with all documented trends.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* Between 0700 and 1100 at Stage 1, the nurse has serial vital signs, mental status observations, drain output characteristics, dressing changes, and urine output data. The nurse must determine which findings represent a clinically significant trend versus expected postoperative recovery. *Correct action:* The nurse should identify the following constellation as a coherent pattern of postoperative hemorrhage with hypovolemic shock rather than isolated expected postoperative findings: the progressive tachycardia from 82 to 102, the falling blood pressure from 134/78 to 112/68, the rising temperature from 37.6 to 38.2 °C, the declining oxygen saturation from 95% to 93%, the new confusion in a patient who was fully oriented two hours earlier, the decreasing urine output from 40 mL/hr to 20 mL/hr, and the shift in Jackson-Pratt drainage from serosanguineous to frankly sanguineous with increasing volume. *Why:* No single finding in isolation is alarming in a postoperative patient, but the trajectory of all parameters in the same direction — toward hypoperfusion — constitutes early clinical deterioration. Recognizing the pattern rather than rationalizing each finding individually is the critical first step in the clinical judgment model. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must determine the most likely explanation for the cluster of findings: the tachycardia, hypotension trend, increasing sanguineous drain output, expanding dressing stain, falling urine output, and new confusion. *Correct action:* The nurse should analyze these cues as most consistent with postoperative hemorrhage — likely from the surgical site given the sanguineous drain output and dressing changes — with early hypovolemic shock. The low-grade fever and rising WBC raise a concurrent concern for early surgical site infection or developing sepsis; infection remains a differential concern given gangrenous cholecystitis, but the immediate life threat is active bleeding. The confusion is not attributable to hydromorphone alone because it developed progressively alongside hemodynamic changes, not acutely after a single standard dose. *Why:* Sanguineous drain output that is increasing in volume and darkening in color after the first 24 hours, combined with hemodynamic instability and end-organ signs of hypoperfusion (oliguria, altered mental status), points to active bleeding. In a patient whose intraoperative findings included gangrenous cholecystitis, the risk of both hemorrhage from a friable surgical bed and intra-abdominal infection is elevated. Attributing the confusion solely to opioids would be an analytic error because the hemodynamic trajectory provides a more dangerous and more likely explanation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse has organized the clinical data and must communicate the concern to the surgical resident. The nurse must structure the communication to convey urgency and obtain a timely response. *Correct action:* The nurse should use the SBAR framework to deliver a concise, organized escalation. The Situation identifies the patient and the nurse's immediate concern: "I am calling about Mr. [patient] in room [X], your postop day-one open cholecystectomy patient. I am concerned he is actively deteriorating and may be bleeding." The Background provides relevant context: the gangrenous cholecystitis, the baseline vitals and labs from this morning, and the current postoperative orders. The Assessment presents the objective trend data in sequence — the vital sign trajectory over four hours, the mental status change, the urine output decline, and the drain and dressing changes — rather than listing isolated values. The Recommendation states what the nurse believes is needed: "I think he needs to be assessed at the bedside now. I am requesting stat labs including a CBC and lactate, and I want to discuss whether he needs imaging and additional IV access." *Why:* SBAR provides a shared mental model that communicates clinical urgency efficiently. Presenting data as a trend rather than isolated snapshots makes the deterioration pattern unmistakable. Including a specific recommendation demonstrates clinical judgment and makes it harder for the recipient to dismiss the concern. Framing the call around the nurse's concern for active deterioration, not just reporting numbers, conveys the urgency that isolated data points may not. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the surgical resident has dismissed the nurse's concern, declined to assess the patient, and offered only acetaminophen. The patient continues to deteriorate over the next 45 minutes with worsening vitals, deepening confusion, near-anuria, and a rising lactate and falling hemoglobin on the stat labs the nurse obtained. The nurse must decide the next action. *Correct action:* The nurse should invoke the chain of command by contacting the attending surgeon directly, following the facility's established chain-of-command policy. The nurse should document the time of the initial call to the resident, the resident's response, the continued deterioration, and the time and content of the escalation to the attending. The nurse should not wait for afternoon rounds, should not simply re-call the same resident, and should not delay escalation to complete routine tasks such as charting or medication passes. *Why:* The chain of command exists to protect patients when the initial provider response is inadequate for the clinical situation. A patient with a hemoglobin drop of 2.1 g/dL in six hours, a lactate of 3.8 mmol/L, progressive hemodynamic instability, and near-anuria is in evolving hemorrhagic shock. Waiting for the resident to reassess at afternoon rounds could result in irreversible harm. The nurse has a professional and legal obligation to escalate when the patient's condition warrants it and the initial response does not match the clinical urgency. Documentation of the escalation sequence protects the patient, the nurse, and the institution, but documentation must not delay the escalation itself. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse has multiple competing priorities: escalating through the chain of command, continuing to monitor the deteriorating patient, obtaining stat labs, maintaining IV access, and documenting the clinical findings and communication. The nurse must determine the correct priority sequence. *Correct action:* The immediate priority is patient safety: the nurse should ensure the patient is being monitored (continuous pulse oximetry, frequent vital signs), maintain IV access, and escalate through the chain of command. Stat labs should be drawn as part of the assessment but should not delay the escalation call if they require leaving the patient's room for an extended period — the nurse can delegate the lab draw to another qualified team member or draw them while making the call. Documentation of the clinical findings and the communication with the resident is essential but occurs concurrently with or immediately after the escalation, never instead of it. Routine tasks such as scheduled medication administration for non-urgent medications, ambulation attempts, or teaching are deferred. *Why:* In a deteriorating patient, the hierarchy of action is stabilize and monitor, escalate for definitive intervention, then document. Documentation is a professional and legal requirement and must be thorough, but a nurse who delays calling the attending in order to complete charting first has inverted the priority. Delegation of tasks within the team (asking another nurse to draw labs, asking the charge nurse to assist with the chain-of-command call) is appropriate use of resources during a clinical emergency. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the attending has arrived, orders have been placed, and the patient is being prepared for emergent re-exploration. The nurse must evaluate whether the interventions initiated so far are producing any stabilization and must prepare a comprehensive handoff to the OR team. *Correct action:* The nurse should evaluate the response to the 500 mL fluid bolus by reassessing vital signs, urine output, and mental status after the bolus completes. The pre-transport vitals show continued deterioration (HR 116, BP 98/58, UO 5 mL/hr, worsening mental status), indicating the patient has not stabilized with fluids alone and confirming the need for operative intervention. The nurse should ensure the type-and-crossmatch blood is available and follow the patient or be accessible during transport. For the SBAR handoff to the OR nurse, the nurse should communicate the full trajectory: the baseline status at 0700, the pattern of deterioration with specific times and values, the initial call to the resident and the response received, the chain-of-command escalation, all interventions performed and their effects, the CT findings, current IV access and infusions, the last set of vitals, allergies, code status, and the status of the surgical consent. *Why:* Evaluating the response to the fluid bolus determines whether the patient is fluid-responsive or in decompensated shock requiring blood products and source control. The continued deterioration despite the bolus confirms that the bleeding source must be controlled operatively. The SBAR handoff to the OR team must include the full clinical trajectory because the receiving nurse was not present for the evolution and needs the complete picture to provide safe intraoperative care. Omitting the communication failures or the timeline of deterioration from the handoff could lead to underestimation of the patient's acuity. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Rationalizing each abnormal finding individually — attributing the tachycardia to pain, the confusion to hydromorphone, the low-grade fever to a normal postoperative inflammatory response, and the decreased urine output to inadequate oral intake — rather than recognizing the collective trajectory as a pattern of deterioration. Each rationalization is plausible in isolation, which is precisely why this error is common. The danger is that it delays recognition of a surgical emergency.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Attributing the new confusion primarily to the hydromorphone dose administered at 0900 and withholding further assessment or escalation because "the opioid will wear off." While opioids can cause sedation and confusion, this patient's mental status change is accompanied by hemodynamic instability, oliguria, and increasing hemorrhagic drain output. Treating the confusion as a medication side effect without investigating the hemodynamic cause delays recognition of shock.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Focusing exclusively on the fever and rising WBC as the primary problem and concluding the patient has a postoperative infection requiring only antibiotics, while overlooking the hemorrhagic indicators (sanguineous drain output, falling blood pressure, tachycardia). Infection may be a contributing factor, but the acute hemoglobin drop and drain changes point to hemorrhage as the more immediately life-threatening process.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Calling the resident and reporting only isolated values — "His temp is 38.2 and his heart rate is 102" — without presenting the trend from baseline or stating a clinical concern. This fragmented communication makes it easy for the recipient to dismiss each value as within an acceptable postoperative range. The power of SBAR lies in the organized presentation of the trajectory and the nurse's explicit statement of concern.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Accepting the resident's reassurance and waiting for afternoon rounds because the nurse does not want to "go over the resident's head" or fears professional conflict. The chain of command is a patient safety mechanism, not a disciplinary action against the resident. Failure to escalate when a patient is objectively deteriorating — falling hemoglobin, rising lactate, near-anuria — constitutes a failure of the nurse's advocacy obligation regardless of interpersonal discomfort.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Re-calling the same resident with the same information, hoping for a different response, rather than escalating to the next level in the chain of command. Repeating the same communication to the same person without new leverage (such as the stat lab results) or escalating to a higher authority wastes critical time in a patient who is actively hemorrhaging.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Stopping to complete thorough documentation of all findings before making the chain-of-command call, reasoning that "I need to have everything charted before I call the attending so I can reference it." While documentation is essential, it must not delay escalation in an actively deteriorating patient. The nurse can document concurrently, immediately after the call, or delegate documentation of vital signs to a team member. The patient's clinical trajectory does not pause for charting.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Providing a handoff to the OR nurse that includes only the current vital signs and the CT result without communicating the timeline of deterioration, the initial failed escalation, or the interventions already attempted and their effects. An incomplete handoff deprives the receiving team of the context needed to anticipate the patient's trajectory and may lead to underestimation of hemodynamic instability.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

The learner should be able to recognize a pattern of subtle postoperative deterioration by synthesizing trends across multiple assessment parameters — vital signs, mental status, urine output, drain characteristics, and wound changes — rather than evaluating each finding in isolation.

The learner should be able to construct an SBAR communication that presents clinical data as a trajectory, includes the nurse's explicit concern, and concludes with a specific recommendation for provider action.

The learner should be able to identify when the initial provider response is inadequate for the severity of the clinical situation and invoke the chain of command without delay, understanding this as a patient safety obligation rather than a hierarchical conflict.

The learner should be able to prioritize competing nursing actions during acute patient deterioration, placing stabilization and escalation before documentation and routine tasks while ensuring that documentation occurs without unnecessary delay.

The learner should be able to evaluate the effectiveness of initial resuscitative interventions by reassessing hemodynamic parameters, urine output, and mental status, and communicate the complete clinical trajectory during handoff to the receiving team.
