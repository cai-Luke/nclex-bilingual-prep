**CASE TITLE**
Complex discharge coordination post right hip arthroplasty with language, health-literacy, and social barriers.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
Mrs. Chen is an 82-year-old Mandarin-speaking woman admitted to an orthopedic surgical unit following an elective right total hip arthroplasty for severe osteoarthritis. She lives alone in a second-floor walk-up apartment; her nearest family is an adult son who lives two hours away and works full-time. She has limited English proficiency — she can exchange simple greetings but cannot reliably discuss medications, follow multi-step instructions, or read English-language documents. She does not own a smartphone and uses only a corded landline telephone at home. Her past medical history includes hypertension managed with amlodipine 5 mg daily, mild chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 1.3 mg/dL, eGFR 42 mL/min), and type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 500 mg twice daily. She has no history of venous thromboembolism. Postoperative orders include rivaroxaban 10 mg by mouth daily for 35 days for VTE prophylaxis, acetaminophen 1 g every 6 hours scheduled, oxycodone 5 mg every 4 hours as needed for breakthrough pain, amlodipine and metformin at home doses, incentive spirometry every 1–2 hours while awake, sequential compression devices while in bed, physical therapy consult on postoperative day 1, and standard hip arthroplasty precautions (posterior approach: no flexion beyond 90 degrees, no internal rotation, no adduction past midline). A social work consult has not yet been placed. Home health services have not been arranged. The anticipated discharge is postoperative day 2 or 3 pending safe mobility and adequate pain control.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The nurse receives Mrs. Chen at 1530 on postoperative day 0 after an uncomplicated surgical procedure. She is drowsy but arousable, communicates in short Mandarin phrases, nods to simple English cues ("pain?"), and holds up fingers when asked to rate pain. She appears cooperative but anxious. A surgical drain is in place with sanguineous output. An IV of lactated Ringer's is infusing at 125 mL/hr. The nurse notes that the admission record lists Mandarin as the primary language but there is no interpreter encounter documented from the preoperative area, and the informed consent was signed with an interpreter present per the surgical team's note, but no ongoing language-access plan has been initiated for the postoperative stay.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (postoperative day 0, 1600): Temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 82 and regular, blood pressure 138/78, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 96% on 2 L nasal cannula. Alert, oriented to person and place (assessed via interpreter phone line), pain rated 5/10 via numeric scale with interpreter assistance. Right hip dressing clean, dry, and intact; drain output 50 mL sanguineous since OR. Distal neurovascular checks intact: right pedal pulses 2+, capillary refill less than 3 seconds, sensation intact to light touch in all toes, able to dorsiflex and plantarflex right foot. Skin warm and dry; no calf tenderness, no edema. Foley catheter draining clear yellow urine. Abdomen soft, bowel sounds present. Patient is wearing hospital-issued non-skid socks. Call light within reach; bed alarm activated. The nurse notes the patient attempted to reach for her water cup by leaning across the bed rail, suggesting she may not understand fall-prevention instructions. These findings — fall-risk behavior, limited English, opioid use, advanced age, living alone — will drive the unfolding discharge-readiness evaluation across the stages.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn in PACU): Hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL (pre-op 12.1 g/dL, trajectory to watch for continued decline), hematocrit 32%, platelets 198,000/µL, WBC 9,200/µL, INR 1.0, aPTT 28 seconds, BUN 24 mg/dL, creatinine 1.4 mg/dL (baseline 1.3, mild rise — trajectory relevant given rivaroxaban clearance), eGFR 38 mL/min, potassium 4.0 mEq/L, sodium 139 mEq/L, glucose 162 mg/dL, albumin 3.1 g/dL. Urinalysis unremarkable. The creatinine and hemoglobin are the values whose trajectory matters most: a rising creatinine affects rivaroxaban dosing decisions, and a falling hemoglobin could signal surgical bleeding or warrant transfusion evaluation before discharge.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Postoperative day 1 (approximately 18 hours after surgery). The nurse begins the morning assessment and initiates a structured discharge-readiness screen. Temperature 37.2 °C, heart rate 78, blood pressure 132/74, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 97% on room air (nasal cannula discontinued overnight). Pain 4/10 at rest, 7/10 with movement (via interpreter phone). Drain removed per surgeon order; dressing changed, incision approximated with staples, mild ecchymosis, no erythema, scant serous drainage. Foley catheter removed; patient voids 250 mL spontaneously. IV saline lock in place; oral medications tolerated. Repeat labs: hemoglobin 10.2 g/dL (down from 10.8 — stable decline, no acute bleeding), creatinine 1.5 mg/dL (trending up from 1.4), eGFR 36 mL/min, glucose 148 mg/dL. The physical therapist arrives and performs the initial mobility session. Mrs. Chen is able to sit at the edge of the bed and stand with a front-wheeled walker with moderate assistance from two people, but she is unsteady and does not demonstrate safe use of the walker — she attempts to lift it rather than slide it, loses her balance once (caught by PT), and is unable to perform a safe sit-to-stand from a standard-height chair independently. PT documents that she is not yet safe for discharge home without continued walker training and a home-safety evaluation, recommending at minimum two more PT sessions before discharge and a strong recommendation for home physical therapy post-discharge. The nurse attempts to conduct basic discharge teaching about hip precautions and the new anticoagulant using a printed English-language handout. Mrs. Chen nods and smiles but cannot demonstrate understanding. When asked via the interpreter phone to repeat back when she should take rivaroxaban and with what, she states she will take it "when I remember" and "with water," omitting the critical instruction to take it with food (rivaroxaban 10 mg can be taken with or without food per labeling, but the 15 mg and 20 mg doses require food — the nurse realizes the current dose is 10 mg and rechecks the order, confirming 10 mg daily, but also identifies that Mrs. Chen cannot state the name of the drug, its purpose, signs of bleeding to report, or what to do if she misses a dose). The nurse documents the failed teach-back and that an in-person certified medical interpreter is needed for comprehensive discharge education. The discharge-readiness screen reveals the following unresolved barriers: (1) patient lives alone with no confirmed support person available for the first 48–72 hours post-discharge, (2) patient cannot demonstrate safe ambulation with walker, (3) discharge education incomplete due to language barrier, (4) no home health referral in place for PT or nursing, (5) rivaroxaban adherence uncertain, (6) second-floor walk-up apartment — stair navigation not yet assessed, (7) no confirmed follow-up appointment, (8) creatinine trending upward in a patient on a renally-cleared anticoagulant.

**Stage 2** — Postoperative day 2 (approximately 42 hours after surgery). Temperature 36.9 °C, heart rate 74, blood pressure 128/72, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Pain 3/10 at rest, 5/10 with movement. Incision site clean, staples intact, minimal ecchymosis, no signs of infection. Repeat creatinine 1.4 mg/dL (improved from 1.5 after IV fluid bolus and adequate oral intake), eGFR 39 mL/min. Hemoglobin 10.0 g/dL (stable). Glucose 138 mg/dL. The nurse contacts the inpatient social worker to address the home-situation barriers. Social work interviews Mrs. Chen (via telephone interpreter) and confirms: (a) her son can take one day off work and be present on the day of discharge and the following day, but not beyond that; (b) Mrs. Chen receives Meals on Wheels but has no other community services; (c) she has traditional Medicare Parts A and B with a supplemental plan; (d) social work/case management determines post-acute eligibility and payer requirements, but she does qualify for Medicare home health services (skilled nursing plus home PT) because she is homebound and requires skilled care. Social work initiates the home health referral. Physical therapy conducts the second session: Mrs. Chen now demonstrates safe walker use on level ground with standby assistance only, performs sit-to-stand from a raised toilet seat independently, but has not yet attempted stairs. PT plans a stair-training session for the afternoon. The nurse arranges for a certified in-person Mandarin interpreter to be present for a comprehensive discharge teaching session scheduled for the afternoon of postoperative day 2. The interpreter arrives at 1400. The nurse conducts a full teach-back session covering: hip precautions (no bending past 90 degrees, no crossing legs, no twisting the operative leg inward — demonstrated with a doll model), rivaroxaban purpose (blood thinner to prevent clots), dose and timing (10 mg once daily at the same time each day for 35 days), signs of bleeding to report (blood in urine or stool, black tarry stools, unusual bruising, bleeding gums, dizziness or weakness), action if a dose is missed (take it as soon as remembered on the same day; do not double up), pain medication instructions (scheduled acetaminophen, oxycodone only for breakthrough pain, do not exceed acetaminophen 3 g/day from all sources), and when to call the surgeon or go to the emergency department (fever above 101.5 °F, increasing redness or drainage at the incision, sudden severe pain, chest pain or shortness of breath, calf swelling or tenderness). Mrs. Chen repeats back each key point accurately in Mandarin, confirmed by the interpreter. She correctly demonstrates hip precautions using the doll. She identifies the rivaroxaban pill in her medication cup. She states she will call her son to call the surgeon's office if she has a problem, because she cannot navigate the English phone tree alone — the nurse flags this as a remaining barrier. The teach-back is documented as successful with interpreter present. In the afternoon PT session, Mrs. Chen negotiates four stairs (up and down) with the walker and one handrail with standby assistance. PT clears her for discharge home with home PT services, a front-wheeled walker (to be provided), a raised toilet seat, and a reacher/grabber. PT provides a written home exercise program — the nurse notes it is in English only and requests a Mandarin-translated version from the rehabilitation department. The surgeon visits and writes a discharge order for postoperative day 3 morning, pending nursing confirmation of discharge readiness.

**Stage 3** — Postoperative day 3 (approximately 66 hours after surgery, morning of discharge). Temperature 36.7 °C, heart rate 72, blood pressure 126/70, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Pain 2/10 at rest, 4/10 with movement, controlled with scheduled acetaminophen alone (patient declined last two doses of oxycodone). Incision clean and dry. Creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (returned to baseline), eGFR 42 mL/min. Hemoglobin 9.8 g/dL (stable, no transfusion indicated). Glucose 132 mg/dL. The nurse conducts a final discharge-readiness verification. The home health agency (Visiting Nurse Association) has accepted the referral; the first home visit (skilled nursing plus PT) is confirmed for the day after discharge. The nurse places a handoff telephone call to the receiving home health nurse. During this call, the nurse communicates the following using a structured format (SBAR): (S) 82-year-old Mandarin-speaking woman, postoperative day 3 from right total hip arthroplasty, being discharged home; (B) lives alone in a second-floor walk-up, limited English, no smartphone, primary caregiver is son who will be present on discharge day and one additional day only, has Medicare, mild CKD at baseline, on rivaroxaban 10 mg daily for 35 days for VTE prophylaxis; (A) currently ambulating with front-wheeled walker with standby assistance, managing stairs with rail, pain controlled on oral acetaminophen, incision healing well, teach-back completed with in-person Mandarin interpreter and documented as successful, Mandarin-language home exercise program provided, patient can identify her rivaroxaban pill and state its purpose but cannot independently navigate the English-language phone system to reach the surgeon's office; (R) requests home health nurse verify medication setup (daily pill organizer), confirm rivaroxaban adherence at each visit, assess for bleeding signs, continue PT for safe mobility and fall prevention, assess home safety especially stair navigation and bathroom setup, and coordinate with a Mandarin interpreter for all teaching encounters — the home health agency confirms they have a Mandarin-speaking home health aide available. The nurse also calls the surgeon's office to confirm the two-week follow-up appointment and requests that a Mandarin interpreter be scheduled for that visit. The son arrives at 0900. The nurse reviews discharge instructions with Mrs. Chen and her son together (interpreter present by video), confirms he understands how to reach the surgeon's office on her behalf, provides him with the home health agency's phone number, and gives Mrs. Chen a pre-filled 7-day pill organizer labeled in Mandarin (prepared by pharmacy). Mrs. Chen is discharged at 1030 via wheelchair to her son's car with the walker, raised toilet seat, and reacher/grabber loaded. The nurse documents that all identified discharge barriers have been addressed or have a confirmed plan of care in the community.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *The situation / question:* On postoperative day 0 (Stage 1 setup), the nurse receives Mrs. Chen and reviews the admission record. The nurse must decide what immediate action to take regarding the language-access gap — there is no documented postoperative language-access plan despite Mandarin being listed as the primary language. *Correct action:* The nurse activates the hospital's interpreter services (telephone interpreter line for immediate needs) and documents the patient's preferred language and need for interpreter services in the care plan, flagging that an in-person certified medical interpreter will be required for discharge teaching. The nurse does not rely on gestures, nodding, or the patient's limited English for any clinical communication. *Why it is correct:* Federal regulations (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act) and The Joint Commission standards require that patients with limited English proficiency receive language-appropriate communication for informed participation in care. Using ad hoc interpretation (family members, bilingual staff who are not certified interpreters) risks miscommunication of critical safety information. Early identification allows time to schedule an in-person interpreter before discharge. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — the nurse must act on the language-access gap as soon as she identifies it at admission to the unit.

2. *The situation / question:* On postoperative day 1 (Stage 1), the nurse attempts discharge teaching using a printed English handout and the teach-back method fails — Mrs. Chen cannot repeat key medication information. The nurse must decide the next step: proceed with discharge planning using simplified English, ask the son to interpret by phone, or formally request an in-person certified medical interpreter and reschedule teaching. *Correct action:* The nurse documents the failed teach-back, does not proceed with discharge, and formally requests an in-person certified Mandarin medical interpreter for a rescheduled comprehensive teaching session. The nurse does not use the son as an interpreter for medical instructions. *Why it is correct:* Teach-back failure is a signal that the patient has not achieved understanding sufficient for safe self-care at home. Using family members as interpreters violates best practice because they may lack medical vocabulary, may filter or editorialize information, and the patient may withhold concerns in front of family. An in-person interpreter is preferred over a telephone interpreter for complex, multi-step discharge teaching that involves demonstration and return demonstration, because visual cues and physical proximity improve communication fidelity. Documenting the failed teach-back creates a record that supports delaying discharge until the barrier is resolved. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — the teach-back failure data is generated during the Day 1 teaching attempt.

3. *The situation / question:* On postoperative day 1 (Stage 1), the nurse reviews PT's documentation that Mrs. Chen is not safe for discharge without further walker training, combined with the nurse's own identification of multiple unresolved barriers (lives alone, no home health referral, language barrier, trending creatinine, anticoagulant education incomplete). The nurse must decide whom to contact and in what order to begin addressing these barriers systematically. *Correct action:* The nurse's priority contact is the inpatient social worker, because social work is the discipline with expertise and authority to evaluate home safety, determine eligibility for post-acute services (home health vs. skilled nursing facility), initiate the home health referral, and coordinate community resources — and these are the barriers that require the longest lead time to resolve. The nurse simultaneously communicates with the charge nurse and the surgeon (or surgical team) that discharge on postoperative day 2 may not be safe given the unresolved barriers, so that the team can adjust the anticipated discharge date. The interpreter request and PT sessions can proceed in parallel but do not require the same lead time. *Why it is correct:* Home health referrals require insurance verification, agency acceptance, and scheduling — processes that take 24–48 hours. Initiating the social work consult on Day 1 rather than Day 2 prevents a discharge delay. The nurse is the coordinator of the interdisciplinary discharge plan and is responsible for identifying barriers, communicating them to the team, and activating the appropriate disciplines. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses (which barrier is most time-sensitive). *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1 — all the barrier data is assembled by the end of Day 1.

4. *The situation / question:* On postoperative day 2 (Stage 2), the nurse notes that Mrs. Chen's creatinine rose to 1.5 mg/dL on Day 1 and has improved to 1.4 mg/dL on Day 2 after hydration, but she is prescribed rivaroxaban, which has partial renal clearance. The nurse must decide whether this trend requires any nursing action regarding the anticoagulant. *Correct action:* The nurse notifies the prescribing provider of the creatinine trend and the current eGFR (39 mL/min on Day 2), confirms that rivaroxaban 10 mg daily remains appropriate at this level of renal function, and documents the provider's response. The nurse also ensures that the home health referral includes a request for periodic renal function monitoring as part of the ongoing plan of care. *Why it is correct:* Rivaroxaban 10 mg for VTE prophylaxis after hip arthroplasty does not require dose adjustment until eGFR falls below 15 mL/min per current labeling, so the current dose is appropriate. However, the nurse's role is not to independently clear the medication — it is to recognize the trend (worsening then improving renal function in a patient on a renally-cleared drug), communicate it to the provider, and ensure ongoing monitoring. A nurse who assumes "it's fine" without notifying the provider omits a critical safety step; a nurse who holds the medication without a provider order is acting outside scope. The creatinine improvement after hydration is reassuring but does not eliminate the need for surveillance in an 82-year-old with baseline CKD on a 35-day anticoagulant course. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — the creatinine trajectory across Days 0, 1, and 2 is needed to make this judgment.

5. *The situation / question:* On postoperative day 2 (Stage 2), the interpreter-mediated teach-back is successful, but the nurse identifies a residual barrier: Mrs. Chen cannot independently navigate the surgeon's English-language phone system to report problems. The nurse must decide how to address this communication gap before discharge. *Correct action:* The nurse develops a multi-layered communication plan: (a) programs the surgeon's office number and the home health agency number into a speed-dial list on Mrs. Chen's landline (or provides a large-print card in Mandarin with the numbers and instructions for her son to call on her behalf), (b) confirms with the son that he is willing and able to serve as the phone contact for the surgeon's office and documents this in the discharge plan, (c) communicates this arrangement to the home health agency during the handoff call so the home health nurse can reinforce it, and (d) ensures the surgeon's office is aware that a Mandarin interpreter must be present for the follow-up visit. *Why it is correct:* Discharge planning must account for the patient's actual capacity to access the healthcare system, not just her understanding of when to seek help. Identifying that a patient knows the danger signs but cannot act on them is a systems-level safety concern. The nurse addresses it by building redundancy — a family contact, a home health professional checking in, and interpreter services at the follow-up — rather than relying on a single channel. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2 — the residual barrier only emerges after the teach-back is completed and the nurse evaluates what the patient can and cannot do independently.

6. *The situation / question:* On postoperative day 3 (Stage 3), the nurse must evaluate whether all discharge barriers identified on Day 1 have been resolved or have a confirmed community plan of care, and must execute the handoff to the home health agency. The nurse must decide what information is essential to communicate during the handoff call to ensure continuity of the safety plan. *Correct action:* The nurse uses a structured handoff format (SBAR) and communicates: the patient's language needs and that all teaching encounters require a Mandarin interpreter, the specific medications including the rivaroxaban course (drug name, dose, duration, purpose, and the need for adherence monitoring), the baseline and current renal function with the recommendation for periodic monitoring, the functional status and PT recommendations, the home environment (second-floor walk-up, lives alone after son leaves), the equipment provided (walker, raised toilet seat, reacher), the son's contact information as the designated family communication link, and the confirmed follow-up appointment with interpreter scheduled. The nurse confirms the home health agency's receipt of orders and the date of the first visit. *Why it is correct:* The handoff is the highest-risk moment in the care transition. Information loss at handoff — particularly regarding language needs, anticoagulant monitoring, and the patient's social isolation — could result in missed bleeding complications, medication non-adherence, falls, or inability to access emergency care. A structured format ensures completeness and allows the receiving nurse to ask clarifying questions in real time. The nurse's role as care coordinator is not complete until the information has been transmitted and acknowledged. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3 — the handoff can only be executed and evaluated after all barriers have been addressed and the discharge is imminent.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: The nurse assumes that because Mrs. Chen nods and smiles, she understands enough English for routine postoperative care, and delays requesting interpreter services until the day of discharge. This is wrong because nodding is a social behavior, not evidence of comprehension, and delaying interpreter access violates federal language-access requirements and leaves the patient unable to report symptoms, understand safety instructions, or participate in care decisions throughout the hospitalization.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse calls Mrs. Chen's son and asks him to interpret the discharge instructions over the phone. This is wrong because using family members as medical interpreters is a patient-safety risk: the son may lack medical vocabulary in Mandarin, may inadvertently omit or alter clinical information, and Mrs. Chen may not disclose sensitive concerns (such as pain, incontinence, or fear) through her son. Additionally, if the son is not present to observe demonstrations (hip precautions, medication identification), phone interpretation alone is insufficient for complex teaching.

Attached to Decision Point 2: The nurse documents "patient educated on discharge medications" after the failed teach-back because the information was provided, even though understanding was not confirmed. This is wrong because documentation of education without confirmed understanding is inaccurate, creates a false sense of readiness, and does not meet the standard of care for discharge teaching, which requires that the patient or caregiver demonstrate comprehension.

Attached to Decision Point 3: The nurse waits until postoperative day 2 to contact social work, reasoning that discharge is not until Day 3 and there is still time. This is wrong because home health referrals require insurance verification, agency acceptance, and scheduling that typically take 24–48 hours. Delaying the referral by one day risks either an unsafe discharge without services in place or an unnecessary additional hospital day, which increases the patient's risk of hospital-acquired complications (falls, deconditioning, infection) and increases costs.

Attached to Decision Point 4: The nurse independently decides that rivaroxaban is safe because the creatinine improved and does not notify the provider. This is wrong because the decision to continue, adjust, or monitor a medication based on changing renal function is a provider-level decision. The nurse's role is to recognize the relevant cue (trending creatinine in a patient on a renally-cleared drug), communicate it, and document the response — not to independently clear the medication.

Attached to Decision Point 4: The nurse holds the rivaroxaban without a provider order because the creatinine rose. This is wrong because withholding a prescribed anticoagulant without an order places the patient at risk for deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism. The appropriate action is to notify the provider and obtain an order before altering the medication regimen.

Attached to Decision Point 5: The nurse identifies the phone-access barrier but documents it as "patient's responsibility to arrange" and takes no further action. This is wrong because discharge planning requires the nurse to address barriers to safe care access, not merely identify them. A patient who knows the signs of bleeding but cannot reach the provider to report them is not safely discharged.

Attached to Decision Point 6: The nurse faxes the discharge summary to the home health agency but does not make a verbal handoff call, assuming the written documents are sufficient. This is wrong because written documents alone do not allow for real-time clarification, may not be reviewed before the first home visit, and may not highlight the critical nuances (language needs, social isolation, anticoagulant monitoring) that a verbal handoff communicates. A structured verbal handoff is the standard of care for high-risk transitions.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

1. Identify and activate appropriate language-access resources (certified medical interpreters, translated materials) for patients with limited English proficiency, distinguishing between acceptable and unacceptable interpretation methods in clinical settings.

2. Apply the teach-back method as a discharge-readiness evaluation tool, recognizing when a failed teach-back requires a change in approach (interpreter, modality, timing) rather than repetition of the same intervention.

3. Prioritize and sequence interdisciplinary referrals (social work, home health, physical therapy, interpreter services, pharmacy) based on lead time, patient acuity, and discharge timeline to prevent unsafe or delayed discharge.

4. Recognize and communicate clinically significant trends in renal function for a patient prescribed a renally-cleared anticoagulant, acting within RN scope to notify the provider and ensure ongoing monitoring without independently adjusting the medication regimen.

5. Execute a structured interdisciplinary handoff to a receiving home health agency that addresses medical, functional, linguistic, and social determinants of safe post-discharge care, and evaluate whether all identified discharge barriers have been resolved or have a confirmed plan prior to releasing the patient.
