**CASE TITLE**
Postpartum preeclampsia with severe features presenting after hospital discharge.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 31-year-old gravida 2, para 2 woman delivered a healthy 3,400-gram infant via uncomplicated vaginal delivery at 39 weeks and 2 days gestation. Her prenatal course was unremarkable: all blood pressures were below 130/80 throughout pregnancy, she had no proteinuria at any prenatal visit, and she passed her one-hour glucose challenge test at 26 weeks. She has no history of hypertension, preeclampsia, renal disease, or seizure disorder. Her only medical history is mild seasonal allergic rhinitis. She was discharged on postpartum day 2 in stable condition, breastfeeding well, with discharge blood pressure 124/78. Discharge medications included ibuprofen 600 mg every six hours as needed for perineal discomfort, prenatal vitamins, and a stool softener. She was given standard postpartum discharge instructions including warning signs to report — headache not relieved by medication, visual changes, severe swelling, epigastric pain, and blood pressure concerns — and was scheduled for a routine postpartum visit at six weeks. She now presents to the emergency department on postpartum day 5, brought in by her partner, who reports that she has been complaining of a worsening headache since yesterday that "won't go away" despite taking ibuprofen and acetaminophen, and that this morning she reported seeing "sparkly spots" in her vision. She is admitted to the postpartum unit for evaluation. The admitting provider orders a complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, lactate dehydrogenase, uric acid, coagulation studies, urinalysis with urine protein-to-creatinine ratio, and continuous blood pressure monitoring every fifteen minutes.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient walks into the triage area carrying her infant in a car seat. She appears fatigued but is conversational and oriented. She states, "I've had this headache for two days and it's getting worse — Motrin and Tylenol aren't touching it. This morning I started seeing these little flashing spots, like sparklers, especially when I look to the side. I also have this weird pain right here," and she places her hand over her right upper quadrant and epigastrium. She denies chest pain, shortness of breath, and seizure activity. She reports her lochia has been decreasing normally and she has been urinating without difficulty. She is breastfeeding every two to three hours and reports the baby is latching well. Her partner adds that her face and hands seem more swollen than they were at discharge and that she has been more irritable and "not acting like herself" since yesterday.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 37.0 °C. Heart rate 92 and regular. Blood pressure 168/112, confirmed on repeat measurement in the opposite arm at 170/108 five minutes later. Respiratory rate 18. SpO₂ 97% on room air. Weight 72 kg (discharge weight on postpartum day 2 was 69 kg — a 3 kg gain in three days).

The patient is alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. She appears fatigued with mild periorbital and facial puffiness and bilateral hand edema; her rings, which she reports wearing throughout pregnancy without difficulty, are now tight and she cannot remove them. Bilateral lower extremity edema is 2+ pitting to the mid-shin. Fundal height is palpated at two fingerbreadths below the umbilicus, firm and midline, consistent with expected involution for postpartum day 5. Lochia is serosa — scant, pinkish-brown, without clots or foul odor. The perineum has a well-approximated second-degree laceration repair with mild surrounding ecchymosis, no erythema, no drainage, and no dehiscence. Breasts are bilaterally engorged, warm, and mildly tender, with colostrum expressed from both nipples; no masses, no erythema, no axillary lymphadenopathy. Lungs are clear to auscultation bilaterally with no crackles or wheezes. Heart sounds are regular with no murmur, rub, or gallop. Abdomen is soft with mild tenderness to palpation in the right upper quadrant and epigastrium; no rebound, no guarding, no hepatomegaly appreciated. Bowel sounds are present in all four quadrants. Deep tendon reflexes are 3+ at the biceps and patellar tendons bilaterally with four beats of ankle clonus. The patient reports the headache as 8 out of 10, frontal and bilateral, constant, and not relieved by her home analgesics. She describes the visual disturbance as intermittent scotomata — "flashing spots" in the peripheral visual fields bilaterally, present for approximately six hours. Cranial nerves II through XII are grossly intact. No facial droop, no speech difficulty, no focal motor or sensory deficits. Foley catheter is not in place; the patient voided 200 mL of clear yellow urine on arrival.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Complete blood count: WBC 9,800/µL, hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL (discharge hemoglobin was 11.4 g/dL — an expected mild postpartum decline), hematocrit 32.4%, platelets 118,000/µL (discharge platelets were 210,000/µL). Comprehensive metabolic panel: sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 1.2 mg/dL (discharge creatinine was 0.7 mg/dL), glucose 94 mg/dL, calcium 9.0 mg/dL, total protein 6.2 g/dL, albumin 3.0 g/dL, AST 142 U/L (reference 10–40), ALT 128 U/L (reference 7–56), alkaline phosphatase 88 U/L, total bilirubin 1.0 mg/dL. LDH 380 U/L (reference 140–280). Uric acid 7.8 mg/dL (reference 2.5–7.0). Coagulation studies: PT 12.4 seconds, INR 1.0, fibrinogen 320 mg/dL. Urinalysis: specific gravity 1.020, trace protein, no blood, no leukocyte esterase, no nitrites. Urine protein-to-creatinine ratio: 0.28 (less than 0.3 is generally considered normal; significant proteinuria is typically above 0.3). Peripheral blood smear: no schistocytes identified.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 2 hours after admission. The admitting provider reviews the laboratory results and blood pressure readings. The diagnosis of postpartum preeclampsia with severe features is established based on: sustained severe-range hypertension (systolic ≥160 or diastolic ≥110 on two measurements), headache unresponsive to analgesics, visual disturbances (scotomata), right upper quadrant and epigastric pain, elevated hepatic transaminases (AST 142, ALT 128), concerning rise in creatinine (0.7 → 1.2), and falling platelets (210,000 → 118,000) — meeting criteria for severe features based on symptoms and severe hypertension, even in the absence of significant proteinuria (urine protein-to-creatinine ratio 0.28) and before platelets drop below 100,000. The provider orders: magnesium sulfate loading dose 4 grams IV in 250 mL normal saline over 20 minutes, followed by a continuous infusion at 1 gram per hour for seizure prophylaxis; IV labetalol 20 mg over 2 minutes for acute blood pressure management, with repeat dosing per the unit's severe hypertension protocol if blood pressure remains systolic ≥160 or diastolic ≥110 at 10-minute intervals (40 mg, then 80 mg); strict intake and output with Foley catheter insertion; continuous pulse oximetry; and magnesium sulfate monitoring per protocol — deep tendon reflexes, respiratory rate, and level of consciousness assessed every hour, with serum magnesium level drawn at 4 hours. The nurse initiates the magnesium sulfate loading dose. During the infusion, the patient reports feeling flushed and warm. Heart rate is 88, blood pressure after the first labetalol dose is 162/104 at 10 minutes. A second dose of labetalol 40 mg IV is administered per protocol. Blood pressure at 20 minutes is 144/92. The patient's headache decreases to 5 out of 10. The visual scotomata persist but the patient reports they are less frequent. The partner, who is holding the newborn in the room, asks, "She didn't have preeclampsia during pregnancy — how can she have it now? Is the baby at risk from the medicines she's getting? Can she still breastfeed?"

**Stage 2** — 2 to 8 hours after admission. Blood pressure stabilizes at 138/86 on continuous magnesium sulfate infusion at 1 gram per hour. Heart rate 84. Respiratory rate 16. SpO₂ 97%. Urine output via Foley catheter: 180 mL over the first four hours (approximately 0.6 mL/kg/hr — adequate but on the lower end). The four-hour serum magnesium level returns at 5.2 mEq/L (therapeutic range for seizure prophylaxis per unit protocol: 4.0–7.0 mEq/L). Deep tendon reflexes are now 2+ at the patellar tendons bilaterally with no clonus — diminished from the 3+ with four beats of clonus on admission, consistent with the therapeutic magnesium effect. Respiratory rate remains 16 and the patient is alert and conversational. The headache has decreased to 3 out of 10. Scotomata have resolved. The right upper quadrant tenderness is still present but the patient reports it is less intense. Repeat labs at 6 hours: platelets 108,000/µL (down from 118,000 at admission), AST 156 U/L (up from 142), ALT 138 U/L (up from 128), creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (up from 1.2), LDH 410 U/L (up from 380). The peripheral smear is repeated: rare schistocytes are now identified. The provider reviews the worsening labs — falling platelets, rising transaminases, rising LDH, and the appearance of schistocytes — and discusses with the attending obstetrician the concern for evolving HELLP syndrome (hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets). The plan is to continue magnesium sulfate, maintain blood pressure control, monitor labs every six hours, and consult maternal-fetal medicine. The provider emphasizes to the nursing team that if platelets fall below 100,000 or the patient develops new symptoms (right upper quadrant pain worsening, shortness of breath, altered mental status), the team should be notified immediately.

**Stage 3** — 8 to 24 hours after admission. At the 12-hour mark, repeat labs show: platelets 94,000/µL, AST 168 U/L, ALT 152 U/L, LDH 680 U/L, creatinine 1.3 mg/dL (stable), hemoglobin 10.2 g/dL. Peripheral smear: schistocytes present. Together with LDH 680, elevated transaminases, and schistocytes, the platelet drop below 100,000 supports HELLP syndrome. The patient reports that her headache is now 2 out of 10 and her vision is clear, but the right upper quadrant pain has returned at 6 out of 10 despite the magnesium infusion. Blood pressure is 142/88. Respiratory rate 14. SpO₂ 96%. Urine output has been 40 mL/hr (adequate). Serum magnesium at 12 hours is 5.8 mEq/L. Deep tendon reflexes are 1+ bilaterally, no clonus. The patient is alert but anxious; she states, "I feel like my body is falling apart. I just had a baby — I'm supposed to be happy. What's wrong with me?" She begins to cry. The maternal-fetal medicine consultant recommends continued magnesium sulfate for a minimum of 24 hours from the last seizure-prophylaxis indication, serial labs every 6 hours, blood pressure management to keep systolic below 150 and diastolic below 100, and close monitoring for signs of hepatic rupture (sudden severe abdominal pain, hemodynamic instability), pulmonary edema (dyspnea, crackles, desaturation), or eclamptic seizure. By 24 hours, repeat labs show: platelets 102,000/µL (slight uptrend), AST 148 U/L (slight downtrend), ALT 140 U/L (stable), LDH 610 U/L (downtrend), creatinine 1.1 mg/dL (improving). The patient's headache has resolved. Right upper quadrant pain is 2 out of 10. Blood pressure is 136/84 on oral nifedipine 30 mg extended-release, which was started at hour 16 when IV labetalol was transitioned to oral therapy. The provider plans to continue magnesium sulfate for an additional 24 hours, then reassess for discontinuation.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* On admission, the nurse reviews the full set of assessment findings and laboratory data. The findings passage contains expected postpartum findings (scant lochia serosa, fundus firm and involuting appropriately, breast engorgement, mild perineal ecchymosis, expected postpartum hemoglobin decline from 11.4 to 10.8, fatigue) alongside findings that require immediate follow-up. The nurse must identify which findings are concerning and which are normal postpartum recovery. *Correct action:* The findings requiring immediate follow-up are: blood pressure 168/112 confirmed at 170/108 (severe-range hypertension), headache rated 8/10 unrelieved by ibuprofen and acetaminophen (refractory headache), visual scotomata (flashing spots in peripheral fields for six hours), right upper quadrant and epigastric tenderness, deep tendon reflexes 3+ with four beats of ankle clonus (hyperreflexia with clonus), 3 kg weight gain in three days with new facial and hand edema (rapid fluid accumulation), platelets 118,000 down from 210,000 at discharge (falling platelet count), AST 142 and ALT 128 (elevated hepatic transaminases), creatinine 1.2 up from 0.7 at discharge (concerning rise in creatinine), and LDH 380 (elevated). The findings that are normal postpartum recovery and do not require immediate follow-up in this context are: scant lochia serosa without clots or odor, fundus firm at two fingerbreadths below the umbilicus, breast engorgement with colostrum expression, well-approximated perineal laceration repair with mild ecchymosis, hemoglobin 10.8 (expected postpartum decline), normal WBC, normal coagulation studies, normal urinalysis without significant proteinuria, clear lungs, and normal bowel sounds. *Why it is correct:* Postpartum preeclampsia with severe features is diagnosed by the presence of severe-range hypertension and/or end-organ dysfunction occurring after delivery. The severe-feature criteria do not require proteinuria — the diagnosis is established by the hypertension combined with any of the following: cerebral symptoms (refractory headache, visual disturbances), hepatic involvement (elevated transaminases, RUQ/epigastric pain), renal dysfunction (rising creatinine), or thrombocytopenia. The normal postpartum findings — appropriate uterine involution, expected lochia pattern, breast changes of early lactation, and the mild hemoglobin decline — are physiologic and do not indicate pathology. Distinguishing the two sets is the foundational recognition task. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Admission data.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the partner asks whether the patient can still breastfeed while receiving magnesium sulfate and labetalol, and how she can have preeclampsia when she did not have it during pregnancy. The nurse must provide accurate, scope-appropriate education. *Correct action:* Explain that preeclampsia can develop for the first time after delivery — it is not limited to the antepartum period — and that the postpartum period, particularly the first week, is a recognized risk window. Regarding breastfeeding, inform the partner that magnesium sulfate is compatible with breastfeeding; the amount transferred into breast milk is small and is not considered harmful to the newborn, though postpartum IV magnesium can delay lactogenesis, so the nurse should offer lactation support. Labetalol levels in breast milk are low and not expected to harm full-term infants, so it is also generally considered compatible with breastfeeding. The nurse should encourage the patient to continue breastfeeding or pumping as she is able, and should facilitate skin-to-skin contact and infant care at the bedside to the extent the patient's condition and monitoring requirements allow. The nurse does not make independent medication-compatibility determinations but can relay the provider's guidance and refer to the unit's lactation resources. *Why it is correct:* Postpartum patients and families frequently do not know that preeclampsia can present after delivery, and fear about medication effects on breastfeeding is one of the most common reasons patients resist necessary treatment. Accurate reassurance about breastfeeding compatibility supports both treatment adherence and the breastfeeding relationship. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the magnesium sulfate loading dose is infusing and the nurse must establish the ongoing monitoring plan. The nurse must identify which parameters to assess and at what frequency to detect both therapeutic effect and magnesium toxicity. *Correct action:* Per the unit's magnesium sulfate monitoring protocol, the nurse assesses deep tendon reflexes (patellar reflex is the standard site), respiratory rate, level of consciousness, and urine output every hour during the infusion. The therapeutic serum magnesium range for seizure prophylaxis per unit protocol is 4.0–7.0 mEq/L, with the first level drawn at 4 hours. The nurse holds the infusion and notifies the provider if deep tendon reflexes are absent (loss of reflexes occurs at approximately 7–10 mEq/L), respiratory rate falls below 12, urine output falls below 30 mL/hr (magnesium is renally excreted and will accumulate if output is inadequate), or the patient becomes unarousable. Calcium gluconate 1 gram must be available at the bedside as the antidote for magnesium toxicity. The flushing and warmth the patient reports during the loading dose are expected vasodilatory effects of magnesium and do not require stopping the infusion, but the nurse documents them and continues monitoring. *Why it is correct:* Magnesium sulfate has a narrow therapeutic-to-toxic window. The progression of toxicity follows a predictable sequence — loss of deep tendon reflexes, then respiratory depression, then cardiac arrest — and hourly assessment of reflexes and respiratory rate is the primary nursing surveillance mechanism. Urine output monitoring is essential because impaired renal clearance (already a concern in this patient with creatinine 1.2 at admission, rising to 1.3 at Stage 2) causes magnesium accumulation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the repeat labs show falling platelets (118,000 → 108,000), rising transaminases (AST 142 → 156, ALT 128 → 138), rising LDH (380 → 410), and new schistocytes on peripheral smear, despite clinical improvement in symptoms (headache and scotomata improving, blood pressure controlled). The nurse must analyze whether the patient is improving or worsening. *Correct action:* Recognize that the clinical symptom improvement (headache decreasing, scotomata resolving, blood pressure controlled) reflects the therapeutic effect of magnesium sulfate and labetalol, but the laboratory trajectory indicates worsening end-organ damage — specifically, the falling platelets, rising liver enzymes, rising LDH, and appearance of schistocytes suggest evolving HELLP syndrome. The patient is simultaneously getting better symptomatically and worse hematologically. The nurse must not be reassured by the symptom improvement alone and must report the laboratory trend to the provider as a change in condition. *Why it is correct:* HELLP syndrome can evolve even as blood pressure is controlled and neurological symptoms improve. The laboratory markers — particularly the platelet trend, the transaminase trend, and the appearance of schistocytes (indicating microangiopathic hemolysis) — are the objective indicators of disease progression. A nurse who focuses only on the patient's subjective improvement and the controlled blood pressure may miss the worsening organ damage. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues, prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient's platelets have fallen below 100,000, supporting HELLP syndrome, and her right upper quadrant pain has worsened to 6 out of 10. The deep tendon reflexes are now 1+ with no clonus, and the serum magnesium is 5.8 mEq/L. The nurse must evaluate the magnesium sulfate's effect and determine whether the reflex change is therapeutic or toxic. *Correct action:* The diminution of reflexes from 3+ with four beats of clonus at admission to 2+ without clonus at Stage 2 to 1+ at Stage 3 represents a continuum. The 1+ reflexes with a magnesium level of 5.8 mEq/L (within the therapeutic range of 4.0–7.0 per unit protocol) indicate that the magnesium is at the higher end of therapeutic and the reflexes are diminishing as expected — but they are still present. The nurse continues the infusion at the current rate, documents the reflex finding, and increases vigilance: if reflexes become absent on the next hourly check, the nurse holds the infusion and notifies the provider immediately, because absent reflexes signal the transition from therapeutic to toxic levels. The nurse does not independently reduce the infusion rate without a provider order but does communicate the trend proactively. *Why it is correct:* The presence of any patellar reflex, even 1+, confirms that the neuromuscular junction is still functioning and the magnesium level has not reached the toxic threshold for respiratory depression. However, the downward trend in reflexes across three assessments warrants heightened monitoring because the next step in the toxicity progression is reflex loss, followed by respiratory depression. This is a nuanced evaluate-outcomes judgment: the finding is not yet alarming but the trajectory requires closer surveillance. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient becomes tearful and states, "I feel like my body is falling apart. I just had a baby — I'm supposed to be happy. What's wrong with me?" The nurse must respond to this emotional distress in the context of a postpartum patient with a serious medical complication. *Correct action:* Acknowledge the patient's feelings with empathic, validating statements — her distress is a normal response to a frightening and unexpected medical complication during what she expected to be a joyful time. Normalize the emotional response without dismissing it: feeling scared, overwhelmed, or sad in this situation does not mean she is failing as a mother or that something is psychologically wrong with her. Provide honest, clear information about her current status — the medical team is closely monitoring her, the lab trends are being tracked, and the treatments are working to protect her. Facilitate the patient's connection with her newborn to the extent safely possible (bringing the infant to the bedside for supervised skin-to-skin if the patient is hemodynamically stable and alert). Assess for additional psychosocial risk: screen for thoughts of self-harm, prior history of depression or anxiety, and adequacy of social support. Document the emotional assessment and communicate it to the provider, as postpartum mood disturbance in the setting of a complicated postpartum course warrants follow-up. Do not prematurely label this as postpartum depression — the emotional response is situationally appropriate — but do ensure that psychosocial follow-up is part of the discharge plan. *Why it is correct:* Postpartum patients experiencing medical complications are at increased risk for postpartum mood disorders, but acute situational distress is not the same as a mood disorder. The nurse's role is to validate, assess, support, and communicate — not to diagnose or dismiss. Facilitating the maternal-infant bond during a medical crisis is a nursing priority that is often overlooked when the focus is on the physiological emergency. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues (emotional distress as a cue requiring assessment), generate solutions (therapeutic communication, psychosocial screening, facilitating bonding). *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Dismissing the headache as a normal postpartum tension headache or attributing it to sleep deprivation and the stress of a new baby. A headache that is unrelieved by appropriate analgesics (ibuprofen and acetaminophen) in a postpartum patient with severe-range blood pressure is a severe feature of preeclampsia until proven otherwise — it is not benign.

- *(Decision 1)* Attributing the right upper quadrant pain to gallbladder disease, gas, or constipation. While these are common postpartum complaints, RUQ and epigastric pain in the setting of elevated transaminases and severe hypertension indicates hepatic involvement from preeclampsia, not a gastrointestinal cause.

- *(Decision 1)* Concluding that preeclampsia is ruled out because the urine protein-to-creatinine ratio is below 0.3. Proteinuria is no longer required for the diagnosis of preeclampsia with severe features; the severe-range blood pressure combined with any end-organ dysfunction (in this case, hepatic, renal, hematologic, and cerebral) is sufficient. Anchoring on the absence of significant proteinuria is a dangerous and outdated misconception.

- *(Decision 1)* Flagging the hemoglobin of 10.8 or the breast engorgement as concerning findings requiring follow-up. The hemoglobin decline from 11.4 to 10.8 is an expected postpartum physiologic change, and breast engorgement in a breastfeeding patient on postpartum day 5 is normal. Highlighting these as abnormal wastes clinical attention and demonstrates failure to distinguish pathologic from physiologic postpartum findings.

- *(Decision 2)* Telling the patient she cannot breastfeed while on magnesium sulfate, or advising her to "pump and dump." Magnesium sulfate is compatible with breastfeeding, and unnecessarily interrupting breastfeeding causes maternal distress, undermines lactation, and is not evidence-based. This error is common and stems from a general assumption that IV medications are unsafe during lactation.

- *(Decision 3)* Monitoring blood pressure alone as the indicator of magnesium sulfate effectiveness while neglecting hourly deep tendon reflex assessment, respiratory rate, and urine output. Blood pressure management is achieved by the antihypertensive (labetalol), not the magnesium sulfate; the magnesium is for seizure prophylaxis, and its therapeutic and toxic effects are tracked through reflexes, respiratory rate, and renal output.

- *(Decision 4)* Being reassured by the patient's symptom improvement (decreasing headache, resolving scotomata, controlled blood pressure) and failing to recognize the worsening laboratory trend as evidence of evolving HELLP syndrome. Symptom improvement and laboratory deterioration can occur simultaneously; the nurse must report the objective data, not just the subjective improvement.

- *(Decision 4)* Interpreting the appearance of schistocytes on peripheral smear as an incidental or unrelated finding. Schistocytes indicate microangiopathic hemolytic anemia — red blood cell fragmentation from endothelial damage — and are a hallmark of the hemolysis component of HELLP syndrome. This is not a benign finding.

- *(Decision 5)* Holding the magnesium sulfate infusion because reflexes have decreased to 1+. Diminished but present reflexes at a magnesium level within the therapeutic range indicate appropriate therapeutic effect, not toxicity. Holding the infusion prematurely removes seizure prophylaxis from a patient who still meets criteria for it. The hold criterion is absent reflexes, not diminished reflexes.

- *(Decision 5)* Conversely, failing to trend, document, and communicate the downward reflex change. While 1+ reflexes with a therapeutic magnesium level do not require holding the infusion, the trajectory from 3+ to 2+ to 1+ across three assessments means the next check could reveal absent reflexes. The nurse must communicate the trend and prepare to act.

- *(Decision 6)* Telling the patient "you're fine, don't worry" or "lots of new moms feel this way" without performing a psychosocial assessment. Minimizing the patient's distress fails to screen for genuine risk and misses an opportunity for therapeutic communication. Conversely, immediately labeling the response as postpartum depression and initiating a psychiatric referral is premature — the distress is situationally appropriate and warrants assessment, not diagnosis.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Distinguish pathologic findings of postpartum preeclampsia with severe features from expected physiologic postpartum changes in a mixed findings passage, including recognition that significant proteinuria is not required for the diagnosis when other severe-feature criteria are met.
- Recognize that preeclampsia can present for the first time after delivery and after discharge, and identify warning signs that warrant immediate evaluation.
- Implement and monitor magnesium sulfate therapy for seizure prophylaxis, including the specific parameters that indicate therapeutic effect versus toxicity and the criteria for holding the infusion.
- Analyze discordant clinical trajectories — simultaneous symptom improvement and laboratory deterioration — to recognize evolving HELLP syndrome and escalate appropriately.
- Provide psychosocially appropriate, scope-consistent support to a postpartum patient experiencing emotional distress during a medical complication, including screening for mood disorder risk without premature labeling.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the blood pressure has been controlled with labetalol, the magnesium sulfate is infusing at therapeutic levels, and the patient's headache and scotomata are improving — but the repeat laboratory data reveal falling platelets (108,000), rising transaminases (AST 156, ALT 138), rising LDH (410), and new schistocytes on peripheral smear, indicating that the disease is progressing toward HELLP syndrome despite symptomatic improvement.

- *Most likely condition:* Postpartum preeclampsia with severe features evolving toward HELLP syndrome. The combination of severe-range postpartum hypertension, refractory headache, visual scotomata, right upper quadrant pain, hyperreflexia with clonus, falling platelets, elevated and rising hepatic transaminases, elevated LDH, rising creatinine, and the new appearance of schistocytes — all occurring on postpartum day 5 in a patient with no antepartum preeclampsia — is unambiguously postpartum preeclampsia with severe features and early HELLP syndrome.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Postpartum migraine or tension headache with incidental lab abnormalities — wrong here: the headache is unresponsive to appropriate analgesics, is accompanied by severe-range hypertension, visual scotomata, hyperreflexia with clonus, and a coherent pattern of end-organ laboratory derangements (falling platelets, rising transaminases, rising creatinine, schistocytes); a primary headache disorder does not produce this constellation. (2) Acute fatty liver of pregnancy (AFLP) — wrong here: AFLP typically presents antepartum with hypoglycemia, coagulopathy (prolonged PT, low fibrinogen), and markedly elevated bilirubin, none of which are present; this patient's glucose is 94, PT and fibrinogen are normal, and bilirubin is 1.0; the laboratory pattern of falling platelets with schistocytes and moderately elevated transaminases fits HELLP, not AFLP.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Continue magnesium sulfate infusion for seizure prophylaxis with hourly monitoring of deep tendon reflexes, respiratory rate, level of consciousness, and urine output — the patient remains at risk for eclamptic seizure, and the magnesium is the primary protective intervention. (2) Notify the provider immediately of the worsening laboratory trend (falling platelets, rising transaminases, rising LDH, new schistocytes) as a change in condition indicating disease progression toward HELLP syndrome, so the care plan can be escalated — this includes the provider's stated threshold of platelets below 100,000 as a trigger for immediate notification.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Discontinuing or reducing the magnesium sulfate because the patient's headache and blood pressure have improved — wrong: the magnesium is for seizure prophylaxis, not blood pressure control, and the patient still meets criteria for seizure risk; stopping it based on symptom improvement ignores the worsening laboratory trajectory and removes a critical safety intervention. (2) Delaying provider notification of the worsening labs because the patient "looks better" and the next scheduled lab draw is in six hours — wrong priority: the falling platelets, rising transaminases, and new schistocytes represent objective disease progression that requires immediate communication regardless of the patient's subjective improvement; waiting for the next scheduled draw risks missing the window for intervention if the trajectory accelerates.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Platelet count trend — the platelet trajectory is the most direct indicator of whether the HELLP process is progressing (continued decline) or stabilizing (nadir followed by uptrend), and crossing below 100,000 triggers escalation per the provider's stated plan. (2) Deep tendon reflexes — reflex assessment serves a dual purpose at this synthesis moment: the presence of reflexes confirms that magnesium is not reaching toxic levels, and the character of the reflexes (hyperactive vs. normal vs. diminished) tracks both the disease activity (hyperreflexia from preeclampsia) and the treatment effect (magnesium-induced reflex suppression).

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Lochia character and volume — the lochia is serosa and scant, consistent with normal postpartum day 5, and is unrelated to the preeclampsia/HELLP process; monitoring it does not inform whether the patient is improving or worsening from the hypertensive disease. (2) Hemoglobin level — while hemolysis is a component of HELLP, the hemoglobin at 10.8 reflects the expected postpartum decline and has not dropped significantly; at this synthesis moment, the schistocytes and LDH are more sensitive and specific markers of the hemolytic process than the hemoglobin, which lags behind and is confounded by the normal postpartum blood loss.
