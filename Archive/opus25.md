**CASE TITLE**
Tuberculosis rule-out in a newly admitted patient with chronic cough and immunosuppression.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 47-year-old man is admitted to the medical unit from the emergency department for evaluation of a persistent productive cough, unintentional weight loss, and night sweats. He has a history of rheumatoid arthritis managed with methotrexate 15 mg oral weekly and prednisone 7.5 mg oral daily for the past two years, hypertension managed with amlodipine 5 mg oral daily, and gastroesophageal reflux managed with omeprazole 20 mg oral daily. He works as a long-haul bus driver and reports frequent travel through the southwestern United States and northern Mexico over the past year. He has no history of prior tuberculin skin testing or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA). He has no known HIV diagnosis; an HIV screening test is ordered as part of the admission workup. He is a former smoker. He lives alone in an apartment. He reports no known tuberculosis contacts but states that several of his regular passengers are migrant agricultural workers and that the buses are often crowded with limited ventilation. His immunizations are up to date except that he has never received a BCG vaccine. The admitting provider orders airborne infection isolation precautions, three sputum specimens for acid-fast bacilli (AFB) smear and mycobacterial culture (to be collected 8–24 hours apart, with at least one early-morning specimen), a chest radiograph, a QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus (IGRA) blood test, a complete metabolic panel, a complete blood count, an HIV antigen/antibody combination test, and a sedimentation rate. The provider also places a dietary consult for high-calorie, high-protein supplementation given the weight loss.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient appears fatigued and thin. He reports that the cough began approximately six weeks ago, initially dry but now producing small amounts of yellowish sputum, occasionally streaked with blood. He describes drenching night sweats three to four times per week, a 7 kg (15 lb) weight loss over the past two months despite no change in appetite, and progressive fatigue. He denies dyspnea at rest but reports mild shortness of breath with exertion. He states he saw a walk-in clinic provider three weeks ago and was prescribed a 10-day course of amoxicillin for "bronchitis," which did not improve his symptoms.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 38.3 °C (101.0 °F), HR 92, BP 118/74, RR 20, SpO₂ 95% on room air. Height 178 cm, weight 64 kg. BMI 20.2. The patient is alert, oriented, and cooperative. He appears cachectic with temporal wasting. Skin is warm and dry. Cervical and supraclavicular lymph nodes are palpable bilaterally — small, firm, non-tender. Chest inspection reveals reduced expansion on the right upper lobe region. Percussion is dull over the right upper posterior chest. Auscultation reveals bronchial breath sounds and coarse crackles over the right upper lobe; the remainder of the lung fields are clear. Heart sounds regular. The patient is placed in a negative-pressure airborne infection isolation room (AIIR). The nurse verifies that the room's negative-pressure indicator is functioning, the door is kept closed, and the patient is instructed to remain in the room. The nurse dons an N95 respirator (fit-tested within the past year per facility policy) before entering the room.

**LABORATORY DATA**
CBC: WBC 11,200/µL, hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL, hematocrit 34.2%, platelets 410,000/µL. CMP: sodium 136 mEq/L, potassium 4.1 mEq/L, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, AST 42 U/L, ALT 48 U/L (mildly elevated; baseline ALT 6 months ago was 35 U/L). ESR 68 mm/hr. HIV antigen/antibody combination test: pending. QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus: pending. Chest radiograph: right upper lobe infiltrate with a 2.3 cm cavitary lesion; no pleural effusion; no hilar lymphadenopathy.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Admission evening through the first 16 hours. The nurse collects the first sputum specimen at 2100, coaching the patient on the proper technique: rinse the mouth with water first (no toothpaste), take several deep breaths, and produce a deep cough into the sterile container. The second specimen is collected at 0530 the following morning (early-morning specimen). The patient's overnight course is notable for drenching night sweats and a temperature spike to 38.9 °C. The nurse documents strict adherence to airborne precautions: AIIR door closed, negative-pressure verified, staff wear N95 respirators, and the patient wears a surgical/procedure mask during the brief transport to radiology to protect others from his exhaled droplets. A nursing assistant asks the nurse whether she can enter the room wearing a standard surgical mask "just to take vitals quickly." The QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus result returns: positive. The HIV test returns: negative.

**Stage 2** — Hours 16–36 after admission. The third sputum specimen is collected at 1400 (more than 8 hours after the second). The AFB smear results from the first two specimens return positive. A nucleic acid amplification test (NAAT) is reflexively performed and returns positive for Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex. The infectious disease service is consulted. The provider initiates standard four-drug therapy: isoniazid (INH), rifampin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol. Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) is ordered concurrently with INH. The provider also orders baseline visual acuity and red-green color discrimination testing before the first dose of ethambutol. The rheumatology service recommends holding methotrexate due to immunosuppressive and hepatotoxic overlap, and writes a tapering schedule for the prednisone. The local public health department has been notified, and a public health nurse contacts the facility to begin the contact investigation.

**Stage 3** — Hours 36–60 after admission. The patient has received three doses of the four-drug regimen. He reports nausea. Vitals: T 37.8 °C. Repeat labs drawn at 48 hours show AST 58 U/L and ALT 72 U/L (rising). The third AFB smear result returns: positive. The patient asks the nurse whether he is "contagious forever" and whether his apartment neighbors are at risk. He expresses guilt about potentially exposing others and feels hopeless about his financial situation and health.

**Stage 4** — Day 5 of hospitalization (approximately 108 hours after admission). The patient is tolerating meals better. Cough is less frequent. Repeat labs: AST 86 U/L, ALT 104 U/L, total bilirubin 1.8 mg/dL. The ALT is now more than twice the admission baseline. The infectious disease provider states that if the next ALT exceeds 120 (3× ULN) with any hepatotoxicity symptoms, the hepatotoxic agents will be held. The nurse coordinates discharge planning, distinguishing three distinct concepts clearly:
1. Diagnostic sputum collection: Three specimens spaced 8–24 hours apart, with at least one early-morning specimen (already completed).
2. Discontinuing inpatient airborne precautions: This generally requires infectious TB being deemed unlikely, or appropriate negative AFB smear criteria/clinical improvement on effective therapy.
3. Discharge home before smear conversion: This may be possible only if the patient is medically stable, adhering to directly observed therapy (DOT), and public-health/home-safety criteria are met (e.g., no high-risk individuals in the home). Three negative smears are not automatically required for discharge home, nor does a positive smear mean the patient must remain hospitalized indefinitely if outpatient isolation is safe.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, a nursing assistant asks whether she can enter the AIIR wearing a standard surgical mask instead of an N95 respirator. *Correct action:* The nurse must instruct the nursing assistant that a standard surgical mask is not acceptable for entering the room. Every person entering the AIIR must wear a fit-tested N95 respirator. *Why:* Surgical masks are designed to protect against large droplets; they do not filter airborne droplet nuclei and do not protect staff from inhaling TB. The patient wears a surgical/procedure mask during transport as source control to reduce respiratory secretion dispersal while outside the AIIR, but staff entering the patient's room require N95 protection. Duration of exposure does not change this requirement. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient asks whether his coworker needs to be tested for TB. *Correct action:* Inform the patient that close contacts will be evaluated through a formal public health contact investigation. Explain the process, but do not independently determine who needs testing. *Why:* Contact investigation is a public health function based on duration, proximity, and ventilation. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, before administering ethambutol, the nurse must identify the essential baseline assessment. *Correct action:* Ensure baseline visual acuity and red-green color discrimination testing are documented, and educate the patient to report visual changes. *Why:* Ethambutol's dose-limiting toxicity is optic neuritis. Baseline testing is essential to detect drug-induced changes safely. *Skill:* Take action, recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the prednisone is tapered rather than abruptly stopped. *Correct action:* Recognize that the taper prevents adrenal insufficiency (adrenal crisis) after chronic use. *Why:* Chronic exogenous corticosteroid use suppresses the HPA axis; abrupt discontinuation is physiologically dangerous. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 4, ALT is 104 (2.6× ULN) with mild symptoms. The protocol threshold to hold drugs is ALT > 3× ULN with symptoms. *Correct action:* Continue the medications as ordered, monitor closely, and repeat labs in 24 hours. Do not independently hold the TB medications yet because the threshold has not been crossed. *Why:* Mild transaminase elevation is common early in TB treatment. Holding drugs prematurely risks subtherapeutic levels and resistance, but the nurse must be prepared to act if the threshold is crossed. *Skill:* Recognize cues, evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 4.

6. *Situation:* Across Stages 2–4, the patient exhibits progressive psychosocial distress, anxiety about isolation, and guilt. *Correct action:* Perform a structured psychosocial assessment, provide accurate information about the treatment timeline (with effective treatment, most patients become non-infectious within weeks), facilitate social work involvement for financial resources, and assess barriers to treatment adherence. *Why:* Psychosocial distress is a major driver of treatment nonadherence in TB. Addressing it is integral to treatment success and infection control. *Skill:* Recognize cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Allowing staff to use surgical masks in the AIIR, falsely believing it protects the wearer from airborne TB particles. Staff need N95s; patients wear surgical masks for transport to protect others.
- *(Decision 2)* Independently reassuring the patient that his coworker does not need testing, bypassing the public health contact investigation.
- *(Decision 3)* Skipping baseline visual testing before ethambutol administration.
- *(Decision 4)* Abruptly stopping prednisone along with the methotrexate.
- *(Decision 5)* Independently holding hepatotoxic TB drugs before the objective protocol threshold is met, or ignoring an upward transaminase trend entirely.
- *(Discharge Planning)* Teaching "three negative smears equals automatic discharge" or that a "positive smear means you must stay in the hospital." Discharge home with a positive smear is often safe if public health criteria for outpatient isolation are met.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Apply airborne transmission-based precautions correctly, distinguishing the use of N95 respirators for staff protection versus surgical masks for patient source control during transport.
- Clarify the distinctions between diagnostic sputum collection, discontinuing inpatient airborne isolation, and criteria for safe discharge home.
- Recognize essential baseline assessments required before initiating specific TB medications (ethambutol and visual acuity).
- Apply a hepatotoxicity monitoring protocol precisely.
- Integrate psychosocial assessment and public health coordination into the care of a patient with active TB.
