**CASE TITLE**
Celiac disease with dermatitis herpetiformis presenting as chronic malabsorption.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 28-year-old woman is seen in an outpatient gastroenterology clinic. She has no significant past medical history and takes no prescription medications. She reports occasional use of over-the-counter ibuprofen for headaches. She has no known drug allergies. She works as a graphic designer, lives with her partner, and describes her diet as varied with no prior dietary restrictions. She has no family history of inflammatory bowel disease, but her maternal aunt was diagnosed with "a thyroid problem" requiring lifelong medication. She was referred by her primary care provider after six months of progressive gastrointestinal symptoms and unintentional weight loss.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient presents to the GI clinic reporting six months of frequent, foul-smelling, pale, bulky stools that are difficult to flush, occurring three to five times daily. She describes persistent abdominal bloating and cramping, worse after meals. She has lost ten pounds unintentionally over this period despite no change in appetite. She also reports fatigue, occasional mouth sores, and a pruritic blistering rash on her elbows and knees that has been present for approximately three months. She has tried over-the-counter hydrocortisone cream on the rash without improvement. She denies bloody stools, fever, joint swelling, or recent travel.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Height 5 feet 6 inches, weight 118 pounds (down from 128 pounds six months ago per patient report). BMI 19.0. T 36.8 °C, HR 82, BP 110/68, RR 16, SpO₂ 99% on room air. General appearance: thin-appearing woman in no acute distress. Oral mucosa shows two small aphthous ulcers on the buccal mucosa. Abdomen is soft, mildly distended, with diffuse mild tenderness to palpation without guarding or rebound; bowel sounds are hyperactive. No hepatosplenomegaly. Skin examination reveals clusters of small, intensely erythematous papulovesicular lesions distributed symmetrically on the extensor surfaces of both elbows and both knees; several lesions are excoriated from scratching; no lesions on the trunk, face, or scalp. Nails show mild koilonychia (spooning) bilaterally. Conjunctivae are pale. No peripheral edema. No lymphadenopathy. Neurological examination is grossly intact; deep tendon reflexes are 2+ and symmetric.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn at the initial clinic visit: CBC shows WBC 6,200/µL, hemoglobin 10.4 g/dL (reference 12.0–16.0), MCV 74 fL (reference 80–100), platelets 310,000/µL. CMP shows sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 3.9 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, CO₂ 24 mEq/L, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.7 mg/dL, glucose 88 mg/dL, calcium 7.6 mg/dL (reference 8.5–10.5), albumin 3.3 g/dL (reference 3.5–5.0), ionized calcium low, total protein 6.0 g/dL, AST 22 U/L, ALT 28 U/L, alkaline phosphatase 142 U/L (reference 44–147). Total IgA level 245 mg/dL (reference 70–400; ordered to rule out IgA deficiency before interpreting tTG-IgA). Tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) is pending at the time of the initial visit. ESR 12 mm/hr. CRP 0.4 mg/dL. Stool studies: fecal fat qualitative is positive for excess fat globules. Stool occult blood is negative. No ova or parasites identified.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Two weeks after the initial visit, the patient returns for follow-up. She reports her symptoms are unchanged. The tTG-IgA result has returned at 128 U/mL (reference < 4 U/mL; markedly elevated). The total IgA was normal at 245 mg/dL, confirming the tTG-IgA is interpretable and not a false negative from IgA deficiency. An esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD) with duodenal biopsy was performed three days prior. The pathology report shows villous atrophy with crypt hyperplasia and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (Marsh type 3a). The gastroenterologist confirms the diagnosis of celiac disease. The provider discusses the need for a strict lifelong gluten-free diet as the primary treatment. Additional labs are ordered to evaluate for malabsorption-related nutritional deficiencies: serum iron 28 µg/dL (reference 60–170), ferritin 8 ng/mL (reference 12–150), folate 3.2 ng/mL (reference > 5.4), vitamin B12 280 pg/mL (reference 200–900), 25-hydroxyvitamin D 14 ng/mL (reference 30–100), and intact PTH 78 pg/mL (reference 15–65). The rash on her elbows and knees is discussed; the gastroenterologist notes its distribution and morphology are classic for dermatitis herpetiformis and recommends a perilesional skin biopsy for definitive confirmation. A referral to dermatology is placed. The nurse is asked to begin patient education on the gluten-free diet and to coordinate the dermatology referral and nutritional supplementation plan.

**Stage 2** — Four weeks after diagnosis (six weeks from initial visit). The patient has been attempting a gluten-free diet for four weeks. She returns to the GI clinic for follow-up. She reports her stool frequency has decreased from three to five times daily to once or twice daily, and the stools are less pale and less malodorous. Bloating has improved but is still present intermittently. She reports the rash has not improved and remains intensely pruritic, disrupting her sleep. She is frustrated and asks whether the diet is working if the rash has not changed. She reports she has been reading labels but admits she did not realize her soy sauce contained wheat, and she has been using it regularly. She also did not know that the communion wafers she receives weekly at church contain gluten. She has been taking the prescribed oral iron sulfate 325 mg daily and vitamin D3 2,000 IU daily as directed, but she has not started the prescribed folate supplement because she "didn't think it was important." The dermatology appointment occurred one week ago; the perilesional skin biopsy showed granular IgA deposits at the dermal papillae on direct immunofluorescence, confirming dermatitis herpetiformis. The dermatologist prescribed dapsone 25 mg daily for symptomatic relief of the rash, with a plan to titrate as needed. Before starting dapsone, dermatology confirmed a normal G6PD level and reviewed baseline CBC with differential, liver function tests, renal function tests, and urinalysis. The nurse teaches the patient that frequent blood work is needed during early therapy and to report cyanosis, shortness of breath, marked fatigue, jaundice, fever, sore throat, or widespread rash. A CBC was drawn at today's visit: hemoglobin 10.8 g/dL, MCV 76 fL. Repeat calcium 7.8 mg/dL. Repeat 25-hydroxyvitamin D 18 ng/mL. Vital signs: T 36.9 °C, HR 78, BP 112/70, RR 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Weight 119 pounds.

**Stage 3** — Four months after diagnosis (approximately five months from initial visit). The patient returns for scheduled follow-up. She reports she identified and eliminated the hidden gluten sources (soy sauce, communion wafers) after the education session at Stage 2, and she has been strictly gluten-free for the past three months. She switched to tamari (wheat-free soy sauce) and gluten-free communion wafers. She is taking all prescribed supplements: iron sulfate 325 mg daily, folic acid 1 mg daily, and vitamin D3 2,000 IU daily. She has been on dapsone 50 mg daily (titrated up from 25 mg by dermatology) for three months. She reports the rash has nearly resolved — only faint post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation remains on her elbows, with no active vesicles or pruritus. Her stools are now formed, brown, once daily, and no longer malodorous. Bloating has resolved. She feels more energetic and her mouth sores have not recurred. She has gained four pounds. Vital signs: T 36.7 °C, HR 74, BP 114/72, RR 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Weight 123 pounds. Repeat labs: hemoglobin 12.1 g/dL, MCV 82 fL, ferritin 22 ng/mL, folate 8.6 ng/mL, 25-hydroxyvitamin D 32 ng/mL, calcium 8.6 mg/dL, intact PTH 42 pg/mL, albumin 3.8 g/dL. Repeat tTG-IgA is 38 U/mL (down from 128 U/mL at diagnosis; reference < 4 U/mL). The gastroenterologist notes the tTG-IgA is trending down but has not yet normalized, which is expected — tTG-IgA often declines gradually after starting a strict gluten-free diet; normalization may take 6–12 months or longer, especially with high baseline titers. A falling titer at four months, together with clinical and nutritional improvement, supports response rather than treatment failure. Given the history of low vitamin D and mildly elevated PTH at diagnosis, the provider orders a dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) scan to assess bone mineral density. The dermatologist plans to begin tapering dapsone now that the rash is controlled, with the goal of discontinuation once the gluten-free diet alone maintains remission. The nurse reviews ongoing dietary adherence strategies and discusses the importance of long-term follow-up.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the initial presentation, the nurse reviews the patient's history and physical examination findings. The patient has steatorrhea, unintentional weight loss, abdominal bloating, aphthous ulcers, microcytic anemia with koilonychia, low calcium, low albumin, and a symmetrical papulovesicular rash on the extensor surfaces of the elbows and knees. The nurse must identify which findings cluster together as a recognizable clinical pattern. *Correct action:* Recognize that the combination of steatorrhea (foul-smelling, pale, bulky stools with positive fecal fat), unintentional weight loss, microcytic anemia with koilonychia (iron-deficiency pattern), low calcium, low albumin, and aphthous ulcers constitutes a fat-malabsorption syndrome, and that the symmetrical papulovesicular rash on extensor surfaces is the classic distribution and morphology of dermatitis herpetiformis — a cutaneous manifestation highly specific to celiac disease. The pale conjunctivae and koilonychia are consistent with iron deficiency from proximal small-bowel malabsorption. The normal ESR, normal CRP, and negative stool occult blood are findings that do not support an inflammatory or hemorrhagic process. *Why it is correct:* Celiac disease causes villous atrophy in the proximal small intestine, impairing absorption of iron, folate, calcium, fat-soluble vitamins, and fat. The resulting steatorrhea, iron-deficiency anemia, hypocalcemia, and hypoalbuminemia form a coherent malabsorption cluster. Dermatitis herpetiformis occurs in approximately 15–25% of celiac patients and presents as grouped papulovesicles on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees, buttocks, scalp); its presence in this distribution is a strong clinical cue pointing specifically to celiac disease rather than other causes of malabsorption. The normal inflammatory markers and absence of bloody stool argue against inflammatory bowel disease. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Initial presentation and assessment findings.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the tTG-IgA returns at 128 U/mL (markedly elevated, reference < 4), the total IgA is normal at 245 mg/dL, and the duodenal biopsy shows Marsh 3a villous atrophy with crypt hyperplasia and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes. The nutritional labs reveal iron 28 µg/dL, ferritin 8 ng/mL, folate 3.2 ng/mL, vitamin D 14 ng/mL, and PTH 78 pg/mL. The nurse must interpret these results in the context of the clinical picture. *Correct action:* Interpret the markedly elevated tTG-IgA combined with characteristic duodenal histology as confirmation of celiac disease. Recognize that the total IgA was checked and is normal, which validates the tTG-IgA result — if total IgA had been deficient, the tTG-IgA could have been falsely negative, and alternative serologic testing (such as deamidated gliadin peptide IgG or tTG-IgG) would have been needed. Interpret the nutritional labs as showing iron deficiency (low iron, low ferritin consistent with the microcytic anemia), folate deficiency, and vitamin D deficiency with secondary hyperparathyroidism (elevated PTH is the body's compensatory response to low calcium and low vitamin D, driving calcium resorption from bone). The B12 at 280 pg/mL is within normal limits, which is expected because B12 is absorbed in the terminal ileum, which is typically spared in celiac disease. *Why it is correct:* tTG-IgA is the first-line serologic test for celiac disease, but it is an IgA-based assay; patients with selective IgA deficiency (which occurs at higher rates in celiac disease than in the general population) will have a falsely negative tTG-IgA. Checking total IgA level before interpreting the tTG-IgA is essential to avoid missing the diagnosis. The biopsy findings of villous atrophy with crypt hyperplasia and intraepithelial lymphocytosis are the histologic hallmark. The pattern of deficiencies — iron, folate, calcium, vitamin D — reflects damage to the proximal duodenum and jejunum where these nutrients are absorbed. The elevated PTH in the setting of low vitamin D and low calcium indicates secondary hyperparathyroidism, which places the patient at risk for decreased bone mineral density over time. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse considers the differential diagnosis. The patient has chronic diarrhea, weight loss, and abdominal symptoms that could suggest inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn disease or ulcerative colitis) or irritable bowel syndrome. She also has a rash that could be interpreted as contact dermatitis or eczema. The nurse must determine which condition best explains the full clinical picture. *Correct action:* Prioritize celiac disease as the primary diagnosis based on the markedly elevated tTG-IgA, the confirmatory duodenal biopsy showing villous atrophy, and the pattern of proximal small-bowel malabsorption deficiencies. Recognize that the rash is dermatitis herpetiformis — a celiac-specific cutaneous manifestation — rather than contact dermatitis. Contact dermatitis would not present as symmetrical grouped papulovesicles specifically on extensor surfaces, would not be refractory to topical corticosteroids in this pattern, and would not co-occur with serologically and histologically confirmed celiac disease. Inflammatory bowel disease is less likely given the normal ESR, normal CRP, negative stool occult blood, and the specific serologic and histologic findings confirming celiac disease. Irritable bowel syndrome does not cause malabsorption, weight loss, anemia, or villous atrophy. *Why it is correct:* The serologic and histologic evidence is definitive for celiac disease. Dermatitis herpetiformis is diagnosed by perilesional skin biopsy showing granular IgA deposits at the dermal papillae on direct immunofluorescence — not by gut biopsy, which confirms the intestinal component but does not diagnose the skin manifestation. The rash's distribution (extensor elbows and knees), morphology (papulovesicular), and intense pruritus are classic for DH and are not characteristic of contact dermatitis, which typically follows an exposure pattern and responds to topical steroids. The referral for perilesional skin biopsy is the appropriate confirmatory step for the rash. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient has been on a gluten-free diet for four weeks with partial GI improvement but no improvement in the rash. She reveals ongoing inadvertent gluten exposure from soy sauce and communion wafers. She has not started her prescribed folate supplement. The perilesional skin biopsy has confirmed dermatitis herpetiformis, and dapsone has been prescribed. The nurse must determine the appropriate plan of care. *Correct action:* Identify the hidden gluten sources (soy sauce containing wheat, communion wafers) as the reason for incomplete response and persistent rash, and provide targeted education on these specific sources. Emphasize that a strict gluten-free diet is the sole definitive treatment for both the GI manifestations and dermatitis herpetiformis, and that even small amounts of gluten can perpetuate villous atrophy and the rash. Educate the patient that dapsone provides symptomatic relief of the DH rash (often within days) but does not treat the underlying intestinal disease — the gluten-free diet is what treats both. Reinforce the importance of taking all prescribed supplements, including the folate, and explain that folate deficiency from malabsorption contributes to anemia and that untreated deficiency has health consequences. Provide practical guidance: suggest tamari or wheat-free soy sauce as an alternative, advise her to request gluten-free communion wafers from her church or speak with her clergy, teach systematic label-reading for hidden gluten in processed foods, and discuss cross-contamination risks (shared cutting boards, toasters, cooking surfaces). Advise that medications and supplements themselves can contain gluten as an inactive ingredient and should be verified with the pharmacist. *Why it is correct:* The gluten-free diet is the only treatment that addresses the root cause of celiac disease — the immune-mediated response to gluten that drives villous atrophy, malabsorption, and dermatitis herpetiformis. Partial improvement in GI symptoms with persistent rash and ongoing inadvertent gluten exposure is a common clinical scenario; the rash is often the last manifestation to resolve and is exquisitely sensitive to even trace gluten. Dapsone suppresses the neutrophilic inflammation in DH and provides rapid symptomatic relief, but it does not heal the gut or alter the disease course. Folate supplementation is important for correcting the documented deficiency and supporting red blood cell production, especially because dapsone can cause dose-dependent hemolysis. Patient education on hidden gluten sources is a core nursing intervention in celiac disease management. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse is conducting the education session. The patient asks specific questions: "Can I eat oats?" "Is the rash going to go away on its own now that I'm on the diet?" "Do I really need to take the folate — I'm already taking iron and vitamin D?" The nurse must provide accurate, actionable teaching. *Correct action:* Regarding oats: explain that pure, uncontaminated oats are tolerated by most people with celiac disease, but commercially available oats are frequently cross-contaminated with wheat during growing, harvesting, or processing; advise her to use only oats labeled "gluten-free" and certified by a recognized program, and to introduce them cautiously after the gut has had time to heal on a strict gluten-free diet. Regarding the rash: explain that dermatitis herpetiformis will resolve with strict adherence to the gluten-free diet, but resolution of the skin manifestation typically takes months to years — much longer than the GI symptoms — which is why dapsone is prescribed for symptomatic relief in the interim. The goal is to eventually taper and discontinue dapsone once the diet alone controls the rash. Regarding folate: explain that her folate level was low at 3.2 ng/mL due to malabsorption, that folate is essential for red blood cell production and DNA synthesis, and that correcting this deficiency is part of treating her anemia. Additionally, since she is now taking dapsone, which can cause dose-dependent hemolysis, maintaining adequate folate stores supports her bone marrow's ability to compensate. All three supplements — iron, vitamin D, and folate — address documented deficiencies caused by her celiac disease and are not optional. *Why it is correct:* Effective patient education requires addressing the patient's specific questions with accurate, individualized information. The oat question is common and the answer is nuanced — blanket prohibition is inaccurate, but unrestricted consumption is unsafe due to contamination risk. The timeline for DH resolution is important for managing expectations and adherence; patients who expect rapid skin clearing may abandon the diet prematurely if they do not understand that the rash lags behind GI improvement. The folate question is an opportunity to connect the supplement to both the underlying deficiency and the safety monitoring for dapsone therapy. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient has been strictly gluten-free for three months after eliminating hidden sources. Her GI symptoms have resolved, the DH rash is nearly cleared, she has gained weight, and her nutritional labs have improved. The repeat tTG-IgA is 38 U/mL, down from 128 U/mL but still above the reference range of less than 4 U/mL. A DEXA scan has been ordered. The nurse must evaluate whether the patient's trajectory indicates successful treatment response or inadequate progress. *Correct action:* Evaluate the overall trajectory as indicating a successful response to the gluten-free diet. The resolution of steatorrhea, normalization of stool character, resolution of bloating, weight gain of four pounds, clearing of the DH rash, resolution of aphthous ulcers, and improvement in energy all indicate clinical response. The hemoglobin has risen from 10.4 to 12.1 g/dL with MCV normalizing from 74 to 82 fL, indicating correction of iron-deficiency anemia. Ferritin has risen from 8 to 22 ng/mL, folate from 3.2 to 8.6 ng/mL, vitamin D from 14 to 32 ng/mL, calcium from 7.6 to 8.6 mg/dL, PTH has normalized from 78 to 42 pg/mL, and albumin has improved from 3.3 to 3.8 g/dL — all consistent with recovering absorptive function. The tTG-IgA declining from 128 to 38 U/mL is an expected serologic response. Normalization may take 6–12 months or longer, especially with high baseline titers, so a persistently elevated but declining tTG-IgA at four months does not by itself indicate treatment failure. The DEXA scan is appropriate given the history of vitamin D deficiency, secondary hyperparathyroidism, and hypocalcemia, which place her at risk for reduced bone mineral density. Reinforce the need for continued strict dietary adherence, ongoing supplementation until deficiencies are fully corrected, and long-term follow-up with periodic tTG-IgA monitoring to confirm eventual normalization and detect any future dietary lapses. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes in celiac disease requires integrating clinical symptom resolution, nutritional lab trends, and serologic trajectory. A common error is interpreting a still-elevated tTG-IgA at four months as evidence of ongoing gluten exposure or treatment failure, when in fact the antibody titer declines gradually and may take six to twelve months to normalize. The clinical and laboratory improvements support effective response to the gluten-free diet and recovering absorptive function. The DEXA scan addresses the bone health risk created by the period of vitamin D deficiency and secondary hyperparathyroidism — even though these values are now improving, bone density may have been affected during the months of undiagnosed malabsorption. Long-term follow-up is essential because celiac disease is a lifelong condition requiring permanent dietary modification. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *When it becomes answerable:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Failing to recognize the rash as dermatitis herpetiformis and instead attributing it to contact dermatitis or eczema. Contact dermatitis does not present as symmetrical grouped papulovesicles specifically on extensor surfaces, and eczema typically involves flexural surfaces. Misidentifying the rash delays recognition of the celiac-specific cluster and may lead to inappropriate treatment with topical steroids alone, which are ineffective for DH.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Dismissing the normal ESR and CRP as unimportant rather than recognizing them as findings that help distinguish celiac disease from inflammatory bowel disease. These normal inflammatory markers are clinically meaningful in narrowing the differential — they are not simply irrelevant background data.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Interpreting the tTG-IgA result without checking whether the total IgA level is normal. If the nurse does not understand why total IgA was ordered, she may not recognize that IgA deficiency — which is more prevalent in celiac patients than in the general population — would render the tTG-IgA falsely negative. This is a critical analytical step: the validity of the serologic test depends on the patient's ability to produce IgA.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Expecting the vitamin B12 to be low because "all vitamins are malabsorbed in celiac disease." B12 is absorbed in the terminal ileum, which is typically unaffected in celiac disease; the proximal small bowel (duodenum and jejunum) is the site of villous atrophy. Misunderstanding the anatomic specificity of malabsorption leads to incorrect analysis of the lab pattern.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Believing that the duodenal biopsy confirms dermatitis herpetiformis. The gut biopsy confirms the intestinal component of celiac disease (villous atrophy), but DH is definitively diagnosed by perilesional skin biopsy showing granular IgA deposits at the dermal papillae on direct immunofluorescence. Confusing the two diagnostic procedures reflects a misunderstanding of the distinct pathologic processes in the gut versus the skin.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Telling the patient that since her GI symptoms are improving, the remaining gluten exposure from soy sauce and communion wafers is "not enough to matter." Any amount of gluten perpetuates the immune response, maintains villous atrophy, and sustains the DH rash. There is no safe threshold of gluten intake in celiac disease. Minimizing the importance of trace gluten sources undermines the treatment plan.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Advising the patient to stop dapsone because the gluten-free diet should treat the rash, without explaining that DH resolution from diet alone takes months to years. Dapsone provides necessary symptomatic relief during the prolonged period before the diet fully controls the cutaneous manifestation. Premature discontinuation leaves the patient with uncontrolled pruritus and sleep disruption.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Telling the patient that all oats must be permanently avoided. This is inaccurate — pure, uncontaminated, certified gluten-free oats are tolerated by the majority of celiac patients. A blanket prohibition is unnecessarily restrictive and may reduce dietary variety and adherence.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Interpreting the tTG-IgA of 38 U/mL at four months as evidence that the patient is not adhering to the diet or that treatment is failing, and escalating concern or recommending repeat biopsy prematurely. The expected serologic trajectory is a gradual decline over six to twelve months; the clinical and nutritional improvements confirm the diet is working. Premature alarm may undermine the patient's confidence and adherence.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Failing to recognize the significance of the DEXA scan order. The patient had documented vitamin D deficiency with secondary hyperparathyroidism and hypocalcemia — conditions that promote bone resorption. Even though these values are now improving, bone mineral density may have been compromised during the months of undiagnosed malabsorption. A nurse who does not connect the DEXA referral to the prior metabolic bone findings misses an opportunity to educate the patient about long-term bone health and the importance of the scan.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Recognize the clinical cluster of fat malabsorption (steatorrhea, weight loss, nutritional deficiencies) combined with dermatitis herpetiformis as highly suggestive of celiac disease, and distinguish this presentation from inflammatory bowel disease and irritable bowel syndrome.

Interpret celiac serologic testing (tTG-IgA) in the context of total IgA level, understanding that IgA deficiency produces false-negative results, and correlate serologic findings with duodenal biopsy histology to confirm the diagnosis.

Identify the specific nutritional deficiencies caused by proximal small-bowel malabsorption in celiac disease (iron, folate, calcium, vitamin D) and explain why B12 is typically spared, based on the anatomic site of absorption.

Provide comprehensive, individualized patient education on the gluten-free diet, including identification of hidden gluten sources, cross-contamination prevention, the role of dapsone in managing dermatitis herpetiformis, and the rationale for each prescribed nutritional supplement.

Evaluate treatment response in celiac disease by integrating clinical symptom resolution, nutritional laboratory trends, and the expected timeline for tTG-IgA normalization, and recognize the indication for bone density assessment in patients with a history of vitamin D deficiency and secondary hyperparathyroidism.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after the patient has been strictly gluten-free for three months with documented clinical, nutritional, and serologic improvement, and the full trajectory of the disease and its treatment response is visible.

*Most likely condition:* Celiac disease with dermatitis herpetiformis. The markedly elevated tTG-IgA with normal total IgA, duodenal biopsy showing Marsh 3a villous atrophy, perilesional skin biopsy confirming granular IgA deposits, the proximal small-bowel malabsorption pattern (iron, folate, calcium, vitamin D deficiencies with spared B12), and the clinical and serologic response to the gluten-free diet together constitute an unambiguous diagnosis.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Crohn disease — wrong here because the inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) are normal, stool occult blood is negative, the duodenal biopsy shows villous atrophy with intraepithelial lymphocytosis rather than transmural granulomatous inflammation, and the tTG-IgA is markedly elevated, which is specific to celiac disease and not a feature of Crohn disease. (2) Irritable bowel syndrome — wrong here because IBS does not cause malabsorption, weight loss, anemia, hypoalbuminemia, villous atrophy on biopsy, or elevated celiac-specific serology; the objective laboratory and histologic abnormalities exclude a functional diagnosis.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Reinforce strict lifelong gluten-free dietary adherence through ongoing education, including review of hidden gluten sources, cross-contamination prevention, and verification that medications and supplements are gluten-free — this is the sole definitive treatment for both the intestinal disease and dermatitis herpetiformis. (2) Coordinate the DEXA scan referral and educate the patient on its purpose — the history of vitamin D deficiency with secondary hyperparathyroidism and hypocalcemia places her at risk for reduced bone mineral density, and baseline assessment is needed to guide further management.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Interpreting the still-elevated tTG-IgA of 38 U/mL as treatment failure and recommending repeat duodenal biopsy or dietary escalation at this time — wrong because the tTG-IgA is on the expected declining trajectory and normalization takes six to twelve months; the clinical and nutritional improvements confirm adequate response, and premature intervention may undermine patient confidence. (2) Discontinuing nutritional supplements because the lab values have improved into or near the normal range — wrong because the absorptive capacity of the intestinal mucosa is still recovering, the ferritin remains low-normal at 22 ng/mL indicating iron stores are not yet fully replete, and premature discontinuation risks recurrence of deficiencies before mucosal healing is complete.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Serial tTG-IgA levels — the declining titer is a useful objective marker of serologic response and possible gluten exposure, but it does not perfectly prove mucosal healing. Continued decline toward normalization supports sustained dietary response, while a plateau or rise would prompt evaluation for inadvertent gluten exposure. (2) Hemoglobin and ferritin — continued normalization of the iron-deficiency anemia and repletion of iron stores confirm that proximal small-bowel absorptive function is recovering; a stall or decline would prompt investigation for ongoing malabsorption or non-adherence.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) ESR and CRP — these inflammatory markers were normal throughout the case because celiac disease is not primarily an inflammatory-marker-driven process in the way that IBD is; tracking them does not provide meaningful information about celiac disease activity or treatment response. (2) Vitamin B12 level — B12 was normal at diagnosis because it is absorbed in the terminal ileum, which is unaffected in celiac disease; serial monitoring of B12 does not track the relevant malabsorption pattern and would not detect relapse or non-adherence.
