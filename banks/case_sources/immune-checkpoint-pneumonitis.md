**CASE TITLE**
Immune checkpoint inhibitor–related pneumonitis in a patient receiving pembrolizumab for non–small cell lung cancer.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 63-year-old woman with stage IV non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) is receiving pembrolizumab 200 mg IV every three weeks as second-line therapy. She completed her fourth cycle nine days ago. Her oncology history includes two prior cycles of carboplatin/pemetrexed, discontinued for disease progression. She has a remote 30-pack-year smoking history (quit eight years ago), well-controlled hypertension on amlodipine 5 mg daily, and mild osteoarthritis managed with acetaminophen. She has no history of autoimmune disease, no prior radiation therapy to the chest, and no known drug allergies. Her baseline pulmonary function was adequate at treatment initiation, with an SpO₂ of 96% on room air and no supplemental oxygen requirement. She presents to the outpatient infusion clinic for a scheduled nursing assessment between cycles. Her current orders include pembrolizumab per the oncology treatment plan, ondansetron 4 mg IV PRN for nausea, and routine pre-infusion labs.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient reports a new dry cough that started about four days ago and has been gradually worsening. She attributes it to seasonal allergies, noting that her husband has also been sneezing at home. She denies hemoptysis, chest pain, or fever. She says she feels "a little more winded than usual" walking from the parking lot but is not in acute distress. She has not taken any new medications and has had no recent sick contacts other than her husband's allergy symptoms.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 37.1 °C, heart rate 92, blood pressure 134/78, respiratory rate 20, SpO₂ 93% on room air. Lung auscultation reveals faint bilateral inspiratory crackles at the bases; no wheezing, no rhonchi, no stridor. No peripheral edema. No jugular venous distension. Skin is warm and dry with no rash. Oral mucosa is moist and intact. She speaks in full sentences but pauses briefly after longer phrases to take a breath. Capillary refill is less than two seconds. Bowel sounds are present in all four quadrants. She is alert, oriented, and conversational, with no signs of confusion or distress at rest.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn today: WBC 6.8 × 10³/µL, hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL, platelets 188,000/µL, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, glucose 102 mg/dL, ALT 28 U/L, AST 31 U/L, TSH 2.4 mIU/L, CRP 18 mg/L (reference < 5 mg/L), LDH 240 U/L (reference 120–246 U/L). No baseline arterial blood gas is available; pulse oximetry has been the monitoring standard per clinic protocol.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Day of clinic visit (nine days after cycle 4). The nurse completes the assessment documented above. The patient's SpO₂ is 93% on room air, down from her documented baseline of 96%. She has the new dry cough and the bilateral basilar crackles. She minimizes the symptoms, saying she thinks it is just allergies. Her CRP is mildly elevated at 18 mg/L. The oncologist is in clinic and available. No imaging has been obtained yet. The patient asks whether she can just take an antihistamine and come back for her next infusion as scheduled.

**Stage 2** — Two hours later, same clinic day. The oncologist, after being notified by the nurse, has ordered a stat chest CT with contrast and an arterial blood gas. CT results show new bilateral ground-glass opacities, predominantly in the lower lobes, with no pleural effusion, no pulmonary embolism, and no lymphangitic carcinomatosis. ABG on room air: pH 7.44, PaCO₂ 36 mmHg, PaO₂ 68 mmHg. The oncologist reviews the imaging and states the findings are consistent with immune-related pneumonitis. The oncologist enters the following orders: hold pembrolizumab, admit the patient for observation, start supplemental oxygen to maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94%, initiate methylprednisolone per the facility's irAE pneumonitis protocol (the protocol-specified dose and route are written in the order), obtain repeat pulse oximetry every two hours, and consult pulmonology. The patient is anxious and asks whether this means her cancer treatment is being stopped permanently. Repeat vitals: T 37.2 °C, HR 98, BP 130/82, RR 22, SpO₂ 91% on room air (down from 93% two hours earlier).

**Stage 3** — 36 hours after admission. The patient has been receiving methylprednisolone per the protocol order and supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula at 3 L/min. Repeat vitals: T 37.0 °C, HR 86, BP 126/76, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on 3 L nasal cannula. She reports the cough is "about the same but I'm not as short of breath." Lung auscultation: crackles are still present bilaterally but are similar to slightly less prominent than on admission. Repeat ABG on 3 L nasal cannula: pH 7.42, PaCO₂ 38 mmHg, PaO₂ 82 mmHg. CRP has decreased to 11 mg/L. The pulmonology consultant recommends a slow corticosteroid taper over several weeks per the facility protocol and states that pembrolizumab should remain discontinued unless oncology makes a documented risk-benefit decision. The oncologist confirms that the patient will not receive the next pembrolizumab cycle and will be re-evaluated for alternative cancer-treatment planning after recovery, and asks the nurse to provide discharge teaching, including specific symptoms the patient must report immediately if they occur at home during the taper. The patient's husband is present and asks whether she should also stop her blood pressure medication.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient presents with a new dry cough, exertional dyspnea, bilateral basilar crackles, and an SpO₂ of 93% — down from her baseline of 96%. She attributes the cough to seasonal allergies. The nurse must identify which findings from the assessment are clinically significant cues that warrant further evaluation versus findings that are within normal limits or unrelated. *Correct action:* The significant cues are the new dry cough developing nine days after a checkpoint inhibitor cycle, the SpO₂ drop from baseline 96% to 93%, the bilateral basilar crackles on auscultation, the exertional dyspnea (pausing after longer phrases), and the elevated CRP at 18 mg/L. The normal or non-significant findings include the temperature of 37.1 °C (afebrile), the normal capillary refill, the absence of peripheral edema or JVD, the moist oral mucosa, the normal bowel sounds, and the normal renal and hepatic labs. *Why it is correct:* A new respiratory syndrome — cough, crackles, desaturation, and dyspnea — arising within days to weeks of checkpoint inhibitor administration is a recognized pattern for immune-related pneumonitis. The SpO₂ decline of three percentage points from a documented baseline is a measurable change, not a normal variant. The elevated CRP supports an inflammatory process. The absence of fever, edema, and JVD helps narrow the differential but does not negate the respiratory findings. Dismissing the cluster as allergies because the patient offers that explanation would delay recognition. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must determine what the cluster of findings most likely represents and distinguish it from the patient's self-diagnosis of seasonal allergies. *Correct action:* The combination of a new dry cough, bilateral basilar crackles, oxygen desaturation from baseline, exertional dyspnea, and an elevated CRP — occurring nine days after the fourth cycle of pembrolizumab in a patient with no fever, no purulent sputum, and no upper-airway symptoms (rhinorrhea, sneezing, watery eyes) — is most consistent with immune-related pneumonitis. Seasonal allergies would present with upper-airway and conjunctival symptoms, not bilateral basilar crackles and progressive desaturation; a simple upper respiratory infection would not typically produce this pattern of lower-lobe crackles and falling SpO₂ without fever or productive cough. *Why it is correct:* Checkpoint inhibitors can cause T-cell–mediated inflammation in the lungs at any point during therapy, and the risk is cumulative across cycles. The absence of the hallmark features of allergic rhinitis (sneezing, rhinorrhea, itchy/watery eyes) and the presence of lower-respiratory findings (crackles, desaturation) make allergies an inadequate explanation. The timing after cycle 4 fits the typical window. Analyzing these cues together — rather than accepting the patient's attribution — is essential for early identification. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the CT confirms bilateral ground-glass opacities consistent with immune-related pneumonitis, the ABG shows a PaO₂ of 68 mmHg on room air, and the SpO₂ has dropped further to 91%. The oncologist has entered orders to hold pembrolizumab, admit, start oxygen, initiate corticosteroids per protocol, and consult pulmonology. The nurse must determine the most urgent nursing priority among the ordered interventions. *Correct action:* The immediate priority is to apply supplemental oxygen to achieve the ordered target of SpO₂ ≥ 94%, because the patient's oxygenation is actively worsening (SpO₂ 91%, PaO₂ 68 mmHg) and tissue hypoxia is the most immediate physiologic threat. Holding pembrolizumab is essential but is not an acute bedside intervention (the next cycle is not due today). Initiating corticosteroids per protocol is urgent and should follow promptly, but oxygenation takes precedence because it addresses the ABCs. *Why it is correct:* Airway-breathing-circulation prioritization places the immediate oxygenation deficit ahead of pharmacologic treatment. The declining SpO₂ trend (96% baseline → 93% → 91%) indicates active deterioration. Supplemental oxygen is a direct, immediate intervention the nurse can initiate; corticosteroids require preparation and verification of the protocol order. Both are done in close sequence, but oxygen comes first. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient is anxious and asks whether her cancer treatment is being stopped permanently. The nurse must determine the appropriate set of actions to address both the clinical situation and the patient's concern. *Correct action:* The nurse should explain that pembrolizumab is being stopped/held now because of a serious lung inflammation, and that the oncology team will discuss the long-term cancer treatment plan after she is stabilized. The nurse should not promise that pembrolizumab will resume or tell the patient independently that it is permanently discontinued; the nurse can state that severe immune-related pneumonitis often requires the checkpoint inhibitor to remain discontinued and that oncology will review safe alternatives. The nurse should also ensure the ordered corticosteroid is initiated per protocol, verify the oxygen delivery is maintaining the target SpO₂ and set up the every-two-hour pulse oximetry monitoring as ordered. *Why it is correct:* The decision to resume or permanently discontinue a checkpoint inhibitor after an irAE is a provider-level determination that depends on the severity of the event; while Grade 3 pneumonitis typically requires permanent discontinuation, the nurse should not state this independently before the oncologist discusses it with the patient. Providing truthful, scope-appropriate information reduces anxiety without offering false reassurance about resuming the same drug. Simultaneously, the clinical interventions (oxygen, corticosteroids, monitoring) must proceed without delay — the teaching moment does not replace the treatment actions. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse has multiple ordered interventions to carry out: apply supplemental oxygen, initiate methylprednisolone as ordered per protocol, set up every-two-hour pulse oximetry, and communicate with the pulmonology consultant. The nurse must determine the correct sequence of actions. *Correct action:* First, apply supplemental oxygen and confirm SpO₂ rises toward the target of ≥ 94%. Second, initiate methylprednisolone as ordered per protocol (verify dose, route, and rate against the written order). Third, establish the every-two-hour pulse oximetry schedule and document the baseline on-oxygen reading. The pulmonology consult notification can proceed concurrently or immediately after these bedside interventions are underway, as it does not require the nurse's continuous presence. *Why it is correct:* Oxygen addresses the immediate physiologic deficit (ABCs). Corticosteroids are the definitive treatment for the inflammatory process and should begin as soon as oxygenation is secured. Monitoring establishes the surveillance framework. The consult is important but is a communication task that does not compete with the bedside interventions for temporal priority. This sequence reflects a clinically real order: stabilize oxygenation → treat the cause → establish monitoring → coordinate care. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, 36 hours into treatment, the nurse must evaluate whether the patient is improving, stable, or deteriorating. The available data include the repeat vitals (HR 86, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on 3 L), the repeat ABG (PaO₂ 82 mmHg on 3 L), the CRP decrease (18 → 11 mg/L), the patient's subjective report ("not as short of breath"), and the lung exam (crackles still present but similar to slightly less prominent). *Correct action:* The patient is improving. The evidence of improvement includes the rising SpO₂ (91% on room air → 96% on 3 L), the improved PaO₂ (68 → 82 mmHg, now on supplemental oxygen), the decreasing CRP (18 → 11 mg/L), the decreasing heart rate (98 → 86) and respiratory rate (22 → 18), and the patient's subjective improvement in dyspnea. The persistent crackles are consistent with a resolving inflammatory process — full radiographic and auscultatory resolution takes longer than 36 hours and does not indicate treatment failure. The nurse should document the improving trend and continue the current plan. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes requires comparing the current data set against the prior trajectory, not against a single threshold. The directional improvement across multiple parameters (oxygenation, inflammatory marker, vital signs, subjective symptoms) confirms a positive response to corticosteroid therapy. Expecting complete resolution of crackles at 36 hours would be unrealistic and could lead to unnecessary escalation. The fact that the patient still requires supplemental oxygen does not negate improvement — the trend matters. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Accepting the patient's self-diagnosis of seasonal allergies and focusing on the absence of fever as reassurance, thereby dismissing the SpO₂ drop and bilateral crackles as unimportant. This is wrong because immune-related pneumonitis is often afebrile in its early stages, and the respiratory findings (crackles, desaturation, dyspnea) are the critical cues — not the temperature. A patient's own attribution of symptoms does not replace the nurse's clinical assessment.

- *(Decision point 2)* Attributing the bilateral crackles and desaturation to progression of the underlying lung cancer rather than considering immune-related pneumonitis. This is a plausible but incorrect analysis at this point because the CT later shows ground-glass opacities without lymphangitic spread or new masses, and the acute onset over four days following a checkpoint inhibitor cycle fits the irAE timeline. Cancer progression typically presents more insidiously and with different imaging findings. Jumping to a cancer-progression conclusion could delay the critical step of holding the drug and initiating corticosteroids.

- *(Decision point 2)* Concluding that the presentation is a community-acquired pneumonia and expecting antibiotics to be the primary treatment. This is wrong because CAP is less supported by the available data and should not be treated as the sole/primary explanation when the ICI pneumonitis pattern is present — the bilateral ground-glass pattern and the checkpoint inhibitor exposure point to an inflammatory, not infectious, etiology. Treating empirically for infection without considering the irAE would delay corticosteroid initiation.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing corticosteroid administration over supplemental oxygen application. While corticosteroids are essential, the patient's SpO₂ of 91% represents an active oxygenation deficit that must be addressed first per airway-breathing-circulation prioritization. Corticosteroids take time to exert their anti-inflammatory effect; oxygen provides immediate physiologic support.

- *(Decision point 4)* Telling the patient that pembrolizumab has been permanently discontinued and that she will need a completely different cancer treatment. This oversteps the nursing scope — the decision to rechallenge or permanently discontinue is made by the oncologist based on the severity and resolution of the pneumonitis. Providing inaccurate information increases the patient's anxiety unnecessarily and may undermine trust in the care team.

- *(Decision point 5)* Delaying all bedside interventions to page the pulmonology consultant first, reasoning that a specialist should direct the oxygen and corticosteroid plan. This is wrong because the oncologist has already entered the orders; the nurse should implement them without waiting for the consultant. The consult is for ongoing co-management, not for permission to carry out existing orders. Delaying oxygen and corticosteroids while awaiting a callback puts the patient at risk.

- *(Decision point 6)* Interpreting the persistent bilateral crackles at 36 hours as evidence that the corticosteroids are not working and escalating to the provider with a recommendation to change therapy. This is wrong because inflammatory lung findings take days to weeks to resolve fully; the overall trajectory — improved oxygenation, lower CRP, improved vital signs, subjective improvement — confirms a positive response. Premature escalation based on a single persistent finding, without considering the full trend, reflects incomplete outcome evaluation.

- *(Decision point 6)* Discontinuing supplemental oxygen at 36 hours because the SpO₂ reads 96%, without recognizing that the 96% is being achieved on 3 L nasal cannula. The patient's room-air SpO₂ was 91% at Stage 2; removing oxygen without a provider order and a supervised room-air trial could result in rapid desaturation. The nurse should continue the ordered oxygen and discuss weaning with the provider when the trend supports it.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the early cue cluster of checkpoint inhibitor–related pneumonitis (new dry cough, bilateral crackles, progressive oxygen desaturation from baseline, elevated inflammatory markers) and distinguish it from benign explanations such as seasonal allergies or upper respiratory infection.

- Analyze assessment and diagnostic findings to differentiate immune-related pneumonitis from competing explanations including cancer progression and community-acquired pneumonia.

- Prioritize oxygenation as the immediate nursing intervention when a patient with confirmed irAE pneumonitis has a declining SpO₂, ahead of pharmacologic treatment and consultation tasks.

- Evaluate the patient's response to corticosteroid therapy by interpreting trends across multiple parameters (oxygenation, vital signs, inflammatory markers, subjective symptoms) rather than relying on a single finding.

- Identify the specific symptoms the patient must report immediately during the outpatient corticosteroid taper — worsening dyspnea, new or increasing cough, fever, chest pain, or any return of the symptoms that prompted admission — and explain why prompt reporting is essential to detect pneumonitis recurrence or steroid-taper flare before it becomes severe.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the chest CT confirms bilateral ground-glass opacities, the ABG shows PaO₂ 68 mmHg, SpO₂ has declined to 91%, and the oncologist has entered orders to hold pembrolizumab, admit, start oxygen and corticosteroids per protocol, and consult pulmonology.

- *Most likely condition:* Immune checkpoint inhibitor–related pneumonitis. The new dry cough, bilateral basilar crackles, progressive oxygen desaturation (96% → 93% → 91%), bilateral ground-glass opacities on CT without effusion or embolism, PaO₂ of 68 mmHg, and elevated CRP — all developing nine days after the fourth cycle of pembrolizumab — form an unambiguous pattern of drug-induced pulmonary inflammation.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Community-acquired pneumonia — wrong here because CAP is less supported by the available data and should not be treated as the sole/primary explanation when the ICI pneumonitis pattern is present; the CT shows diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities rather than a focal lobar consolidation, and the checkpoint inhibitor exposure and timeline make an inflammatory irAE far more likely than an infectious process. (2) Progression of the underlying NSCLC with lymphangitic carcinomatosis — wrong here because the CT shows no lymphangitic spread, no new masses, and no malignant pleural effusion; the acute onset over four days is inconsistent with the typically more gradual radiographic progression of metastatic disease, and the bilateral ground-glass pattern is characteristic of pneumonitis, not tumor spread.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Apply supplemental oxygen immediately to achieve the ordered target SpO₂ of ≥ 94%, because the patient's oxygenation is actively declining and tissue hypoxia is the most immediate physiologic threat. (2) Initiate methylprednisolone per the facility irAE pneumonitis protocol order, because corticosteroids are the definitive treatment for the immune-mediated inflammation driving the respiratory compromise, and delay worsens outcomes.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Administering the next scheduled pembrolizumab infusion on the grounds that the cancer treatment should not be interrupted — wrong because continuing the checkpoint inhibitor in the setting of active immune-related pneumonitis will worsen the inflammatory process and can be fatal; the drug must be held. (2) Delaying oxygen and corticosteroids to page the pulmonology consultant first and await specialist direction before implementing any intervention — wrong because the oncologist has already entered the orders, the patient's SpO₂ is 91% and falling, and waiting for a consultant callback delays treatment of an actively worsening respiratory status.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Oxygen saturation trend (pulse oximetry every two hours as ordered, compared against the trajectory of 96% → 93% → 91%) — a rising SpO₂ on supplemental oxygen supports improving gas exchange when interpreted with respiratory rate, work of breathing, symptoms, and inflammatory trend. (2) Respiratory rate and work of breathing — a decreasing respiratory rate and reduced dyspnea on exertion confirm that the ventilatory demand is easing, which tracks the overall pulmonary recovery trajectory.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) TSH level — although checkpoint inhibitors can cause immune-related thyroiditis, the TSH is normal at 2.4 mIU/L and thyroid function is not the active problem; monitoring it at this synthesis moment does not evaluate whether the pneumonitis is resolving. (2) Platelet count — platelets are 188,000/µL and within normal limits; they are unrelated to the pulmonary inflammatory process and do not track the response to corticosteroid therapy for pneumonitis.
