**CASE TITLE**
Postpartum hemorrhage from uterine atony in a grand multiparous client with pregnancy-induced hypertension.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 32-year-old woman, gravida 5 para 5, is in the postpartum recovery area of a labor and delivery unit. She delivered a 4.2 kg (9.2 lb) male infant vaginally approximately one hour ago. Her second stage of labor lasted three hours, and oxytocin augmentation was used during the first stage. The placenta was delivered intact and appeared complete on inspection by the delivering provider. Her prenatal history is significant for pregnancy-induced hypertension diagnosed at 34 weeks' gestation, managed with oral labetalol 200 mg twice daily throughout the remainder of the pregnancy. She has no history of bleeding disorders, prior postpartum hemorrhage, or uterine surgery. Her antepartum blood pressure at admission to labor and delivery was 148/94 mmHg. Her admission hemoglobin was 11.8 g/dL and hematocrit 35.2%. A peripheral IV of lactated Ringer's is infusing at 125 mL/hr in the left forearm via an 18-gauge catheter. Oxytocin 20 units in 1 L lactated Ringer's was initiated immediately after placental delivery per standing postpartum order and has been infusing for approximately 50 minutes. She received no other uterotonics. Her blood type is O-positive, and a type and screen was drawn on admission.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The nurse arrives for the one-hour postpartum assessment. The client states she feels "dizzy and wet." She is pale and diaphoretic. A large amount of blood is visible on the underpads beneath her, and the perineal pad placed 20 minutes ago is saturated. She is alert and oriented but anxious, gripping the side rail and asking, "Is this normal?"

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Vital signs: temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 118 beats per minute, blood pressure 90/62 mmHg, respiratory rate 22 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Fundal assessment: the uterus is palpable two fingerbreadths above the umbilicus, deviated to the right, and boggy — it does not firm with gentle massage and immediately relaxes when the nurse's hand is removed. Lochia is heavy, with a steady flow of dark red blood and several small clots expressed during the fundal check. The perineum shows an intact second-degree laceration repair with no active bleeding from the suture line and no visible hematoma. The bladder is palpable above the symphysis pubis. Skin is cool and clammy to the touch, with capillary refill of three seconds at the nail beds. The client's estimated blood loss at this point is 600 mL and visibly increasing. She denies chest pain or shortness of breath but reports feeling lightheaded when she lifts her head from the pillow.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn on admission to labor and delivery (approximately 16 hours prior to current assessment): hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, hematocrit 35.2%, platelet count 188,000/µL, fibrinogen 410 mg/dL, INR 1.0, aPTT 28 seconds, blood type O-positive, antibody screen negative. Basic metabolic panel: sodium 138 mEq/L, potassium 3.9 mEq/L, creatinine 0.7 mg/dL, glucose 92 mg/dL. Urinalysis: 1+ protein (consistent with her pregnancy-induced hypertension), otherwise unremarkable.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — One hour postpartum (current moment). The nurse identifies the boggy, displaced fundus and the escalating blood loss. She begins bimanual fundal massage and calls for assistance. The charge nurse arrives. The bladder is distended and a straight catheterization yields 450 mL of clear urine; after drainage the uterus descends to the umbilicus but remains boggy despite continuous massage. The existing oxytocin infusion (20 units in 1 L LR) is confirmed to be running; the rate is increased per standing order to infuse the remaining volume over 30 minutes. Two large-bore (16-gauge) peripheral IVs are placed in the right antecubital fossa and left hand. A second liter of warmed lactated Ringer's is hung wide open. A quantitative blood loss measurement is initiated: weighed pads and underpads now total an estimated blood loss of 800 mL. The provider is called to bedside. Vital signs at this point: HR 124, BP 86/58, RR 24, SpO₂ 96% on room air. The client remains alert but is increasingly restless and reports worsening dizziness.

**Stage 2** — Approximately 90 minutes postpartum (30 minutes after Stage 1 interventions). The provider is at bedside. Despite continuous fundal massage and the increased oxytocin infusion, the uterus remains intermittently boggy, firming briefly with vigorous massage but relaxing within seconds. Quantitative blood loss has risen to 1,200 mL. Vital signs: HR 132, BP 80/52, RR 26, SpO₂ 95% on room air. The client is now confused, asking the same question repeatedly, and her skin is markedly pale with delayed capillary refill of four seconds. The provider orders a second uterotonic agent and requests a stat CBC and coagulation panel. The provider also performs a bedside uterine exploration and confirms no retained placental fragments and no cervical or vaginal lacerations beyond the repaired second-degree perineal tear. The nurse receives a verbal order for the second uterotonic and must select the appropriate agent from the medication options available on the unit: methylergonovine (Methergine) 0.2 mg IM, misoprostol (Cytotec) 800 mcg rectally, or carboprost tromethamine (Hemabate) 250 mcg IM. The client's chart prominently documents her pregnancy-induced hypertension and current labetalol therapy. Stat lab results return: hemoglobin 8.1 g/dL, hematocrit 24.3%, platelet count 162,000/µL, fibrinogen 280 mg/dL, INR 1.1, aPTT 30 seconds. A type and crossmatch for two units of packed red blood cells is sent. Tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 minutes is ordered per the unit's postpartum hemorrhage protocol, which specifies administration within three hours of birth onset for hemorrhage not responding to first-line uterotonics.

**Stage 3** — Approximately two hours postpartum (30 minutes after Stage 2 interventions). Misoprostol 800 mcg was administered rectally, tranexamic acid 1 g IV was infused over 10 minutes, and aggressive bimanual compression was performed by the provider. The uterus is now firm and contracted at the umbilicus. Lochia has decreased to a moderate, dark red flow without clots. Quantitative blood loss has plateaued at 1,450 mL with no further active accumulation over the past 15 minutes. Vital signs: HR 112, BP 94/60, RR 20, SpO₂ 97% on 2 L nasal cannula (placed during Stage 2). The client is alert, oriented, and no longer confused, though she reports fatigue and mild continued dizziness when repositioning. The first unit of packed red blood cells is infusing through the dedicated large-bore IV. Repeat labs drawn 20 minutes ago return: hemoglobin 7.4 g/dL, hematocrit 22.1%, platelet count 148,000/µL, fibrinogen 240 mg/dL, INR 1.2, aPTT 32 seconds. The Foley catheter placed during Stage 2 is draining 25 mL/hr of concentrated amber urine. The provider writes orders for continued fundal checks every 15 minutes, continued oxytocin infusion, a second unit of packed red blood cells to follow the first, and a repeat CBC two hours after transfusion completion. The nurse must now evaluate the client's trajectory and determine what findings confirm improvement versus what findings would signal the need for escalation to surgical intervention.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse performs the one-hour postpartum assessment and encounters a constellation of findings: a boggy uterus two fingerbreadths above the umbilicus and deviated to the right, heavy lochia with clots, saturated perineal pads, estimated blood loss of 600 mL, heart rate 118, blood pressure 90/62 (down from an antepartum baseline of 148/94), cool and clammy skin, a palpable bladder, and an intact perineal laceration repair with no active suture-line bleeding. The nurse must identify which of these findings are the critical cues that define the clinical problem. *Correct action:* The critical cues are the boggy displaced uterus, the heavy lochia with clots, the EBL of 600 mL (exceeding the 500 mL threshold for postpartum hemorrhage after vaginal delivery), the tachycardia of 118, and the blood pressure of 90/62 — which represents a drop of nearly 60 mmHg systolic from this client's hypertensive baseline of 148/94 and therefore indicates a more severe hemodynamic compromise than the absolute number alone would suggest in a normotensive patient. The palpable bladder is a contributing cue because bladder distension displaces the uterus and impedes contraction. The intact perineal repair with no suture-line bleeding is a relevant normal finding that helps narrow the bleeding source to the uterus rather than a laceration. The temperature of 36.8 °C and the SpO₂ of 97% are within normal limits and do not define the current problem. *Why it is correct:* Uterine atony — failure of the myometrium to contract and compress the spiral arteries at the placental site — accounts for approximately 80% of early postpartum hemorrhage. This client has multiple risk factors for atony: grand multiparity (the myometrium of a uterus that has carried five pregnancies has reduced contractile efficiency), fetal macrosomia (a 4.2 kg infant overdistends the uterus), and a prolonged second stage of labor (three hours of active pushing fatigues the myometrium). The boggy, displaced fundus is the hallmark physical finding. The blood pressure must be interpreted against the client's own baseline: a reading of 90/62 in a client whose pressures have been running 140s/90s represents a proportionally larger drop than the same reading in a client whose baseline is 110/70, and it signals significant volume loss even though 90/62 is not profoundly hypotensive in absolute terms. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse has identified the boggy uterus, the hemorrhage, and the hemodynamic changes. The nurse must now interpret the relationship among these findings and determine the most likely cause of the hemorrhage. The differential includes uterine atony, genital tract laceration, retained placental tissue, and uterine inversion. *Correct action:* The most likely cause is uterine atony. The boggy, relaxed uterus that does not sustain tone after massage is the defining finding. Genital tract laceration is less likely because the perineal repair is intact and not actively bleeding, and the uterus — not the lower tract — is the source of the ongoing flow. Retained placental tissue is possible but less likely given that the placenta was reported as intact on delivery; it has not yet been ruled out by uterine exploration, but the boggy fundus points to atony as the primary mechanism. Uterine inversion would present with the fundus not palpable in its expected abdominal position or a mass protruding from the cervix, often with severe pain and vasovagal shock; the fundus here is palpable above the umbilicus, which rules out inversion. *Why it is correct:* Systematic analysis of the cues against each differential diagnosis eliminates the competing hypotheses based on the findings available at this stage. The boggy uterus is pathognomonic for atony and, combined with the risk-factor profile (grand multiparity, macrosomia, prolonged second stage), makes atony overwhelmingly the most likely cause. The deviation to the right and the palpable bladder further support atony complicated by bladder distension preventing uterine contraction. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues and prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the provider orders a second uterotonic agent because the uterus has not responded adequately to oxytocin and fundal massage. The nurse has three agents available: methylergonovine (Methergine) 0.2 mg IM, misoprostol (Cytotec) 800 mcg rectally, or carboprost tromethamine (Hemabate) 250 mcg IM. The client has a documented history of pregnancy-induced hypertension and is currently taking labetalol. The nurse must determine which agent is safe to administer and which is contraindicated. *Correct action:* Administer misoprostol 800 mcg rectally. Methylergonovine is contraindicated in this client because it is a potent vasoconstrictor; in a client with pregnancy-induced hypertension, it can precipitate a dangerous hypertensive crisis, including stroke or seizure. Although the client's blood pressure is currently low due to hemorrhagic volume loss, the contraindication is based on the underlying hypertensive disorder, not the transient hypotension — once volume is restored, the vasoconstrictive effect of methylergonovine on top of the underlying endothelial dysfunction of pregnancy-induced hypertension creates an unacceptable risk of severe rebound hypertension. Carboprost is also a reasonable choice in the absence of asthma (this client has no documented asthma), but misoprostol is the most broadly safe second-line agent in this clinical context and is available for immediate rectal administration without the need for repeated dosing at 15-minute intervals. *Why it is correct:* Methylergonovine's mechanism of action includes sustained smooth-muscle contraction of both the uterus and the vascular system. In clients with hypertensive disorders of pregnancy — preeclampsia, eclampsia, pregnancy-induced hypertension, or chronic hypertension — the drug's vasoconstrictive properties can cause acute, severe blood pressure elevation. This is a well-established absolute contraindication. The nurse must recognize this contraindication from the medication history and the documented diagnosis before administering the drug, regardless of the current blood pressure reading. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2 (the verbal order for a second uterotonic and the specific medication options are presented at Stage 2; the hypertension history is available from the background but the decision point crystallizes when the nurse must choose among the three agents).

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the quantitative blood loss has reached 1,200 mL, the client is confused and markedly pale with a heart rate of 132 and blood pressure of 80/52, and the provider has ordered tranexamic acid and a type and crossmatch. The nurse must determine the priority sequence of actions at this moment. Multiple tasks are pending: administer the selected uterotonic, infuse tranexamic acid, send the crossmatch specimen, continue fundal massage, and continue fluid resuscitation. *Correct action:* The actions that must occur essentially simultaneously and without delay are continued bimanual fundal massage (or assisting the provider with bimanual uterine compression), administration of the uterotonic agent, and continuation of rapid IV fluid resuscitation through the large-bore access already established. These are concurrent priorities — they address the source of bleeding (uterotonic and massage) and the volume deficit (fluids) at the same time. Tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 minutes should be initiated as soon as the uterotonic is administered, as the unit protocol specifies it for hemorrhage not responding to first-line uterotonics and it must be given within three hours of birth. The type and crossmatch specimen should be sent immediately by the assisting nurse or charge nurse so that blood products are available without delay; the quantitative blood loss of 1,200 mL is approaching the threshold at which massive transfusion protocol activation may be needed (the unit's protocol activates at EBL greater than 1,500 mL). These are not strictly sequential steps with one mandatory first action — they are a coordinated team response. The critical principle is that source control (massage and uterotonics) and volume replacement must not be delayed for any other task. *Why it is correct:* In postpartum hemorrhage management, the simultaneous goals are to stop the bleeding (uterine contraction) and to replace lost volume (crystalloid, then blood products). Delaying either goal to complete the other worsens outcomes. The team-based response — one nurse on fundal massage, another drawing labs and hanging fluids, the provider performing bimanual compression — reflects the reality that these are concurrent, not sequential, interventions. Tranexamic acid is an adjunct that inhibits fibrinolysis and has been shown to reduce death from postpartum hemorrhage when given within three hours; it does not replace uterotonics or mechanical compression but augments them. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the uterus is firm, the bleeding has plateaued, the client is more alert, and the first unit of packed red blood cells is infusing. However, the repeat hemoglobin is 7.4 g/dL (lower than the Stage 2 value of 8.1 g/dL), the fibrinogen has dropped from 280 to 240 mg/dL, the urine output is only 25 mL/hr of concentrated urine, and the heart rate remains elevated at 112 though improved from 132. The nurse must evaluate whether the client is improving, stable, or deteriorating, and identify which findings confirm improvement versus which findings warrant continued concern. *Correct action:* The client is showing early signs of stabilization but has not yet recovered, and several findings require continued close monitoring. Findings confirming improvement: the uterus is firm and contracted (the source of bleeding is controlled), the lochia has decreased to moderate flow without clots, the quantitative blood loss has plateaued with no new accumulation over 15 minutes, the client's mental status has cleared (no longer confused), and the blood pressure has risen slightly from 80/52 to 94/60. Findings warranting continued concern: the hemoglobin of 7.4 g/dL is lower than the Stage 2 value because hemodilution from crystalloid resuscitation is now unmasking the true degree of red cell loss — this does not indicate new bleeding but does confirm the need for the second unit of packed red blood cells. The fibrinogen of 240 mg/dL, while still above the critical threshold of 200 mg/dL that would suggest developing disseminated intravascular coagulation, has trended downward from 410 to 280 to 240 and must be monitored for further decline. The urine output of 25 mL/hr is below the minimum acceptable threshold of 30 mL/hr and indicates ongoing renal hypoperfusion from the volume deficit. The heart rate of 112, though improved, remains tachycardic. If the uterus were to become boggy again, or if the EBL were to resume climbing, or if the fibrinogen were to fall below 200 mg/dL, the nurse should anticipate escalation to intrauterine balloon tamponade or surgical intervention (B-Lynch compression suture or hysterectomy). *Why it is correct:* Evaluation of postpartum hemorrhage response requires assessment of both the source (uterine tone and ongoing blood loss) and the systemic effects (hemodynamics, end-organ perfusion, coagulation status). A firm uterus and cessation of active bleeding are the most important indicators that the hemorrhage is controlled. However, the hemoglobin nadir often occurs after resuscitation as crystalloid dilutes the remaining red cell mass, so a dropping hemoglobin in the context of controlled bleeding reflects hemodilution, not rebleeding. Fibrinogen is the earliest coagulation factor to be consumed in obstetric hemorrhage, and a level below 200 mg/dL is associated with progression to DIC; the downward trend here is a warning that must be tracked. Urine output is a sensitive marker of renal perfusion and overall volume status. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Interpreting the blood pressure of 90/62 as "not that low" and failing to recognize the severity of the hemodynamic change. In a normotensive client, 90/62 might represent a modest drop, but in a client whose baseline has been 148/94, this represents a systolic drop of nearly 60 mmHg and indicates significant hemorrhagic volume loss. Failing to contextualize the blood pressure against the client's hypertensive baseline delays recognition of the hemorrhage's severity.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Dismissing the palpable bladder as unrelated to the hemorrhage. A full bladder displaces the uterus and physically prevents it from contracting effectively. Bladder drainage is a necessary early intervention in postpartum hemorrhage management, and overlooking it delays restoration of uterine tone.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Attributing the bleeding to a laceration because the client had a prolonged second stage and a perineal tear, and therefore delaying fundal massage and uterotonics in favor of requesting a repeat perineal inspection. The intact suture line with no active bleeding and the boggy uterus together point clearly to atony, not laceration. Focusing on the perineum while the uterus remains atonic allows continued hemorrhage from the placental site.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Administering methylergonovine because the client's blood pressure is currently low (90/62 at Stage 1, 80/52 at Stage 2) and reasoning that the vasoconstrictive effect would be beneficial for both the uterus and the blood pressure. This is a dangerous error. The contraindication to methylergonovine in hypertensive disorders of pregnancy is absolute and is based on the underlying disease process, not the current blood pressure reading. Once volume resuscitation restores circulating volume, the vasoconstrictive effect of methylergonovine superimposed on the endothelial dysfunction of pregnancy-induced hypertension can produce a severe, rebound hypertensive crisis with risk of stroke, seizure, or pulmonary edema. The transient hypotension of hemorrhagic shock does not negate the contraindication.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Stopping fundal massage to draw labs or hang the tranexamic acid, reasoning that the provider has ordered these tasks and they should be completed first. Source control — maintaining uterine compression — must not be interrupted for ancillary tasks. In a team response, one nurse maintains massage while another performs the other tasks simultaneously. Releasing the uterus to perform a non-urgent task allows the atonic uterus to fill with blood and increases the total blood loss.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Delaying tranexamic acid administration because the nurse is uncertain whether it is indicated or wants to wait for the provider to return to confirm the order. The unit's postpartum hemorrhage protocol specifies tranexamic acid for hemorrhage not responding to first-line uterotonics, and the three-hour window from birth is a time-sensitive constraint. Unnecessary delay reduces the drug's efficacy. The order has been given and the protocol criteria are met.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Interpreting the drop in hemoglobin from 8.1 to 7.4 g/dL as evidence of continued active bleeding and calling for emergent surgical intervention, despite the firm uterus and plateaued blood loss. The hemoglobin drop in this context reflects hemodilution from crystalloid resuscitation unmasking the true red cell deficit, not ongoing hemorrhage. The appropriate response is to continue the transfusion and recheck the hemoglobin after the second unit, not to escalate to surgery when the source of bleeding is controlled.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Considering the client stable because the uterus is firm and the bleeding has slowed, and therefore reducing the frequency of fundal checks or vital sign monitoring. The client's urine output is below 30 mL/hr, the heart rate remains elevated, and the fibrinogen is trending downward. Uterine atony can recur, and the client has not yet demonstrated sustained hemodynamic recovery. Premature relaxation of monitoring frequency risks missing re-atony or early coagulopathy.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Recognize the risk factors for uterine atony (grand multiparity, fetal macrosomia, prolonged second stage of labor) and identify the clinical cues of postpartum hemorrhage, including the significance of interpreting blood pressure changes against a hypertensive baseline.
- Analyze assessment findings to differentiate uterine atony from other causes of postpartum hemorrhage (genital tract laceration, retained placental tissue, uterine inversion) based on the physical examination.
- Apply pharmacologic knowledge to select a safe uterotonic agent in a client with pregnancy-induced hypertension, recognizing the absolute contraindication of methylergonovine in hypertensive disorders.
- Coordinate the simultaneous priorities of postpartum hemorrhage management — source control through fundal massage and uterotonics, volume resuscitation, blood product preparation, and adjunctive tranexamic acid — as a team-based, concurrent response rather than a rigid sequence.
- Evaluate the client's response to hemorrhage management by distinguishing findings that confirm stabilization (firm uterus, cessation of active bleeding, improving mental status) from findings that reflect ongoing physiologic compromise (hemodilution-related hemoglobin drop, declining fibrinogen, oliguria, persistent tachycardia) and identify the thresholds for escalation to surgical intervention.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the uterus has failed to respond to oxytocin and fundal massage, the quantitative blood loss has reached 1,200 mL, the client is confused and hemodynamically unstable with a heart rate of 132 and blood pressure of 80/52, uterine exploration has ruled out retained tissue and laceration, and the provider has ordered a second uterotonic.

Most likely condition: Postpartum hemorrhage due to uterine atony. The boggy uterus that does not sustain tone despite oxytocin and massage, the escalating blood loss, and the risk-factor profile (grand multiparity, macrosomia, prolonged second stage) unambiguously identify atony as the cause. Uterine exploration has confirmed no retained fragments and no unrepaired lacerations, eliminating the two most common alternative etiologies.

Plausible competing conditions: (1) Cervical or vaginal laceration — wrong here because the provider has performed a bedside uterine and vaginal exploration at Stage 2 and found no lacerations beyond the already-repaired second-degree perineal tear; the continued bleeding is from a boggy uterus, not a traumatic source. (2) Coagulopathy (early disseminated intravascular coagulation) — wrong as the primary diagnosis at this moment because the Stage 2 coagulation studies show fibrinogen 280 mg/dL, INR 1.1, and aPTT 30 seconds, all still within functional range; the coagulation profile is trending in a concerning direction but has not crossed the threshold that would make coagulopathy the primary driver of the hemorrhage rather than a consequence of it.

Two priority actions: (1) Administer misoprostol 800 mcg rectally as the second-line uterotonic — this addresses the source of hemorrhage (the atonic uterus) with an agent that is safe in the setting of pregnancy-induced hypertension, unlike methylergonovine. (2) Initiate tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 minutes per the unit's postpartum hemorrhage protocol — the hemorrhage has not responded to first-line uterotonics, the protocol criteria are met, and the three-hour window from birth is time-sensitive; this adjunct reduces fibrinolysis and has been shown to decrease mortality from postpartum hemorrhage.

Two wrong actions: (1) Administering methylergonovine 0.2 mg IM — wrong because methylergonovine is absolutely contraindicated in this client's pregnancy-induced hypertension; its vasoconstrictive properties risk precipitating a hypertensive crisis once volume is restored, regardless of the current hypotension. (2) Stopping fundal massage to complete other tasks such as drawing labs or documenting — wrong because releasing compression on the atonic uterus allows the placental-site spiral arteries to bleed freely; source control must be maintained continuously while other team members perform concurrent tasks.

Two parameters to monitor: (1) Uterine tone and fundal firmness — direct palpation of the fundus is the most immediate indicator of whether the uterotonic and massage interventions are achieving myometrial contraction and controlling the source of hemorrhage. (2) Quantitative blood loss trend — ongoing measurement of cumulative blood loss (weighed pads, drapes, and canisters) determines whether the hemorrhage is plateauing or continuing to escalate, and crossing the 1,500 mL threshold would trigger activation of the massive transfusion protocol.

Two irrelevant parameters: (1) Fetal Apgar scores or neonatal status — the infant has been delivered and is being cared for separately; neonatal assessment does not inform the management of the maternal hemorrhage at this moment. (2) Serum creatinine — while renal perfusion is a concern in hemorrhagic shock, the creatinine was normal at baseline and changes in creatinine lag hours to days behind acute volume changes; urine output is the real-time renal perfusion marker, and creatinine is not the parameter that tracks the immediate trajectory of the hemorrhage.
