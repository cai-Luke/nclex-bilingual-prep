**CASE TITLE**
Acute gallstone pancreatitis with hypocalcemia and hemoconcentration.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 45-year-old woman presents to the emergency department via ambulance. Her medical history includes cholelithiasis diagnosed two years ago on right upper quadrant ultrasound; she declined elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy at that time, citing fear of procedural complications. She has a body mass index of 34, diet-controlled type 2 diabetes (hemoglobin A1c 6.4% three months ago, no medications), and no history of alcohol use. She takes no prescription medications and has no known drug allergies. She is a nonsmoker and works as a middle-school teacher. She lives with her husband and two teenage children.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient reports sudden-onset severe epigastric pain that began approximately six hours ago, roughly forty-five minutes after eating a large meal of fried chicken, coleslaw, and biscuits at a family gathering. She describes the pain as a deep, boring, constant ache rated 9 out of 10, radiating straight through to her mid-back between the scapulae. She has vomited four times since onset — initially food contents, then bilious fluid. She reports no relief from any position, though she says leaning forward slightly is marginally less uncomfortable than lying flat. She denies fever, chills, diarrhea, hematemesis, melena, chest pain, and shortness of breath. She has not urinated since the pain began.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 37.3 °C (oral), heart rate 112 beats per minute and regular, blood pressure 118/74 mmHg, respiratory rate 24 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 96% on room air. She appears diaphoretic and in obvious distress, lying on her side with knees drawn up. Skin is warm and dry on the extremities; mucous membranes are tacky. Sclera are mildly icteric. Abdomen is distended with marked epigastric tenderness and voluntary guarding; bowel sounds are hypoactive. There is no rebound tenderness and no palpable mass. No flank ecchymosis (Grey Turner sign) and no periumbilical ecchymosis (Cullen sign). Heart sounds are regular without murmur. Lungs are clear bilaterally with slightly diminished breath sounds at the left base. Peripheral pulses are strong and equal. Capillary refill is two seconds. No peripheral edema. She is alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation. No carpopedal spasm is noted at rest. No facial twitching is observed spontaneously.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Lipase 1,840 U/L (reference 10–140 U/L). Amylase 1,260 U/L (reference 25–125 U/L). AST 210 U/L (reference 10–40 U/L). ALT 280 U/L (reference 7–56 U/L). Total bilirubin 3.2 mg/dL (reference 0.1–1.2 mg/dL). Direct bilirubin 2.4 mg/dL (reference 0.0–0.3 mg/dL). Alkaline phosphatase 310 U/L (reference 44–147 U/L). WBC 14,500/µL (reference 4,500–11,000/µL). Hemoglobin 15.8 g/dL (reference 12.0–16.0 g/dL). Hematocrit 48% (reference 36–46%). Platelets 238,000/µL (reference 150,000–400,000/µL). BUN 22 mg/dL (reference 7–20 mg/dL). Creatinine 0.9 mg/dL (reference 0.6–1.2 mg/dL). Sodium 139 mEq/L. Potassium 4.1 mEq/L. Chloride 101 mEq/L. CO₂ 22 mEq/L. Calcium 7.6 mg/dL (reference 8.5–10.5 mg/dL). Ionized calcium 3.6 mg/dL (reference 4.5–5.3 mg/dL). Magnesium 1.7 mg/dL (reference 1.7–2.2 mg/dL). Glucose 180 mg/dL (reference 70–100 mg/dL fasting). Lactate 1.8 mmol/L (reference 0.5–2.0 mmol/L). Triglycerides 168 mg/dL (reference < 150 mg/dL). Albumin 3.2 g/dL (reference 3.5–5.0 g/dL). INR 1.1. Urinalysis: concentrated, specific gravity 1.030, no blood, no infection.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Zero to four hours after ED arrival. The provider orders moderately aggressive, goal-directed lactated Ringer's solution with bolus and rate adjustments for hypovolemia and close reassessment for overload. The provider also orders ondansetron 4 mg IV every six hours as needed for nausea, hydromorphone 0.5 mg IV every three hours as needed for pain, and NPO status while the patient is actively vomiting. A Foley catheter is placed. The nurse initiates the lactated Ringer's infusion as ordered and administers the first doses of ondansetron and hydromorphone. Over the first two hours, the patient's pain decreases from 9/10 to 7/10. Foley output for the first two hours totals 45 mL (approximately 22 mL/hr). Repeat vitals at hour two: heart rate 106, blood pressure 122/78, respiratory rate 22, temperature 37.2 °C, SpO₂ 97% on room air. At hour four, the nurse performs a focused neuromuscular assessment: tapping the facial nerve anterior to the ear produces a brief twitch of the ipsilateral upper lip (positive Chvostek sign). The nurse inflates a blood pressure cuff on the upper arm to 20 mmHg above systolic and holds it for three minutes; at two minutes and forty seconds, the patient develops carpopedal spasm of the hand with flexion of the wrist and metacarpophalangeal joints (positive Trousseau sign). The patient also reports intermittent tingling in her fingertips and around her mouth. No cardiac rhythm changes are noted on the bedside monitor — sinus tachycardia without prolonged QT. The provider is notified of the positive signs and the symptomatic hypocalcemia. An order is received for calcium gluconate 2 grams in 50 mL of dextrose 5% in water, infused intravenously over 20 minutes via an infusion pump, with continuous cardiac monitoring during the infusion. The nurse also confirms that the magnesium level is 1.7 mg/dL (low-normal) and anticipates that magnesium repletion may be needed if calcium does not correct, since hypomagnesemia impairs parathyroid hormone response. Foley output for hours two through four totals 110 mL (approximately 55 mL/hr), an improvement from the initial two hours.

**Stage 2** — Hours 12 to 24 after admission. The patient has received goal-directed fluid resuscitation with lactated Ringer's over the first twelve hours, and the continuous rate was recently decreased to 200 mL/hr. Repeat vitals at hour 16: temperature 38.4 °C (oral), heart rate 118, blood pressure 108/62, respiratory rate 26, SpO₂ 94% on two liters nasal cannula (desaturation noted at hour 14, oxygen applied). The patient's pain has increased back to 8/10 despite scheduled hydromorphone. She appears flushed and more lethargic than earlier, though she remains oriented to person, place, time, and situation. Abdomen is more distended with diffuse tenderness now extending beyond the epigastrium; bowel sounds are absent. Breath sounds are diminished at the left base with dullness to percussion (developing left-sided pleural effusion). Repeat labs at hour 18: WBC 19,200/µL, hematocrit 38% (down from 48% with fluid resuscitation), BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.8 mg/dL, calcium 7.9 mg/dL (improved from 7.6 after calcium gluconate), ionized calcium 4.0 mg/dL, lipase 2,640 U/L (rising), glucose 220 mg/dL, lactate 2.6 mmol/L, CRP 285 mg/L (reference < 10 mg/L). Foley output over the preceding six hours totals 180 mL (30 mL/hr — declining from the improved rate seen after initial resuscitation). The patient now meets systemic inflammatory response syndrome criteria: temperature above 38 °C, heart rate above 90, respiratory rate above 20, and WBC above 12,000. The provider orders a STAT right upper quadrant ultrasound, which shows a dilated common bile duct at 11 mm with a shadowing stone in the distal duct, gallbladder wall thickening, and pericholecystic fluid. Blood cultures are drawn. The provider discusses the need for urgent ERCP within 24 hours given the persistent biliary obstruction and the clinical trajectory suggesting early cholangitis (fever and jaundice with confirmed CBD obstruction raise concern for evolving cholangitis). The gastroenterology team is consulted. An insulin sliding scale is initiated for the rising glucose. The nurse notes that the patient has not vomited in eight hours and asks about her interest in attempting clear liquids, but the patient declines, stating she feels too nauseated.

**Stage 3** — Hours 36 to 48 after admission. The patient underwent ERCP at hour 28; a 9-mm stone was extracted from the distal common bile duct via sphincterotomy and balloon sweep. Post-ERCP, the patient's temperature has decreased to 37.6 °C. Vitals at hour 40: heart rate 92, blood pressure 124/76, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air (nasal cannula discontinued at hour 36). She reports her pain is now 4/10, localized to the epigastrium, and she describes it as a dull ache rather than the prior boring quality. She is more alert and conversational. Abdomen is less distended; bowel sounds have returned, though they are still hypoactive. The left basilar breath sounds remain diminished but are improved from Stage 2. Repeat labs at hour 42: WBC 11,800/µL, lipase 980 U/L (trending down from 2,640), total bilirubin 1.8 mg/dL (down from 3.2), AST 88 U/L, ALT 140 U/L, calcium 8.4 mg/dL, ionized calcium 4.4 mg/dL, glucose 148 mg/dL, CRP 190 mg/L (still elevated but trending down from 285), lactate 1.2 mmol/L, hematocrit 36%. Foley output over the preceding six hours totals 300 mL (50 mL/hr). The patient states she is hungry and asks if she can eat. The dietitian has been consulted. The provider writes an order to advance diet to low-fat solid food as tolerated. The nurse offers the patient a low-fat meal tray. The patient tolerates clear liquids at hour 38 and a small portion of toast and applesauce at hour 42 without increased pain or vomiting. The surgical team has been consulted for interval cholecystectomy during this admission once the acute inflammation resolves, to prevent recurrence. A contrast-enhanced CT of the abdomen is not ordered at this time because the patient is clinically improving; the team documents that CT with contrast would be indicated if the patient fails to improve or deteriorates, to evaluate for necrotizing pancreatitis, and that the appropriate timing for such imaging is 72 to 96 hours after symptom onset to allow necrosis to demarcate.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At initial presentation, the nurse reviews the full set of admission findings — the history of gallstones, the post-fatty-meal onset, the lipase of 1,840 U/L, the AST of 210, the ALT of 280, the total bilirubin of 3.2, the direct bilirubin of 2.4, the alkaline phosphatase of 310, the WBC of 14,500, the hematocrit of 48%, the calcium of 7.6, the glucose of 180, the scleral icterus, the heart rate of 112, and the respiratory rate of 24. The nurse must identify which cluster of findings points to the underlying etiology and which findings are severity markers rather than etiologic clues. *Correct action:* The elevated transaminases (AST 210, ALT 280 with ALT greater than AST), the elevated direct bilirubin (2.4 mg/dL), the elevated alkaline phosphatase (310 U/L), the scleral icterus, and the known gallstone history together identify a biliary etiology — this is gallstone pancreatitis, not alcohol-related or hypertriglyceridemia-related disease. The lipase above three times the upper limit of normal confirms acute pancreatitis. The hematocrit of 48% (above the upper reference of 46% for women), the calcium of 7.6 mg/dL, the glucose of 180 mg/dL, and the WBC of 14,500 are severity and prognostic markers, not etiologic indicators. The tacky mucous membranes and the concentrated urine (specific gravity 1.030) are consistent with intravascular volume depletion but do not point to etiology. *Why it is correct:* In acute pancreatitis, an ALT greater than three times the upper limit of normal has a high positive predictive value for a gallstone etiology. The direct hyperbilirubinemia and elevated alkaline phosphatase reflect biliary obstruction. Distinguishing etiology from severity is essential because the biliary etiology determines the need for potential ERCP and eventual cholecystectomy — interventions that do not apply to other causes. The hematocrit elevation reflects hemoconcentration from third-spacing of fluid into the retroperitoneum and peritoneal cavity, which is a marker of intravascular depletion and disease severity, not a clue to cause. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation and admission data.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse has elicited a positive Chvostek sign, a positive Trousseau sign, and the patient reports perioral and fingertip tingling. The admission calcium was 7.6 mg/dL with an ionized calcium of 3.6 mg/dL. The nurse must interpret the mechanism of the hypocalcemia in the context of acute pancreatitis and determine its clinical significance. *Correct action:* The hypocalcemia is caused by calcium sequestration through fat saponification — pancreatic lipase released during pancreatitis digests peripancreatic and omental fat, releasing free fatty acids that bind ionized calcium and form insoluble calcium soaps in the retroperitoneum. The low albumin (3.2 g/dL) also contributes to a lower total calcium, but the ionized calcium of 3.6 mg/dL confirms true hypocalcemia beyond what hypoalbuminemia alone would explain. The positive Chvostek and Trousseau signs with perioral tingling indicate symptomatic neuromuscular irritability requiring treatment. Hypocalcemia in acute pancreatitis is also an independent marker of disease severity — it is one of the original Ranson criteria at 48 hours and correlates with more extensive fat necrosis. *Why it is correct:* Understanding the mechanism prevents the nurse from attributing the low calcium to a benign laboratory artifact of low albumin or to simple dietary deficiency. The ionized calcium confirms the deficit is physiologically significant. Recognizing that the positive provocative signs represent symptomatic hypocalcemia — not just a low number — drives the urgency of treatment and the need to notify the provider. The severity implication informs the nurse's ongoing assessment intensity. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1 (the provocative signs and the patient's symptoms are elicited at Stage 1; the lab values are from admission).

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient has developed fever (38.4 °C), rising WBC (19,200), tachycardia (118), hypotension (108/62), tachypnea (26), declining SpO₂ (94% on 2 L), declining urine output (30 mL/hr), rising lipase (2,640), rising lactate (2.6), and a CRP of 285. She meets full SIRS criteria. The ultrasound shows a dilated common bile duct with an impacted distal stone. The nurse must determine the most likely primary complication driving the deterioration and distinguish it from other possibilities. *Correct action:* The primary complication is evolving cholangitis from persistent biliary obstruction by the impacted common bile duct stone, superimposed on the acute pancreatitis. The fever, jaundice (bilirubin was already 3.2 and the stone remains impacted), and the confirmed obstructing stone satisfy two of three Charcot triad criteria with imaging confirmation. This is distinct from infected pancreatic necrosis, which would not be expected this early (necrosis typically demarcates over 72 to 96 hours and infection of necrosis usually occurs after the first week), and the clinical trajectory — worsening biliary obstruction markers with new fever — points to the biliary tree as the source. Sepsis from another source (urinary, line-related) is less likely given the clear urinalysis, the absence of a central line, and the direct correlation between the obstructing stone and the clinical deterioration. *Why it is correct:* Persistent common bile duct obstruction in gallstone pancreatitis creates stasis and bacterial colonization of bile, leading to ascending cholangitis. Recognizing cholangitis as the driver — rather than attributing the deterioration solely to worsening pancreatitis or assuming infected necrosis — is critical because the treatment is urgent ERCP for stone extraction and biliary decompression, a time-sensitive intervention. Misidentifying the complication could lead to delayed ERCP or unnecessary CT imaging for necrosis at a time point when necrosis cannot yet be reliably assessed. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must determine the appropriate set of interventions for this patient who has worsening pancreatitis with evolving cholangitis, declining urine output, new oxygen requirement, rising glucose, and persistent pain. Multiple interventions are needed; the nurse must identify the correct combination and the rationale for each. *Correct action:* The priority interventions are: (a) notify the provider and reassess volume status, anticipating a bolus or rate adjustment because the declining urine output (30 mL/hr) and rising lactate (2.6) suggest hypoperfusion despite prior resuscitation — lactated Ringer's is preferred over normal saline because it produces less hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis and less systemic inflammation in pancreatitis; (b) ensure blood cultures have been drawn and anticipate initiation of empiric IV antibiotics per provider order for suspected cholangitis; (c) support the urgent ERCP consultation — the patient needs biliary decompression within 24 hours given the obstructing stone with signs of cholangitis; (d) titrate supplemental oxygen to maintain SpO₂ above 95% and monitor for worsening respiratory status from the developing pleural effusion; (e) continue IV analgesics — hydromorphone or morphine are both appropriate, as opioid-induced sphincter of Oddi spasm is not a clinically significant contraindication to morphine use in pancreatitis; (f) manage the hyperglycemia per the insulin sliding scale. The nurse should not initiate oral feeding at this time despite the eight-hour emesis-free interval, because the patient herself reports feeling too nauseated and has declined; however, the nurse should plan to reassess readiness for early enteral nutrition once the acute deterioration is addressed, because prolonged NPO status worsens outcomes in pancreatitis — early enteral feeding (oral if tolerated, or nasojejunal tube feeding if oral intake is not possible) is preferred over prolonged bowel rest. *Why it is correct:* Lactated Ringer's is the evidence-based crystalloid for pancreatitis resuscitation because normal saline's high chloride load worsens the inflammatory milieu and the metabolic acidosis already developing (CO₂ was 22 at baseline). The declining urine output despite prior fluid loading indicates ongoing third-spacing and the need for re-escalation. Cholangitis with an obstructing stone requires both antibiotics and mechanical decompression — antibiotics alone will not resolve the obstruction. The morphine question is clinically important: the historical teaching that morphine is contraindicated in biliary and pancreatic disease because it causes sphincter of Oddi spasm has not been supported by clinical evidence showing worse outcomes, and withholding effective analgesia based on this outdated concern causes unnecessary suffering. Early enteral nutrition maintains gut mucosal integrity and reduces bacterial translocation, infectious complications, and mortality compared with prolonged NPO management. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient has undergone successful ERCP with stone extraction. She reports hunger and asks to eat. The provider has ordered advancement to low-fat solid food as tolerated. The lipase is still elevated at 980 U/L, though it is trending down. The nurse must decide whether to advance the diet and what criteria guide that decision. *Correct action:* The nurse should advance the diet as ordered. The criteria for resuming oral intake in acute pancreatitis are clinical — decreasing pain (now 4/10, down from 8/10), return of bowel sounds (present, though hypoactive), absence of vomiting, and the patient's expressed hunger and willingness to eat. The lipase level of 980 U/L, while still above normal, is not a criterion for withholding oral nutrition. Waiting for lipase to normalize before feeding is an outdated practice that prolongs NPO status unnecessarily and worsens outcomes. A low-fat solid diet is appropriate as the initial refeeding choice; there is no requirement to progress through a stepwise clear-liquid-to-full-liquid-to-solid sequence if the patient tolerates solids. *Why it is correct:* Current evidence demonstrates that early oral feeding guided by clinical tolerance — not by normalization of pancreatic enzymes — reduces hospital length of stay and complications. The lipase may remain elevated for days to weeks after clinical resolution and does not correlate with readiness to eat. Prolonged NPO status leads to gut mucosal atrophy, increased bacterial translocation from the gut lumen into the bloodstream, and higher rates of infectious complications including infected pancreatic necrosis. The low-fat specification reduces pancreatic stimulation during recovery. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse evaluates the patient's overall trajectory to determine whether she is improving, stable, or deteriorating, and to identify what findings would trigger escalation. The team has documented that a contrast-enhanced CT would be indicated if the patient fails to improve, to evaluate for necrotizing pancreatitis, and that the appropriate timing is 72 to 96 hours after symptom onset. *Correct action:* The patient is improving. The markers confirming improvement are: decreasing pain (9/10 → 8/10 → 4/10), resolving tachycardia (112 → 118 → 92), improving blood pressure (108/62 → 124/76), normalizing temperature (38.4 → 37.6 °C), improving oxygenation (SpO₂ 94% on 2 L → 96% on room air), rising urine output (22 mL/hr → 30 mL/hr → 50 mL/hr), falling WBC (14,500 → 19,200 → 11,800), falling bilirubin (3.2 → 1.8), improving calcium (7.6 → 7.9 → 8.4), falling lactate (1.8 → 2.6 → 1.2), and tolerance of oral intake. Lipase may trend down but is not a management target. The findings that would trigger escalation and the need for contrast-enhanced CT include: recurrence or worsening of fever, new or worsening organ dysfunction (rising creatinine, worsening oxygenation, hemodynamic instability), failure of pain to continue improving, or inability to tolerate oral nutrition after 72 hours. The CT should not be obtained before 72 to 96 hours from symptom onset because pancreatic necrosis has not yet fully demarcated and early imaging underestimates the extent of necrosis, leading to false reassurance or unnecessary intervention. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes requires tracking multiple parameters across the trajectory, not relying on a single value. The improvement in hemodynamics and urine output confirms that fluid resuscitation has been effective. The falling WBC and temperature confirm that the cholangitis is responding to ERCP and biliary decompression. The falling bilirubin confirms resolution of the biliary obstruction. The improving calcium reflects decreasing fat saponification as the inflammatory process subsides. The CT timing principle prevents premature imaging that would be unreliable and potentially expose the patient to contrast nephropathy risk without actionable information. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Attached to Decision Point 1: Attributing the elevated transaminases and bilirubin to hepatitis or liver disease rather than recognizing them as markers of biliary obstruction in the context of known gallstones. This error delays recognition of the biliary etiology and the potential need for ERCP. The pattern of ALT greater than AST with direct hyperbilirubinemia and elevated alkaline phosphatase in a patient with known gallstones is characteristic of choledocholithiasis, not primary hepatocellular injury.

Attached to Decision Point 1: Interpreting the hematocrit of 48% as a normal or reassuring value rather than recognizing it as hemoconcentration indicating significant intravascular volume depletion. In a woman with a reference range of 36–46%, a hematocrit of 48% in the setting of acute pancreatitis with tacky mucous membranes and concentrated urine reflects third-spacing, not adequate hydration. This error leads to underestimation of the fluid resuscitation needed.

Attached to Decision Point 2: Dismissing the low total calcium as an artifact of the low albumin (3.2 g/dL) without checking the ionized calcium. While hypoalbuminemia does lower total calcium, the ionized calcium of 3.6 mg/dL (reference 4.5–5.3) confirms true, physiologically significant hypocalcemia. Failing to assess for neuromuscular irritability (Chvostek and Trousseau signs) delays recognition of symptomatic hypocalcemia requiring IV calcium replacement.

Attached to Decision Point 3: Assuming the Stage 2 deterioration represents infected pancreatic necrosis and requesting or expecting a contrast-enhanced CT at hour 18. Infected necrosis is exceedingly unlikely this early in the disease course — necrosis requires 72 to 96 hours to demarcate, and infection of necrotic tissue typically occurs after the first week. A CT obtained at 18 hours would be unreliable for assessing necrosis and would divert attention from the actual problem: an obstructing common bile duct stone causing cholangitis, which requires urgent ERCP, not imaging.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Withholding morphine or hydromorphone because of the belief that opioids cause clinically significant sphincter of Oddi spasm that worsens pancreatitis. This is an outdated teaching not supported by evidence of worse clinical outcomes. Withholding effective analgesia causes unnecessary suffering and can worsen the physiologic stress response, increasing tachycardia, hypertension, and catecholamine release. Both morphine and hydromorphone are appropriate analgesics in acute pancreatitis.

Attached to Decision Point 4: Administering unmonitored large-volume normal saline instead of lactated Ringer's for fluid resuscitation. While lactated Ringer's is generally preferred for large-volume resuscitation when not contraindicated due to its physiologic electrolyte composition, unmonitored large-volume normal saline loads carry a higher risk of hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, which can worsen the inflammatory milieu in pancreatitis.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Withholding oral nutrition because the lipase remains elevated at 980 U/L, believing that the patient should remain NPO until the lipase normalizes. This practice is not evidence-based and prolongs bowel rest unnecessarily. Lipase levels do not correlate with clinical readiness to eat and may remain elevated for days after clinical improvement. Prolonged NPO status increases the risk of gut mucosal atrophy, bacterial translocation, and infectious complications. The decision to feed is based on clinical criteria: decreasing pain, return of bowel function, absence of vomiting, and patient willingness.

Attached to Decision Point 5: Insisting on a stepwise diet progression from clear liquids to full liquids to soft diet to regular diet before allowing solid food. Current evidence supports advancing directly to a low-fat solid diet as tolerated, without a mandatory liquid-diet phase. The stepwise approach delays adequate caloric intake without improving safety.

Attached to Decision Point 6: Relying solely on the lipase trend to evaluate improvement while ignoring the hemodynamic, respiratory, renal, and clinical parameters. A falling lipase is one data point but does not capture the full clinical picture — a patient could have a falling lipase while developing organ failure from necrotizing pancreatitis. Comprehensive evaluation requires tracking vital signs, urine output, oxygenation, pain trajectory, inflammatory markers, and tolerance of oral intake.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Interpret the mechanism of hypocalcemia in acute pancreatitis (fat saponification and calcium sequestration), assess for neuromuscular irritability using Chvostek and Trousseau signs, and recognize when symptomatic hypocalcemia requires IV calcium replacement.

Differentiate evolving cholangitis from infected pancreatic necrosis based on clinical timeline, imaging findings, and the pattern of deterioration, and recognize the indication for urgent ERCP in gallstone pancreatitis with persistent biliary obstruction.

Apply evidence-based fluid resuscitation principles in acute pancreatitis, including the rationale for lactated Ringer's over normal saline, and identify the clinical criteria for resuming oral nutrition — guided by clinical tolerance, not lipase normalization.

Evaluate the trajectory of a patient recovering from acute gallstone pancreatitis across multiple parameters and identify the findings that would trigger escalation, including the appropriate timing and indication for contrast-enhanced CT to assess for necrotizing pancreatitis.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the patient has deteriorated with fever, SIRS criteria, declining urine output, new oxygen requirement, and ultrasound confirmation of an obstructing common bile duct stone — the full clinical picture has declared.

*Most likely condition:* Acute gallstone pancreatitis complicated by evolving cholangitis from persistent common bile duct obstruction. The combination of confirmed choledocholithiasis on ultrasound (11-mm dilated duct with distal stone), new fever (38.4 °C), worsening jaundice (bilirubin 3.2 at admission with the stone still impacted), and the biliary etiology already established by the transaminase and bilirubin pattern makes cholangitis superimposed on gallstone pancreatitis the unifying diagnosis.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Infected pancreatic necrosis — wrong here because the timeline is far too early for this complication; necrosis requires 72 to 96 hours to demarcate on imaging, and infection of necrotic tissue typically occurs after the first week; the fever and SIRS at 16 hours are explained by the obstructing stone and cholangitis, not by necrosis that has not yet had time to develop. (2) Sepsis from a non-biliary source such as aspiration pneumonia (given the vomiting and left basilar findings) — wrong because the left basilar dullness and diminished breath sounds are consistent with a reactive pleural effusion, which is a common finding in acute pancreatitis from diaphragmatic inflammation, not an infectious infiltrate; the clear temporal correlation between the obstructing stone and the clinical deterioration, combined with the absence of productive cough or infiltrate pattern, makes a biliary source far more likely than a pulmonary one.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Notify the provider of the clinical deterioration and SIRS criteria, ensure blood cultures have been drawn, and anticipate initiation of empiric IV antibiotics per provider order for suspected cholangitis — the biliary infection requires antimicrobial coverage while awaiting definitive decompression. (2) Facilitate urgent ERCP consultation and preparation — the obstructing common bile duct stone requires mechanical extraction and biliary decompression within 24 hours; this is the definitive intervention that addresses the source of the cholangitis and will not resolve with antibiotics alone.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Requesting or expecting a contrast-enhanced CT to evaluate for pancreatic necrosis at this time point — wrong because necrosis cannot be reliably assessed at 18 hours; this imaging would delay the urgent ERCP that the patient actually needs and would not provide actionable information about necrosis this early. (2) Maintaining the current fluid rate (200 mL/hr) without re-escalating resuscitation despite the declining urine output (30 mL/hr) and rising lactate (2.6 mmol/L) — wrong because these findings indicate inadequate tissue perfusion and ongoing intravascular depletion from third-spacing; the patient needs re-escalation of aggressive IV fluid resuscitation to prevent progression to organ failure.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Urine output — the trend from 22 mL/hr to 55 mL/hr to 30 mL/hr tracks the adequacy of fluid resuscitation and renal perfusion in real time; recovery of urine output above 0.5 mL/kg/hr confirms that resuscitation is restoring end-organ perfusion. (2) Temperature and WBC trajectory after ERCP and antibiotics — resolution of fever and a downward WBC trend confirm that the cholangitis is responding to biliary decompression and antimicrobial therapy; persistent or worsening fever after ERCP would indicate incomplete decompression, a secondary source, or a complication requiring further evaluation.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Lipase level — while it is trending (1,840 → 2,640), the lipase does not track whether the cholangitis is resolving or whether the patient's hemodynamic status is improving; it reflects ongoing pancreatic inflammation but does not guide the acute management decisions at this synthesis moment. (2) Serial amylase — like lipase, this tracks pancreatic inflammation but does not guide the acute management of cholangitis or hemodynamics at this synthesis moment; its elevation or trend is not a decisional parameter for the immediate interventions.
