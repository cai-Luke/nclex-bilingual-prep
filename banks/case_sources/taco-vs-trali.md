**CASE TITLE**
Transfusion-associated circulatory overload versus transfusion-related acute lung injury in a patient with chronic kidney disease.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 74-year-old woman with a history of chronic kidney disease stage 3b (baseline eGFR 38 mL/min/1.73 m²), compensated heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (last echocardiogram six months ago showing EF 52%, grade I diastolic dysfunction), and type 2 diabetes is admitted to a medical-surgical unit for symptomatic anemia. She has been experiencing progressive fatigue, exertional dyspnea (on climbing one flight of stairs), and lightheadedness over the past two weeks. Her outpatient hemoglobin returned at 6.4 g/dL, prompting admission for transfusion. She has no history of prior transfusion reactions. Her home medications include furosemide 40 mg daily, lisinopril 10 mg daily, metformin 500 mg twice daily (dose-adjusted for renal function), and insulin glargine 18 units at bedtime.

The provider orders two units of packed red blood cells to be transfused sequentially. Per facility transfusion protocol, each unit is to be infused over 2 hours (not to exceed 4 hours per unit), with vital signs obtained at baseline, 15 minutes after initiation, every 30 minutes during infusion, and at completion. The provider also orders furosemide 20 mg IV between units if the patient shows signs of fluid overload. Two registered nurses independently verify the blood product at the bedside: patient identity (name and date of birth matched to the wristband and the blood bank tag), ABO/Rh type (A-positive on both the patient's blood bank sample and the unit tag), unit number cross-matched between the blood bank slip and the unit label, and expiration date confirmed as current. Verification is completed without discrepancy, and the first unit is initiated.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At the start of the first transfusion, the patient is resting comfortably in bed at 30 degrees. She reports feeling tired but denies chest pain, shortness of breath at rest, or any new symptoms. She is alert and oriented, conversational, and in no acute distress.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (pre-transfusion): T 36.8 °C, HR 92, BP 138/82, RR 16, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Weight 78 kg (consistent with her outpatient dry weight of 77 kg). Lung sounds clear bilaterally with faint bibasilar crackles that she reports are chronic and unchanged. Heart sounds regular, S1/S2 present, no S3 or S4 appreciated. Jugular venous distension not present when the head of bed is at 45 degrees. Lower extremities show trace bilateral pitting edema at the ankles, which the patient states is her baseline. Skin warm, dry, pale. Capillary refill 3 seconds. No rash, urticaria, or pruritus. Abdomen soft, non-distended, no hepatojugular reflux. Foley catheter in place, draining clear yellow urine; intake/output sheet shows 200 mL urine output over the past 4 hours (50 mL/hr).

**LABORATORY DATA**
Pre-transfusion: Hemoglobin 6.4 g/dL, hematocrit 19.2%, WBC 7,200/µL, platelets 188,000/µL. BUN 34 mg/dL, creatinine 2.1 mg/dL (her baseline), eGFR 38 mL/min/1.73 m². Potassium 4.6 mEq/L, sodium 139 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L. BNP 280 pg/mL (mildly elevated, consistent with her compensated heart failure). Albumin 3.0 g/dL. Blood type A-positive, antibody screen negative, crossmatch compatible.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — First unit infusing, 15 minutes into the transfusion through completion of unit one (approximately 2 hours).

Fifteen-minute vital signs: T 36.9 °C, HR 90, BP 140/84, RR 16, SpO₂ 96%. No complaints. The first unit infuses without incident over 2 hours. End-of-unit vital signs: T 37.1 °C, HR 88, BP 142/86, RR 18, SpO₂ 96%. The patient reports feeling slightly less fatigued. Lung sounds remain clear with the same faint bibasilar crackles. No JVD, no change in edema. Urine output over the 2-hour infusion period: 90 mL (45 mL/hr — slightly decreased from her earlier rate). The nurse notes the mild blood pressure elevation and the slight dip in urine output but the patient is asymptomatic and hemodynamically stable. The provider is updated; the order for furosemide 20 mg IV between units is not yet triggered because the patient does not meet the protocol's threshold for signs of fluid overload (no new dyspnea, no new edema, no JVD, no desaturation). The second unit is verified by two RNs using the same bedside protocol and initiated.

**Stage 2** — Second unit infusing, 45 minutes into the second transfusion (approximately 2 hours and 45 minutes after the first unit was started).

The patient activates the call light reporting that she feels short of breath and "can't get comfortable." She is now sitting upright in bed, gripping the side rails. Vital signs: T 37.2 °C, HR 110, BP 168/96, RR 28, SpO₂ 89% on room air. Lung auscultation reveals new bilateral crackles extending from the bases to the mid-lung fields — markedly different from her baseline faint bibasilar crackles. An S3 gallop is now audible. Jugular venous distension is visible at 45 degrees. Lower extremity edema has increased to 2+ bilateral pitting, up from trace at baseline. The patient's face is flushed but there is no urticaria, no hives, no angioedema, and no wheezing. She denies itching, throat tightness, or abdominal cramping. She has a new nonproductive cough. Urine output over the past 45 minutes: 15 mL. The patient is anxious and says, "I feel like I'm drowning."

**Stage 3** — 30 minutes after the transfusion is stopped and initial interventions are implemented.

The transfusion was stopped immediately upon recognition of the respiratory distress. The IV line was kept patent with normal saline at a keep-vein-open rate. The patient was repositioned to high Fowler's position. Oxygen was applied via non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min. The provider was notified and arrived at the bedside. Furosemide 40 mg IV was administered per the provider's stat order. The blood bank was notified and the remaining blood in the tubing and bag was sent to the blood bank along with a post-transfusion blood sample and a urine sample per facility protocol.

Thirty minutes after these interventions: T 37.1 °C, HR 98 (down from 110), BP 152/88 (down from 168/96), RR 22 (down from 28), SpO₂ 94% on the non-rebreather (up from 89% on room air). The patient reports feeling "a little better" and "less like drowning." Lung crackles have receded slightly — still present at the bases and lower-mid fields but no longer extending to the mid-lung fields. JVD is less prominent. Urine output in the 30 minutes since furosemide: 180 mL of clear yellow urine. The S3 gallop is still present but softer. Edema remains 2+ but is not worsening. A portable chest X-ray obtained during this interval shows bilateral pulmonary vascular congestion, bilateral pleural effusions (small), cardiomegaly, and a widened vascular pedicle — findings consistent with hydrostatic pulmonary edema. No bilateral patchy infiltrates characteristic of non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema are seen. BNP redrawn: 890 pg/mL (up from 280 pg/mL at baseline). Post-transfusion hemoglobin: 8.1 g/dL.

**Stage 4** — 4 hours after the transfusion was stopped.

The patient has received a second dose of furosemide 20 mg IV per provider order 2 hours after the first dose, given continued but improving fluid overload. Current vital signs: T 36.9 °C, HR 84, BP 136/78, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on 4 L nasal cannula (weaned from non-rebreather). The patient is resting comfortably at 45 degrees, conversational, and states she can breathe much more easily. Lung sounds: faint bibasilar crackles only, similar to her admission baseline. JVD is no longer visible. Edema has returned to trace bilateral. S3 is no longer audible. Total urine output since the initial furosemide dose: 1,400 mL over 4 hours. Repeat BNP: 420 pg/mL (trending down from 890). The provider documents the event as transfusion-associated circulatory overload and enters orders: hold the second unit of packed red blood cells pending reassessment, resume home furosemide dose in the morning, obtain a repeat hemoglobin in 6 hours, and consult nephrology regarding future transfusion planning (slower rate, pre-transfusion diuretic, single-unit strategy). A transfusion reaction report is completed per facility policy.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient develops acute dyspnea, hypertension, tachycardia, desaturation, new bilateral crackles extending beyond her baseline, a new S3 gallop, JVD, worsening peripheral edema, decreased urine output, and a nonproductive cough — all during the second unit of packed red blood cells. Identify which of these findings represent significant new changes from the pre-transfusion baseline and which are unchanged or clinically insignificant. *Correct:* The significant new cues are the acute dyspnea, the blood pressure rise to 168/96 (from 138/82), the heart rate increase to 110 (from 92), the respiratory rate increase to 28 (from 16), the SpO₂ drop to 89% (from 96%), the bilateral crackles now extending to the mid-lung fields (baseline was faint bibasilar only), the new S3 gallop (not present at baseline), the new JVD (absent at baseline), the edema increase from trace to 2+ pitting, and the urine output decline to 15 mL over 45 minutes (from 45–50 mL/hr). The temperature of 37.2 °C is not a meaningful change from the baseline of 36.8 °C and does not represent a significant cue. The flushed face without urticaria, hives, or angioedema is a nonspecific finding in the context of the hemodynamic changes and is not a cue pointing toward an allergic mechanism. *Why:* Recognizing the full cluster of new cardiopulmonary and volume-overload findings — and distinguishing them from the patient's chronic baseline findings (trace edema, faint bibasilar crackles) and from clinically insignificant changes (trivial temperature drift) — is the first step in identifying a transfusion reaction. *Skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must determine what type of transfusion reaction is occurring. The differential includes transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO), transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI), febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction, allergic/urticarial reaction, and anaphylactic transfusion reaction. Classify the patient's findings to identify the most likely reaction type. *Correct:* The presentation is most consistent with TACO. The evidence supporting TACO includes hypertension (168/96), JVD, new S3 gallop, worsening peripheral edema, bilateral crackles in a pattern of ascending pulmonary congestion, decreased urine output, and the patient's high-risk profile (age 74, CKD stage 3b with reduced clearance, compensated heart failure, low albumin reducing oncotic pressure). TRALI is the closest competing diagnosis because it also causes acute respiratory distress and bilateral lung findings during transfusion, but TRALI characteristically presents with hypotension (not hypertension), bilateral patchy infiltrates on imaging (not vascular congestion), and the absence of volume-overload signs (JVD, S3, worsening edema) — this patient has the opposite hemodynamic picture. Febrile non-hemolytic reaction is excluded because the temperature is not meaningfully elevated (less than 1 °C rise) and the dominant findings are cardiopulmonary, not isolated fever and chills. Allergic/urticarial reaction is excluded because there is no urticaria, hives, or pruritus. Anaphylaxis is excluded because there is no hypotension, no angioedema, no bronchospasm/wheezing, no throat tightness, and no abdominal cramping. *Why:* TACO and TRALI are both acute respiratory transfusion reactions, but they have opposite hemodynamic signatures and opposite management — correctly differentiating them determines whether diuresis is appropriate (TACO) or harmful (TRALI). The patient's risk factors (advanced age, cardiac disease, renal impairment, hypoalbuminemia) and the hemodynamic profile (hypertension, elevated filling pressures, volume overload signs) all converge on TACO. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse has identified multiple problems: acute hypoxemia (SpO₂ 89%), fluid overload with pulmonary congestion, hypertension, tachycardia, and patient distress. Determine which problem demands the most immediate nursing response. *Correct:* The acute hypoxemia with respiratory distress is the highest-priority problem. The SpO₂ of 89% with a respiratory rate of 28 and the patient's subjective sensation of drowning represent an immediate threat to oxygenation and gas exchange. While the fluid overload is the underlying cause and must be addressed, the airway-breathing-circulation framework places the oxygenation deficit as the first priority — the patient needs oxygen support and positioning to improve ventilation before the volume problem can be corrected with diuretics. *Why:* In any acute deterioration, impaired oxygenation takes priority because hypoxemia can progress to respiratory failure rapidly. Stopping the transfusion removes the ongoing fluid load, and positioning and oxygen address the immediate breathing compromise, creating the physiologic window for diuresis to work. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must determine the appropriate management plan for this TACO presentation. The plan must address the immediate crisis and the underlying volume overload, and the nurse must distinguish which interventions are appropriate for TACO versus those that would be appropriate for TRALI. *Correct:* The management plan for TACO includes stopping the transfusion immediately, positioning the patient in high Fowler's position to reduce preload and improve diaphragmatic excursion, applying supplemental oxygen to correct hypoxemia, notifying the provider for diuretic orders (anticipating IV furosemide), maintaining the IV line with normal saline at a keep-vein-open rate, sending the blood product and post-transfusion samples to the blood bank per protocol, and monitoring intake and output closely with attention to diuretic response. This plan is specifically appropriate for TACO because diuresis treats the hydrostatic fluid overload. If this were TRALI, diuresis would be contraindicated because TRALI involves non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema from capillary leak — the patient is not volume-overloaded but rather has fluid shifting into the alveoli from endothelial injury, and diuresis would worsen hypotension and reduce cardiac output. TRALI management would instead focus on respiratory support (potentially mechanical ventilation), hemodynamic support with fluids or vasopressors for hypotension, and supportive care. *Why:* The distinction between TACO and TRALI management is clinically critical because the treatments are physiologically opposite. Diuresing a TRALI patient worsens hypotension; failing to diurese a TACO patient allows continued pulmonary congestion. The correct identification of TACO in decision point 2 drives the selection of diuresis as a cornerstone of the plan. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse must implement the immediate response. The actions that must occur include stopping the transfusion, repositioning the patient upright, applying oxygen, notifying the provider, and sending blood bank samples. These actions occur essentially simultaneously in a rapid-response situation — the nurse stops the pump and repositions the patient in the same moments, applies oxygen, and calls for help. The decision is which actions take priority in this concurrent set, not a rigid sequential order. *Correct:* The immediate priority actions are stopping the transfusion (to halt the ongoing fluid load contributing to the overload), positioning the patient in high Fowler's (to reduce venous return and improve respiratory mechanics), and applying high-flow oxygen (to address the SpO₂ of 89%). These three actions are performed together as the first-priority set because they are all immediately within RN scope, require no orders, and directly address the two most urgent problems — ongoing fluid administration and hypoxemia. Notifying the provider is the next priority because diuretic administration requires an order (unless a standing protocol covers it), and the provider needs to assess for further interventions. Sending the blood product and samples to the blood bank is important for investigation and is performed per protocol but does not take precedence over stabilizing the patient's respiratory status. *Why:* In a transfusion reaction with respiratory compromise, the nurse's independent actions — stopping the infusion, positioning, and oxygenating — are performed immediately because they require no order and directly mitigate harm. Provider notification follows because it enables the next tier of treatment (diuretics, imaging, further orders). Blood bank notification and sample collection are protocol-driven tasks that support investigation but do not change the patient's immediate clinical trajectory. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3 and Stage 4, the nurse must evaluate whether the interventions are working and the patient is improving. Determine which parameters confirm that the TACO is resolving and the management plan is effective. *Correct:* The parameters that confirm resolution include the trend in SpO₂ (89% → 94% → 96%, with ability to wean from non-rebreather to nasal cannula), the trend in respiratory rate (28 → 22 → 18), the trend in blood pressure (168/96 → 152/88 → 136/78, returning toward her baseline of 138/82), the trend in heart rate (110 → 98 → 84), the robust urine output in response to furosemide (1,400 mL over 4 hours), the resolution of JVD, the return of lung sounds to baseline (faint bibasilar crackles only), the disappearance of the S3 gallop, the return of edema to trace, and the downtrending BNP (280 → 890 → 420). The chest X-ray showing vascular congestion and pleural effusions at Stage 3 is a point-in-time finding that would need repeat imaging to confirm clearing, but the clinical trajectory already demonstrates improvement. *Why:* TACO resolves with diuresis and fluid removal, so the markers of resolution are the reversal of the volume-overload signs: improving oxygenation (the most critical parameter because respiratory failure was the acute threat), decreasing blood pressure and heart rate (reflecting reduced preload and afterload), brisk diuresis (confirming the kidneys are responding to furosemide and the excess volume is being cleared), and resolution of the physical exam findings of congestion (JVD, S3, crackles, edema). The BNP trend supports the clinical picture — it rose with acute decompensation and is falling with treatment. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3 and Stage 4.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Treating the faint bibasilar crackles at Stage 2 as a new finding rather than recognizing they were present at baseline — this leads to either underestimating the change (because "she always has crackles") or overweighting a chronic finding. The critical cue is not the presence of crackles but their extension from the bases to the mid-lung fields, which represents a new and significant change. Similarly, focusing on the temperature of 37.2 °C as a significant cue suggesting a febrile reaction, when the rise from 36.8 °C is clinically trivial and does not meet the threshold for a febrile non-hemolytic reaction (typically defined as a temperature rise of 1 °C or greater above 38 °C).

- *(Decision point 2)* Misidentifying the reaction as TRALI because the patient has acute respiratory distress and bilateral lung findings during a transfusion. This is a high-stakes error because TRALI and TACO have opposite hemodynamic profiles and opposite treatments. The key differentiating features are the blood pressure (hypertension in TACO versus hypotension in TRALI), the presence of volume-overload signs (JVD, S3, worsening edema — present in TACO, absent in TRALI), and the chest X-ray pattern (vascular congestion with cardiomegaly and widened vascular pedicle in TACO versus bilateral patchy infiltrates without cardiomegaly in TRALI). A student who does not systematically compare these features may default to TRALI because it is a more dramatic-sounding diagnosis.

- *(Decision point 2)* Identifying the reaction as a febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction because the patient has a mildly elevated temperature and is receiving a transfusion. Febrile non-hemolytic reactions are the most common transfusion reaction, so students may anchor on frequency rather than on the actual clinical picture. In this case, the dominant findings are cardiopulmonary and hemodynamic, not febrile — the temperature is not meaningfully elevated, and fever/chills in isolation would be the hallmark of a febrile non-hemolytic reaction, not the constellation of dyspnea, hypertension, JVD, S3, and pulmonary congestion seen here.

- *(Decision point 3)* Prioritizing the hypertension (168/96) as the most immediately dangerous problem and focusing nursing actions on blood pressure reduction rather than on oxygenation and fluid removal. While the hypertension is significant and contributes to afterload, it is a consequence of the volume overload, not the primary threat — the SpO₂ of 89% with respiratory distress is the immediate danger. The blood pressure will improve as the volume overload is corrected; treating hypertension in isolation (for example, requesting an antihypertensive) without addressing the underlying fluid overload misses the mechanism.

- *(Decision point 4)* Planning to administer diuretics for a patient who actually has TRALI — this error flows from the misidentification in decision point 2. If the nurse incorrectly identifies the reaction as TRALI and then correctly applies TRALI management (supportive respiratory care, no diuresis), the patient with TACO would not receive the diuresis needed to resolve the volume overload. Conversely, if the nurse correctly identifies TACO but then hesitates to advocate for diuresis because of a general concern about the patient's renal function (CKD stage 3b), the delay allows continued pulmonary congestion. The patient's kidneys can respond to furosemide — as demonstrated by the 1,400 mL output — and the acute volume overload is a more immediate threat than the chronic renal impairment.

- *(Decision point 5)* Continuing the transfusion at a slower rate rather than stopping it, reasoning that slowing the infusion will reduce the fluid load gradually. This is unsafe — once a transfusion reaction with respiratory compromise is identified, the transfusion must be stopped, not slowed. The remaining blood in the tubing must not be infused. Slowing the rate may be a preventive strategy for future transfusions in a TACO-prone patient, but it is not an appropriate response to an active reaction with hypoxemia.

- *(Decision point 5)* Prioritizing blood bank sample collection and paperwork over stabilizing the patient's respiratory status. While sending the blood product and post-transfusion samples to the blood bank is an important protocol step, it does not take precedence over stopping the transfusion, positioning, oxygenating, and notifying the provider. A student who is focused on "following the transfusion reaction protocol steps" may treat sample collection as the first action rather than recognizing that patient stabilization comes first.

- *(Decision point 6)* Evaluating the patient as "improved" based solely on the SpO₂ reaching 96% without assessing the full constellation of volume-overload markers (JVD resolution, lung sounds returning to baseline, edema trending down, urine output response, BNP trend). A single normalized vital sign does not confirm resolution of TACO — the patient could still have significant pulmonary congestion masked by high-flow oxygen. The ability to wean supplemental oxygen while maintaining saturation, combined with resolution of the physical exam findings of congestion, is what confirms true improvement.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the cue cluster of an acute transfusion reaction with respiratory compromise and differentiate new findings from the patient's chronic baseline.
- Differentiate TACO from TRALI based on hemodynamic profile, physical examination findings, imaging characteristics, and patient risk factors, and distinguish both from febrile non-hemolytic, allergic, and anaphylactic transfusion reactions.
- Prioritize acute hypoxemia and respiratory distress as the immediate threat in a transfusion reaction presenting with volume overload.
- Plan type-appropriate management for TACO (stop transfusion, position upright, oxygenate, diurese) and articulate why the same plan would be harmful in TRALI.
- Evaluate resolution of TACO using trending vital signs, physical examination findings, urine output response to diuretics, and BNP trajectory.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after the transfusion has been stopped, initial interventions implemented, and the chest X-ray and clinical trajectory have confirmed the diagnosis.

- *Most likely condition:* Transfusion-associated circulatory overload. The combination of acute dyspnea with hypertension (168/96), JVD, new S3 gallop, bilateral ascending pulmonary crackles, worsening peripheral edema, decreased urine output, and a chest X-ray showing bilateral vascular congestion, pleural effusions, cardiomegaly, and a widened vascular pedicle — occurring during the second unit of packed red blood cells in a 74-year-old patient with CKD, compensated heart failure, and hypoalbuminemia — is unambiguously TACO.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Transfusion-related acute lung injury — wrong here: TRALI presents with hypotension, not hypertension; bilateral patchy infiltrates on imaging without cardiomegaly, not vascular congestion with a widened pedicle; and the absence of volume-overload signs (JVD, S3, worsening edema), all of which are prominently present in this patient. The hemodynamic profile is the opposite of TRALI. (2) Anaphylactic transfusion reaction — wrong here: anaphylaxis presents with hypotension, bronchospasm/wheezing, angioedema, urticaria, and often abdominal symptoms; this patient has hypertension, no wheezing, no urticaria, no angioedema, and no throat tightness or abdominal cramping. The respiratory distress in anaphylaxis is from airway compromise, not pulmonary congestion.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Maintain the patient in high Fowler's position with supplemental oxygen and monitor respiratory status closely, including continuous pulse oximetry and readiness to escalate respiratory support if oxygenation does not continue to improve — this directly addresses the acute hypoxemia that is the immediate threat. (2) Administer IV furosemide per provider order and monitor urine output to confirm diuretic response — this is the definitive treatment for the hydrostatic volume overload causing the pulmonary congestion.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Administering IV fluids to support blood pressure, as would be appropriate in TRALI with hypotension — wrong here because the patient is hypertensive and volume-overloaded; additional fluid would worsen pulmonary congestion and could precipitate respiratory failure. (2) Resuming the transfusion at a slower rate once the SpO₂ improves to 94% — wrong because the reaction is ongoing, the volume overload has not resolved, and restarting the transfusion adds more intravascular volume before the excess has been cleared; the remaining unit should be held pending full reassessment and provider re-evaluation.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) SpO₂ trend and ability to wean supplemental oxygen — this directly tracks whether the pulmonary congestion is clearing and gas exchange is improving, which is the most critical marker of whether the patient is moving away from respiratory failure. (2) Urine output in response to diuretic therapy — brisk diuresis confirms that the excess intravascular volume is being cleared, which is the mechanism by which TACO resolves; inadequate urine output would signal the need for escalation (additional diuretic dosing, nephrology consultation, or consideration of ultrafiltration).

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Temperature trend — the patient's temperature has been clinically insignificant throughout (36.8–37.2 °C) and is not a marker of TACO resolution; monitoring temperature would be relevant if the reaction were febrile non-hemolytic, but it does not track the volume-overload physiology driving this patient's crisis. (2) Post-transfusion hemoglobin level — while the hemoglobin of 8.1 g/dL confirms the transfusion delivered red cells, it does not evaluate whether the TACO is resolving; it is a measure of transfusion efficacy, not of the reaction's trajectory, and checking it does not change the acute management of the volume overload.
