**CASE TITLE**
Lithium toxicity precipitated by thiazide diuretic initiation and volume depletion in a patient with bipolar disorder.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 52-year-old woman with a fifteen-year history of bipolar I disorder, well controlled on lithium carbonate 900 mg orally twice daily, is admitted to a medical-surgical unit from the emergency department. She also carries diagnoses of type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 1000 mg twice daily and newly diagnosed stage 1 hypertension. Ten days ago her primary care provider started hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ) 25 mg daily for the hypertension. She lives alone, works as a librarian, and has a strong therapeutic alliance with her outpatient psychiatrist, whom she sees every three months. Her last outpatient lithium level, drawn six weeks ago, was 0.9 mEq/L (therapeutic range per her psychiatry clinic protocol: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L). She has no history of renal disease; her most recent outpatient creatinine four months ago was 0.9 mg/dL. She reports that over the past week she has had poor oral intake because of persistent nausea and has been urinating frequently. She attributes the nausea to "a stomach bug" and did not contact either provider. Her current medication administration record includes lithium carbonate 900 mg PO BID, HCTZ 25 mg PO daily, and metformin 1000 mg PO BID. There is no standing order for lithium level monitoring on admission; the admitting provider has ordered a stat serum lithium level, a basic metabolic panel, and a 12-lead ECG.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient arrives on the unit at 0800 accompanied by her adult daughter. She is drowsy but arousable to voice, oriented to person and place but not to date. She complains of unrelenting nausea, coarse bilateral hand tremor, and feeling "shaky all over." Her daughter reports that over the past two days the patient has become increasingly confused, has had several episodes of vomiting and diarrhea, and "can't keep water down." The daughter adds that her mother has seemed "not herself" for about a week — more irritable, unsteady on her feet, and slurring words intermittently — but the family initially attributed this to fatigue.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 37.1 °C, heart rate 102 and regular, blood pressure 98/62, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Weight 64 kg (her documented outpatient weight four weeks ago was 68 kg). Mucous membranes dry and tacky. Skin turgor decreased, with tenting over the sternum lasting three seconds. Pupils equal, round, reactive, 3 mm bilaterally. Coarse, irregular tremor in both hands at rest and with intention; no tremor at her last outpatient visit per psychiatry notes. Deep tendon reflexes 3+ bilaterally at the patellar and Achilles tendons with three beats of non-sustained ankle clonus. Gait not assessed because of unsteadiness; the patient is placed on fall precautions. Bowel sounds hyperactive in all four quadrants. Abdomen soft, non-tender. Lungs clear bilaterally. Heart sounds regular, no murmur. Peripheral pulses palpable but thready. Capillary refill 3 seconds in the fingernail beds. Foley catheter inserted per ED; urine output in the ED over the preceding two hours was 40 mL total (approximately 0.3 mL/kg/hr). The patient is able to follow simple commands but drifts off between questions. Glasgow Coma Scale: eye opening to voice (3), confused verbal responses (4), localizes to pain (5) — total 12.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Serum lithium level: 2.8 mEq/L (therapeutic range per clinic protocol: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L; levels above 1.5 mEq/L are considered toxic). Sodium 148 mEq/L, potassium 3.1 mEq/L, chloride 96 mEq/L, bicarbonate 30 mEq/L, BUN 38 mg/dL, creatinine 1.9 mg/dL (baseline 0.9 mg/dL), glucose 142 mg/dL, calcium 9.4 mg/dL, magnesium 1.6 mg/dL (reference 1.7–2.2 mg/dL), phosphorus 3.8 mg/dL. eGFR calculated at 28 mL/min/1.73 m². Serum osmolality 312 mOsm/kg (reference 275–295). ECG: sinus tachycardia at 102 bpm, flattened T waves in leads V4–V6, no ST changes, QTc 460 ms.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0800 to 1200 (first four hours on the unit). The admitting provider reviews the labs and orders: discontinue lithium, discontinue HCTZ, hold metformin (eGFR < 30), initiate normal saline (0.9% NaCl) IV bolus 500 mL over one hour then 200 mL/hr continuous, strict intake and output, serum lithium level and basic metabolic panel to be redrawn at 1200, continuous cardiac monitoring, and seizure precautions. The nurse initiates the IV fluid bolus and places the patient on telemetry. At 0930 the patient vomits 150 mL of bilious fluid and becomes more lethargic; she now opens her eyes only to loud verbal stimulation and her verbal responses are inappropriate words. Heart rate rises to 112, blood pressure 94/58. The nurse repositions the patient on her side, suctions the oropharynx, and ensures the head of bed is elevated to 30 degrees. The daughter, who has been at the bedside, becomes tearful and asks, "Is she going to be okay? She was fine two weeks ago — is this from the new blood pressure pill?" She also asks whether her mother's "mental illness medicine" caused this and whether it should be stopped permanently.

**Stage 2** — 1200 to 1800 (hours four through ten). Repeat labs at 1200: lithium 2.6 mEq/L, sodium 146 mEq/L, potassium 3.4 mEq/L, creatinine 1.8 mg/dL, BUN 34 mg/dL, eGFR 30 mL/min/1.73 m². Urine output over the preceding four hours: 120 mL (0.5 mL/kg/hr — improved from 0.3 but still marginal). The lithium level has dropped only 0.2 mEq/L despite aggressive IV hydration. The patient remains obtunded; she now has intermittent myoclonic jerks of the upper extremities and new-onset coarse fasciculations in the facial muscles. Deep tendon reflexes remain 3+ with clonus. ECG unchanged. The provider discusses the case with nephrology; the nephrologist recommends emergent hemodialysis given the persistently elevated lithium level, worsening neurotoxicity, and impaired renal clearance. A temporary hemodialysis catheter is to be placed at the bedside. The provider also orders potassium chloride 20 mEq IV in 100 mL NS over one hour (peripheral line, infusion pump) to address the hypokalemia before dialysis. The daughter, still at the bedside, states she feels guilty for not bringing her mother in sooner and asks the nurse whether her mother will have permanent brain damage.

**Stage 3** — 1800 to 0600 the following morning (hours ten through twenty-two). Hemodialysis is initiated at 1900 and runs for four hours. During the first hour of dialysis the patient's blood pressure drops to 86/52 and heart rate rises to 118. The dialysis nurse reduces or pauses ultrafiltration per protocol and gives NS; blood pressure recovers to 102/66 within twenty minutes. Post-dialysis labs at 2300: lithium 1.1 mEq/L, sodium 142 mEq/L, potassium 3.8 mEq/L, creatinine 1.4 mg/dL, BUN 26 mg/dL, eGFR 42 mL/min/1.73 m². The patient is now opening her eyes spontaneously, following commands, and speaking in full sentences, though she reports feeling "foggy" and fatigued. Tremor has diminished to a fine, intermittent resting tremor in the right hand only. Myoclonic jerks have ceased. Deep tendon reflexes are 2+ without clonus. Heart rate 86, blood pressure 112/70, urine output 55 mL/hr. At 0200 a repeat lithium level is drawn per the nephrologist's order to check for rebound: lithium 1.3 mEq/L. The nephrologist states that a second dialysis session may be needed if the level continues to rise; the patient is to remain on continuous telemetry and have lithium levels drawn every four hours. By 0600 the lithium level is 1.2 mEq/L and the patient is alert, oriented to person, place, and date, and tolerating sips of clear liquids. She asks the nurse, "Will I ever be able to take lithium again, or do I need a different medicine?"

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At admission, the nurse reviews the medication list, the new HCTZ order from ten days ago, the patient's history of poor oral intake and vomiting, and the serum lithium level of 2.8 mEq/L. The nurse must identify the mechanism that precipitated the toxic lithium level. *Correct action:* Recognize that the thiazide diuretic increased sodium and water excretion at the distal tubule, causing volume depletion; the kidneys compensated by increasing proximal tubular reabsorption of sodium — and lithium, which is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule alongside sodium. Concurrent vomiting and poor oral intake worsened the volume deficit, further reducing renal lithium clearance. The interaction between HCTZ and lithium, compounded by dehydration, is the precipitant. *Why it is correct:* Lithium is almost entirely renally excreted and is handled like sodium in the proximal tubule. Any state that increases proximal sodium reabsorption — volume depletion, thiazide diuretics, low-sodium diet, NSAID use — reduces lithium clearance and raises serum levels. Identifying the mechanism guides the nurse's teaching, monitoring, and anticipation of the treatment plan (aggressive volume repletion and drug discontinuation). *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Admission data (before Stage 1 changes).

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient vomits and becomes more obtunded (eyes open only to loud voice, inappropriate words). Heart rate is 112, blood pressure 94/58. The nurse must decide the priority set of actions. *Correct action:* Protect the airway — lateral positioning, suctioning, head-of-bed elevation — and immediately notify the provider of the declining neurological status and hemodynamic instability. These actions occur essentially simultaneously; airway protection and provider notification are the priority pair, not a strict sequence of one before the other. Continuing the ordered IV fluid resuscitation supports the hemodynamics. *Why it is correct:* The patient's declining level of consciousness with active vomiting creates aspiration risk; airway compromise is the most immediate life threat. The worsening obtundation and hypotension signal that the current treatment plan (IV fluids alone) may be insufficient and the provider must be informed so the plan can be escalated. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the lithium level has fallen only from 2.8 to 2.6 mEq/L despite four hours of aggressive IV hydration, and the patient now has myoclonic jerks and facial fasciculations with impaired renal function. The nephrologist recommends emergent hemodialysis. The nurse must understand why dialysis is indicated and prepare the patient. *Correct action:* Recognize that dialysis is indicated because severe lithium toxicity is accompanied by decreased level of consciousness/progressive neurotoxicity and impaired renal clearance, with minimal improvement after IV fluids. The lithium level supports severity, but the neurotoxicity is the decisive escalation cue. Lithium is a small molecule with negligible protein binding and a low volume of distribution, making it highly dialyzable. The nurse prepares by ensuring large-bore IV access, verifying consent for the dialysis catheter, placing the patient on continuous cardiac monitoring, having emergency equipment at the bedside, communicating the potassium of 3.4 mEq/L to nephrology/dialysis, clarifying the IV potassium order against protocol, and verifying that the dialysis prescription addresses potassium management. *Why it is correct:* When renal clearance is impaired and neurotoxicity is progressing, the kidneys cannot clear lithium fast enough even with hydration. Hemodialysis removes lithium directly from the blood. Preparation includes addressing hypokalemia because dialysis shifts potassium further, risking arrhythmia. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the provider orders potassium chloride 20 mEq IV in 100 mL NS over one hour via peripheral line on an infusion pump. The nurse must verify the safety of this order before administering. *Correct action:* Before administering, the nurse checks the facility IV potassium protocol and recognizes that the ordered rate/concentration exceeds the unit’s usual peripheral limit. The nurse holds the infusion and clarifies the order, anticipating a safer order such as 10 mEq in 100 mL over one hour via peripheral line, or central-line administration/modified dialysis prescription if more rapid replacement is required. *Why it is correct:* IV potassium at high concentrations or rates can cause fatal arrhythmia. FDA labeling for concentrated ready-to-use potassium chloride says it must be given with a calibrated infusion device, recommends continuous cardiac monitoring/frequent potassium checks for concentrated products, and says usual administration should not exceed 10 mEq/hour when serum potassium is >2.5 mEq/L. ISMP’s high-alert medication self-assessment also emphasizes protocols specifying maximum concentration/rate, central-line thresholds, and required monitoring for IV electrolytes. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, post-dialysis lithium is 1.1 mEq/L and the patient has improved markedly, but a recheck at 0200 shows lithium has risen to 1.3 mEq/L. The nurse must evaluate this finding. *Correct action:* Recognize that the rise from 1.1 to 1.3 mEq/L represents lithium rebound — redistribution of lithium from the intracellular and tissue compartments back into the serum after dialysis has cleared the extracellular lithium. This is expected and is the reason the nephrologist ordered serial lithium levels every four hours. The nurse continues to monitor the trend; if the level continues to rise above 1.5 mEq/L or neurological status worsens, the nurse notifies the provider because a second dialysis session may be needed. The 0600 level of 1.2 mEq/L confirms the rebound is stabilizing. *Why it is correct:* Lithium distributes into cells and brain tissue; hemodialysis clears only the extracellular compartment. Post-dialysis rebound is well described and can push levels back into the toxic range, necessitating repeat dialysis. A single post-dialysis level does not confirm resolution. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* Throughout the admission, the daughter is distressed, expressing guilt and asking whether her mother will have brain damage and whether lithium should be stopped permanently. The patient, once alert at Stage 3, asks whether she can ever take lithium again. The nurse must address the psychosocial and educational needs of both the patient and the family member. *Correct action:* Acknowledge the daughter's fear and guilt with empathic, non-judgmental statements; provide factual reassurance that the neurological improvement already observed is a positive sign, while being honest that the medical team will continue to monitor. Clarify that lithium toxicity was precipitated by a specific drug interaction and dehydration, not by a failure of the patient's psychiatric treatment or the family's vigilance. For the patient's question about future lithium use, explain that the decision will be made collaboratively by the psychiatrist and the patient once she has fully recovered, and that many patients safely resume lithium with closer monitoring and avoidance of interacting drugs. Do not make promises about the absence of long-term effects or commit to a specific future medication plan — those are provider-scope decisions. *Why it is correct:* Psychosocial support and accurate health teaching are within RN scope and are essential for therapeutic alliance, adherence, and family coping. Premature reassurance ("she'll be completely fine") or premature medication decisions ("she should never take lithium again") are outside the nurse's scope and could be inaccurate. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues (emotional distress), generate solutions (therapeutic communication and teaching). *Answerable from:* Stage 1 for the daughter's initial distress; Stage 3 for the patient's question about future lithium use.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Attributing the toxic lithium level solely to the patient's poor oral intake and vomiting while overlooking the role of the newly added HCTZ. Dehydration alone could raise the level, but the thiazide is the primary precipitant because it directly impairs renal lithium clearance through proximal tubular reabsorption. Missing the drug interaction means the nurse cannot teach the patient to avoid this combination in the future or anticipate that volume repletion alone may not be sufficient.

- *(Decision 1)* Assuming the lithium dose was increased or that the patient took extra lithium. The dose has been stable for years and the last level was therapeutic; the change was the addition of HCTZ, not a lithium dose change. Anchoring on "too much lithium" rather than "impaired clearance" leads to the wrong teaching and the wrong anticipatory plan.

- *(Decision 2)* Prioritizing a repeat lithium level draw or a full neurological assessment before protecting the airway in a vomiting, obtunded patient. While both are important, aspiration is the immediate life threat and must be addressed first. Assessment and lab draws can proceed once the airway is secured.

- *(Decision 2)* Administering an antiemetic as the first action to stop the vomiting, rather than positioning and suctioning. Antiemetics take time to work and do not protect the airway of an obtunded patient who is actively vomiting now.

- *(Decision 3)* Believing that continued aggressive IV hydration will be sufficient to clear the lithium and that dialysis is unnecessary. The Stage 2 data show that four hours of vigorous IV fluids dropped the level by only 0.2 mEq/L with impaired renal function and worsening neurotoxicity — the kidneys cannot keep up. Delaying dialysis in this setting risks irreversible neurological injury.

- *(Decision 4)* Administering the IV potassium as ordered without verifying the concentration against the unit's peripheral limits. Rapid or concentrated peripheral IV potassium infusion can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmia or severe phlebitis. This is a high-alert medication requiring adherence to strict maximum concentration and rate protocols.

- *(Decision 5)* Interpreting the post-dialysis lithium of 1.1 mEq/L as definitive resolution and discontinuing serial monitoring. Lithium rebound is expected; the rise to 1.3 mEq/L at 0200 confirms redistribution is occurring. Stopping monitoring prematurely could miss a clinically significant rebound requiring repeat dialysis.

- *(Decision 6)* Telling the daughter definitively that her mother will have no lasting effects, or telling the patient she should never take lithium again. Both statements exceed RN scope and may be inaccurate. The appropriate response is honest, empathic communication that acknowledges uncertainty and defers definitive prognostic and prescribing decisions to the medical team.

- *(Decision 6)* Dismissing the daughter's guilt or redirecting the conversation entirely to clinical tasks. The daughter's emotional distress is a legitimate nursing concern; ignoring it undermines the therapeutic relationship and may impair the family's ability to participate in discharge planning and future medication safety.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Explain the pharmacokinetic mechanism by which thiazide diuretics precipitate lithium toxicity, including the role of proximal tubular sodium and lithium reabsorption in the setting of volume depletion.
- Prioritize airway protection and provider notification in a patient with declining consciousness and active vomiting during lithium toxicity.
- Identify the clinical and laboratory criteria that indicate the need for emergent hemodialysis in lithium toxicity — specifically, a persistently elevated level despite hydration, impaired renal function, and progressive neurotoxicity.
- Apply medication safety principles to IV potassium administration, including concentration limits, pump delivery, cardiac monitoring, and site assessment.
- Evaluate post-hemodialysis lithium levels for rebound and recognize when repeat dialysis may be necessary.
- Provide psychosocially appropriate, scope-consistent communication to a distressed family member and to a patient asking about future medication management after a toxicity event.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, after the repeat lithium level confirms minimal clearance despite aggressive IV hydration (2.8 → 2.6 mEq/L over four hours), renal function remains impaired, and the patient has developed new myoclonic jerks and facial fasciculations on top of persistent obtundation — the full picture of severe lithium neurotoxicity with inadequate renal clearance has declared, and the nephrologist has recommended emergent hemodialysis.

- *Most likely condition:* Severe lithium toxicity precipitated by thiazide diuretic–lithium interaction and volume depletion. The supratherapeutic lithium level of 2.6 mEq/L (down from 2.8), the progressive neurotoxicity (obtundation, myoclonus, hyperreflexia with clonus, facial fasciculations), the acute kidney injury (creatinine 1.8 from a baseline of 0.9, eGFR 30), and the clear temporal relationship to HCTZ initiation ten days ago with concurrent dehydration make this diagnosis unambiguous.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Bipolar disorder exacerbation or manic episode — wrong here: the neurological findings (obtundation, myoclonus, hyperreflexia, clonus) are not features of mania; the supratherapeutic lithium level and the acute kidney injury with volume depletion explain the entire presentation, and the patient's psychiatric history has been stable. (2) Acute gastroenteritis with dehydration causing the altered mental status — wrong: while the vomiting and diarrhea contributed to volume depletion, simple dehydration does not produce myoclonus, hyperreflexia with clonus, or a lithium level of 2.6 mEq/L; the neurotoxic syndrome is specific to lithium and cannot be explained by dehydration alone.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Prepare the patient for emergent hemodialysis — verify consent, ensure the dialysis catheter placement site is prepped, confirm continuous cardiac monitoring, have emergency equipment at the bedside, and clarify potassium replacement and coordinate potassium management with dialysis (to address dialysis-related hypokalemia and arrhythmia risk). (2) Continue to protect the airway and maintain aspiration precautions — the patient remains obtunded with a history of active vomiting; lateral positioning, suction at the bedside, and head-of-bed elevation must be maintained throughout the pre-dialysis and dialysis period.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Continuing IV fluid resuscitation as the sole treatment and deferring dialysis — wrong: four hours of vigorous hydration dropped the lithium by only 0.2 mEq/L with impaired renal function and worsening neurotoxicity; the kidneys cannot clear lithium fast enough, and delaying dialysis risks irreversible neurological injury. (2) Administering a dose of lithium that was due before the discontinuation order, reasoning that the patient needs her psychiatric medication — wrong and dangerous: lithium is the cause of the toxicity and has been discontinued; giving another dose would further elevate the serum level and worsen neurotoxicity.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Serial serum lithium levels — tracking the lithium concentration before, immediately after, and in the hours following dialysis is essential to confirm adequate clearance and to detect post-dialysis rebound, which may necessitate a second dialysis session. (2) Neurological status, specifically level of consciousness, presence or absence of myoclonus and tremor, and deep tendon reflex intensity — improvement in these findings (progressing from obtundation to alertness, cessation of myoclonic jerks, normalization of reflexes) is the clinical confirmation that neurotoxicity is resolving and correlates with falling lithium levels.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Serum glucose — the glucose of 142 mg/dL is mildly elevated and related to the patient's diabetes and stress response, but it is not a marker of lithium toxicity resolution or dialysis effectiveness; monitoring it here is not the priority evaluation metric. (2) Serum calcium — serum calcium is normal here and is not a priority marker for acute lithium clearance or post-dialysis rebound, although chronic lithium therapy can affect calcium/parathyroid physiology.
