**CASE TITLE**
Acute ischemic stroke with tPA eligibility evaluation in a patient on warfarin.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 68-year-old man with a history of hypertension managed with amlodipine 10 mg daily and atrial fibrillation managed with warfarin and metoprolol 50 mg twice daily. He has been on warfarin for three years for stroke prophylaxis. His most recent INR at his anticoagulation clinic five days ago was 2.3 and within his therapeutic range of 2.0–3.0. He lives with his wife, who witnessed the onset of his symptoms. He has no history of prior stroke, no recent surgery, no known bleeding disorders, and no prior intracranial hemorrhage. He is brought to the emergency department by EMS after his wife called 911 when she noticed he suddenly could not speak clearly and dropped his coffee cup with his right hand. EMS documented the last known well time as 45 minutes prior to ED arrival, based on the wife's report that he was completely normal when she handed him the coffee.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient arrives on the EMS stretcher. He is awake, eyes open, looking to the left. He attempts to speak but produces only single-word utterances and short, effortful phrases that are often incorrect — he says "chair" when he appears to mean "arm," and he nods or shakes his head to yes/no questions but cannot reliably repeat sentences. He follows simple one-step commands with his left side but does not move his right arm or right leg to command. His wife is at the bedside and reports he was in his usual state of health this morning, took his medications including warfarin, ate breakfast, and was reading the newspaper when she went to the kitchen. When she returned approximately five minutes later, he was slumped to the right in his chair, the coffee cup was on the floor, and he could not form words. She states he has never had anything like this before.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Vital signs: temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 88 beats per minute and irregularly irregular, blood pressure 188/104 mmHg (confirmed on repeat manual cuff in the left arm: 186/102), respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 98% on room air. Weight 92 kg per EMS report.

Neurological examination: the patient is alert and attentive but with a forced leftward gaze preference that can be partially overcome. He has a dense expressive aphasia — he comprehends simple commands but produces only effortful, paraphasic single words; repetition is severely impaired. Right-sided facial droop involving the lower face. Right upper extremity: no movement against gravity, drifts immediately to the bed when passively lifted. Right lower extremity: trace movement at the hip, no movement at the ankle, drifts to the bed within five seconds. Left upper and lower extremities: full strength, moves all joints against gravity and resistance. Sensation: diminished to light touch on the right arm and leg; intact on the left. Right plantar response is extensor (Babinski positive). No dysarthria distinguishable from the aphasia. NIHSS score on arrival is 14, consistent with a moderate-to-severe stroke.

Cardiac: irregularly irregular rhythm, no murmurs, no gallops. Lungs: clear bilaterally. Abdomen: soft, non-tender, non-distended. Skin: warm, dry, no bruising, no petechiae. Peripheral pulses palpable and symmetric. No signs of head trauma. Pupils equal, round, reactive to light bilaterally at 3 mm.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Stat labs drawn on arrival: INR 1.8, PT 21.4 seconds, aPTT 34 seconds, platelet count 188,000/µL, glucose 142 mg/dL, sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, BUN 18 mg/dL, hemoglobin 14.1 g/dL, WBC 9,200/µL, troponin I less than 0.01 ng/mL.

Non-contrast CT head completed within 12 minutes of arrival: no evidence of intracranial hemorrhage, no hyperdense vessel sign, no early ischemic changes. No midline shift.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0 to 30 minutes after ED arrival. The stroke team is activated on arrival. The non-contrast CT is completed and read as negative for hemorrhage. The stat INR returns at 1.8. The stroke neurologist reviews the case at the bedside. The patient's blood pressure remains elevated at 186/102 on repeat measurement. The neurologist discusses the tPA eligibility checklist with the ED team. The patient is within the time window — last known well was 45 minutes ago, now approximately 75 minutes from onset. There is no hemorrhage on CT. However, the stat INR is 1.8. Per the institutional acute stroke protocol, IV alteplase is contraindicated when the INR exceeds 1.7 at the time of intended administration. The neurologist communicates that the patient is not a candidate for IV alteplase due to the INR and orders a CT angiography of the head and neck to evaluate for large vessel occlusion and potential mechanical thrombectomy candidacy. The patient's neurological examination is unchanged: NIHSS remains 14, right hemiplegia and expressive aphasia persist. The wife asks if her husband is going to get "the clot-busting medicine" she has heard about.

**Stage 2** — 30 to 90 minutes after ED arrival. The CTA is completed and demonstrates a complete occlusion of the left middle cerebral artery at the M1 segment, with robust collateral flow. CT perfusion imaging shows a large ischemic penumbra with a small infarct core — a favorable mismatch pattern indicating substantial salvageable brain tissue. The neurointerventionalist is consulted and agrees the patient is a candidate for mechanical thrombectomy. The patient is consented through the wife, who holds durable power of attorney for healthcare decisions, as the patient cannot provide informed consent due to his aphasia. The blood pressure must be managed before and during the procedure. The provider orders IV nicardipine infusion, starting at 5 mg/hr per the institutional stroke protocol, titrated to maintain systolic blood pressure below 185 mmHg. After 20 minutes of nicardipine, the blood pressure is 168/94. The patient is prepared for transfer to the neurointerventional suite. His neurological status is unchanged: NIHSS 14, right hemiplegia, expressive aphasia. He remains alert. Heart rate is 84, irregularly irregular. SpO₂ 97% on room air. A peripheral IV is patent in the left antecubital fossa; a second 18-gauge IV is placed in the left forearm. The nurse places the patient on continuous cardiac monitoring and ensures the stroke flow sheet is current. The patient has received nothing by mouth since arrival.

**Stage 3** — 2 to 4 hours after ED arrival (post-thrombectomy). The mechanical thrombectomy is performed successfully. Angiography confirms recanalization of the left MCA with restored flow (TICI 2b/3). The patient is transferred to the neuro-ICU for post-procedural monitoring. On arrival to the neuro-ICU, the patient's vital signs are: temperature 37.1 °C, heart rate 82 irregularly irregular, blood pressure 156/88 on nicardipine at 5 mg/hr, respiratory rate 14, SpO₂ 98% on 2 L nasal cannula. The post-procedure blood pressure target per the neurointerventionalist's orders is systolic below 180 mmHg for the first 24 hours. The patient's neurological examination shows improvement: he is now producing two- to three-word phrases with effort, follows two-step commands, and has antigravity movement in the right lower extremity (can lift the leg off the bed briefly before it drifts down). Right upper extremity remains plegic — no movement against gravity. Right facial droop persists but is less pronounced. NIHSS is now 9, down from 14. Pupils remain equal and reactive. He is alert and appears to recognize his wife. The nicardipine infusion is running. Warfarin is held. No anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents have been administered. The neurointerventionalist's post-procedure orders include: neurological checks every 15 minutes for 2 hours then every 30 minutes for 6 hours, blood pressure monitoring every 15 minutes during nicardipine titration, groin site checks every 15 minutes for 2 hours (right femoral artery access), strict NPO until a formal bedside dysphagia screening is completed by a trained nurse, and a repeat non-contrast CT head in 24 hours or sooner if any neurological deterioration.

Forty-five minutes into the neuro-ICU stay, the patient becomes drowsy. He stops following commands and his verbal output ceases. His right pupil is now 5 mm and sluggishly reactive; the left remains 3 mm and briskly reactive. Blood pressure is 174/96. Heart rate is 72, regular. The right groin site dressing is clean, dry, and intact with no hematoma.

**Stage 4** — Immediate response to neurological deterioration. The neuro-ICU nurse recognizes the acute change: new somnolence, loss of verbal output, right pupil dilation, and cessation of command-following represent a significant neurological decline from the post-procedure baseline. The nurse immediately notifies the neurointerventionalist and the neurology team. An emergent non-contrast CT head is obtained. The CT shows a 3 cm left frontoparietal intraparenchymal hemorrhage with surrounding edema and 4 mm of leftward-to-rightward midline shift. The nicardipine infusion is titrated upward to achieve a systolic blood pressure target below 140 mmHg per the provider's stat order. Neurosurgery is consulted. The patient is intubated for airway protection due to declining consciousness (GCS 7: eyes open to pain, no verbal response, abnormal flexion). The wife is updated and the goals-of-care discussion is initiated by the attending neurologist. Repeat labs are drawn: INR 1.6, hemoglobin 12.8 g/dL (down from 14.1), platelets 174,000/µL. The provider orders IV vitamin K 10 mg and four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate to reverse the residual anticoagulation effect. The patient is stabilized in the neuro-ICU with the head of bed elevated to 30 degrees, seizure precautions in place, and continuous ICP monitoring being arranged.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse reviews the patient's presentation — sudden-onset right-sided hemiplegia, expressive aphasia, right facial droop, forced leftward gaze preference, NIHSS 14, irregularly irregular heart rhythm, and a history of atrial fibrillation on warfarin — and must identify which findings are consistent with a left middle cerebral artery territory ischemic stroke and which are unrelated or do not fit that pattern. The intact left-sided strength, symmetric and reactive pupils, clear lungs, and normal troponin are also present in the assessment. *Correct action:* The right-sided hemiplegia (arm worse than leg), expressive aphasia (Broca area), right lower facial droop, and leftward gaze preference are all classic findings of a left MCA territory stroke. The sudden onset and the atrial fibrillation point to a cardioembolic mechanism. The intact left-sided strength, symmetric reactive pupils, clear lungs, soft abdomen, and normal troponin are not part of the stroke syndrome — they are normal or unrelated findings. *Why it is correct:* The left MCA supplies the motor strip (contralateral arm and leg, with arm typically more affected than leg in MCA strokes), Broca's area in the dominant hemisphere (producing expressive aphasia), the lower face motor cortex (contralateral lower facial droop), and the frontal eye fields (producing ipsilateral gaze preference acutely). Atrial fibrillation is the most common cause of cardioembolic stroke. Recognizing the full constellation and distinguishing it from unrelated normal findings is the first step in activating the correct treatment pathway. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the stat INR returns at 1.8, the non-contrast CT shows no hemorrhage, and the patient is 75 minutes from last known well. The nurse must interpret these results in the context of IV alteplase eligibility. *Correct action:* The nurse recognizes that while the CT is negative for hemorrhage and the patient is well within the 3-to-4.5-hour window from last known well, the stat INR of 1.8 exceeds the tPA eligibility threshold. Per the institutional acute stroke protocol, IV alteplase is contraindicated when the INR exceeds 1.7 at the time of intended administration. The nurse communicates this critical finding to the stroke neurologist to ensure the team is aware that the patient is not a candidate for IV thrombolysis and that an alternative reperfusion strategy must be pursued. *Why it is correct:* IV alteplase carries a significant risk of hemorrhagic transformation. In patients on warfarin with an INR above 1.7, the bleeding risk is unacceptably elevated because the coagulation cascade is already impaired. The relevant INR is the stat value drawn at the time of the acute presentation — not the clinic value from five days ago (which was 2.3 and would also have been disqualifying, but is not the current value). The 45-minute onset-to-arrival time and the absence of hemorrhage on CT satisfy two of the major eligibility criteria, but the INR alone is sufficient to exclude the patient from IV alteplase. The nurse's role is to ensure this lab result is communicated promptly so the team can pivot to mechanical thrombectomy evaluation without delay. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the team must determine the most likely stroke mechanism and confirm the stroke subtype to guide the next steps. The patient has atrial fibrillation, is on warfarin, has a sudden-onset focal deficit consistent with a single large-vessel territory, and the CT shows no hemorrhage. *Correct action:* The dominant hypothesis is an acute ischemic stroke of cardioembolic origin, most likely a left MCA occlusion from an atrial fibrillation-related thrombus. Hemorrhagic stroke is ruled out by the CT. A lacunar infarct is unlikely given the severity (NIHSS 14) and the cortical signs (aphasia, gaze preference). The immediate priority is to pursue mechanical thrombectomy evaluation since IV alteplase is contraindicated. *Why it is correct:* Atrial fibrillation is the leading cause of cardioembolic stroke. The sudden onset of a large-territory deficit (motor, language, gaze) without prodrome or stepwise progression is the classic cardioembolic pattern. The negative CT excludes hemorrhage. Lacunar strokes produce isolated motor or sensory deficits without cortical signs and typically have NIHSS scores below 5–7. With IV alteplase excluded by the INR, the next best reperfusion option for a large vessel occlusion is mechanical thrombectomy, which requires CTA confirmation of the occlusion site. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the CTA confirms a left M1 occlusion with favorable perfusion mismatch, and the patient is a thrombectomy candidate. The blood pressure is 186/102 and must be reduced before the procedure. The nurse must identify the appropriate interventions and their rationale. *Correct action:* Initiate the IV nicardipine infusion as ordered at 5 mg/hr, titrated to achieve a systolic blood pressure below 185 mmHg. Nicardipine (or IV labetalol as an alternative) is the appropriate agent — IV nitroprusside is not used in acute ischemic stroke because it raises intracranial pressure and can cause unpredictable hypotension that worsens ischemic injury. Simultaneously, the nurse ensures the patient remains NPO, maintains two patent large-bore IVs, continues cardiac monitoring, and prepares the patient for transfer to the neurointerventional suite. The nurse also verifies that informed consent has been obtained through the wife as the healthcare proxy, given the patient's inability to consent due to aphasia. *Why it is correct:* Elevated blood pressure in acute ischemic stroke is initially a compensatory mechanism to maintain perfusion through collaterals. However, blood pressure must be below 185/110 before any reperfusion therapy (IV alteplase or thrombectomy) to reduce the risk of hemorrhagic transformation. Nicardipine is a titratable calcium channel blocker that allows precise blood pressure control without the risks of nitroprusside (cyanide toxicity, ICP elevation, precipitous drops). The NPO status is essential because a formal dysphagia screen has not been performed, and stroke patients are at high risk for aspiration. Consent through the proxy is appropriate when the patient lacks decision-making capacity due to the neurological deficit. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, forty-five minutes into the neuro-ICU stay, the patient acutely deteriorates — new somnolence, loss of verbal output, cessation of command-following, and a new right pupil dilation from 3 mm to 5 mm with sluggish reactivity. The post-thrombectomy baseline had shown improvement (NIHSS 9, producing phrases, following commands). The nurse must act immediately. *Correct action:* The nurse recognizes this as acute neurological deterioration consistent with a post-reperfusion intracranial hemorrhage until proven otherwise. The immediate actions are: notify the neurointerventionalist and neurology team stat, obtain an emergent non-contrast CT head, maintain airway patency and prepare for possible intubation, continue blood pressure monitoring, and perform a rapid focused neurological assessment to document the change from baseline. These actions occur essentially simultaneously as a coordinated emergency response — the notification and the CT order happen together, not in a rigid sequence where one waits for the other. *Why it is correct:* Intracranial hemorrhage is the most feared complication after any reperfusion therapy for ischemic stroke. The hallmark is acute neurological decline from the post-procedure baseline — not gradual fatigue or medication sedation. The new pupil asymmetry (ipsilateral dilation on the side of the hemorrhage due to uncal herniation compressing cranial nerve III) is an ominous sign of mass effect. The emergent CT confirms the diagnosis and guides the next intervention (reversal of anticoagulation, neurosurgical consultation, possible surgical evacuation). Every minute of delay worsens outcomes. The nurse does not wait for the next scheduled neurological check — the change is recognized and acted on immediately. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3 (the deterioration occurs at Stage 3, and the actions are taken at the transition to Stage 4).

6. *Situation:* At Stage 4, the CT confirms a left frontoparietal hemorrhage with midline shift. The patient is intubated, anticoagulation is being reversed, and neurosurgery is consulted. The nurse must identify which parameters confirm whether the patient is stabilizing or continuing to deteriorate. *Correct action:* The two most important parameters to monitor are the neurological examination (specifically the level of consciousness via GCS, pupil size and reactivity, and motor response — tracked serially every 15 minutes) and the blood pressure response to the uptitrated nicardipine (confirming that the systolic is maintained below 140 mmHg per the stat order, as uncontrolled hypertension drives hematoma expansion). A repeat CT will be needed to assess hematoma stability, but the bedside parameters that the nurse continuously evaluates are the neurological examination and the blood pressure. *Why it is correct:* In acute intracerebral hemorrhage, hematoma expansion is the primary driver of early deterioration, and it is promoted by elevated blood pressure and residual coagulopathy. The neurological examination is the most sensitive real-time indicator of whether the hemorrhage is expanding (further decline in GCS, worsening pupil asymmetry, loss of motor responses) or stabilizing. Blood pressure control to below 140 mmHg systolic in acute ICH reduces the rate of hematoma expansion. The INR reversal (vitamin K and four-factor PCC) addresses the coagulopathy, and the nurse monitors for successful reversal by tracking the repeat INR and watching for clinical signs of ongoing bleeding (declining hemoglobin, worsening examination). *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 4.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Decision point 1: Failing to recognize the leftward gaze preference as part of the stroke syndrome and instead attributing it to the patient "looking at his wife" or being inattentive. Gaze preference toward the side of the lesion is a classic cortical sign in large MCA strokes and contributes to the NIHSS score. Missing it underestimates the stroke severity.

Decision point 2: Using the INR from the anticoagulation clinic five days ago (2.3) instead of the stat INR (1.8) to determine tPA eligibility. The eligibility determination requires the INR at the time of intended alteplase administration, not a historical value. While both values exceed 1.7 in this case, the principle matters — a patient whose clinic INR was 1.9 but whose stat INR is 1.5 would be eligible, and using the old value would wrongly exclude them.

Decision point 2: Assuming that because the CT shows no hemorrhage and the patient is within the time window, tPA can be given — overlooking the INR contraindication. The CT and the time window are necessary but not sufficient conditions. The INR must also be at or below 1.7. Administering alteplase to a patient with an INR of 1.8 significantly increases the risk of hemorrhagic transformation.

Decision point 3: Attributing the stroke to a hypertensive emergency because the blood pressure is 188/104, rather than recognizing the elevated blood pressure as a compensatory response to the acute ischemia. Hypertensive encephalopathy produces diffuse symptoms (headache, confusion, visual changes, seizures), not a focal deficit in a single vascular territory with cortical signs. Misidentifying the mechanism delays reperfusion therapy.

Decision point 4: Administering IV nitroprusside to lower the blood pressure rapidly. Nitroprusside is contraindicated in acute ischemic stroke because it is a potent cerebral vasodilator that raises intracranial pressure and can cause precipitous, unpredictable hypotension that worsens cerebral ischemia. Nicardipine or labetalol are the appropriate agents because they allow controlled, titratable reduction without these risks.

Decision point 4: Giving the patient oral medications, ice chips, or water before a formal dysphagia screen has been completed. Stroke patients have a high incidence of dysphagia and silent aspiration. Administering anything by mouth before screening risks aspiration pneumonia, which is a leading cause of post-stroke morbidity and mortality. The NPO status must be maintained until the screen is passed.

Decision point 5: Interpreting the patient's somnolence as post-procedural sedation wearing off or fatigue, rather than recognizing it as acute neurological deterioration. The patient had been alert and improving (NIHSS 9, producing phrases) after thrombectomy. A decline to somnolence with loss of verbal output and new pupil asymmetry is not a normal post-procedural course — it is a neurological emergency. Delaying the response to "let the patient rest" or waiting for the next scheduled neurological check could be fatal.

Decision point 5: Focusing on the groin access site as the source of the deterioration because the patient had a femoral artery puncture. While groin site hemorrhage and retroperitoneal bleeding are real post-thrombectomy complications, the clinical picture here — somnolence, loss of speech, pupil asymmetry — is neurological, not hemorrhagic shock. The blood pressure is 174/96 (not hypotensive), the heart rate is 72 (not tachycardic), and the groin site is clean and intact. The neurological signs point to intracranial hemorrhage, not peripheral bleeding.

Decision point 6: Monitoring only the blood pressure and assuming that if the systolic is below 140, the patient is stable. Blood pressure control is necessary but not sufficient. The neurological examination must be tracked in parallel — a patient whose blood pressure is controlled but whose GCS continues to decline or whose pupil asymmetry worsens may have ongoing hematoma expansion despite adequate blood pressure management, requiring repeat imaging and possible surgical intervention.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Identify the clinical findings of a left middle cerebral artery territory ischemic stroke and distinguish them from unrelated normal findings in the assessment.

Apply the IV alteplase eligibility criteria — including the INR threshold of 1.7 or below, the blood pressure requirement of below 185/110, and the time window from last known well — and recognize when a contraindication requires pivoting to an alternative reperfusion strategy such as mechanical thrombectomy.

Select the appropriate antihypertensive agent for blood pressure management in acute ischemic stroke and explain why nitroprusside is contraindicated in this setting.

Recognize the signs of post-reperfusion intracranial hemorrhage — acute neurological decline, new pupil asymmetry, loss of consciousness — and initiate the immediate nursing response including emergent notification, CT imaging, and airway management.

Identify the priority monitoring parameters in acute intracerebral hemorrhage, including serial neurological examination and blood pressure response to treatment, and distinguish them from lower-priority or irrelevant parameters.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 4, after the emergent CT has confirmed a left frontoparietal intracerebral hemorrhage with midline shift, the patient is intubated, anticoagulation reversal is initiated, and the nurse must synthesize the full clinical picture to guide ongoing management.

Most likely condition: Post-reperfusion symptomatic intracerebral hemorrhage. The patient had a successful mechanical thrombectomy for a left MCA occlusion, showed initial neurological improvement (NIHSS 14 to 9), and then acutely deteriorated with somnolence, loss of verbal output, and ipsilateral pupil dilation — all confirmed by CT showing a 3 cm hemorrhage with midline shift. The residual anticoagulation effect (INR 1.6 at the time of deterioration) and the reperfusion itself are contributing factors.

Plausible competing conditions: (1) Re-occlusion of the left MCA after thrombectomy — wrong here because re-occlusion would produce worsening of the original ischemic deficit (deepening right hemiplegia, worsening aphasia) without the new pupil asymmetry and mass effect signs; the CT confirms hemorrhage, not new ischemia, and the angiogram had shown successful recanalization. (2) Post-procedural sedation or medication effect causing the somnolence — wrong because the patient had been alert and improving for 45 minutes post-procedure, the decline is abrupt rather than gradual, the new pupil asymmetry is a structural sign that no sedative produces, and the CT confirms a hemorrhagic lesion with mass effect.

Two priority actions: (1) Maintain serial neurological assessments every 15 minutes — tracking GCS, pupil size and reactivity, and motor responses — to detect any further decline that would indicate ongoing hematoma expansion and the need for emergent neurosurgical intervention. (2) Ensure the blood pressure is maintained below 140 mmHg systolic by titrating the nicardipine infusion per the provider's stat order, as uncontrolled hypertension is the primary modifiable driver of hematoma expansion in acute intracerebral hemorrhage.

Two wrong actions: (1) Administering antiplatelet therapy (aspirin or clopidogrel) because the patient had an ischemic stroke and "needs secondary prevention" — wrong because the patient now has an active intracranial hemorrhage, and any antiplatelet or anticoagulant agent would worsen the bleeding; secondary prevention decisions are deferred until the hemorrhage is stable and neurology reassesses. (2) Reducing the frequency of neurological checks to every hour because the patient is intubated and "can't participate in the exam" — wrong because the intubated patient can still be assessed for pupil reactivity, motor response to stimulation, and changes in ICP signs; reducing monitoring frequency in the acute phase of ICH risks missing hematoma expansion during the highest-risk period.

Two parameters to monitor: (1) Neurological examination — specifically the GCS score, pupil size and reactivity, and motor response pattern — because any further decline (worsening GCS, fixed and dilated pupil, extensor or absent motor responses) indicates hematoma expansion or increasing intracranial pressure and triggers repeat imaging and neurosurgical re-evaluation. (2) Systolic blood pressure trend on the nicardipine infusion — because sustained control below 140 mmHg reduces the rate of hematoma expansion, and failure to achieve or maintain this target despite uptitration requires escalation to the provider for additional agents or dose adjustment.

Two irrelevant parameters: (1) NIHSS score — while it was the appropriate tracking tool during the ischemic stroke phase, it is not the right instrument for an intubated patient with an intracerebral hemorrhage and declining consciousness; the GCS and pupil examination are more appropriate and actionable in this context, and attempting a full NIHSS on an intubated, non-verbal patient produces unreliable scores that do not guide ICH management. (2) Troponin level — the initial troponin was normal and there are no signs of acute coronary syndrome; the patient's deterioration is neurological, not cardiac, and serial troponins would not change the management of the intracerebral hemorrhage at this moment.
