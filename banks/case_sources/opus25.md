

**CASE TITLE**
Tuberculosis rule-out in a newly admitted patient with chronic cough and immunosuppression.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 47-year-old man is admitted to the medical unit from the emergency department for evaluation of a persistent productive cough, unintentional weight loss, and night sweats. He has a history of rheumatoid arthritis managed with methotrexate 15 mg oral weekly and prednisone 7.5 mg oral daily for the past two years, hypertension managed with amlodipine 5 mg oral daily, and gastroesophageal reflux managed with omeprazole 20 mg oral daily. He works as a long-haul bus driver and reports frequent travel through the southwestern United States and northern Mexico over the past year. He has no history of prior tuberculin skin testing or interferon-gamma release assay (IGRA). He has no known HIV diagnosis; an HIV screening test is ordered as part of the admission workup. He is a former smoker (12 pack-year history, quit 5 years ago). He lives alone in an apartment. He reports no known tuberculosis contacts but states that several of his regular passengers are migrant agricultural workers and that the buses are often crowded with limited ventilation. He has no history of incarceration. He has not traveled outside the United States and Mexico. His immunizations are up to date except that he has never received a BCG vaccine. The admitting provider orders airborne isolation precautions, three sputum specimens for acid-fast bacilli (AFB) smear and mycobacterial culture (to be collected 8–24 hours apart, with at least one early-morning specimen), a chest radiograph, a QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus (IGRA) blood test, a complete metabolic panel, a complete blood count, an HIV antigen/antibody combination test, and a sedimentation rate. The provider also places a dietary consult for high-calorie, high-protein supplementation given the weight loss.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient appears fatigued and thin. He reports that the cough began approximately six weeks ago, initially dry but now producing small amounts of yellowish sputum, occasionally streaked with blood. He describes drenching night sweats three to four times per week, a 7 kg (15 lb) weight loss over the past two months despite no change in appetite, and progressive fatigue that has made it difficult to complete his driving shifts. He denies dyspnea at rest but reports mild shortness of breath with exertion such as climbing stairs. He denies chest pain, joint swelling, or rash. He states he saw a walk-in clinic provider three weeks ago and was prescribed a 10-day course of amoxicillin for "bronchitis," which did not improve his symptoms.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 38.3 °C (101.0 °F), HR 92, BP 118/74, RR 20, SpO₂ 95% on room air. Height 178 cm, weight 64 kg (documented weight 14 months ago at a rheumatology visit was 79 kg). BMI 20.2. The patient is alert, oriented to person, place, time, and situation, and cooperative. He appears cachectic with temporal wasting and prominent clavicles. Skin is warm and dry, no rashes or lesions. Oral mucosa is pale pink, moist, no thrush. Cervical and supraclavicular lymph nodes are palpable bilaterally — small, firm, non-tender, mobile. Chest inspection reveals reduced expansion on the right upper lobe region. Percussion is dull over the right upper posterior chest. Auscultation reveals bronchial breath sounds and coarse crackles over the right upper lobe; the remainder of the lung fields are clear. Heart sounds are regular, no murmurs. Abdomen soft, non-tender, non-distended, bowel sounds present in all quadrants. Extremities show no edema, no joint swelling or deformity. Muscle strength 4/5 globally, attributed to deconditioning and weight loss. The patient is placed in a negative-pressure airborne infection isolation room (AIIR). The nurse verifies that the room's negative-pressure indicator is functioning (smoke test or manometer reading confirms inward airflow), the door is kept closed, and the patient is instructed to remain in the room. The nurse dons an N95 respirator (fit-tested within the past year per facility policy) before entering the room.

**LABORATORY DATA**
CBC: WBC 11,200/µL (differential: neutrophils 72%, lymphocytes 16%, monocytes 10%, eosinophils 1%, basophils 1%), hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL, hematocrit 34.2%, platelets 410,000/µL. CMP: sodium 136 mEq/L, potassium 4.1 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, CO₂ 24 mEq/L, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 104 mg/dL, calcium 9.8 mg/dL, albumin 3.0 g/dL, total protein 7.2 g/dL, AST 42 U/L, ALT 48 U/L (mildly elevated; baseline ALT 6 months ago was 35 U/L on methotrexate). ESR 68 mm/hr. HIV antigen/antibody combination test: pending. QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus: pending. Chest radiograph: right upper lobe infiltrate with a 2.3 cm cavitary lesion; no pleural effusion; no hilar lymphadenopathy.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Admission evening through the first 16 hours. The nurse collects the first sputum specimen at 2100 on the admission evening, coaching the patient on the proper technique: rinse the mouth with water first (no toothpaste or mouthwash), take several deep breaths, and produce a deep cough into the sterile specimen container, aiming for at least 5 mL of sputum rather than saliva. The container is labeled, sealed, placed in a biohazard bag, and sent to the laboratory promptly. The second specimen is collected at 0530 the following morning (early-morning specimen). The patient's overnight course is notable for two episodes of productive coughing with blood-streaked sputum, drenching night sweats requiring a linen change, and a temperature spike to 38.9 °C (102.0 °F) at 0200. He reports poor appetite and eats only 30% of his dinner and 20% of his breakfast. The nurse documents strict adherence to airborne precautions: the AIIR door remains closed, the negative-pressure indicator is checked every shift, all staff entering the room wear N95 respirators, and the patient wears a surgical mask during the brief transport to radiology for the chest radiograph (which occurred before the room was set up, in the ED). A nursing assistant asks the nurse whether she can enter the room wearing a standard surgical mask "just to take vitals quickly." Vitals at 0600: T 38.1 °C, HR 88, BP 122/76, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. The patient asks whether his coworker, who drove him to the ED, needs to be tested for TB. The QuantiFERON-TB Gold Plus result returns: positive. The HIV antigen/antibody test returns: negative.

**Stage 2** — Hours 16–36 after admission. The third sputum specimen is collected at 1400 (more than 8 hours after the second). The AFB smear results from the first two specimens return: Specimen 1 — AFB smear positive (2+); Specimen 2 — AFB smear positive (3+). Mycobacterial cultures are inoculated and pending (final culture results take 2–6 weeks). A nucleic acid amplification test (NAAT) is reflexively performed on the first smear-positive specimen per laboratory protocol and returns positive for Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex. The infectious disease service is consulted. The provider initiates standard four-drug therapy per the facility's TB treatment protocol: isoniazid (INH) 300 mg oral daily, rifampin 600 mg oral daily, pyrazinamide 25 mg/kg oral daily (patient weight 64 kg, dose 1,600 mg daily), and ethambutol 15 mg/kg oral daily (dose 960 mg daily). Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) 25 mg oral daily is ordered concurrently with isoniazid to prevent peripheral neuropathy. The provider also orders baseline visual acuity testing and red-green color discrimination testing before the first dose of ethambutol, with instructions to report any visual changes immediately. The rheumatology service is contacted regarding the methotrexate: the rheumatologist recommends holding methotrexate during active TB treatment due to the immunosuppressive effect and the overlapping hepatotoxicity risk with isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide. The prednisone is not abruptly discontinued; the rheumatologist writes a tapering schedule to avoid adrenal crisis. Repeat vitals: T 38.4 °C, HR 94, BP 116/70, RR 20, SpO₂ 95% on room air. The patient is anxious and asks how long he will need to stay in isolation. He also expresses concern about missing work and losing income. The nurse notes that the patient's affect is flat and he has stopped engaging in conversation, answering questions with one-word responses. He declines lunch. The local public health department has been notified of the positive NAAT result by the laboratory (per mandatory reporting requirements), and a public health nurse contacts the facility to begin the contact investigation process. The public health nurse asks the hospital nurse to help obtain a list of the patient's close contacts over the past three months.

**Stage 3** — Hours 36–60 after admission. The patient has received three doses of the four-drug regimen. He reports nausea after taking the medications and has vomited once. He continues to have a productive cough but reports the hemoptysis has not worsened. His appetite remains poor; he has consumed less than 40% of meals since admission. The dietary consult recommends calorie-dense supplements between meals. Vitals: T 37.8 °C, HR 86, BP 120/72, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. The low-grade fever is trending down. Repeat labs drawn at 48 hours: AST 58 U/L, ALT 72 U/L (rising from admission values of 42 and 48, respectively), total bilirubin 1.4 mg/dL (admission value 0.9 mg/dL). The albumin remains 3.0 g/dL. The nurse notes the transaminase elevation and reviews the medication list: the patient is now receiving isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide — three hepatotoxic drugs — and was previously on methotrexate (now held) and continues on omeprazole. The third AFB smear result returns: positive (2+). Culture and drug-susceptibility results remain pending. The patient asks the nurse whether he is "contagious forever" and whether he will be able to return to his job as a bus driver. He also asks whether his apartment neighbors are at risk. The public health department has identified the patient's coworker, his landlord (who shares a common ventilation system), and several family members he visited during a holiday gathering three weeks ago as close contacts requiring screening. The patient expresses guilt about potentially exposing others and becomes tearful. The nurse assesses his psychosocial state and notes that he is sleeping poorly (waking frequently from cough and anxiety), has expressed feelings of isolation and shame, and has asked twice whether "this will ever end." He has no active suicidal ideation when directly assessed, but he describes feeling hopeless about his financial situation and his health.

**Stage 4** — Day 5 of hospitalization (approximately 108 hours after admission). The patient's nausea has improved with administration of the TB medications with a small snack (per provider adjustment). He is tolerating approximately 60% of meals and drinking calorie-dense supplements. Vitals: T 37.2 °C, HR 78, BP 122/74, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Cough continues but is less frequent and the sputum is no longer blood-streaked. Repeat labs: AST 86 U/L, ALT 104 U/L, total bilirubin 1.8 mg/dL. The transaminases have continued to rise — ALT is now more than twice the upper limit of normal (ULN 40 U/L) and more than twice the admission baseline. The patient denies right upper quadrant pain, jaundice is not clinically apparent, but the nurse notes a faint yellow tinge to the sclerae. The infectious disease provider is notified of the liver function trend. Per the facility's TB hepatotoxicity monitoring protocol, the threshold for holding hepatotoxic TB drugs and obtaining urgent hepatology consultation is: ALT greater than 3 times ULN with symptoms (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, jaundice) or ALT greater than 5 times ULN regardless of symptoms. The patient's ALT of 104 is 2.6 times ULN, and he has had nausea (though improving) and early scleral icterus. The provider orders liver function tests to be repeated in 24 hours and instructs the nurse to monitor closely for worsening symptoms. The provider states that if the next ALT exceeds 120 (3× ULN) with any hepatotoxicity symptoms, the hepatotoxic agents (isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide) will be held and hepatology will be consulted. The patient's psychosocial status has improved slightly: the social worker has connected him with a TB patient support group via telephone, and the public health nurse has explained that with effective treatment, he will eventually be non-infectious and able to return to work. He remains concerned about finances and asks about disability benefits during treatment. The nurse is coordinating discharge planning, which will require: confirmed adherence to directly observed therapy (DOT) arrangements with the public health department, a safe living environment (his apartment ventilation concern is being assessed by public health), outpatient follow-up with infectious disease and rheumatology, ongoing liver function monitoring, and a clear plan for when airborne precautions can be discontinued (per facility protocol: airborne precautions are maintained until the patient has three consecutive negative AFB smears collected 8–24 hours apart, is on effective therapy, and is clinically improving — or per public health department guidance).

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, a nursing assistant asks whether she can enter the AIIR wearing a standard surgical mask instead of an N95 respirator "just to take vitals quickly." The nurse must determine the correct response. *Correct action:* The nurse must instruct the nursing assistant that a standard surgical mask is not acceptable for entering the room of a patient on airborne precautions. Every person entering the AIIR must wear a fit-tested N95 respirator (or a powered air-purifying respirator if unable to be fit-tested for an N95), regardless of the duration of the encounter. The nurse should verify that the nursing assistant has been fit-tested for an N95 within the required interval per facility policy and, if not, should either provide the care herself or arrange for a fit-tested staff member to enter. *Why:* Mycobacterium tuberculosis is transmitted via airborne droplet nuclei (1–5 micrometers) that remain suspended in air and are not filtered by standard surgical masks. Surgical masks are designed to block large-particle droplets, not airborne-sized particles. The duration of exposure does not change the transmission-based precaution category — airborne precautions require N95-level respiratory protection for every entry. This is a non-negotiable infection control standard, not a judgment call based on exposure time. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient asks whether his coworker who drove him to the ED needs to be tested for TB. The nurse must provide an accurate, scope-appropriate response. *Correct action:* The nurse should inform the patient that close contacts — people who have shared air space with him for prolonged periods during the infectious period — will need to be screened for TB infection. The coworker may qualify as a close contact depending on the duration and proximity of exposure. The nurse should explain that the public health department will conduct a formal contact investigation to identify and notify individuals who need screening, and that the patient can help by providing a list of people he has been in close contact with over the past several months. The nurse should not independently determine who does or does not need testing — that is the public health department's role in the contact investigation — but should validate the patient's concern and explain the process. *Why:* Contact investigation is a public health function with established protocols for defining exposure risk (duration, proximity, ventilation of the shared space). The nurse's role is to educate the patient about the process, facilitate cooperation with public health, and help compile the contact list — not to make individual screening determinations. Telling the patient "no, he's fine" would be inaccurate and outside the nurse's scope; telling the patient "yes, he definitely needs testing" without knowing the exposure details would also overstep. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse receives the order for ethambutol and must identify the essential baseline assessment that must be completed before the first dose is administered. *Correct action:* The nurse must ensure that baseline visual acuity testing and red-green color discrimination testing are performed and documented before administering the first dose of ethambutol. The nurse should also educate the patient to report any changes in vision — blurred vision, decreased acuity, difficulty distinguishing red from green, or scotomata — immediately at any point during treatment. *Why:* Ethambutol's dose-limiting toxicity is optic neuritis (retrobulbar neuritis), which can cause irreversible vision loss if not detected early. The risk is dose-related and increases with renal impairment, though this patient's renal function is currently normal. Baseline visual testing establishes the reference point against which any subsequent changes are measured. Without a documented baseline, a later visual complaint cannot be reliably attributed to the drug versus a pre-existing condition, and the decision to continue or discontinue ethambutol becomes ambiguous. Administering ethambutol without this baseline assessment is an omission that could delay detection of a serious adverse effect. *Skill:* Take action, recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the rheumatologist recommends holding methotrexate and tapering prednisone rather than abruptly stopping it. The nurse must understand why the prednisone is tapered rather than simply discontinued. *Correct action:* Recognize that the prednisone taper is necessary to prevent adrenal insufficiency (adrenal crisis). The patient has been taking prednisone 7.5 mg daily for two years, which is sufficient to suppress the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Abrupt discontinuation could result in acute adrenal insufficiency — hypotension, hypoglycemia, weakness, nausea, and potentially cardiovascular collapse. The nurse should administer the prednisone per the tapering schedule, monitor for signs of adrenal insufficiency during the taper (fatigue, orthostatic hypotension, nausea, dizziness), and educate the patient not to skip doses or stop the medication on his own. *Why:* Chronic exogenous corticosteroid use suppresses endogenous cortisol production. The adrenal glands require a gradual reduction in exogenous steroid to resume adequate cortisol synthesis. This is a physiologic necessity, not a preference. The methotrexate, by contrast, can be stopped without a taper because it does not cause physiologic dependence or rebound in this context — it is held because of immunosuppression and hepatotoxicity overlap, and its discontinuation does not carry the same acute risk. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 4, the nurse reviews the trend in liver function tests — ALT rising from 48 at admission to 72 at 48 hours to 104 at day 5, with total bilirubin rising from 0.9 to 1.8 and early scleral icterus — in a patient receiving isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide. The facility's TB hepatotoxicity monitoring protocol states that hepatotoxic TB drugs should be held when ALT exceeds 3 times ULN (120 U/L) with symptoms or 5 times ULN (200 U/L) regardless of symptoms. The current ALT is 104 (2.6× ULN). The nurse must determine the correct action. *Correct action:* The ALT has not yet crossed the protocol threshold for holding medications (3× ULN with symptoms = 120, and the current value is 104). The correct action is to continue the medications as ordered, monitor the patient closely for worsening hepatotoxicity symptoms (increasing nausea, vomiting, right upper quadrant pain, deepening jaundice, dark urine, clay-colored stools), repeat the liver function tests in 24 hours as ordered, and notify the provider immediately if symptoms worsen before the next lab draw. The nurse should document the trend, the current clinical findings (improving nausea, early scleral icterus), and the plan. The nurse should not independently hold the TB medications at this point, because the protocol threshold has not been met — but the nurse must recognize that the trajectory is concerning and that the threshold may be crossed at the next measurement, requiring readiness to act. *Why:* Hepatotoxicity is a known and serious adverse effect of isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide. Monitoring protocols exist precisely because some degree of transaminase elevation is common and expected in early TB treatment (mild elevations occur in up to 20% of patients and often stabilize), while progressive elevation with symptoms signals drug-induced liver injury requiring drug interruption. The protocol threshold provides an objective decision point. Holding the drugs prematurely — before the threshold is met and without provider direction — risks interrupting effective TB treatment unnecessarily, which can promote drug resistance. Conversely, ignoring the trend and failing to monitor closely risks missing the threshold crossing and allowing progression to fulminant hepatotoxicity. The nurse's role is to apply the protocol precisely: monitor, trend, report, and be prepared to act when the threshold is met. *Skill:* Recognize cues, evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 4.

6. *Situation:* Across Stages 2–4, the patient exhibits progressive psychosocial distress: anxiety about isolation and contagiousness, flat affect, withdrawal from conversation, poor sleep, guilt about exposing contacts, tearfulness, feelings of hopelessness about finances and health, and expressed concern that "this will ever end." The nurse must assess and address the psychosocial dimension of care. *Correct action:* The nurse should perform a structured psychosocial assessment that includes direct screening for depression and suicidal ideation (which was done — no active SI identified), validate the patient's emotional responses as normal reactions to a serious communicable disease diagnosis with social and occupational consequences, provide accurate information about the treatment timeline and prognosis (with effective treatment, most patients become non-infectious within weeks and can eventually return to normal activities), facilitate social work involvement for financial resource navigation (disability benefits, medication assistance programs), connect the patient with peer support resources (the TB patient support group), and document the psychosocial findings and interventions as part of the plan of care. The nurse should also assess for barriers to treatment adherence that the psychosocial distress may create — a patient who feels hopeless and overwhelmed is at higher risk for nonadherence to a 6–9 month treatment regimen. *Why:* TB carries significant psychosocial burden: stigma, isolation (both physical and social), occupational disruption, financial stress, guilt about transmission, and the daunting length of treatment. These factors are well-documented contributors to treatment nonadherence, which is the primary driver of drug-resistant TB. Addressing the psychosocial dimension is not ancillary to the medical treatment — it is integral to treatment success. The nurse's role includes holistic assessment, therapeutic communication, resource coordination, and adherence support. Neglecting the psychosocial component while focusing exclusively on the pharmacologic and infection-control aspects of care is an incomplete nursing response. *Skill:* Recognize cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3 (when the distress pattern becomes fully apparent), with continued relevance through Stage 4.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Allowing the nursing assistant to enter the AIIR with a surgical mask because "it's just for a minute." Airborne precautions are not modified by duration of exposure. M. tuberculosis droplet nuclei remain suspended in air and can be inhaled in a single breath. A surgical mask does not filter airborne-sized particles. This error exposes the nursing assistant to infection risk and violates the transmission-based precaution standard.

- *(Decision 1)* Believing that the negative-pressure room itself protects staff inside the room, so respiratory protection is less important. Negative pressure prevents airborne particles from escaping the room into the hallway — it protects people *outside* the room. Staff *inside* the room are in the highest-concentration environment and require N95 protection regardless of the room's engineering controls.

- *(Decision 2)* Telling the patient that his coworker "probably doesn't need testing" because TB requires prolonged close contact and a single car ride is unlikely to transmit infection. The nurse does not have enough information about the duration, frequency, or ventilation conditions of the exposure to make this determination, and the contact investigation is a public health function. Premature reassurance could lead the coworker to decline screening.

- *(Decision 3)* Administering the first dose of ethambutol without ensuring that baseline visual acuity and color discrimination testing have been completed, reasoning that "the eye test can be done tomorrow." Without a documented baseline, any subsequent visual complaint cannot be reliably evaluated against the patient's pre-treatment status, and a window for early detection of optic neuritis is lost. The baseline must precede the first dose.

- *(Decision 4)* Stopping the prednisone abruptly along with the methotrexate, reasoning that both immunosuppressants should be discontinued as quickly as possible in a patient with active TB. While the impulse to reduce immunosuppression is clinically logical, abrupt prednisone discontinuation after two years of daily use risks adrenal crisis — a potentially fatal complication that is entirely preventable with a taper. The methotrexate can be stopped without a taper; the prednisone cannot.

- *(Decision 5)* Independently holding the TB medications because the ALT is "trending up and the patient looks jaundiced," without recognizing that the protocol threshold has not yet been met. Premature interruption of TB therapy risks subtherapeutic drug levels and the development of drug resistance. The protocol exists to distinguish expected mild hepatic stress from true drug-induced liver injury, and the nurse's role is to apply the protocol — monitor, trend, and report — not to unilaterally discontinue prescribed therapy below the action threshold.

- *(Decision 5)* Conversely, dismissing the transaminase trend as "just a side effect" and failing to increase monitoring vigilance or communicate the trend to the provider. The trajectory from 48 → 72 → 104 over five days, with rising bilirubin and early icterus, is clinically significant even though the protocol threshold has not been crossed. Failing to recognize the trend and prepare for the possibility that the next value will cross the threshold is a failure of cue recognition.

- *(Decision 6)* Focusing exclusively on the infection control and pharmacologic aspects of care while dismissing the patient's emotional distress as an expected and self-limiting reaction that does not require nursing intervention. Psychosocial distress in TB patients is a documented risk factor for treatment nonadherence, and the nurse has a responsibility to assess, intervene, and coordinate resources. Telling the patient to "stay positive" without structured assessment, validation, resource referral, and adherence-risk evaluation is an inadequate response.

- *(Decision 6)* Assuming that because the patient denies active suicidal ideation, no further psychosocial intervention is needed. The absence of SI does not mean the absence of clinically significant distress. The patient's hopelessness, withdrawal, poor sleep, guilt, and flat affect constitute a pattern that warrants ongoing assessment, therapeutic communication, and interdisciplinary referral — not just a single screening question.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Apply airborne transmission-based precautions correctly, including the distinction between surgical masks and N95 respirators, the function of negative-pressure rooms, and the non-negotiability of respiratory protection regardless of exposure duration.
- Identify the nurse's role in facilitating the public health contact investigation process, including patient education, contact list compilation, and appropriate scope boundaries between nursing and public health functions.
- Recognize essential baseline assessments required before initiating specific TB medications — particularly visual acuity and color discrimination testing before ethambutol — and the rationale for each.
- Apply a hepatotoxicity monitoring protocol precisely, distinguishing expected mild transaminase elevation from a trajectory approaching the action threshold, and balancing the risks of premature drug interruption against the risks of delayed recognition of drug-induced liver injury.
- Integrate psychosocial assessment and intervention into the care of a patient with a communicable disease diagnosis, recognizing the relationship between psychosocial distress and treatment adherence.
