**CASE TITLE**
Acute pulmonary embolism with right ventricular strain following total knee arthroplasty.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 68-year-old man is on post-operative day 2 following an elective right total knee arthroplasty. His medical history includes hypertension managed with lisinopril 10 mg daily, hyperlipidemia managed with atorvastatin 40 mg daily, a BMI of 33, and a 30-pack-year smoking history (quit 5 years ago). He has no prior history of venous thromboembolism, no known coagulopathy, and no personal or family history of bleeding disorders. His surgical course was uncompllenicated; estimated blood loss was 250 mL. Post-operative orders include enoxaparin 40 mg subcutaneous daily for VTE prophylaxis (first dose given 12 hours post-operatively, second dose given on post-operative day 1), sequential compression devices (SCDs) to bilateral lower extremities, incentive spirometry every hour while awake, and early ambulation with physical therapy twice daily. Nursing documentation from the overnight shift notes that the patient refused SCDs at 2200, stating they were "too uncomfortable," and that he declined the 0600 ambulation attempt, reporting knee pain rated 7/10. He received his scheduled enoxaparin dose on post-operative day 1 but the post-operative day 2 morning dose has not yet been administered. His surgical drain was removed on post-operative day 1 with minimal output. The surgical wound is clean, dry, and intact with a compression dressing in place.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
At 0815, the patient activates his call light. The nurse finds him sitting upright in bed, visibly distressed, clutching the right side of his chest. He states, "I can't catch my breath — it came on all of a sudden, and it hurts right here when I breathe in." He is diaphoretic, pale, and speaking in short phrases of three to four words between breaths. He denies any preceding trauma, coughing, or position change. He reports that the pain began approximately ten minutes ago while he was lying in bed watching television and has been worsening.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Vital signs: temperature 37.2 °C, heart rate 122 beats per minute and regular, blood pressure 100/70 mmHg (pre-operative baseline 138/82), respiratory rate 28 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 87% on room air. Neurological: alert and oriented to person, place, time, and situation; anxious and restless; GCS 15. Cardiovascular: tachycardic, regular rhythm, no murmurs or gallops; peripheral pulses 2+ bilaterally in upper extremities; right dorsalis pedis pulse 1+ and left dorsalis pedis pulse 2+. Jugular venous distension is visible at 45 degrees. Respiratory: tachypneic with shallow respirations; lung sounds clear bilaterally with no wheezes, crackles, or diminished breath sounds; using accessory muscles; speaking in short phrases. Skin: diaphoretic, cool and clammy to touch, pale. Abdomen: soft, non-tender, normoactive bowel sounds. Right lower extremity: the surgical site dressing is clean, dry, and intact; the right calf is warm, erythematous, and edematous compared to the left, with a circumference measuring 42 cm on the right versus 37 cm on the left at the widest point; the patient reports tenderness to palpation of the right posterior calf; a positive Homans sign is elicited (though the nurse notes this is a non-specific finding in a post-surgical limb). Left lower extremity: no edema, no calf tenderness, SCD in place. Urinary output via indwelling catheter: 120 mL over the past 4 hours (30 mL/hr).

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn on post-operative day 1: WBC 11,200/µL, hemoglobin 11.8 g/dL, hematocrit 35.4%, platelets 198,000/µL, INR 1.0, aPTT 28 seconds, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, eGFR >60 mL/min, sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.1 mEq/L, glucose 142 mg/dL. No baseline troponin or BNP was drawn.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0815 to 0845 (initial recognition and response). The nurse immediately applies supplemental oxygen via simple face mask at 10 L/min. SpO₂ rises to 90% but does not improve further. The nurse switches to a non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min; SpO₂ climbs to 92%. The nurse activates the rapid response team (RRT) and notifies the orthopedic surgeon. While awaiting the RRT, the nurse raises the head of the bed to high Fowler's position, establishes a second peripheral IV (18-gauge in the left antecubital fossa; the existing 20-gauge right forearm IV is patent), and places the patient on continuous cardiac monitoring. The cardiac monitor shows sinus tachycardia at 118–126 bpm with a right axis deviation. A 12-lead ECG is obtained by the RRT and shows sinus tachycardia at 124 bpm, an S wave in lead I, a Q wave in lead III, and an inverted T wave in lead III (S1Q3T3 pattern), along with T-wave inversions in leads V1–V3 consistent with right ventricular strain. The RRT physician orders stat labs: troponin I, BNP, D-dimer, CBC, BMP, coagulation studies, ABG, and type and screen. A portable chest X-ray is ordered. Repeat vitals at 0840: HR 126, BP 96/68, RR 30, SpO₂ 92% on non-rebreather at 15 L/min. The patient remains alert but increasingly anxious, stating, "Am I going to die?"

**Stage 2** — 0845 to 0930 (diagnostic confirmation and initial treatment). Stat lab results return: troponin I 0.38 ng/mL (reference <0.04), BNP 620 pg/mL (reference <100), D-dimer >5,000 ng/mL FEU (reference <500), hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL, platelets 192,000/µL, aPTT 29 seconds, INR 1.0, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, potassium 4.3 mEq/L, lactate 3.2 mmol/L (reference <2.0). ABG on non-rebreather: pH 7.47, PaCO₂ 28 mmHg, PaO₂ 68 mmHg, HCO₃ 20 mEq/L, SaO₂ 93%. The portable chest X-ray shows no infiltrate, no pneumothorax, and no pleural effusion; the cardiac silhouette is at the upper limit of normal. The RRT physician determines the patient is hemodynamically stable enough for transport and orders a stat CT pulmonary angiography (CT-PA). The CT-PA reveals a large saddle embolus at the bifurcation of the main pulmonary artery extending into bilateral lobar arteries, with right ventricular dilation (RV/LV ratio 1.4, reference <0.9) and interventricular septal bowing toward the left ventricle. No aortic dissection flap is seen. The pulmonary medicine service is consulted. The provider orders unfractionated heparin (UFH) per the facility's PE protocol: an 80 units/kg IV bolus followed by a continuous infusion at 18 units/kg/hr, with aPTT monitoring every 6 hours and a target aPTT of 60–80 seconds. The orthopedic surgeon is notified of the anticoagulation plan and confirms no active surgical-site bleeding. The nurse administers the UFH bolus at 0920 and initiates the drip. Repeat vitals at 0930: HR 118, BP 98/66, RR 28, SpO₂ 93% on non-rebreather. Urine output over the past hour: 20 mL.

**Stage 3** — 0930 to 1330 (monitoring, reassessment, and evolving clinical picture). At 1000, the patient reports the chest pain has decreased from 8/10 to 6/10. Vitals: HR 112, BP 102/70, RR 26, SpO₂ 94% on non-rebreather. Urine output: 25 mL over the past 30 minutes. The pulmonary medicine team classifies the PE as submassive based on the elevated troponin, elevated BNP, RV dilation on CT-PA, and the ECG findings of RV strain, but with a systolic blood pressure that has remained above 90 mmHg. They document that systemic thrombolysis is not indicated at this time given the hemodynamic stability and the recent surgical procedure (post-operative day 2 from major orthopedic surgery, which is a relative contraindication to systemic thrombolysis), but that the patient should be transferred to the ICU for close hemodynamic monitoring and reassessment. The team notes that if the patient develops sustained hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg for ≥15 minutes) or signs of obstructive shock despite fluid resuscitation, the case would be reclassified as massive PE and catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy would be considered, with the understanding that systemic thrombolysis carries significant bleeding risk given the recent surgery. At 1100, the first aPTT on the heparin drip returns at 52 seconds (below the target of 60–80 seconds). Per the facility's UFH titration protocol, the infusion rate is increased by 2 units/kg/hr and the aPTT is to be rechecked in 6 hours. The nurse assesses the surgical site: the dressing remains clean, dry, and intact with no evidence of new bleeding or expanding hematoma. The right calf remains warm and edematous. At 1200, the patient is transferred to the ICU. ICU admission vitals: HR 106, BP 108/72, RR 24, SpO₂ 95% on non-rebreather. The patient reports chest pain at 4/10. He is alert and oriented, less anxious, and able to speak in full sentences. Urine output has improved to 35 mL/hr over the past 2 hours. At 1330, repeat aPTT returns at 64 seconds (within the target range of 60–80 seconds). Repeat troponin I is 0.42 ng/mL (slight rise from 0.38). Repeat lactate is 2.4 mmol/L (down from 3.2). The ICU team continues the current heparin infusion rate and plans for repeat echocardiography in 24 hours to reassess RV function. The team discusses the anticoagulation transition plan: once the patient is clinically stable for 24–48 hours, the plan is to transition from IV UFH to therapeutic-dose enoxaparin (1 mg/kg subcutaneous every 12 hours, with anti-Xa monitoring given the recent surgery) as a bridge to oral anticoagulation, with the choice of long-term agent to be determined by the inpatient team in consultation with the orthopedic surgeon.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the initial presentation (Stage 1), the nurse encounters a post-operative day 2 total knee arthroplasty patient with sudden-onset pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, tachycardia, hypoxemia, diaphoresis, borderline hypotension, jugular venous distension, and unilateral calf swelling with warmth and tenderness. Among these findings, the nurse must identify which constellation of cues is most concerning and what they collectively suggest. The patient also has clear lung sounds, a clean surgical dressing, and a temperature of 37.2 °C. *Correct action:* Recognize that the sudden pleuritic chest pain, SpO₂ of 87% on room air, tachycardia at 122, diaphoresis, borderline hypotension (100/70 from a baseline of 138/82), jugular venous distension, and the warm, swollen, tender right calf in a post-surgical patient with documented immobility and refusal of SCDs are the critical cues pointing to acute pulmonary embolism with possible right ventricular compromise. The clear lung sounds, normal temperature, and intact surgical dressing are not the priority findings at this moment. *Why it is correct:* The combination of a major VTE risk factor (recent orthopedic surgery with immobility and non-adherence to mechanical prophylaxis), a clinical presentation consistent with acute PE (sudden dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, hypoxemia), and signs suggesting hemodynamic compromise (hypotension relative to baseline, JVD indicating elevated right-sided pressures, diaphoresis) constitutes a high-probability Wells score for PE. The unilateral calf findings suggest a concurrent deep vein thrombosis as the embolic source. Clear lung sounds help differentiate this from pneumonia or pulmonary edema, and the normal temperature argues against an infectious etiology — these are relevant for differential reasoning but are not the priority cues driving the immediate clinical concern. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse receives the stat lab results (troponin I 0.38, BNP 620, D-dimer >5,000, lactate 3.2, ABG showing respiratory alkalosis with hypoxemia) and the CT-PA results (saddle embolus, RV/LV ratio 1.4, septal bowing). The nurse must interpret these findings in the context of the ECG from Stage 1 (S1Q3T3, T-wave inversions V1–V3) and the clinical picture to determine the severity classification and its implications. *Correct action:* Analyze the findings as consistent with a submassive (intermediate-risk) PE: the elevated troponin and BNP indicate myocardial injury and right ventricular strain; the RV/LV ratio of 1.4 with septal bowing confirms right ventricular dilation and pressure overload; the S1Q3T3 pattern and precordial T-wave inversions on ECG reflect acute right heart strain; the ABG shows an acute respiratory alkalosis (compensatory hyperventilation) with a PaO₂ of 68 mmHg despite high-flow oxygen, indicating significant ventilation-perfusion mismatch; and the elevated lactate suggests tissue hypoperfusion. However, the systolic blood pressure has remained above 90 mmHg, which distinguishes this from a massive PE. The D-dimer is markedly elevated but is expected to be elevated in any post-surgical patient and does not independently classify severity — its value here is confirmatory rather than discriminatory. *Why it is correct:* PE severity classification drives treatment decisions. Submassive PE is defined by evidence of RV dysfunction (imaging or biomarkers) without sustained systemic hypotension. Massive PE requires sustained systolic BP below 90 mmHg for 15 minutes or more, or the need for vasopressors, or pulselessness. This distinction is critical because it determines whether systemic thrombolysis is considered — a decision with particular weight in a patient who is post-operative day 2 from major surgery. The ABG interpretation (respiratory alkalosis with persistent hypoxemia on high-flow O₂) confirms the severity of the gas-exchange impairment and the inadequacy of compensatory hyperventilation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the CT-PA has confirmed a saddle PE with RV dilation. The patient has acute chest pain, hypotension relative to baseline, tachycardia, and hypoxemia. The nurse must consider the differential diagnoses that could present similarly — particularly acute coronary syndrome (STEMI) and aortic dissection — and confirm that PE is the primary hypothesis driving the plan of care. *Correct action:* Prioritize PE as the primary condition. Aortic dissection is ruled out by the CT-PA, which showed no dissection flap, and the clinical presentation (pleuritic rather than tearing pain, no blood pressure differential between arms, no mediastinal widening on chest X-ray) does not support it. STEMI is unlikely: the ECG shows sinus tachycardia with an S1Q3T3 pattern and right precordial T-wave inversions consistent with RV strain from PE, not ST-elevation in a coronary distribution; the troponin elevation is modest and consistent with RV myocardial strain from pressure overload rather than a primary coronary event. The unilateral calf findings, the post-surgical immobility, and the CT-PA confirmation of a saddle embolus with RV dilation make PE the unambiguous primary diagnosis. *Why it is correct:* Acute chest pain with hypotension and tachycardia has a broad differential, and the nurse must be able to distinguish PE from other life-threatening causes. The S1Q3T3 pattern is a classic (though not universal) ECG finding in acute right heart strain from PE and should not be confused with ST-elevation myocardial infarction. The troponin elevation in PE reflects RV wall stress from acute pressure overload, not coronary artery occlusion. Aortic dissection can mimic PE with sudden chest pain and hypotension, but the CT-PA definitively excludes it. Confirming PE as the primary hypothesis ensures that the treatment plan (anticoagulation, potential escalation to catheter-directed therapy) is appropriate and that the team does not pursue inappropriate interventions such as percutaneous coronary intervention. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the provider orders unfractionated heparin per the facility's PE protocol rather than low-molecular-weight heparin. The nurse must understand the rationale for this choice in a post-surgical patient and identify the treatment options and their appropriateness. The pulmonary medicine team has also documented that systemic thrombolysis is not indicated at this time. *Correct action:* Recognize that UFH is the appropriate initial parenteral anticoagulant in this clinical context. UFH is preferred over LMWH in this post-operative patient because of its short half-life (60–90 minutes), its ability to be rapidly titrated, and its reversibility with protamine sulfate — all critical considerations in a patient who is 2 days post-major orthopedic surgery and at risk for surgical-site bleeding. Systemic thrombolysis is appropriately withheld because the patient meets criteria for submassive (not massive) PE and is within 10 days of major surgery, which is a relative contraindication to systemic thrombolytics. The nurse should anticipate that if the patient deteriorates to sustained hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg for ≥15 minutes) despite supportive care, the case would be reclassified as massive PE and catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy would be considered as alternatives to systemic thrombolysis given the surgical contraindication. *Why it is correct:* The choice of anticoagulant in PE is influenced by the clinical context. While LMWH is generally favored for initial parenteral anticoagulation in PE due to predictable pharmacokinetics and reduced need for monitoring, UFH is preferred when there is a need for rapid dose adjustment, a high bleeding risk, or the possibility that an invasive procedure (such as catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy) may be needed urgently — all of which apply here. The recent surgery creates a tension between the need for anticoagulation (to prevent clot propagation) and the risk of bleeding (from the surgical site). UFH's reversibility with protamine provides a safety margin that LMWH does not offer to the same degree. The thrombolysis decision reflects the balance between the benefit of clot dissolution and the risk of life-threatening hemorrhage in a recent surgical patient: in submassive PE, the risk-benefit ratio does not favor systemic thrombolysis, particularly with a relative contraindication. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the UFH bolus and infusion have been ordered. The nurse must execute the immediate interventions in the correct priority. Multiple actions are needed: administer the UFH bolus, maintain high-flow oxygen, position the patient appropriately, monitor the surgical site for bleeding, ensure continuous cardiac monitoring, and prepare for ICU transfer. *Correct action:* Administer the UFH bolus as ordered (this is time-sensitive — anticoagulation delays in PE are associated with worse outcomes), while maintaining the patient on the non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min with SpO₂ monitoring, keeping the head of bed elevated to at least 45 degrees (high Fowler's or semi-recumbent position to optimize ventilation and reduce RV preload), initiating the continuous UFH infusion immediately after the bolus, and maintaining continuous cardiac monitoring with particular attention to rhythm changes. The nurse should assess the surgical site for any new bleeding or hematoma formation after initiating anticoagulation and document the assessment. Preparation for ICU transfer should proceed concurrently but should not delay the initiation of anticoagulation or oxygen therapy. *Why it is correct:* In acute PE, anticoagulation is the cornerstone of treatment and should be initiated as soon as possible once the diagnosis is confirmed (or even when clinical suspicion is high, before imaging, in some protocols). Positioning in high Fowler's or semi-recumbent reduces venous return, which is beneficial in RV failure because the failing right ventricle cannot handle increased preload — supine positioning would worsen RV distension and could precipitate hemodynamic collapse. Oxygen therapy addresses the life-threatening hypoxemia. Surgical-site monitoring is essential because the initiation of therapeutic anticoagulation in a post-operative patient creates a new bleeding risk that must be actively surveilled. These actions occur essentially simultaneously rather than in a strict linear sequence, but the UFH bolus and oxygen are the highest-priority interventions because they address the two immediate threats (clot propagation and hypoxemia). *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient has been on the UFH infusion for approximately 4 hours. The nurse reviews the trending data: heart rate has decreased from 126 to 106, blood pressure has improved from 96/68 to 108/72, SpO₂ has risen from 92% to 95% on the non-rebreather, respiratory rate has decreased from 30 to 24, chest pain has decreased from 8/10 to 4/10, urine output has improved from 20 mL/hr to 35 mL/hr, lactate has decreased from 3.2 to 2.4, and the aPTT is now 64 seconds (within the target range of 60–80). However, the troponin has risen slightly from 0.38 to 0.42. The nurse must determine whether the patient is improving, stable, or deteriorating. *Correct action:* Evaluate the overall trajectory as improving. The hemodynamic parameters (heart rate, blood pressure, urine output), the oxygenation (SpO₂, respiratory rate, ability to speak in full sentences), the tissue perfusion marker (lactate), and the symptom report (pain reduction) are all trending in the correct direction. The aPTT is within the therapeutic target, confirming adequate anticoagulation. The slight troponin rise from 0.38 to 0.42 does not indicate deterioration — troponin levels in acute PE commonly continue to rise for 12–24 hours after the initial insult before peaking and declining, reflecting the myocardial injury that has already occurred rather than ongoing new injury. The key parameters confirming improvement are the hemodynamic stabilization (HR and BP trending toward normal, urine output recovering) and the improving oxygenation, not the troponin trend. The nurse should continue the current plan, monitor for any signs of deterioration (recurrent hypotension, worsening tachycardia, declining SpO₂, decreased level of consciousness, new bleeding), and anticipate the repeat echocardiography in 24 hours to reassess RV function. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes in acute PE requires integrating multiple data streams. The hemodynamic and oxygenation trends are the most reliable real-time indicators of whether the patient is responding to treatment. Troponin kinetics in PE differ from those in acute coronary syndrome: the troponin reflects RV wall stress from the acute pressure overload, and a modest continued rise in the hours after the event is expected and does not by itself indicate clinical worsening. Misinterpreting the troponin rise as deterioration could lead to unnecessary escalation (such as requesting thrombolysis in a patient who is clinically improving and has a surgical contraindication). The lactate decline from 3.2 to 2.4 confirms improving tissue perfusion. Urine output recovery reflects improving cardiac output and renal perfusion. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Decision point 1: Attributing the dyspnea and tachycardia to post-operative atelectasis or anxiety and failing to recognize the constellation of findings as a PE presentation. The patient has clear lung sounds (arguing against atelectasis or pneumonia), and while anxiety is present, it is a response to the acute event, not the cause. The combination of sudden pleuritic chest pain, severe hypoxemia, tachycardia, hypotension, JVD, and unilateral calf swelling in a post-surgical patient with documented immobility demands immediate evaluation for PE, not reassurance and incentive spirometry.

Decision point 1: Focusing on the right calf swelling as the primary problem (DVT) and failing to recognize that the cardiopulmonary findings indicate the clot has already embolized. While the DVT is the likely source, the immediate life threat is the pulmonary embolism causing hemodynamic compromise, not the residual clot in the calf. Treating the DVT alone (e.g., elevating the leg and applying compression) without addressing the PE would be a dangerous delay.

Decision point 2: Interpreting the D-dimer value as the key diagnostic finding that confirms PE severity. In a post-surgical patient, the D-dimer is expected to be elevated from the surgery itself and has poor specificity. Its value in this case is confirmatory at best; the severity classification depends on the biomarkers of RV strain (troponin, BNP), the imaging findings (RV dilation, septal bowing), and the hemodynamic status — not the D-dimer level.

Decision point 3: Misinterpreting the S1Q3T3 pattern and troponin elevation as evidence of a STEMI and prioritizing cardiac catheterization over anticoagulation for PE. The ECG pattern in this case reflects acute right ventricular strain from PE, not a left-sided coronary event. The troponin elevation is from RV pressure overload, not coronary occlusion. Pursuing cardiac catheterization would delay appropriate PE treatment and could be harmful.

Decision point 4: Assuming that systemic thrombolysis should be administered immediately because the PE is large (saddle embolus) and the patient is hypoxemic. The classification as submassive — not massive — means the patient does not meet the hemodynamic criteria for thrombolysis, and the recent major surgery (post-operative day 2) is a relative contraindication that significantly increases the risk of life-threatening hemorrhage. Thrombolysis in this scenario could cause catastrophic bleeding at the surgical site.

Decision point 4: Questioning the UFH order and advocating for LMWH because LMWH is generally preferred for initial PE anticoagulation. While LMWH is indeed favored in many PE presentations, this patient's recent surgery, the potential need for urgent invasive intervention (catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy if he deteriorates), and the need for rapid reversibility make UFH the appropriate choice. Switching to LMWH at this point would sacrifice the safety advantages of UFH's short half-life and protamine reversibility.

Decision point 5: Placing the patient in a supine or Trendelenburg position to address the hypotension. In right ventricular failure from PE, the right ventricle is already volume-overloaded and pressure-overloaded. Increasing venous return by lowering the head of bed would further distend the failing RV, worsen septal bowing into the left ventricle, decrease left ventricular filling and cardiac output, and could precipitate cardiovascular collapse. The semi-recumbent or high Fowler's position reduces preload and optimizes respiratory mechanics.

Decision point 6: Interpreting the slight troponin rise from 0.38 to 0.42 as evidence of clinical deterioration and requesting escalation to thrombolysis or catheter-directed therapy. Troponin in acute PE commonly continues to rise for hours after the initial insult before peaking. The overall clinical trajectory — improving heart rate, blood pressure, oxygenation, urine output, lactate, and symptom report — clearly indicates the patient is responding to anticoagulation. Escalating treatment based on the troponin alone, while ignoring the improving hemodynamic and oxygenation trends, would expose the patient to unnecessary procedural risk, particularly given the recent surgery.

Decision point 6: Considering the patient stable for discharge planning or step-down because the SpO₂ has reached 95% and the pain has improved. The patient remains on a non-rebreather mask at 15 L/min, the troponin is still rising, the RV function has not been reassessed by echocardiography, and he has been on therapeutic anticoagulation for only 4 hours. He requires continued ICU-level monitoring for at least 24–48 hours to ensure sustained hemodynamic stability, to confirm RV recovery on repeat imaging, and to monitor for complications including recurrent embolism, heparin-induced thrombocytopenia, and surgical-site bleeding.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Recognize the classic presentation of acute pulmonary embolism in a post-surgical patient and identify the constellation of cues (sudden pleuritic chest pain, hypoxemia, tachycardia, unilateral calf swelling, immobility) that distinguish PE from other causes of post-operative chest pain and dyspnea.
- Analyze diagnostic findings (ECG, troponin, BNP, CT-PA, ABG) to classify PE severity as submassive versus massive and understand how this classification drives treatment decisions.
- Evaluate the rationale for selecting unfractionated heparin over low-molecular-weight heparin in a recent post-surgical patient, including the considerations of reversibility, titrability, and the potential need for urgent invasive intervention.
- Apply the clinical reasoning required to determine when systemic thrombolysis is and is not indicated, particularly in the context of a relative contraindication (recent major surgery) and a submassive rather than massive PE classification.
- Evaluate the patient's response to treatment by integrating multiple trending parameters (hemodynamics, oxygenation, lactate, urine output, symptoms) and distinguish expected biomarker kinetics (continued troponin rise) from true clinical deterioration.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after CT-PA has confirmed a saddle PE with RV dilation, the patient has been classified as submassive PE, UFH has been initiated and is within therapeutic range, and the clinical trajectory over 4 hours shows hemodynamic and oxygenation improvement.

*Most likely condition:* Submassive pulmonary embolism. The saddle embolus on CT-PA with RV dilation (RV/LV ratio 1.4), elevated troponin and BNP indicating RV myocardial strain, the S1Q3T3 ECG pattern with right precordial T-wave inversions, and the clinical presentation of sudden pleuritic chest pain with hypoxemia and tachycardia in a post-surgical patient with DVT risk factors — all while maintaining a systolic blood pressure above 90 mmHg — constitute an unambiguous submassive PE.

*Plausible competing conditions:* (1) ST-elevation myocardial infarction — wrong here: the ECG shows an S1Q3T3 pattern and right precordial T-wave inversions consistent with RV strain, not ST elevation in a coronary territory; the troponin elevation is modest and consistent with RV pressure overload; and the CT-PA confirms the diagnosis as PE with no coronary findings. (2) Tension pneumothorax — wrong here: the chest X-ray shows no pneumothorax, lung sounds are clear bilaterally without absent breath sounds on one side, and the CT-PA shows no pneumothorax; the JVD and hypotension are explained by RV failure from the embolus, not mediastinal shift.

*Two priority actions:* (1) Continue the UFH infusion at the current rate (aPTT is 64 seconds, within the target of 60–80) and monitor the aPTT per protocol every 6 hours to maintain therapeutic anticoagulation — this is the definitive medical treatment preventing clot propagation and allowing endogenous fibrinolysis. (2) Maintain continuous hemodynamic monitoring in the ICU with specific attention to blood pressure, heart rate, SpO₂, urine output, and level of consciousness, with a clear escalation plan: if systolic BP falls below 90 mmHg for 15 minutes or more despite supportive care, notify the provider immediately for reclassification to massive PE and consideration of catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy.

*Two wrong actions:* (1) Requesting systemic thrombolysis because the troponin has risen slightly from 0.38 to 0.42 — wrong because the overall clinical trajectory is improving (HR, BP, SpO₂, lactate, urine output, and symptoms are all trending favorably), the troponin rise is an expected kinetic pattern in acute PE, and the patient is post-operative day 2 from major orthopedic surgery, making systemic thrombolysis a high-risk intervention that is not indicated in a hemodynamically stable submassive PE. (2) Positioning the patient supine or in Trendelenburg to address the borderline blood pressure — wrong because the failing right ventricle cannot tolerate increased preload; augmenting venous return would worsen RV distension, exacerbate septal bowing, compromise left ventricular filling, and could precipitate hemodynamic collapse.

*Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Heart rate and blood pressure trend — sustained improvement in tachycardia and blood pressure toward baseline confirms that the right ventricle is decompressing and cardiac output is recovering; conversely, recurrent tachycardia or hypotension would signal deterioration requiring escalation. (2) SpO₂ and respiratory rate — improving oxygenation with decreasing supplemental oxygen requirements and a normalizing respiratory rate confirm that ventilation-perfusion matching is improving as the clot burden is stabilized; failure to wean oxygen or worsening hypoxemia would indicate clot propagation or recurrent embolism.

*Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) D-dimer level — already markedly elevated and expected to remain elevated for days in a post-surgical patient with confirmed PE; serial D-dimer monitoring does not track acute treatment response and would not change management at this synthesis moment. (2) Surgical wound drainage — the dressing is clean, dry, and intact, and while surgical-site monitoring is part of routine post-operative care and is important after initiating anticoagulation, it is not the parameter that evaluates whether the PE itself is resolving; it monitors a complication of treatment, not the trajectory of the primary condition.
