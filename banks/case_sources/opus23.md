**CASE TITLE**
Nonaccidental trauma in a toddler with occult fractures and inconsistent caregiver history.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 22-month-old boy is brought to the emergency department by his mother's boyfriend, who states the child "fell off the couch about an hour ago and won't stop crying." The child's mother is at work and has not yet been contacted. The boyfriend reports he has been the primary daytime caregiver for approximately three months. The child has no significant past medical history, was born full-term, has no bleeding disorders, no metabolic bone disease, and immunizations are up to date per the state registry. There is no prior child protective services (CPS) involvement documented in the system. The child's last well-child visit was four months ago with no concerns noted. The ED physician orders a skeletal survey, CT head without contrast, basic metabolic panel, CBC with differential, coagulation studies, hepatic transaminases, lipase, and urinalysis. The child is placed on continuous cardiorespiratory monitoring and a peripheral IV is established.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The boyfriend carries the child into the ED. The child is crying inconsolably and holds his left arm against his body, refusing to move it. The boyfriend appears calm and states the child rolled off the couch onto a carpeted floor. When asked about the child's level of distress, the boyfriend says, "He's always been a crybaby — he does this for attention." He reports no loss of consciousness, no vomiting, and no seizure activity. He declines to call the child's mother initially, stating "she's busy at work and I can handle this."

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Weight 12.4 kg (50th percentile), length 86 cm (55th percentile). T 37.2 °C, HR 152, BP 96/58, RR 28, SpO₂ 99% on room air. The child is crying but arousable and tracks faces. Anterior fontanelle is closed and flat. Pupils equal, round, reactive to light bilaterally. No hemotympanum. Oral mucosa moist, no frenulum tear noted. The left upper extremity demonstrates swelling and point tenderness over the mid-humerus with guarding; the child screams and withdraws when the area is palpated. No crepitus appreciated. Radial pulse intact distally, capillary refill less than 2 seconds in the left hand, fingers pink and warm. The right upper extremity moves freely without distress. A cluster of four circular, well-demarcated bruises, each approximately 1 cm in diameter, is noted on the left lateral chest wall. Two linear bruises, approximately 3 cm and 5 cm in length, are present on the posterior right thigh. A fading yellowish-green bruise approximately 2 × 3 cm is noted on the left scapular region. The abdomen is soft, non-distended, no guarding or rebound. No bruising to the abdomen. Genitalia without bruising or abnormality. The child bears weight on both lower extremities when held upright but favors the left arm. Skin survey reveals no additional lesions. No petechiae.

**LABORATORY DATA**
CBC: WBC 9,800/µL, Hgb 11.4 g/dL, Hct 34.2%, platelets 312,000/µL. BMP: sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.3 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, CO₂ 22 mEq/L, BUN 12 mg/dL, creatinine 0.3 mg/dL, glucose 94 mg/dL, calcium 9.8 mg/dL. PT 12.8 seconds, INR 1.0, PTT 28 seconds. AST 34 U/L, ALT 28 U/L, lipase 42 U/L. Urinalysis: clear, yellow, specific gravity 1.018, no blood, no protein, no leukocyte esterase.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 90 minutes after arrival: The skeletal survey results return. Radiology identifies a transverse fracture of the left humeral shaft with minimal displacement, a healing posterior rib fracture at the left 8th rib with callus formation estimated at 2–3 weeks old, and a healing classic metaphyseal lesion (corner fracture) of the right distal tibia also with early callus formation. CT head is negative for intracranial hemorrhage or skull fracture. The ED physician speaks with the boyfriend, who now states the child "falls a lot" and "bruises easily." When asked specifically about the rib and tibial injuries, the boyfriend says he was unaware of any prior injuries and suggests the child may have hurt himself in his crib. The child's mother arrives and is visibly upset. She states she was unaware of any injuries other than today's and says the child has not been seen by any other provider recently. She reports the boyfriend "gets frustrated sometimes" but denies witnessing any physical discipline. The nurse notes the child clings to his mother and turns away from the boyfriend. HR has decreased to 128 with the mother present. The ED physician states the injury pattern is highly concerning for nonaccidental trauma and that a mandatory report to CPS is being filed. The boyfriend becomes agitated and states, "You can't just accuse me — kids get hurt all the time."

**Stage 2** — 3 hours after arrival: A CPS investigator arrives and begins interviewing the mother and boyfriend separately. The ED social worker is present. The mother, when interviewed alone, tearfully discloses that the boyfriend has "grabbed" the child roughly on several occasions and that she once saw a bruise on the child's chest about two weeks ago but the boyfriend told her the child bumped into a doorknob. She expresses fear that the boyfriend will "lose his temper" if she says anything. She asks the nurse, "Can you just not tell him what I said? I'm scared he'll hurt me too." Hospital security has been notified and the boyfriend is asked to wait in a separate area. The child is given weight-based ibuprofen for pain per the ED physician's order and a posterior splint is applied to the left upper extremity by orthopedics. The child is calmer, HR 118, BP 92/56, RR 24, SpO₂ 99%. Pain assessed using the FLACC scale: score of 4 (down from 8 at presentation). The CPS investigator requests that the child not be discharged until a safety plan is established. The ED physician places an order to admit the child to the pediatric unit for further evaluation, pain management, and coordination with CPS and the child abuse team.

**Stage 3** — 8 hours after arrival, now on the pediatric unit: The child abuse pediatrician has completed a comprehensive evaluation and documents the findings as "multiple injuries in various stages of healing, in a pre-ambulatory-risk pattern, with a history inconsistent with the injury mechanism — findings are diagnostic of physical abuse." A follow-up skeletal survey is ordered for two weeks per the child abuse team protocol to identify any additional occult fractures not visible on initial imaging. The mother is at the bedside. She asks the nurse, "What's going to happen to my son? Will they take him away from me?" She is tearful and states she feels guilty for not protecting him. The child is resting comfortably, HR 110, BP 90/54, RR 22, SpO₂ 99%, FLACC score 2. He is taking oral fluids and eating small amounts. The CPS investigator informs the nursing staff that a temporary safety plan has been established: the boyfriend is not permitted to have unsupervised contact with the child, and the mother has agreed to a safety plan that includes a temporary protective order. The CPS investigator asks the nurse to document the child's behavioral responses to each visitor and to notify CPS immediately if the boyfriend attempts to visit. Discharge planning is initiated with the social worker, CPS, and the mother.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At initial presentation, before imaging results, the nurse evaluates the bruising pattern and the caregiver's explanation. The child has circular bruises on the lateral chest wall, linear bruises on the posterior thigh, and a healing bruise on the scapula — areas that are not typical contact points in a mobile toddler's accidental falls. The caregiver describes a simple couch fall onto carpet. The nurse must recognize whether these findings are consistent with the stated mechanism. *Correct action:* Recognize that the bruising pattern — specifically the location on the torso, posterior thigh, and scapular region, the patterned shapes, and the presence of bruises in different stages of healing — is inconsistent with a short fall onto a soft surface and raises concern for inflicted injury. Document the size, shape, color, and exact anatomic location of every bruise using a body map, and communicate the concern to the ED physician. *Why it is correct:* Accidental bruising in toddlers typically occurs over bony prominences on the anterior body (shins, forehead, knees). Bruises on the torso, back, posterior thighs, and ears are sentinel locations for abuse. Patterned bruises (circular, linear) suggest an object or grip. Bruises in different stages of healing indicate repeated injury over time. The stated mechanism of a short fall onto carpet does not produce this distribution. Precise documentation preserves forensic evidence and supports the mandatory report. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the skeletal survey reveals three fractures in different stages of healing — an acute humeral shaft fracture, a healing posterior rib fracture, and a healing classic metaphyseal lesion of the distal tibia. The caregiver offers vague, shifting explanations. The nurse must identify the significance of this constellation. *Correct action:* Analyze the fracture pattern as highly specific for nonaccidental trauma. Posterior rib fractures in infants and toddlers result from anteroposterior thoracic compression and are rarely caused by accidental mechanisms. Classic metaphyseal lesions result from shearing forces at the growth plate from pulling or twisting of an extremity. Multiple fractures in different stages of healing indicate repeated episodes of trauma. The nurse should ensure the mandatory CPS report has been filed and confirm that the child abuse team has been consulted. *Why it is correct:* Posterior rib fractures and classic metaphyseal lesions are classified as high-specificity fractures for abuse by the American Academy of Pediatrics. Their presence in a pre-ambulatory-risk child with an inconsistent history effectively rules out accidental causation. The different stages of healing prove the injuries did not occur in a single event. Mandatory reporting is a legal obligation for nurses in all US jurisdictions when there is reasonable suspicion of child abuse — the nurse does not need certainty, only reasonable suspicion, and the threshold has been met. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues, prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the mother discloses domestic violence concerns and asks the nurse not to share her statements with the boyfriend. She is fearful. The nurse must determine the appropriate response to the mother's disclosure and her request for confidentiality. *Correct action:* Acknowledge the mother's fear and validate her courage in disclosing. Inform her that the nursing staff will protect her confidentiality to the greatest extent possible and that her statements to CPS are part of a protected investigation. Ensure the social worker and CPS investigator are aware of the domestic violence disclosure so that the safety plan addresses both the child's and the mother's safety. Provide the mother with domestic violence resources, including the National Domestic Violence Hotline number. Do not share the mother's specific statements with the boyfriend. Ensure security measures are in place. *Why it is correct:* The mother is both a potential protective factor for the child and a potential victim herself. Breaching her confidence to the alleged perpetrator could escalate danger to both her and the child. Domestic violence and child abuse frequently co-occur, and addressing the mother's safety is integral to the child's safety plan. The nurse's role includes advocacy, safety planning, and connecting the family to resources — not investigating the abuse or confronting the alleged perpetrator. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the boyfriend becomes agitated when told a CPS report is being filed and states the team "can't accuse" him. The nurse must determine the correct response regarding the mandatory reporting obligation. *Correct action:* Remain calm and non-confrontational. Inform the boyfriend that healthcare providers are legally mandated to report any reasonable suspicion of child abuse to CPS, that this is a legal obligation and not an accusation, and that the investigation will be conducted by CPS and law enforcement — not by the hospital staff. Do not engage in debate about guilt or innocence. Do not attempt to interview the boyfriend about the injuries — that is the role of CPS and law enforcement. Ensure hospital security is aware of the agitation and that the boyfriend is separated from the child and the mother. *Why it is correct:* Mandatory reporting laws require healthcare providers to report suspected abuse; the obligation is triggered by reasonable suspicion and does not require proof. The nurse's role is to report, document, and ensure safety — not to investigate or adjudicate. Engaging in confrontation or accusation can escalate a dangerous situation, compromise the legal investigation, and is outside the nursing scope. Maintaining a therapeutic, non-judgmental communication approach protects the nurse, the patient, and the integrity of the investigation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the mother asks the nurse what will happen to her son and expresses guilt. The child is stable and a safety plan is in place. The nurse must determine the priority nursing actions for discharge planning and psychosocial support. *Correct action:* Provide the mother with honest, supportive information: CPS's goal is child safety, and the current plan allows the child to remain with her as long as the safety conditions are met. Reinforce that she is taking protective action by cooperating with the safety plan. Ensure the mother understands the follow-up skeletal survey in two weeks, the orthopedic follow-up for the humeral fracture, and the importance of keeping all appointments. Coordinate with the social worker to confirm that community resources — including domestic violence services, counseling referrals, and a CPS caseworker contact — are provided before discharge. Document the mother's emotional state, her statements, and the child's behavioral responses to her presence. *Why it is correct:* The mother's guilt and fear are expected psychosocial responses. Therapeutic communication that validates her protective actions and provides concrete information reduces anxiety and promotes engagement with the safety plan. Ensuring she understands the medical follow-up — particularly the two-week follow-up skeletal survey, which may reveal additional fractures not visible on initial imaging — is essential for ongoing medical management. Coordinating multidisciplinary discharge planning (social work, CPS, medical follow-up) is a core nursing responsibility in child abuse cases. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision point 1)* Accepting the caregiver's explanation at face value because the child is a toddler who "falls a lot" and dismissing the bruising as accidental. This is wrong because the bruise locations (torso, posterior thigh, scapula), the patterned shapes, and the multiple stages of healing are inconsistent with typical toddler falls, which produce bruises over bony prominences on the anterior body. Normalizing these findings delays recognition and reporting.

- *(Decision point 1)* Failing to document the bruises with precise measurements, anatomic locations, and descriptions of color and shape, instead writing only "multiple bruises noted." This is unsafe because vague documentation undermines the forensic record, weakens the CPS investigation, and fails to meet the standard of care for suspected abuse documentation.

- *(Decision point 2)* Believing that the nurse must have definitive proof of abuse before filing a mandatory report, and therefore waiting for the child abuse team's final determination before notifying CPS. This is wrong because mandatory reporting is triggered by reasonable suspicion, not confirmed diagnosis. Delaying the report violates the law and leaves the child in potential danger during the delay.

- *(Decision point 2)* Attributing the posterior rib fracture and metaphyseal lesion to rough play or a prior unwitnessed fall because the child is active and mobile. This is wrong because posterior rib fractures require significant compressive force not generated by falls or play, and classic metaphyseal lesions result from shearing forces inconsistent with accidental mechanisms in a toddler. These are high-specificity fractures for inflicted injury.

- *(Decision point 3)* Telling the mother that the nurse is required to share all information with both caregivers or with the boyfriend because he brought the child in. This is wrong because the boyfriend is the suspected perpetrator, not the legal guardian making medical decisions, and sharing the mother's disclosure with him could endanger both the mother and the child. The nurse's obligation is to the patient's safety, and the mother's statements are part of a protected CPS investigation.

- *(Decision point 4)* Attempting to confront the boyfriend with the radiologic findings or to elicit a confession by presenting the evidence of multiple fractures. This is wrong because forensic interviewing is outside the nursing scope of practice, confrontation can escalate violence, and any statements obtained through improper questioning may be inadmissible and can compromise the legal case.

- *(Decision point 5)* Focusing discharge teaching exclusively on the orthopedic injury (cast care, pain management, follow-up) without addressing the safety plan, the follow-up skeletal survey, or psychosocial resources. This is wrong because the medical management of the fracture is only one component of the discharge plan; failure to reinforce the CPS safety plan and connect the family to domestic violence and counseling resources leaves the underlying danger unaddressed and increases the risk of recurrence.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Recognize bruising patterns and fracture types that are inconsistent with stated mechanisms and raise concern for nonaccidental trauma in a pediatric patient.
- Apply mandatory reporting obligations correctly, understanding that reasonable suspicion — not confirmed diagnosis — triggers the duty to report.
- Demonstrate therapeutic communication with a caregiver who discloses domestic violence in the context of child abuse, including appropriate confidentiality boundaries and resource provision.
- Differentiate the nursing role (document, report, ensure safety, advocate) from the investigative role (CPS, law enforcement) when nonaccidental trauma is suspected.
- Coordinate multidisciplinary discharge planning that addresses medical follow-up, psychosocial support, and child safety in collaboration with CPS and social work.
