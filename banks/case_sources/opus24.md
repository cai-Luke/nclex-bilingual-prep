**CASE TITLE**
Elder neglect with medication mismanagement in a home health setting.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
An 81-year-old woman lives with her adult son in a single-story home. She has a history of heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (last echocardiogram EF 30%), type 2 diabetes managed with insulin glargine 18 units subcutaneously at bedtime and metformin 500 mg twice daily, hypertension, mild cognitive impairment (Montreal Cognitive Assessment score 21/30 six months ago), and chronic kidney disease stage 3a (baseline eGFR 52 mL/min/1.73 m²). She was discharged from the hospital ten days ago after an acute heart failure exacerbation. Discharge medications include furosemide 40 mg oral twice daily, lisinopril 10 mg oral daily, carvedilol 12.5 mg oral twice daily, potassium chloride 20 mEq oral daily, insulin glargine 18 units subcutaneous at bedtime, and metformin 500 mg oral twice daily. A home health nurse has been ordered to visit three times weekly for medication reconciliation, heart failure assessment, and wound monitoring of a stage II pressure injury on the sacrum that was documented at discharge (2 cm × 1.5 cm, shallow, pink wound bed, no tunneling, no exudate). The son is listed as the primary caregiver and was educated on the medication regimen, daily weight monitoring, dietary sodium restriction, and pressure injury repositioning schedule before discharge.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The home health nurse arrives for the first scheduled visit, ten days after discharge. The son answers the door and states his mother is "doing fine, just sleeping a lot." He reports he has been "giving her the pills" but appears vague about which medications she takes and when. He says he has not been checking her blood glucose or weighing her daily because "she doesn't like being bothered." The patient is found in a recliner in the living room. She is drowsy but arousable to voice, oriented to person and place but not to date. She states she feels "weak" and "not hungry." She reports she has been sitting in the recliner "most of the time" because her son told her it was easier than getting in and out of bed. She says she has not had much to eat or drink in several days. A faint odor of urine is noted on her clothing.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 36.4 °C, HR 54, BP 92/58, RR 18, SpO₂ 94% on room air. Weight not obtainable at this visit (no functioning scale in the home; the son says the battery died). The patient appears thin, with dry oral mucosa, poor skin turgor on the forearm, and sunken eyes. Bilateral lower extremities show no edema — a change from the 2+ pitting edema documented at discharge. Capillary refill 4 seconds in the fingers. Heart sounds are regular but bradycardic; no S3 gallop appreciated today. Lungs have faint bibasilar crackles, decreased from the diffuse crackles noted at discharge. Abdomen soft, non-distended, bowel sounds hypoactive. The patient has difficulty rising from the recliner without maximal assistance. Muscle strength is grossly 3/5 in all extremities. The sacral pressure injury is now approximately 3 cm × 2.5 cm, deeper than at discharge, with a pale wound bed and a thin rim of macerated periwound skin; a moderate amount of serous drainage is present on the cushion beneath her. No staging change is documented yet, but the wound has clearly worsened. The patient has a soiled incontinence brief that she reports has not been changed since the previous evening. Several medication bottles are on the kitchen counter; the furosemide bottle appears nearly empty, the potassium chloride bottle appears full, and the insulin pen is in the refrigerator with no evidence of recent use (the pen's dose counter is at the same position as a new pen). The metformin bottle has an appropriate number of pills missing. The son becomes defensive when asked about the medication schedule and states, "I'm doing the best I can — she's old."

**LABORATORY DATA**
Point-of-care fingerstick blood glucose: 268 mg/dL. The nurse draws labs per home health protocol for heart failure follow-up. Results return later that day: sodium 149 mEq/L, potassium 2.9 mEq/L, chloride 98 mEq/L, CO₂ 30 mEq/L, BUN 48 mg/dL, creatinine 1.8 mg/dL (baseline 1.2), eGFR 28 mL/min/1.73 m², magnesium 1.4 mEq/L, hemoglobin A1c (drawn at discharge) 8.4%, albumin 2.6 g/dL, prealbumin 11 mg/dL.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Day of first home visit (day 10 post-discharge). The nurse completes the assessment described above. The clinical picture is a frail, dehydrated, malnourished elderly woman with bradycardia and hypotension, hypernatremia, severe hypokalemia, acute-on-chronic kidney injury, uncontrolled hyperglycemia from apparent insulin omission, and a worsening pressure injury — all in the context of a caregiver who cannot describe the medication regimen, has not performed any of the monitoring tasks taught at discharge, and has left the patient in a single position for prolonged periods in soiled clothing. The home environment shows no functioning scale, no blood glucose log, and medication bottles whose fill levels are inconsistent with adherence to the prescribed regimen. The furosemide appears to have been given (bottle nearly empty, consistent with twice-daily dosing over ten days), but the potassium chloride bottle is nearly full (suggesting it was rarely or never given), and the insulin pen appears unused. The nurse contacts the home health agency supervisor and the patient's primary care provider to report the findings. The provider orders the patient to be sent to the emergency department for evaluation of the electrolyte derangements, dehydration, and acute kidney injury. The nurse also initiates the agency's mandatory reporting protocol for suspected elder neglect.

**Stage 2** — Emergency department, approximately 3 hours after the home visit. The patient arrives by ambulance. She is now more lethargic, oriented only to person, and complains of muscle weakness and "fluttering" in her chest. Vitals: T 36.2 °C, HR 48, BP 88/52, RR 20, SpO₂ 93% on room air. Telemetry shows sinus bradycardia with prominent U waves and flattened T waves. A 12-lead ECG confirms these findings with a prolonged QT interval. IV access is established. The emergency provider orders a 500 mL normal saline bolus, IV potassium chloride 20 mEq in 100 mL NS to infuse over 1 hour via infusion pump on a peripheral line (per ED protocol: peripheral IV potassium infusions limited to 20 mEq/hr at a concentration no greater than 20 mEq/100 mL, with continuous telemetry), and IV magnesium sulfate 2 g in 50 mL NS over 1 hour. Furosemide is held. Carvedilol is held pending hemodynamic stabilization. The provider orders fingerstick blood glucose every 4 hours and a low dose of insulin correction per sliding scale; the standing insulin glargine is held until the patient is eating and the clinical picture stabilizes. Repeat labs are ordered for 2 hours after the potassium infusion. An adult protective services (APS) referral has been initiated by the home health agency; the ED social worker is also notified and begins a parallel assessment. The son is present in the ED and states he "didn't know the potassium pill was important" and "forgot about the insulin because she wasn't eating."

**Stage 3** — Six hours after ED arrival. The patient has received the NS bolus, the IV potassium, and the IV magnesium. Repeat labs: sodium 146 mEq/L, potassium 3.4 mEq/L, creatinine 1.6 mg/dL, eGFR 33 mL/min/1.73 m², magnesium 1.8 mEq/L, blood glucose 198 mg/dL. Vitals: T 36.6 °C, HR 62, BP 100/64, RR 18, SpO₂ 96% on 2 L nasal cannula. Telemetry now shows normal sinus rhythm; U waves have resolved, T waves are upright, and QT interval has shortened. The patient is more alert, oriented to person and place, and is taking small sips of fluid. Urine output over the past 4 hours is 120 mL (30 mL/hr). The provider orders a second 20 mEq IV potassium infusion and continued IV fluids at 100 mL/hr. The wound care nurse evaluates the sacral pressure injury and documents it as a worsened stage II injury, now 3 cm × 2.5 cm with a pale base, consistent with sustained pressure and poor nutrition; a pressure-redistribution mattress is ordered, and a repositioning schedule every 2 hours is initiated. The social worker reports that the APS referral is active, a case investigator has been assigned, and a care conference is being arranged to evaluate the patient's discharge safety. The metformin is reviewed in light of the eGFR of 28 mL/min/1.73 m²; the provider discontinues metformin given the eGFR is below 30 and plans to reassess renal function as hydration continues. The nurse is coordinating the ongoing plan of care, including nutritional support (dietary consult ordered, albumin 2.6 and prealbumin 11 indicating protein-calorie malnutrition), safe medication administration, skin integrity interventions, and the social work and APS processes.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the home health nurse must recognize which constellation of findings — the medication bottle discrepancies, the soiled clothing, the worsened pressure injury, the prolonged immobility in a recliner, the absence of monitoring, and the caregiver's inability to describe the regimen — constitutes a pattern, and classify it correctly. *Correct action:* Identify the pattern as indicators of elder neglect (failure to provide adequate care, hygiene, nutrition, and medication management by the responsible caregiver) rather than attributing each finding to the patient's chronic illness alone. *Why:* Neglect is the most common form of elder mistreatment. The convergence of caregiver knowledge deficits, omitted care tasks, environmental evidence of medication non-administration, prolonged immobility, soiled clothing, malnutrition, dehydration, and a worsening wound in a cognitively impaired patient who depends on the caregiver for ADLs meets the clinical threshold for suspected neglect. Attributing the decline solely to disease progression would miss the modifiable human factor and delay protective intervention. *Skill:* Recognize cues, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must determine the most dangerous medication-related problem by correlating the medication bottle evidence with the patient's vital signs and clinical presentation. *Correct action:* Identify that the patient has likely been receiving furosemide without potassium supplementation (furosemide bottle nearly empty, potassium bottle nearly full), producing severe hypokalemia in the setting of concurrent bradycardia and hypotension — a combination that poses an immediate cardiac risk. *Why:* Furosemide causes renal potassium wasting. Without the prescribed potassium chloride replacement, the patient's potassium has dropped to 2.9 mEq/L. Severe hypokalemia causes cardiac conduction abnormalities (U waves, flattened T waves, prolonged QT, risk of torsades de pointes) and profound muscle weakness. The bradycardia and hypotension compound the danger. The insulin omission and hyperglycemia are also clinically significant but do not carry the same immediate lethality as the potassium deficit with cardiac effects. *Skill:* Analyze cues, prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 1 (labs confirm when they return, but the medication bottle evidence and the vital signs are available at the visit).

3. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must decide the correct sequence of professional actions after completing the assessment. *Correct action:* The nurse must notify the home health agency supervisor and the primary care provider of the acute clinical findings and the suspected neglect, arrange emergent transfer to the ED for the electrolyte and hemodynamic instability, and initiate the mandatory report of suspected elder neglect per the agency's reporting protocol and state law. All three actions are required; the mandatory report must not be deferred until after the medical situation is resolved, because nurses are mandated reporters and the obligation is triggered by reasonable suspicion, not by confirmed diagnosis. *Why:* The patient's hemodynamic instability and electrolyte derangements require a level of monitoring and intervention beyond home health capability. Simultaneously, the nurse has a legal and ethical duty as a mandated reporter to report suspected abuse or neglect to the designated authority (adult protective services or equivalent). Delaying the report to "gather more evidence" or deferring it to the hospital team does not fulfill the nurse's individual reporting obligation. *Skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse in the ED must administer IV potassium safely. The order is 20 mEq KCl in 100 mL NS over 1 hour via peripheral IV with continuous telemetry, per ED protocol. The nurse must identify the critical safety parameters for this infusion. *Correct action:* Administer the potassium via infusion pump (never free-flow), verify continuous telemetry monitoring is active before starting the infusion, monitor the IV site for infiltration and phlebitis (peripheral potassium infusions are vesicants at higher concentrations), and monitor for signs of hyperkalemia during and after the infusion (the patient's eGFR is 28, so potassium clearance is impaired and overcorrection is possible). The nurse should also verify that the magnesium is being repleted concurrently, because hypomagnesemia impairs renal potassium reabsorption and hypokalemia is refractory until magnesium is corrected. *Why:* IV potassium is a high-alert medication. Rapid or unmonitored infusion can cause fatal hyperkalemia. The impaired renal function in this patient increases the risk of overcorrection. Concurrent magnesium repletion is essential for effective potassium correction. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the provider discontinues metformin because the eGFR is 28 mL/min/1.73 m². The nurse must evaluate whether this decision is consistent with the patient's clinical data and understand the rationale. *Correct action:* Recognize that discontinuing metformin is appropriate. Metformin is contraindicated when eGFR falls below 30 mL/min/1.73 m² due to the risk of lactic acidosis from impaired renal clearance of the drug. The patient's eGFR was 52 at baseline (stage 3a CKD, where metformin at the current dose was acceptable) but has now declined to 28 in the setting of acute kidney injury from dehydration. Even if the AKI partially resolves with rehydration, the drug should remain held until renal function is reassessed and confirmed above the safe threshold. *Why:* Metformin is renally cleared. Below an eGFR of 30, accumulation increases the risk of lactic acidosis, a rare but life-threatening adverse effect. The distinction matters: at eGFR 30–45, dose reduction and monitoring are recommended; below 30, the drug is contraindicated. The nurse must understand this threshold to safely manage the medication list going forward and to educate any future caregiver. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse must evaluate the overall response to treatment and identify which findings confirm improvement versus which require continued vigilance. *Correct action:* The resolution of U waves and normalization of T-wave morphology on telemetry, the rise in potassium from 2.9 to 3.4 mEq/L, the improving heart rate from 48 to 62, the rising blood pressure from 88/52 to 100/64, the improving mental status, and the onset of urine output at 30 mL/hr all confirm that the acute dehydration and hypokalemia are responding to treatment. However, the potassium is still below normal (target ≥ 3.5), the sodium remains elevated at 146, the creatinine is still above baseline at 1.6, the blood glucose remains elevated at 198, the pressure injury has not yet begun to heal, and the nutritional markers (albumin 2.6, prealbumin 11) reflect a deficit that will take days to weeks to correct. The APS investigation and safe discharge planning are unresolved. The nurse should continue telemetry monitoring, strict intake and output, serial electrolyte checks, blood glucose monitoring, pressure injury care with repositioning every 2 hours, nutritional support, and coordination with social work regarding the neglect investigation and discharge safety. *Why:* Partial improvement does not equal resolution. Premature relaxation of monitoring — or premature discharge planning back to the same unsafe environment — would place the patient at risk for recurrence of the same harms. The nurse's advocacy role includes ensuring that the systemic issues (caregiver capacity, APS involvement, safe discharge destination) are addressed before the patient returns to the community. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Attributing the patient's decline entirely to her chronic illnesses and cognitive impairment — "She has heart failure and dementia, so of course she's declining" — rather than recognizing the pattern of caregiver neglect. This error normalizes preventable harm and delays protective intervention. The convergence of omitted medications, soiled clothing, immobility, worsening wound, and caregiver defensiveness is not a natural disease trajectory; it is a failure of care provision.

- *(Decision 2)* Focusing on the hyperglycemia (blood glucose 268) as the most dangerous medication-related finding because the number looks dramatically abnormal, while overlooking the hypokalemia. A blood glucose of 268 is clinically significant and requires correction, but it does not carry the same immediate lethality as a potassium of 2.9 with bradycardia and ECG changes. Prioritizing the glucose over the potassium delays recognition of the cardiac emergency.

- *(Decision 2)* Assuming the bradycardia is solely a carvedilol effect and therefore expected, without connecting it to the hypokalemia. While carvedilol does lower heart rate, a heart rate of 54 with hypotension in a dehydrated, hypokalemic patient is not a benign beta-blocker effect — it reflects compounding insults, and the hypokalemia is the correctable, life-threatening component.

- *(Decision 3)* Deferring the mandatory report of suspected neglect because the nurse wants to "confirm neglect first" or believes the report should come from the hospital rather than the home health nurse. Mandated reporting is triggered by reasonable suspicion, not confirmed findings, and the obligation belongs to the individual nurse who identifies the concern. Deferring the report violates the legal duty and leaves the patient unprotected during the interim.

- *(Decision 3)* Attempting to manage the electrolyte abnormalities and dehydration in the home setting with oral fluids and oral potassium rather than arranging emergent transfer. The severity of the hypokalemia (2.9 mEq/L with cardiac effects), the hemodynamic instability, and the acute kidney injury exceed the safe scope of home health intervention. Oral replacement is too slow and unreliable in a patient who is not eating.

- *(Decision 4)* Infusing the IV potassium without an infusion pump or without verifying that telemetry is active, reasoning that "it's only 20 mEq." Any IV potassium infusion requires pump-controlled delivery and cardiac monitoring because of the risk of fatal arrhythmia from inadvertent rapid infusion. The patient's impaired renal function makes this even more critical.

- *(Decision 4)* Neglecting to check whether magnesium is being repleted concurrently with potassium. Hypokalemia is often refractory to potassium replacement alone when concurrent hypomagnesemia is present, because magnesium is required for renal potassium conservation. Failing to address the magnesium (1.4 mEq/L, below normal) would result in persistent hypokalemia despite adequate potassium dosing.

- *(Decision 5)* Continuing metformin because the patient "has always taken it" or because the nurse does not connect the eGFR change to the drug's safety profile. The baseline eGFR of 52 permitted metformin use; the current eGFR of 28 does not. Failing to recognize this threshold crossing exposes the patient to lactic acidosis risk.

- *(Decision 6)* Interpreting the improved potassium of 3.4 and the better heart rate as evidence that the patient is "stable for discharge" without recognizing that the potassium is still below normal, the renal function is still impaired, the nutritional deficit is unaddressed, the pressure injury is worsening, and no safe discharge environment has been established. Premature discharge back to the same caregiver without APS resolution and a revised care plan would recreate the conditions that caused the deterioration.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the clinical indicators of elder neglect in a home health setting, distinguishing caregiver-related harm from expected disease progression.
- Correlate medication administration evidence (bottle fill levels, pen counters, caregiver knowledge gaps) with the patient's clinical and laboratory findings to identify the most dangerous medication-related problem.
- Apply the mandated reporter obligation correctly — initiating the report based on reasonable suspicion, without deferring to another provider or waiting for confirmed findings.
- Implement safe IV potassium administration, including pump use, telemetry monitoring, concurrent magnesium repletion, and awareness of renal-impairment-related overcorrection risk.
- Evaluate partial clinical improvement against unresolved problems — including ongoing electrolyte deficits, renal impairment, nutritional deficiency, wound deterioration, and unresolved social safety concerns — to prevent premature relaxation of the plan of care.
