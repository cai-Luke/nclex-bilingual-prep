**CASE TITLE**
Safe discharge teaching for a limited-English-proficient patient with a new colostomy.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 62-year-old Spanish-speaking woman is on postoperative day three following an open sigmoid colectomy with end colostomy formation for perforated diverticulitis. She was admitted emergently four days ago with acute abdomen, underwent surgery, and has been recovering on a surgical unit. Her medical history includes type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 1,000 mg twice daily (held perioperatively, restarted this morning after confirmed adequate oral intake and a serum creatinine of 0.9 mg/dL), hypertension managed with lisinopril 10 mg daily, and obesity (BMI 34). She lives with her adult daughter, who speaks fluent English and Spanish and has been at the bedside throughout the stay. The patient speaks and reads only Spanish; her documented preferred language is Spanish, and the medical record contains no documentation that qualified interpreter services have been offered or used during this admission. Current orders include regular diet as tolerated, ambulation four times daily, saline lock, acetaminophen 650 mg every six hours as needed, oxycodone 5 mg every four hours as needed (she has used two doses in the past 24 hours), wound and stoma assessments every shift, and discharge planning to begin today. A wound-ostomy-continence (WOC) nurse consult was placed on postoperative day one but has not yet been completed. The surgeon has written a conditional discharge order: "Discharge when patient demonstrates independent pouch emptying and verbalization of signs of stoma complications."

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The day-shift nurse assumes care at 0700 on postoperative day three. The patient is sitting up in bed, appears comfortable, and smiles but responds to English questions with brief nods and single words ("yes," "okay") that do not clearly match the content of the questions. Her daughter, present at the bedside, has been translating instructions from the night nurse and states, "I already showed her how to empty the bag — she did it once with me helping. We're ready to go home." The night-shift documentation notes: "Patient educated on ostomy care via daughter interpreting. Patient nodded understanding." There is no teach-back documented, no WOC nurse note, and no record of qualified interpreter use.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 37.2 °C, HR 78, BP 134/82, RR 16, SpO₂ 97% on room air. Abdomen soft, mildly tender around the incision, no distension. Midline incision clean, dry, approximated with staples, mild surrounding erythema consistent with expected healing, no drainage. Stoma is round, beefy red, moist, approximately 3 cm in diameter, protrudes about 1 cm above skin level, with audible bowel sounds in all quadrants. The pouching system is a two-piece, cut-to-fit wafer with a drainable pouch; the wafer was applied by the night nurse and fits appropriately with approximately 1/8-inch clearance around the stoma. The pouch contains a moderate amount of thick, greenish-brown stool — the first formed output since surgery. Peristomal skin is intact, no redness or breakdown. The patient guards her abdomen slightly when repositioning but rates pain 3/10 on a numeric scale (demonstrated with fingers). She is ambulatory to the bathroom with a steady gait.

**LABORATORY DATA**
WBC 9,800/µL, Hgb 11.4 g/dL, platelets 268,000/µL, BUN 14 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, potassium 4.0 mEq/L, sodium 139 mEq/L, glucose 148 mg/dL, albumin 3.1 g/dL. No labs are pending.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 0700–0900, postoperative day three. The nurse reviews the chart and identifies the gaps: no qualified interpreter has been used, no WOC nurse visit has occurred, and the discharge criteria require demonstrated independent pouch emptying and verbalized understanding of stoma complications. The daughter offers to continue interpreting and states she will be living with the patient after discharge. When the nurse asks the patient — through the daughter — to describe what a normal stoma looks like, the daughter answers for her: "It should be red and wet." The patient nods. When the nurse asks the patient directly (in simple English with gestures) to show how she would empty the pouch, the patient looks to her daughter for direction. The daughter begins guiding her hands. The nurse contacts the hospital's qualified medical interpreter service and requests a Spanish interpreter for discharge teaching. The interpreter is available by video remote within 15 minutes. The nurse also pages the WOC nurse, who confirms she can arrive by 1000.

**Stage 2** — 1000–1200. The WOC nurse arrives and conducts a full ostomy teaching session using the qualified video interpreter. With interpretation, the patient asks several questions she had not previously raised: whether she can shower, whether the pouch will smell, and whether she can eat beans and rice. She also reveals she has been afraid to look at the stoma and has been letting her daughter handle all pouch care. The WOC nurse guides the patient through independent pouch emptying; the patient performs it correctly on the second attempt. The WOC nurse then teaches wafer removal and reapplication using a pre-cut demonstration wafer. The patient removes the old wafer, inspects the peristomal skin (intact), cleans the area, and applies the new wafer with verbal coaching only — no hands-on assistance. During the session the nurse notes the stoma output has increased; the pouch required emptying twice in two hours, with output now thinner and more yellow-green. Vitals: T 37.1 °C, HR 74, BP 130/78, RR 14, SpO₂ 98%. The patient rates pain 2/10. The WOC nurse provides written ostomy-care instructions in Spanish (available from the facility's patient-education library) and a list of ostomy supply vendors that accept the patient's insurance. The nurse documents the teaching session, interpreter use (interpreter ID and language), return demonstration results, and the patient's verbalized understanding — through the interpreter — of three key warning signs: stoma color change from red to dark purple or black, peristomal skin breakdown, and no stoma output for more than six hours accompanied by cramping or distension.

**Stage 3** — 1300–1500, discharge preparation. The surgeon rounds, reviews the WOC nurse's documentation, and confirms discharge criteria are met. The nurse prepares discharge paperwork. Using the qualified interpreter again, the nurse conducts a comprehensive discharge review: medication reconciliation (metformin restarted, lisinopril continued, acetaminophen as needed, oxycodone prescription for five days with instructions on safe storage and disposal), follow-up appointments (surgeon in one week, WOC nurse outpatient visit in two weeks), dietary guidance (advance slowly, chew thoroughly, introduce gas-producing foods one at a time), activity restrictions (no lifting greater than 10 pounds for six weeks per surgeon's order), and when to call the surgeon or go to the emergency department. During the medication review, the patient asks through the interpreter whether she should check her blood sugar at home now that she is back on metformin. The nurse confirms the patient's home glucometer is available and reviews the target fasting glucose range per her primary care provider's standing instructions (70–130 mg/dL). The daughter is present and participates, but the nurse directs all teach-back questions to the patient through the interpreter. The patient correctly verbalizes, in her own words (interpreted), the three stoma warning signs, how to empty and change the pouch, when to call the surgeon, and her medication schedule. The nurse provides a 24-hour ostomy-support hotline number and confirms the patient has enough ostomy supplies for two weeks. Vitals at discharge: T 37.0 °C, HR 76, BP 128/76, RR 14, SpO₂ 98%. Pain 1/10. Stoma remains red, moist, and functioning. The patient is discharged home with her daughter at 1445.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse discovers that all prior ostomy education was conducted using the daughter as an ad hoc interpreter, with no qualified medical interpreter and no documented teach-back. The daughter is willing and bilingual. Should the nurse proceed with discharge teaching through the daughter or arrange a qualified interpreter? *Correct action:* Arrange a qualified medical interpreter before conducting discharge teaching. The daughter may remain present for support, but she should not serve as the primary interpreter for clinical education and informed decision-making. *Why:* Federal regulations (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, operationalized through CMS Conditions of Participation) require that hospitals provide qualified interpreter services to patients with limited English proficiency at no cost. In this case, the daughter has been answering questions on the patient's behalf rather than interpreting the patient's own words, the patient has not demonstrated independent understanding, and the discharge criteria explicitly require the patient's own verbalization. An ad hoc family interpreter in this context introduces risks of filtering, omission, and role conflict — the daughter is simultaneously a caregiver and a family member with her own anxieties — and the record shows no evidence the patient was ever offered qualified services. The specific facts that defeat informal interpretation here are: the daughter answered for the patient rather than translating the patient's response, the patient could not independently demonstrate the skill, and the clinical stakes are high (new ostomy self-care at home). *Skill:* Recognize cues, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must decide whether the patient currently meets the surgeon's stated discharge criteria ("demonstrate independent pouch emptying and verbalization of signs of stoma complications"). *Correct action:* The patient does not yet meet discharge criteria. The nurse should communicate this to the care team and advocate for delaying discharge until the criteria are fulfilled with the support of a qualified interpreter and the WOC nurse. *Why:* The patient has not independently emptied the pouch — the daughter guided her hands — and her "verbalization" of complications was actually the daughter's statement. Without a language-concordant assessment, the nurse cannot confirm the patient's own comprehension. Discharging now would not satisfy the conditional order and would place the patient at risk for complications she cannot recognize or manage. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient reveals she has been afraid to look at the stoma and has been relying entirely on her daughter. The WOC nurse is present. What is the priority nursing action? *Correct action:* Address the patient's psychosocial readiness before proceeding with technical skill teaching. Acknowledge her fear, provide brief supportive counseling (normalizing the emotional response to a new ostomy), and then guide her through a graduated first look at the stoma with the interpreter facilitating communication. *Why:* A patient who avoids looking at the stoma will not perform self-care at home regardless of technical instruction. Psychosocial adaptation — body-image adjustment and self-efficacy — is a prerequisite for effective ostomy self-management. Proceeding with hands-on teaching before addressing the emotional barrier wastes the teaching opportunity and risks reinforcing avoidance. *Skill:* Analyze cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the stoma output has changed from thick greenish-brown stool to thinner, more frequent yellow-green output. Should the nurse be concerned, and what action is appropriate? *Correct action:* This change is expected as bowel function normalizes after sigmoid colostomy; the output is transitioning as the patient advances her diet and peristalsis increases. The nurse should document the output character and volume, ensure adequate oral fluid intake, and continue routine monitoring — no escalation is needed. *Why:* A sigmoid colostomy typically produces semi-formed to formed stool once fully functioning, but in the early postoperative period, output may be looser and more frequent as the bowel recovers. The patient's vitals are stable, she is tolerating diet, and there are no signs of obstruction or infection. Thinner output alone, without fever, abdominal distension, high-volume watery losses, or electrolyte derangement, does not warrant intervention beyond monitoring and hydration. *Skill:* Analyze cues, evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, during discharge medication reconciliation, the patient asks whether she should monitor her blood sugar now that metformin has been restarted. The nurse must determine whether this is within scope to address and what to teach. *Correct action:* Confirm that home glucose monitoring is appropriate per the patient's existing diabetes management plan, review the target range from her primary care provider's standing instructions, and ensure she has a functioning glucometer and test strips at home. Reinforce that metformin alone carries a low risk of hypoglycemia but that she should report persistent readings above 200 mg/dL or symptoms of hyperglycemia to her primary care provider, especially during the postoperative recovery period when stress hyperglycemia may occur. *Why:* The patient's glucose is 148 mg/dL — mildly elevated but expected in the postoperative/stress state. Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia as a primary effect; the concern with metformin is lactic acidosis in the setting of renal impairment, which is not present here (creatinine 0.9). Reinforcing self-monitoring supports continuity of her chronic disease management during a vulnerable transition. *Skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse must determine whether the discharge teaching has been effective and the patient is safe to go home. What constitutes adequate evaluation? *Correct action:* The nurse evaluates effectiveness by confirming, through the qualified interpreter, that the patient can independently perform pouch emptying and wafer change (return demonstration already completed in Stage 2), verbalize the three stoma warning signs in her own words, state when to call the surgeon, recite her medication schedule, and describe her activity restrictions. The nurse also confirms the patient has written instructions in Spanish, adequate supplies, scheduled follow-up, and a support contact (the 24-hour hotline). All of these elements are documented. *Why:* Teach-back through a qualified interpreter is the standard for confirming comprehension in a patient with limited English proficiency. Return demonstration confirms psychomotor skill. Documentation of interpreter-facilitated education protects the patient and satisfies regulatory and accreditation requirements. A discharge is safe only when the patient — not a surrogate — demonstrates understanding. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Accepting the daughter as an adequate interpreter because she is bilingual and willing. This is wrong in this context because the daughter has been answering for the patient rather than interpreting, the patient has not demonstrated independent understanding, and the clinical stakes (surgical self-care at home) require accuracy and completeness that ad hoc interpretation cannot guarantee. The error conflates bilingual ability with qualified medical interpretation.

- *(Decision 1)* Assuming the patient understands because she nods and says "yes" and "okay" in English. Acquiescent responses in a non-primary language are a well-recognized false signal of comprehension. The nurse must assess understanding through the patient's own explanations in her preferred language, not through yes/no responses to English questions.

- *(Decision 2)* Proceeding with discharge on the basis that the daughter will manage ostomy care at home, reasoning that a capable caregiver satisfies the discharge criteria. The surgeon's order specifies the *patient's* demonstration and verbalization. Caregiver involvement is valuable but does not substitute for patient self-management competency, and discharging without meeting the stated criteria is unsafe and outside the nurse's authority.

- *(Decision 3)* Skipping the psychosocial assessment and moving directly into hands-on pouch-change instruction because time is limited and discharge is planned for today. This ignores the patient's stated fear and avoidance, which will undermine adherence at home. Technical teaching without emotional readiness is inefficient and unsafe.

- *(Decision 4)* Reporting the thinner, more frequent stoma output to the surgeon as possible diarrhea or early obstruction, potentially delaying discharge unnecessarily. In the absence of high-volume watery output, abdominal distension, or systemic signs, this change is a normal postoperative progression and does not require escalation.

- *(Decision 5)* Warning the patient that metformin may cause low blood sugar and instructing her to keep juice at the bedside. Metformin does not cause hypoglycemia as a primary effect; attributing this risk to metformin is a common pharmacologic misconception. The patient is not on insulin or a sulfonylurea.

- *(Decision 6)* Documenting "patient educated" without recording interpreter use, teach-back responses, or return-demonstration results, and considering the discharge teaching complete. This fails to meet documentation standards, provides no evidence of comprehension, and leaves the facility without proof that language-access requirements were met.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize when a limited-English-proficient patient's comprehension has not been adequately assessed and identify the specific cues that necessitate qualified interpreter services for clinical teaching.
- Advocate for delaying discharge when stated discharge criteria have not been met, even under time pressure or when a willing family caregiver is available.
- Integrate psychosocial assessment into ostomy teaching by addressing body-image concerns and fear before proceeding with technical skill instruction.
- Differentiate expected early postoperative colostomy output changes from findings that require escalation.
- Evaluate discharge readiness using interpreter-facilitated teach-back and return demonstration, and document the process completely.
