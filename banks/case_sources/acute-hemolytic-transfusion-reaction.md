**CASE TITLE**
Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction during packed red blood cell administration.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 72-year-old woman with a history of chronic kidney disease stage 3b (eGFR 38 mL/min), heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (EF 55% on last echocardiogram eight months ago), hypertension, and type 2 diabetes is admitted to a medical-surgical unit for symptomatic anemia. She has had progressive fatigue, exertional dyspnea on walking to the bathroom, and lightheadedness over the past week. Her outpatient hemoglobin was 6.4 g/dL, prompting her primary care provider to send her to the emergency department. She has no history of prior transfusions and no known allergies. Her blood type is A-positive, confirmed by the blood bank on a current type-and-screen specimen drawn in the ED.

Current orders include two units of packed red blood cells (pRBCs) to be transfused sequentially, each unit over two hours per facility protocol. Furosemide 20 mg IV is ordered between units to reduce volume-overload risk given her heart failure history. She has a 20-gauge peripheral IV in the left forearm with normal saline infusing at 75 mL/hr. Telemetry monitoring is in place. Standing orders include vital signs before transfusion, fifteen minutes after the start of each unit, every thirty minutes during infusion, and within one hour after completion. The facility transfusion policy requires two registered nurses to independently verify patient identity (name and date of birth confirmed verbally with the patient and matched to the wristband), the provider order, the blood bank compatibility tag, the unit label (donor unit number, ABO/Rh type, expiration date), and the patient's blood bank identification number — all at the bedside, immediately before spiking the unit.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient is resting in bed, alert and conversational but visibly pale. She reports feeling "washed out" and short of breath when she tries to sit up in bed. She is eager to receive the transfusion and states she hopes it will help her feel stronger. The first unit of pRBCs has arrived from the blood bank and is at the bedside, within the thirty-minute window for initiating infusion.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Baseline (pre-transfusion): Temperature 36.8 °C (98.2 °F), heart rate 96 beats per minute and regular, blood pressure 138/82 mmHg, respiratory rate 18 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 95% on room air. Lung sounds reveal faint bibasilar crackles that clear with coughing — consistent with her known heart failure. No peripheral edema. Skin is pale, warm, and dry. Mucous membranes are pale but moist. Capillary refill is three seconds. She is oriented to person, place, time, and situation. No jaundice, no rash, no urticaria. Abdomen is soft, non-tender, with normoactive bowel sounds. Urine in the Foley catheter drainage bag is clear, pale yellow, approximately 200 mL collected over the past three hours.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline (drawn in the ED four hours ago): Hemoglobin 6.4 g/dL, hematocrit 19.2%, MCV 82 fL, reticulocyte count 1.8%, WBC 7,200/µL, platelets 188,000/µL. BUN 34 mg/dL, creatinine 1.9 mg/dL (eGFR 38), potassium 4.6 mEq/L, sodium 139 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 22 mEq/L. Total bilirubin 0.8 mg/dL, direct bilirubin 0.2 mg/dL, LDH 210 U/L, haptoglobin 120 mg/dL. INR 1.0, aPTT 28 seconds. BNP 280 pg/mL (mildly elevated, consistent with her compensated heart failure). Blood type confirmed A-positive; antibody screen negative.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Pre-transfusion verification and initiation of the first unit.

The primary nurse retrieves the first unit of pRBCs from the blood bank transport cooler and asks a second RN to perform the bedside verification. Together, they approach the patient. The primary nurse asks the patient to state her full name and date of birth; the patient responds correctly. Both nurses compare the patient's verbal response and wristband information against the blood bank compatibility tag and the unit label. The unit is labeled as type A-positive, the donor unit number on the bag matches the compatibility tag, the expiration date is valid, and the patient's blood bank identification number on the tag matches the wristband. Both nurses sign the transfusion verification form. The primary nurse spikes the unit using blood administration tubing with an in-line filter, connects it to the existing IV, and begins the infusion slowly — approximately 2 mL/min for the first fifteen minutes per facility protocol. The patient is instructed to report any unusual symptoms immediately, including chills, itching, back pain, difficulty breathing, or feeling "not right."

Fifteen-minute vital signs: Temperature 36.9 °C, heart rate 92, blood pressure 134/80, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 96%. The patient reports no symptoms. The nurse increases the rate to complete the unit over the remaining time (approximately two hours total). The first unit completes without incident. Post-unit vital signs: Temperature 37.0 °C, heart rate 88, blood pressure 130/78, respiratory rate 16, SpO₂ 96%. Furosemide 20 mg IV is administered per order. Over the next forty-five minutes, the patient produces 350 mL of clear yellow urine. She reports feeling slightly less fatigued.

**Stage 2** — Initiation of the second unit and onset of symptoms.

The second unit of pRBCs arrives from the blood bank. The primary nurse is busy with another patient's acute need, so a covering nurse — RN B — retrieves the unit and asks a third RN to perform the two-nurse bedside verification. During the check, RN B notices a discrepancy: the blood bank compatibility tag lists the patient's blood bank identification number as ending in 4217, but the patient's wristband identification number ends in 4271. The donor unit number on the bag matches the compatibility tag, and the unit is labeled type B-positive. The patient's confirmed blood type on her chart and the first unit's records is A-positive. RN B stops the verification process and does not spike the unit. She notifies the blood bank immediately of the discrepancy. The blood bank confirms that the unit was issued for a different patient on the same floor — a clerical pick-up error. The correct second unit, type A-positive, is reissued after a new verification cycle.

Forty minutes into the infusion of the correct second unit, the patient presses the call light. She reports sudden onset of low back pain that she describes as a deep ache "like someone is squeezing my kidneys." She feels flushed and has developed rigors — visible shaking chills. She says she feels "hot all over" and anxious. Vital signs: Temperature 38.9 °C (102.0 °F), heart rate 118, blood pressure 98/60 (down from 130/78 pre-unit), respiratory rate 24, SpO₂ 94% on room air. Her skin is flushed and diaphoretic. The nurse notes that the urine in the Foley catheter tubing has changed color — it is now dark reddish-brown, a stark contrast to the clear yellow output documented less than an hour ago.

**Stage 3** — Immediate response and stabilization.

The nurse immediately stops the transfusion, clamps the blood tubing, disconnects the blood tubing at the IV hub while keeping the bag and tubing intact, and maintains IV access with new normal-saline tubing per facility policy. She is careful not to flush the blood tubing. She keeps the vein open with normal saline per protocol, preparing to administer the ordered bolus once prescribed. She remains at the bedside, performs a rapid assessment: the patient is alert but increasingly anxious, oriented but restless. Lung sounds are unchanged from baseline — faint bibasilar crackles, no new crackles, no wheezing, no stridor. No urticaria or angioedema. The back pain persists and the rigors continue. The nurse elevates the head of bed to 30 degrees and applies supplemental oxygen at 2 L/min via nasal cannula, bringing SpO₂ to 96%.

The nurse notifies the provider using SBAR: the patient is 40 minutes into the second unit of pRBCs, has developed acute onset of fever, rigors, flank pain, hypotension, tachycardia, and dark reddish-brown urine — findings consistent with an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. The provider orders stat labs: repeat CBC, direct antiglobulin test (DAT/direct Coombs), serum haptoglobin, LDH, total and direct bilirubin, free hemoglobin, coagulation studies (PT/INR, aPTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer), basic metabolic panel, and urinalysis. The provider also orders a 500 mL normal saline bolus to support blood pressure and maintain renal perfusion, and to send the blood unit with attached tubing and a new post-reaction blood sample (drawn from the opposite arm) to the blood bank for investigation.

The nurse labels the post-reaction blood sample, and sends both the discontinued blood unit and the sample to the blood bank with a completed transfusion reaction report form. She documents the time the transfusion was stopped, the volume infused, the onset time and description of symptoms, all vital signs, the interventions performed, and the provider notification.

Stat lab results return within the hour: Hemoglobin 7.1 g/dL (expected rise from the first unit, but no increment from the second), haptoglobin less than 10 mg/dL (was 120 at baseline — now undetectable), LDH 680 U/L (was 210), total bilirubin 3.2 mg/dL (was 0.8), direct bilirubin 0.6 mg/dL, potassium 5.8 mEq/L (was 4.6), creatinine 2.4 mg/dL (was 1.9). DAT is positive. Urinalysis shows hemoglobinuria. Fibrinogen 140 mg/dL (low), D-dimer elevated at 2.4 µg/mL. After the saline bolus, blood pressure improves to 108/68 and heart rate decreases to 104. Urine output over the next hour is 25 mL — oliguria despite the fluid bolus. The provider orders continued aggressive IV normal saline to target urine output greater than 100 mL/hr and close monitoring for signs of disseminated intravascular coagulation and acute kidney injury. The blood bank reports that the DAT is strongly positive and that the initial transfusion-reaction workup supports immune-mediated acute hemolysis. The detailed serologic/root-cause investigation is ongoing and is outside the immediate bedside nursing scope.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, before initiating the first unit, the two nurses are performing the bedside verification. From the set of verification elements — patient's verbal statement of name and date of birth, wristband match, blood bank compatibility tag, donor unit number, ABO/Rh type on the unit label, expiration date, patient blood bank identification number, and the provider order — identify which elements are essential components of the pre-transfusion safety check and which, if any, could be omitted. *Correct:* Every element listed is essential and none may be omitted. The two-nurse independent verification must confirm concordance across all identifiers: the patient's own verbal identification matched to the wristband, the compatibility tag matched to the unit label (donor number, ABO/Rh, expiration), the patient's blood bank ID number matched between the tag and the wristband, and the provider order confirming the product and indication. Omitting any single element breaks the verification chain. *Why:* Clerical and identification errors are the leading preventable cause of fatal hemolytic transfusion reactions. The redundancy is intentional — each element catches a different category of error (wrong patient, wrong unit, expired product, order mismatch). The two-nurse requirement ensures independent confirmation rather than a single point of failure. *Skill:* Recognize cues (identifying the complete set of required verification elements as safety-critical cues before any action is taken). *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 2, RN B identifies that the blood bank identification number on the compatibility tag (ending 4217) does not match the patient's wristband (ending 4271), and the unit is labeled type B-positive while the patient is type A-positive. Analyze what these discrepancies mean. *Correct:* The number mismatch indicates the unit was crossmatched and issued for a different patient. The ABO type mismatch (B-positive unit for an A-positive patient) confirms that infusing this unit would cause an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction — the patient's naturally occurring anti-B antibodies would attack the donor B-antigen red cells, triggering complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis. This is not a minor labeling variation; it is a near-miss for a potentially fatal error. *Why:* ABO incompatibility is the most dangerous transfusion error because it causes immediate, complement-mediated intravascular hemolysis with the potential for shock, DIC, renal failure, and death. The identification number mismatch is the upstream signal that the wrong unit was pulled — the ABO mismatch is the downstream consequence. Recognizing both layers of the error is essential: the ID mismatch alone should halt the process even before the type discrepancy is noticed. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, after the correct second unit has been infusing for 40 minutes, the patient develops sudden back pain, rigors, fever to 38.9 °C, hypotension (98/60), tachycardia (118), and dark reddish-brown urine. Multiple reaction types could present with fever and chills — febrile non-hemolytic reaction, acute hemolytic reaction, bacterial contamination/septic reaction, or an allergic reaction. Determine which is the most likely and most dangerous hypothesis. *Correct:* Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction is the priority hypothesis. The combination of fever with rigors, acute flank/back pain, hypotension, tachycardia, and dark urine (hemoglobinuria) occurring during active transfusion is the classic cue cluster for intravascular hemolysis. *Why:* A febrile non-hemolytic reaction — the most common transfusion reaction — presents with fever and chills but does not produce back pain, hemodynamic instability, or dark urine; it is a diagnosis of exclusion after hemolysis is ruled out. An allergic reaction presents with urticaria, pruritus, and potentially anaphylaxis (hypotension, bronchospasm, angioedema) but not back pain or hemoglobinuria. Bacterial contamination can mimic hemolytic reactions with fever and shock but is more common with platelet transfusions and does not typically produce the specific triad of back pain plus dark urine plus acute onset during pRBC infusion. Back/flank pain and dark urine during transfusion are classic warning cues for intravascular hemolysis and renal involvement, and highly concerning for intravascular hemolysis in this context. Until proven otherwise, this presentation must be treated as an acute hemolytic reaction because it carries the highest mortality risk and demands the most aggressive response. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse has identified the cue cluster suggesting an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. Determine the appropriate set of immediate nursing actions. *Correct:* The immediate response is a coordinated set of priority actions, not a rigid linear sequence beyond the universal first step. The universal first step is to stop the transfusion immediately, clamp the blood tubing, disconnect the blood tubing at the IV hub while keeping the bag and tubing intact, and maintain IV access with new normal-saline tubing per facility policy. Do not flush the blood tubing. This is always first because it halts further antigen exposure. The remaining priority actions occur essentially together and as rapidly as possible: stay at the bedside and perform a focused assessment (airway, breathing, circulation, neurological status, skin, urine color), obtain a new set of vital signs, keep the vein open with normal saline per protocol (administer the ordered bolus once prescribed / under emergency protocol) to support blood pressure and renal perfusion, notify the provider with an SBAR report including the reaction type suspected, and notify the blood bank. These actions are a priority set performed as rapidly as the single nurse and available help allow — not a fabricated step-by-step sequence. *Why:* Stopping the transfusion is the only action with a true "must be first" rationale — every additional milliliter of incompatible blood worsens the hemolytic cascade. The remaining actions address simultaneous needs: assessment guides the urgency of the provider call, saline supports perfusion, and early notification activates the blood bank investigation. Forcing these into an artificial order (e.g., "assess before calling" or "call before assessing") misrepresents clinical reality, where a nurse assesses while calling or delegates one task to perform another. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after stopping the transfusion and initiating normal saline, the nurse must carry out the hemovigilance and specimen-handling actions. The blood unit with attached tubing must be returned to the blood bank, a post-reaction blood sample must be drawn from the opposite arm, a transfusion reaction report form must be completed, and thorough documentation of the event must be entered in the medical record. Determine the correct action for handling the discontinued blood unit. *Correct:* The nurse disconnects the blood administration tubing at the IV hub, keeping the blood bag and all attached tubing intact and sealed (clamped, not cut or drained), and sends the unit with the tubing to the blood bank along with the labeled post-reaction blood sample and the completed transfusion reaction report form. The unit must not be discarded, flushed, or disconnected from its tubing. *Why:* The blood bank must have the actual unit and tubing to perform the investigation — repeat ABO/Rh typing on the donor unit, a direct antiglobulin test on the post-reaction sample, visual inspection of the donor plasma for hemolysis, and bacterial culture if contamination is suspected. Discarding the unit or separating it from the tubing destroys the chain of evidence needed to confirm the reaction type and identify the root cause. The post-reaction sample must come from a different site than the IV to avoid contamination with donor blood. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after the transfusion has been stopped, normal saline bolus administered, and labs drawn, the nurse evaluates the patient's response. Vital signs have partially improved (BP 108/68, HR 104), but urine output is only 25 mL over the next hour despite the fluid bolus. Stat labs show undetectable haptoglobin, LDH 680, bilirubin 3.2, potassium 5.8, creatinine 2.4, positive DAT, and hemoglobinuria. Determine whether the patient is improving, stable, or deteriorating, and identify which findings support the reaction type and which signal ongoing danger. *Correct:* The patient is partially stabilized hemodynamically (blood pressure responding to fluids) but is showing signs of ongoing hemolysis and early acute kidney injury. The undetectable haptoglobin (consumed by binding free hemoglobin), the markedly elevated LDH, the rising bilirubin, the positive DAT, and the hemoglobinuria together strongly support acute intravascular hemolysis. The rising potassium (4.6 → 5.8) reflects both hemolysis (potassium released from lysed red cells) and worsening renal function. The oliguria (25 mL/hr despite a 500 mL bolus) and rising creatinine (1.9 → 2.4) signal acute kidney injury from hemoglobin deposition in the renal tubules — this is the most dangerous ongoing complication and the reason aggressive IV hydration targeting urine output greater than 100 mL/hr is ordered. The hemodynamic improvement alone does not mean the patient is out of danger. *Why:* Acute hemolytic reactions can progress to DIC, renal failure, and death even after the transfusion is stopped, because the hemolytic cascade is already activated. The low fibrinogen and elevated D-dimer suggest early DIC. Evaluating outcomes requires looking beyond the vital signs to the laboratory markers of hemolysis and organ function — a nurse who sees the blood pressure improve and concludes the crisis is over will miss the evolving renal failure and coagulopathy. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Treating the two-nurse verification as a formality — one nurse reading aloud while the other passively agrees without independently inspecting each identifier. This "read-back without look-back" pattern is the most common real-world failure mode in transfusion verification. Each nurse must independently examine the wristband, the compatibility tag, and the unit label; passive confirmation defeats the purpose of the redundant check.

- *(Decision 1)* Omitting the patient's verbal self-identification because the patient is "known" to the nurse or because the wristband is present. The verbal confirmation is a distinct check that catches wristband errors (wrong band on the patient, illegible band) and ensures the patient is alert enough to participate in their own safety. Skipping it removes a layer of protection.

- *(Decision 2)* Noticing the identification number discrepancy but proceeding with the transfusion because the unit "looks like the right type" or because the nurse assumes the number is a clerical typo. Any single discrepancy in any identifier is an absolute stop — the verification is a pass/fail system, not a majority-rules system. A transposed number is exactly the kind of error that leads to a wrong-unit transfusion.

- *(Decision 3)* Attributing the fever and chills to a febrile non-hemolytic reaction (the most common reaction type) and failing to escalate urgently. A febrile non-hemolytic reaction is a diagnosis of exclusion — it cannot be assumed when back pain, hypotension, and dark urine are present. Treating a hemolytic reaction as benign delays lifesaving intervention.

- *(Decision 3)* Suspecting transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO) because the patient has a heart failure history and is now tachycardic and dyspneic. TACO presents with hypertension (not hypotension), jugular venous distension, new or worsening pulmonary edema (crackles, orthopnea, frothy sputum), and elevated BNP — a volume-overload picture. This patient's blood pressure has dropped, her lung exam is unchanged from baseline, and she has back pain and dark urine — none of which fit TACO. Confusing the two delays the correct response.

- *(Decision 4)* Slowing the transfusion rate instead of stopping it completely. For any suspected hemolytic, anaphylactic, septic, or TRALI reaction, the transfusion must be stopped — not slowed. Slowing the rate is appropriate only for mild allergic reactions (isolated urticaria without systemic symptoms) per provider order after assessment. In this case, the hemodynamic instability and hemoglobinuria demand a full stop.

- *(Decision 4)* Disconnecting the IV line entirely to "get the blood away from the patient" and then attempting to restart a new IV for saline. This loses IV access during a hemodynamic emergency. The correct action is to clamp/disconnect the blood tubing at the IV hub and attach new normal-saline tubing to the existing IV catheter, preserving functional vascular access.

- *(Decision 5)* Discarding the blood unit or disconnecting the tubing from the bag before sending it to the blood bank. The intact unit-and-tubing assembly is required for the blood bank investigation. Once discarded, the root cause of the reaction may be unrecoverable.

- *(Decision 5)* Drawing the post-reaction blood sample from the same IV site that was used for the transfusion. Blood drawn through or near the transfusion line may be contaminated with donor red cells, producing a false-positive DAT or confounding the ABO recheck. The sample must come from a separate venipuncture site.

- *(Decision 6)* Concluding the patient is stable because the blood pressure has improved after the saline bolus, without evaluating the urine output, the rising creatinine, the rising potassium, or the hemolysis markers. Hemodynamic stabilization is necessary but not sufficient — the hemolytic cascade can continue to damage the kidneys and trigger DIC even after the blood pressure normalizes. The oliguria is the most ominous ongoing finding and must be recognized as a sign of acute kidney injury requiring continued aggressive intervention.

- *(Decision 6)* Interpreting the rising potassium (4.6 → 5.8) as a dietary or medication issue rather than a consequence of hemolysis and acute kidney injury. In the context of a confirmed hemolytic reaction with falling renal function, the potassium rise is directly caused by red cell lysis releasing intracellular potassium and by the kidneys' decreasing ability to excrete it. Failing to connect this finding to the reaction delays recognition of the severity of the ongoing crisis.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Perform and articulate the rationale for each element of the two-nurse pre-transfusion verification process, recognizing that every identifier must independently match and that any single discrepancy is an absolute contraindication to proceeding.

- Differentiate the cue clusters of the major transfusion reaction types — acute hemolytic, febrile non-hemolytic, allergic/anaphylactic, TACO, and TRALI — and prioritize the most dangerous hypothesis when cues overlap.

- Execute the universal first response to a suspected transfusion reaction (stop the transfusion, maintain IV access with normal saline, assess, notify) and distinguish actions that must be sequenced from those that are performed as a simultaneous priority set.

- Carry out hemovigilance responsibilities — returning the intact unit and tubing, drawing a post-reaction sample from a separate site, completing the transfusion reaction report — and explain why each step is necessary for the blood bank investigation.

- Evaluate post-reaction outcomes by integrating hemodynamic data, hemolysis markers, renal function, and urine output rather than relying on a single improving parameter to declare stability.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, after the transfusion has been stopped, the initial resuscitation with normal saline is underway, and the stat laboratory results have returned indicating acute intravascular hemolysis with early acute kidney injury.

- *Most likely condition:* Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. The cue cluster of fever with rigors, acute flank/back pain, hypotension, tachycardia, and dark reddish-brown urine occurring 40 minutes into a pRBC transfusion, supported by undetectable haptoglobin, markedly elevated LDH, rising bilirubin, positive DAT, hemoglobinuria, and rising creatinine with oliguria, strongly indicates an acute intravascular hemolytic reaction.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction — wrong here: although it is the most common transfusion reaction and shares the fever-and-chills presentation, it does not produce back pain, hypotension, hemoglobinuria, or laboratory evidence of hemolysis (haptoglobin would remain normal, DAT would be negative); it is a diagnosis of exclusion that cannot be assumed when hemolytic cues are present. (2) Transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO) — wrong here: TACO presents with hypertension (not hypotension), worsening pulmonary edema with new crackles, elevated BNP rising above baseline, jugular venous distension, and orthopnea — a volume-overload picture; this patient's blood pressure has fallen, her lung exam is unchanged, and she has back pain and hemoglobinuria, none of which are consistent with TACO.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Maintain aggressive IV normal saline infusion targeting urine output greater than 100 mL/hr per provider order to preserve renal perfusion and flush free hemoglobin from the renal tubules — the oliguria (25 mL/hr despite a 500 mL bolus) signals that acute kidney injury is the most dangerous evolving complication. (2) Notify the provider of the stat lab results — specifically the undetectable haptoglobin, rising creatinine and potassium, oliguria, and early DIC markers (low fibrinogen, elevated D-dimer) — using SBAR to communicate that the patient requires escalation of care, possible transfer to a higher level of monitoring, and anticipatory management for DIC and hyperkalemia.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Restarting the transfusion at a slower rate because the blood pressure has partially improved — wrong and dangerous: the hemolytic cascade is already confirmed, and any further exposure to the incompatible donor cells would worsen hemolysis, organ damage, and the risk of DIC; the transfusion must remain stopped. (2) Administering furosemide now to treat oliguria despite hypotension and no evidence of volume overload is wrong: the oliguria is currently driven by hemoglobin-mediated renal tubular injury and hypoperfusion; diuresis in the setting of hypotension and intravascular volume depletion would worsen renal perfusion and accelerate kidney injury; the correct initial approach is volume expansion, not diuresis.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Hourly urine output and urine color — recovery of urine volume toward the target of greater than 100 mL/hr and clearing of the dark reddish-brown color toward normal yellow directly confirms that renal perfusion is being maintained and free hemoglobin is being cleared from the tubules; persistent oliguria despite adequate fluid resuscitation signals progression to established acute kidney injury requiring escalation. (2) Serum potassium on repeat metabolic panels — the potassium has risen from 4.6 to 5.8 due to hemolysis and declining renal function; a continued rise toward dangerous levels (greater than 6.0–6.5 mEq/L) would signal worsening hemolysis or renal failure and the need for emergent potassium-lowering intervention, while stabilization or decline confirms that the hemolytic burden is not overwhelming the kidneys' remaining excretory capacity.

- *Two lower-priority or less reliable indicators of improvement at this moment:* (1) Serum hemoglobin level as a marker of recovery — the hemoglobin reflects the net balance of transfused cells and destroyed cells, but it does not track whether the acute hemolytic process is resolving or whether the kidneys are being protected; a stable hemoglobin could coexist with ongoing renal failure, and a falling hemoglobin is expected from the hemolysis itself; it is not the parameter that confirms the patient is turning the corner at this moment. (2) Temperature trending — while the fever was an early cue of the reaction, temperature normalization lags behind the hemolytic process and does not reliably indicate whether hemolysis has stopped or whether renal injury is progressing; antipyretics may normalize the temperature independently of the underlying crisis, making it a misleading marker of improvement.
