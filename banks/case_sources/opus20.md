**CASE TITLE**
Clostridioides difficile infection with dehydration and contact-precaution teaching for family visitors.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 71-year-old woman is transferred from a skilled nursing facility to a medical-surgical unit with a three-day history of profuse, watery, foul-smelling diarrhea. She completed a 10-day course of oral amoxicillin-clavulanate for community-acquired pneumonia six days ago. Her medical history includes type 2 diabetes managed with metformin 1,000 mg twice daily, hypertension treated with lisinopril 20 mg daily, osteoarthritis, and mild chronic kidney disease (baseline creatinine 1.4 mg/dL, baseline eGFR 42 mL/min/1.73 m²). She lives with her adult daughter and son-in-law, who visit daily and assist with meals and hygiene. On admission, the provider orders contact precautions, stool specimen for C. difficile toxin PCR, IV lactated Ringer's at 125 mL/hr, strict intake and output, daily basic metabolic panel, and oral vancomycin 125 mg every 6 hours per facility protocol for suspected C. difficile infection. Metformin is held pending evaluation of renal function. A Braden scale assessment is ordered every shift.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient reports six to eight loose, watery stools per day for the past three days, accompanied by diffuse crampy abdominal pain, poor oral intake, and increasing fatigue. She states she feels lightheaded when she stands and has not been eating well because "everything makes my stomach worse." She is alert and oriented but appears fatigued and dry.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 38.1 °C (100.6 °F), heart rate 104, blood pressure 98/62, respiratory rate 18, SpO₂ 96% on room air. Weight 64 kg (her documented weight two weeks ago was 68 kg). Mucous membranes dry and tacky, skin turgor sluggish over the sternum (tenting approximately 3 seconds), capillary refill 3 seconds in nail beds. Abdomen soft, diffusely tender to palpation in all four quadrants without rebound or guarding; hyperactive bowel sounds in all quadrants. Perianal skin intact but erythematous. No peripheral edema. She is continent but reports urgency and occasional near-incontinence. Braden scale score 15 (mild risk): sensory perception 4, moisture 2, activity 3, mobility 3, nutrition 2, friction/shear 1.

**LABORATORY DATA**
WBC 18,200/µL (elevated), hemoglobin 13.8 g/dL, platelets 210,000/µL. Sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 3.3 mEq/L, chloride 96 mEq/L, bicarbonate 20 mEq/L, BUN 38 mg/dL, creatinine 2.1 mg/dL (baseline 1.4), eGFR 24 mL/min/1.73 m², glucose 188 mg/dL. Lactate 2.4 mmol/L. Albumin 2.8 g/dL. Stool C. difficile toxin PCR pending.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 8 hours after admission. The C. difficile toxin PCR returns positive. The patient has had four more watery stools since admission. Intake: 1,000 mL IV fluids; output: 200 mL urine plus an estimated 1,200 mL stool. She remains tachycardic (HR 100) and hypotensive (BP 100/64). Repeat potassium drawn at 6 hours is 3.1 mEq/L. The provider is notified and orders IV potassium chloride 20 mEq in 100 mL normal saline over 1 hour via peripheral IV, with a repeat potassium one hour after the infusion. The patient's daughter and son-in-law arrive for their evening visit. The nurse observes the daughter enter the room wearing gloves but no gown, and the son-in-law follows her in without any personal protective equipment. The daughter is carrying a container of homemade soup she intends to feed the patient. The son-in-law picks up the bedside TV remote and then touches the patient's hand.

**Stage 2** — 20 hours after admission. The patient's stool frequency has decreased to three episodes over the past 8 hours. Repeat labs: potassium 3.6 mEq/L (post-replacement), creatinine 1.9 mg/dL, BUN 34 mg/dL, lactate 1.8 mmol/L. Vitals: T 37.6 °C, HR 92, BP 108/68, RR 16, SpO₂ 97%. Urine output over the past 8 hours is 280 mL (approximately 35 mL/hr). She is tolerating small sips of clear liquids. The perianal erythema has worsened: the skin is now macerated with a shallow 1 cm × 0.5 cm area of partial-thickness skin loss at the 6-o'clock position. The Braden moisture subscale has dropped to 1 (constantly moist). The daughter asks the nurse, "Can I just use hand sanitizer instead of washing my hands every time? It's faster." She also asks whether she needs to wear a gown and gloves when she is "just sitting in the chair and not touching Mom."

**Stage 3** — 44 hours after admission. Stool frequency is down to two formed-to-semiformed stools in the past 12 hours. Repeat labs: potassium 3.8 mEq/L, creatinine 1.6 mg/dL, BUN 28 mg/dL, eGFR 33 mL/min/1.73 m², lactate 1.2 mmol/L, WBC 12,400/µL. Vitals: T 37.2 °C, HR 82, BP 118/72, RR 16, SpO₂ 98%. Urine output over the past 8 hours is 400 mL (50 mL/hr). The patient is eating a soft diet and drinking fluids independently. The perianal wound has a clean base with no extension; a moisture barrier cream was applied at Stage 2 and the surrounding maceration is improving. The provider asks the nurse to evaluate readiness for transition back to oral medications and to assess whether metformin can be restarted. The daughter demonstrates correct donning and doffing of gown and gloves and performs hand hygiene with soap and water before and after the visit without prompting.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse must identify the cluster of findings that confirms the primary clinical problem and its severity. The patient has a positive C. difficile toxin PCR, profuse diarrhea, tachycardia, hypotension, a 4-kg weight loss, rising creatinine from baseline, hypokalemia, elevated lactate, and leukocytosis. *Correct action:* Recognize that the constellation represents C. difficile colitis complicated by moderate-to-severe dehydration with prerenal acute kidney injury and ongoing electrolyte losses. The rising creatinine (1.4 → 2.1), elevated BUN-to-creatinine ratio, reduced urine output, tachycardia, hypotension, and dry mucous membranes together point to volume depletion as the immediate physiologic threat beyond the infection itself. *Why it is correct:* C. difficile diarrhea causes large-volume isotonic fluid and potassium losses. The BUN-to-creatinine ratio above 20:1, the oliguria, and the clinical signs of poor perfusion are classic prerenal markers. Identifying dehydration as the driver of the renal injury directs the nurse to prioritize fluid resuscitation and electrolyte monitoring rather than attributing the creatinine rise to intrinsic renal disease. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues, analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse observes the family visitors entering the contact-precaution room incorrectly — the daughter wearing gloves but no gown, the son-in-law wearing nothing, and both touching surfaces and the patient. The nurse must determine the priority teaching and immediate corrective actions. *Correct action:* Politely interrupt the visit, ask both visitors to step out, perform hand hygiene with soap and water, and then re-enter only after donning both a gown and gloves. Explain that C. difficile spores are not killed by alcohol-based hand sanitizer and that soap-and-water hand washing is required. Explain that both a gown and gloves are necessary for anyone entering the room, regardless of whether they plan to touch the patient, because spores contaminate environmental surfaces. The son-in-law should wash his hands with soap and water immediately. Demonstrate correct donning and doffing sequence. *Why it is correct:* Contact precautions for C. difficile require gown and gloves for all persons entering the room because spores persist on bed rails, remotes, call lights, and other fomites. Alcohol-based sanitizers do not inactivate C. difficile spores; only mechanical removal through soap-and-water friction is effective. Partial PPE (gloves without a gown) leaves clothing contaminated, which can transfer spores to other patients and to the home environment. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the daughter asks whether alcohol-based hand sanitizer is acceptable and whether she needs PPE when she is "just sitting in the chair." The nurse must respond accurately. *Correct action:* Reinforce that alcohol-based hand sanitizer is not effective against C. difficile spores and that soap-and-water hand washing is required every time — before and after entering the room. Clarify that a gown and gloves are required for all entry into the room, even if the visitor does not intend to touch the patient, because spores are present on environmental surfaces including the chair, side table, and bed linens. *Why it is correct:* C. difficile spores have a protective coat that resists alcohol denaturation. The CDC and facility contact-precaution protocols specify soap and water as the hand-hygiene method for C. difficile. Environmental contamination is well documented; sitting in a chair in the room exposes clothing to spores, which can then be carried out of the room. Consistent, repeated teaching with return demonstration is the evidence-based approach to family compliance with infection-control measures. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse assesses the worsening perianal skin — maceration and a new partial-thickness wound — in the context of ongoing diarrhea and a Braden moisture subscale of 1. The nurse must determine the priority skin-integrity intervention. *Correct action:* Apply a moisture barrier cream or ointment (such as dimethicone-based barrier or zinc oxide) to the perianal area after gentle cleansing with a pH-balanced perineal cleanser, avoid friction from dry wiping (use soft disposable cloths or a peri-bottle rinse), institute a toileting/checking schedule to minimize prolonged moisture contact, and document the wound characteristics for ongoing assessment. Consider a brief-style incontinence product that wicks moisture away from skin. Notify the provider of the new skin breakdown for wound-care orders if not already covered by a standing skin-care protocol. *Why it is correct:* Moisture-associated skin damage from alkaline liquid stool rapidly progresses without barrier protection. The Braden moisture subscale of 1 signals the highest moisture risk. Mechanical friction from wiping compounds the injury. A barrier product creates a protective layer between the stool and the compromised epidermis. Early intervention prevents progression to a full-thickness wound or secondary infection. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the provider asks the nurse to evaluate whether metformin can be restarted. The patient's eGFR has improved from 24 to 33 mL/min/1.73 m², her baseline eGFR is 42, and metformin was held on admission. *Correct action:* The nurse should communicate to the provider that the eGFR remains at 33 mL/min/1.73 m², which is in the range (30–45) where metformin use requires caution, dose review, and closer monitoring of renal function, and that the eGFR has not yet returned to the patient's baseline of 42. The nurse should recommend continued holding of metformin until renal function recovers further toward baseline and the patient is consistently tolerating oral intake, and should clarify with the provider the specific eGFR threshold at which metformin will be resumed. *Why it is correct:* Metformin carries a risk of lactic acidosis that increases with declining renal function. At eGFR 30–45, guidelines call for dose reduction and enhanced monitoring rather than full-dose resumption; below 30, metformin is generally contraindicated. Although the patient's eGFR has improved from 24 to 33, it has not returned to her baseline of 42, and she is still recovering from dehydration. Restarting metformin prematurely while renal function is still depressed and fluid status is still normalizing increases the risk of lactic acidosis. The nurse's role is to communicate the relevant data, advocate for the patient's safety, and clarify the restart plan with the provider. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the nurse evaluates the overall trajectory to determine whether the patient is improving. Multiple data points have changed from admission and Stage 2. *Correct action:* Identify the constellation of findings that confirms clinical improvement: decreasing stool frequency (from 6–8/day to 2 in 12 hours, with improving consistency), resolving tachycardia (HR 104 → 82), normalizing blood pressure (98/62 → 118/72), improving creatinine (2.1 → 1.6), falling lactate (2.4 → 1.2), declining WBC (18,200 → 12,400), increasing urine output (oliguria → 50 mL/hr), and tolerance of oral diet. The perianal wound is stable with no extension. These collectively indicate that the C. difficile infection is responding to oral vancomycin and that the dehydration and prerenal injury are resolving. *Why it is correct:* Evaluating outcomes requires comparing current data against baseline and prior stages across multiple systems — gastrointestinal, renal, hemodynamic, and infectious markers. No single value in isolation confirms improvement; the nurse must synthesize the trend. Persistent abnormalities (eGFR still below baseline, WBC still mildly elevated) do not negate the overall improving trajectory but do indicate the need for continued monitoring. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Attributing the creatinine elevation to the patient's chronic kidney disease rather than recognizing it as an acute prerenal injury from dehydration. The creatinine has risen from her known baseline of 1.4 to 2.1, the BUN-to-creatinine ratio is elevated, and she has clinical signs of volume depletion — this is an acute change superimposed on chronic disease, not her baseline renal status. Failing to recognize the acute component delays aggressive fluid resuscitation.

- *(Decision 1)* Focusing on the leukocytosis as the most urgent finding and prioritizing infection-related interventions over volume and electrolyte correction. While the elevated WBC confirms the infectious process, the immediate physiologic threats are the hemodynamic instability and hypokalemia from fluid losses. The infection is already being treated with oral vancomycin; the dehydration requires active nursing management of fluid replacement and monitoring.

- *(Decision 2)* Allowing the daughter to remain in the room with gloves only, reasoning that gloves are "good enough" since she is not a healthcare worker. Partial PPE defeats the purpose of contact precautions: the gown protects clothing from spore contamination, and without it the visitor carries spores on her clothing to other areas of the hospital and home. The standard applies to all persons entering the room.

- *(Decision 2)* Waiting until the end of the visit to address the PPE breach rather than intervening immediately. Every minute of unprotected contact increases environmental contamination and the risk of spore transmission. The nurse should correct the situation in real time, respectfully but promptly.

- *(Decision 3)* Telling the daughter that alcohol-based hand sanitizer is acceptable "as long as she also washes with soap and water afterward." This is misleading and may lead the visitor to rely on sanitizer alone in practice. The correct message is that soap and water is the only acceptable method for C. difficile, and sanitizer should not be substituted or used as a first step.

- *(Decision 3)* Telling the daughter she does not need a gown if she is only sitting in the chair and not touching the patient. Environmental surfaces in a C. difficile room — including chairs, side tables, and bed rails — are contaminated with spores. Contact with any surface in the room can transfer spores to clothing. The gown-and-gloves requirement applies regardless of intended patient contact.

- *(Decision 4)* Treating the perianal skin breakdown as inevitable and not intervening beyond routine hygiene. Moisture-associated skin damage is a preventable complication that worsens rapidly without barrier protection. Delaying barrier cream application and continuing to use dry wipes with friction accelerates tissue loss.

- *(Decision 5)* Restarting metformin at Stage 3 because the creatinine is "trending down" without considering that the eGFR is still 33 — within the caution range — and has not returned to baseline. The improving trend does not mean the patient has recovered enough to safely resume a drug that depends on adequate renal clearance. The nurse should advocate for continued holding until the eGFR is closer to baseline and the patient is hemodynamically stable with reliable oral intake.

- *(Decision 5)* Assuming metformin is contraindicated at any eGFR below 45 and documenting it as such. At eGFR 30–45, metformin is not contraindicated but requires dose adjustment and closer monitoring. Overstating the restriction could lead to unnecessary permanent discontinuation of an effective medication. The nurse's role is to communicate the current eGFR accurately and clarify the provider's restart criteria, not to make a blanket contraindication call.

- *(Decision 6)* Interpreting the still-elevated WBC of 12,400 as evidence that the infection is not improving and escalating unnecessarily. The WBC has dropped from 18,200 and is trending toward normal; a mildly elevated value during active treatment does not indicate failure. The nurse should evaluate the full constellation of improving markers rather than reacting to a single value in isolation.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the clinical findings that distinguish dehydration-related prerenal acute kidney injury from baseline chronic kidney disease in a patient with C. difficile infection.
- Apply correct contact-precaution practices for C. difficile, including the rationale for soap-and-water hand hygiene over alcohol-based sanitizer and the requirement for full gown-and-glove use by all persons entering the room.
- Provide effective, culturally sensitive infection-control education to family visitors, including correction of PPE breaches and reinforcement through return demonstration.
- Implement evidence-based interventions to prevent and manage moisture-associated perianal skin breakdown in a patient with frequent diarrhea.
- Evaluate renal function trends to determine the safety of restarting a renally cleared medication, communicating relevant data and advocating for the patient when the clinical picture does not yet support resumption.
