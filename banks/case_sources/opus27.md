

**CASE TITLE**
Intimate partner violence disclosure during routine prenatal care.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 28-year-old woman, gravida 2 para 1, at 26 weeks gestation presents for a scheduled prenatal visit at an outpatient obstetric clinic. Her pregnancy has been uncomplicated to date. Her first child is a 3-year-old son who lives with her and her partner. She has no significant past medical history. She takes a prenatal vitamin daily and has no known drug allergies. Her prenatal labs at 12 weeks were unremarkable: blood type A positive, antibody screen negative, rubella immune, HIV negative, hepatitis B surface antigen negative, RPR nonreactive, GBS status pending at 36 weeks. Her anatomy scan at 20 weeks was normal. She has attended all scheduled prenatal appointments, though she has arrived late to the last two visits and cancelled one appointment, rescheduling it a week later.

Her partner accompanies her to every visit and typically answers questions directed at the patient. The clinic's standard protocol includes routine intimate partner violence (IPV) screening at the initial prenatal visit, at 28 weeks, and at the postpartum visit, using a validated screening tool. The protocol specifies: "Screening must be conducted privately — the patient must be separated from any accompanying person, including partners, family members, and children over age 3, before screening questions are asked. If the patient discloses abuse, the nurse documents using the patient's own words, provides information about community resources including the National Domestic Violence Hotline, develops a preliminary safety plan with the patient, and notifies the provider. The nurse does not contact law enforcement or the partner without the patient's explicit consent unless mandatory reporting criteria are met (suspected child abuse or neglect, or injury by a weapon). Mandatory reporting of IPV against an adult varies by state; this clinic follows the state law that does not mandate reporting of IPV against a competent adult without the patient's consent."

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient arrives for her 26-week visit with her partner, who is seated beside her in the exam room. She is quiet and avoids eye contact with the nurse. She is wearing a long-sleeved shirt despite warm weather. When the nurse asks how she has been feeling, her partner answers, "She's been fine — just tired. The baby's been kicking a lot." The patient nods but does not elaborate. The nurse notes that the 28-week IPV screening is approaching and that the clinic protocol permits screening at the current visit if the opportunity arises during a private moment. The nurse needs to arrange a private encounter with the patient.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 36.8 °C, HR 82, BP 118/74, RR 16, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Weight 68.2 kg, up 1.4 kg from the visit two weeks ago, consistent with expected gestational weight gain. Fundal height 26 cm, appropriate for gestational age. Fetal heart tones 148 bpm by Doppler, regular. No vaginal bleeding or leaking of fluid reported. Mild bilateral ankle edema, symmetric, non-pitting. The patient winces when the nurse takes her blood pressure on her left arm. When the sleeve is raised for the cuff, the nurse observes a cluster of bruises on the left upper arm and inner forearm in varying stages of healing — some yellow-green, some deep purple. The patient quickly pulls her sleeve down and says, "I bumped into the door frame — I'm so clumsy lately."

**LABORATORY DATA**
Urine dipstick: protein trace, glucose negative, leukocyte esterase negative, nitrites negative. Hemoglobin 11.4 g/dL (drawn at today's visit per routine 26-week panel). Hematocrit 34%. Platelet count 218,000/µL. One-hour glucose challenge test (50 g glucola): pending, drawn today. Blood type and antibody screen: no new antibodies. All values within expected range for 26 weeks gestation.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — During the visit, the nurse uses a standard clinical workflow to create a private moment: she asks the partner to step to the waiting area while the patient provides a urine sample and undergoes the physical examination, explaining that this is routine clinic procedure for all patients. Once alone with the patient, the nurse conducts the IPV screening. She begins with a normalizing statement: "We ask all of our pregnant patients these questions because we know that safety at home can affect the health of both the mother and the baby." She uses the clinic's validated screening tool. The patient is initially guarded, giving one-word answers. When asked, "Has anyone at home hit, kicked, or physically hurt you in the past year?" the patient's eyes fill with tears. After a pause, she says quietly, "He doesn't mean to. It only happens when he's been drinking. He grabbed my arm last week — that's what the bruises are from. He pushed me into the wall a few weeks ago. He's never hurt our son. He's a good father." She then becomes anxious and says, "Please don't tell him I said anything. He'll know I told. It will get worse." She asks the nurse to promise not to tell anyone.

**Stage 2** — The nurse has documented the disclosure using the patient's own words and has notified the provider per protocol. The provider has seen the patient and confirmed no acute injury requiring emergency intervention. The nurse now sits with the patient to discuss safety. The patient reveals additional information: her partner controls the household finances, monitors her phone, and has threatened to take their son if she leaves. She states she is not ready to leave the relationship. She says, "He's been stressed about money. Once the baby comes, things will get better." She asks the nurse, "Do you think I'm a bad mother?" She reports that her partner has never directly harmed their 3-year-old son but that the child has witnessed the partner pushing and grabbing her on at least two occasions. She denies any access to weapons in the home. She has no independent transportation — her partner drives her to all appointments. She has a sister who lives approximately 30 minutes away but has not told her about the abuse. The patient states she does not want to call any hotline today but is willing to take a resource card "if it's small enough to hide."

**Stage 3** — Before the visit ends, the nurse works with the patient on a preliminary safety plan. The patient identifies her sister as a potential emergency contact and agrees to memorize the National Domestic Violence Hotline number rather than storing it in her phone, which her partner monitors. She agrees to pack a small bag with essential documents (her ID, insurance card, her son's birth certificate) and keep it at her sister's home. She asks the nurse to schedule her next prenatal appointment at a time when her partner is at work so she can come alone, and to call her only on a secondary phone number she provides. The nurse provides a resource card designed to look like a routine clinic card. The patient asks, "Will this be in my medical chart? Can he see it?" The nurse must address the documentation and confidentiality question. At the end of the visit, the partner returns to the exam room and asks, "Everything okay? What took so long?" The patient looks at the nurse.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the initial presentation, the nurse observes that the partner answers questions directed at the patient, the patient avoids eye contact, and the patient has bruises in varying stages of healing with an inconsistent explanation. The nurse must determine the appropriate next step before any screening can occur. *Correct action:* Arrange a private encounter with the patient by separating her from her partner using a routine, non-suspicious clinical rationale — such as asking the partner to wait outside during the physical exam and urine collection, which is standard procedure for all patients. Do not screen for IPV in the partner's presence. Do not confront the partner or indicate suspicion of abuse. *Why it is correct:* Screening for IPV in the presence of the suspected abuser is unsafe and ineffective — it may trigger retaliation against the patient and will almost certainly prevent disclosure. The clinic protocol requires private screening. Using a routine clinical workflow to separate the patient from the partner avoids alerting the partner and protects the patient. The bruises and behavioral cues are concerning but are not sufficient for the nurse to act on without first speaking to the patient privately — the nurse's role is to create the conditions for safe disclosure, not to confront or accuse. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient discloses physical abuse but immediately asks the nurse to promise not to tell anyone, expressing fear that disclosure will make the violence worse. The nurse must respond to this request while fulfilling professional and legal obligations. *Correct action:* Do not promise absolute confidentiality. Respond honestly and compassionately: explain that the nurse cannot keep a promise of total secrecy because there is a professional obligation to document and to involve the provider in ensuring her safety, but reassure the patient that the information will be shared only with the clinical team involved in her care and will not be disclosed to her partner. Clarify the limits of confidentiality specific to this situation: under the clinic's state law, IPV against a competent adult is not subject to mandatory reporting to law enforcement without the patient's consent, so the nurse will not contact police or the partner. However, explain that if there is concern about the safety of a child, mandatory reporting obligations may apply — this must be addressed transparently, not avoided. *Why it is correct:* Promising absolute confidentiality is dishonest and may later damage the patient's trust when she discovers the disclosure was documented or shared with the provider. Transparency about the limits of confidentiality — delivered with empathy and specificity about what will and will not happen — preserves trust while meeting professional obligations. The patient needs to understand that the nurse is her advocate within a system that has defined boundaries, not an ally who will hide information from the treatment team. Vague reassurances ("Don't worry, everything will be fine") are neither honest nor therapeutic. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action (therapeutic communication within legal and ethical boundaries). *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient reveals that her 3-year-old son has witnessed the partner pushing and grabbing her on at least two occasions. The nurse must determine whether this information triggers any mandatory reporting obligation beyond the IPV disclosure itself. *Correct action:* Recognize that a child witnessing domestic violence may constitute a reportable concern under child abuse and neglect statutes in many jurisdictions. Per the clinic protocol, mandatory reporting is required for suspected child abuse or neglect. The nurse must consult with the provider and, if required by the facility's interpretation of the applicable state law, initiate a report to child protective services. This must be communicated to the patient transparently — the nurse explains that the law requires reporting when a child may be at risk, that the report is about protecting her son, and that it does not mean she is a bad mother. The nurse frames this as an action taken to support the safety of both the patient and her child, not as a punitive measure. *Why it is correct:* Nurses are mandated reporters for suspected child abuse and neglect in all US jurisdictions. While the definition of whether witnessing domestic violence alone constitutes neglect varies by state, the nurse cannot independently decide to withhold this information — the obligation is to report the concern and allow the investigative agency to make the determination. Failing to report because the patient asked for confidentiality, or because the child has not been directly struck, does not discharge the mandatory reporting duty. The nurse's therapeutic relationship with the patient is important but does not override the legal obligation to protect a minor. Communicating this transparently and compassionately — rather than reporting secretly or avoiding the issue — maintains as much trust as possible within the legal constraint. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses (competing obligations: patient autonomy vs. child safety mandate), take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient states she is not ready to leave the relationship. The nurse must determine the appropriate response. *Correct action:* Respect the patient's autonomy and her decision not to leave at this time. Do not pressure, coerce, or express judgment. Affirm that leaving is her decision to make when she is ready, and that the nurse and the clinical team will continue to support her regardless of her choice. Provide information about available resources — the National Domestic Violence Hotline number, local shelter information, legal advocacy services — without requiring her to use them immediately. Ensure she knows she can access these resources at any future visit. Continue to assess safety at every subsequent prenatal visit. *Why it is correct:* A patient experiencing IPV is the expert on her own level of danger and the feasibility of leaving. Research consistently shows that the most dangerous period for an IPV victim is immediately after leaving the relationship, and that premature departure without a safety plan increases risk. Pressuring a patient to leave before she is ready may cause her to disengage from prenatal care entirely — losing the clinical team's only point of contact and safety monitoring. The nurse's role is to provide information, support autonomy, plan for safety, and maintain the therapeutic relationship so the patient continues to attend appointments where ongoing assessment can occur. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues (understanding the cycle of violence and readiness for change). *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient asks whether the disclosure will appear in her medical chart and whether her partner can access it. The nurse must address documentation and confidentiality accurately. *Correct action:* Explain that the disclosure is documented in the medical record because it is clinically relevant to her care and safety, and that the documentation uses her own words as she reported them. Reassure her that under HIPAA, her medical record cannot be released to her partner without her written authorization. Advise her not to grant her partner access to her patient portal or to sign any release of information that includes him. If the clinic uses a patient portal, discuss whether she needs to adjust her portal settings or whether the documentation can be placed in a restricted section of the chart per clinic policy. Ensure she understands that the record is protected and that the clinical team will not share information with her partner verbally, by phone, or in person. *Why it is correct:* Patients have a right to understand how their information is documented and protected. In IPV situations, documentation must be handled with heightened attention to confidentiality because the abuser may attempt to access the record through coercion (forcing the patient to sign a release), through a shared patient portal login, or by requesting records during custody proceedings. The nurse's responsibility is to document accurately — omitting the disclosure would compromise continuity of care and safety planning — while ensuring the patient understands and can exercise her privacy protections. Vague reassurance ("It's confidential") without explaining the specific mechanisms of protection is insufficient. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions (balancing documentation obligations with safety-specific confidentiality measures). *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At the end of Stage 3, the partner returns and asks, "Everything okay? What took so long?" while the patient looks at the nurse with visible anxiety. The nurse must respond in a way that protects the patient's safety and does not reveal the disclosure. *Correct action:* Provide a truthful but non-revealing response that normalizes the length of the visit — for example, "We had some routine lab work and screening to go over today, and we discussed some things about the third trimester. Everything is on track with the pregnancy." Do not lie about a medical finding (fabricating a complication), but do not disclose or hint at the IPV screening or the patient's statements. Take the cue from the patient's anxiety and do not look to her for confirmation or put her in a position of having to respond. Maintain a calm, routine demeanor. *Why it is correct:* The nurse has a duty to protect the patient's safety, which in this moment means not alerting the partner to the disclosure. A response that is vague, evasive, or visibly nervous may arouse suspicion. A response that fabricates a medical problem may create new complications (the partner may demand details, seek a second opinion, or use a fabricated diagnosis to control the patient further). A truthful, routine, and brief explanation — anchored in the normal activities of a prenatal visit — is both honest and protective. The nurse's nonverbal communication is equally important: maintaining a relaxed posture and matter-of-fact tone signals normalcy to the partner and reassurance to the patient. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action (safety-preserving communication). *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Conducting the IPV screening with the partner present in the room, reasoning that asking the questions in front of him will "show support" or that his reaction will be informative — this is unsafe and directly violates the screening protocol. The patient will almost certainly deny abuse, and the partner may retaliate after the visit.

- *(Decision 1)* Directly confronting the partner about the bruises or asking him to explain them — this escalates danger for the patient, is outside the nurse's role, and may cause the patient to withdraw from care.

- *(Decision 1)* Documenting the bruises as "consistent with patient's stated mechanism" without pursuing private screening — the pattern of bruises in varying stages of healing on the upper arm and inner forearm is inconsistent with a single accidental bump into a door frame, and the behavioral cues (partner answering for the patient, avoidance of eye contact, long sleeves in warm weather) warrant private assessment.

- *(Decision 2)* Promising the patient absolute confidentiality to encourage further disclosure — this promise cannot be kept (documentation and provider notification are required, and mandatory reporting may be triggered), and breaking it later will severely damage trust.

- *(Decision 2)* Immediately calling law enforcement after the disclosure without the patient's consent — under the clinic's stated state law, IPV against a competent adult is not subject to mandatory reporting to law enforcement. Calling police without consent removes the patient's agency, may escalate danger, and may cause her to disengage from care.

- *(Decision 3)* Deciding independently that because the child has not been directly struck, there is no reportable concern — the determination of whether witnessing domestic violence constitutes neglect is made by the investigative agency, not by the nurse. The nurse's obligation as a mandated reporter is to report the concern, not to investigate or adjudicate it.

- *(Decision 3)* Avoiding the mandatory reporting discussion entirely to preserve the therapeutic relationship — this prioritizes the nurse-patient relationship over the legal duty to protect a minor and may leave the child in an unsafe situation.

- *(Decision 4)* Pressuring the patient to leave the relationship immediately, telling her she is endangering her children by staying, or expressing frustration that she is "choosing" to remain — this is judgmental, clinically counterproductive, and ignores the complex dynamics of IPV including financial control, isolation, trauma bonding, and the increased danger associated with leaving without a plan.

- *(Decision 4)* Accepting the patient's statement that "things will get better after the baby comes" at face value and not providing resources — IPV typically escalates during pregnancy and the postpartum period, not the reverse. The nurse should gently provide this information without being alarmist, and ensure resources are available regardless of the patient's current readiness.

- *(Decision 5)* Telling the patient that the disclosure will not be documented to reassure her — omitting clinically relevant safety information from the medical record compromises continuity of care, prevents other providers from conducting appropriate safety assessments, and may have legal implications if the patient or child is later harmed.

- *(Decision 6)* Responding to the partner's question by looking nervously at the patient, hesitating visibly, or saying something that implies the visit was unusual — nonverbal cues of anxiety or evasion may alert the partner that something significant was discussed, increasing the patient's risk.

- *(Decision 6)* Fabricating a medical complication to explain the length of the visit — this creates a false clinical narrative that may have downstream consequences (unnecessary follow-up, partner using the fabricated diagnosis to restrict the patient's activities) and is dishonest.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Implement a safe, protocol-driven IPV screening process by separating the patient from the accompanying person using routine clinical workflow before initiating screening.
- Respond to an IPV disclosure with therapeutic communication that balances empathy, honesty about confidentiality limits, and respect for patient autonomy.
- Distinguish between the mandatory reporting obligations for IPV against a competent adult and the mandatory reporting obligations triggered when a child is exposed to domestic violence.
- Develop a patient-centered safety plan that respects the patient's stated readiness and provides actionable resources without coercion.
- Protect the patient's safety through appropriate documentation practices, HIPAA-informed confidentiality measures, and safety-preserving communication in the presence of the suspected abuser.
