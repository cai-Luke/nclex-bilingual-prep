**CASE TITLE**
Superior vena cava syndrome secondary to mediastinal malignancy.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 58-year-old man is admitted to a medical-surgical unit from the emergency department with a two-week history of progressive facial swelling and dyspnea on exertion. He has a 40-pack-year smoking history and was diagnosed three months ago with non-small cell lung cancer with a large right mediastinal mass documented on prior imaging. He has not yet started treatment; his oncology appointment for treatment planning is scheduled for next week. He has no history of deep vein thrombosis or heart failure. Current admission orders include IV normal saline at 75 mL/hr, oxygen via nasal cannula to maintain SpO₂ above 92%, head-of-bed elevation to at least 45 degrees at all times, twice-daily weights, strict intake and output, and a consult to oncology. He takes no anticoagulants and has no known drug allergies. A peripheral IV is placed in the right forearm.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient reports that his face has been "puffy" for about two weeks and that he has had increasing difficulty breathing when walking to the bathroom. He states the swelling is worse in the morning and slightly better by evening. Over the past three days he has noticed his shirt collars feel tight and his wedding ring still fits normally. He denies chest pain, hemoptysis, or lower-extremity swelling. He appears mildly anxious and is sitting upright in bed, leaning slightly forward.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
General: alert and oriented, mild respiratory effort at rest, speaking in full sentences. Skin: facial plethora with visible periorbital edema bilaterally; neck appears full with distended bilateral external jugular veins that do not collapse with inspiration or when the head of bed is elevated to 45 degrees. Upper chest shows faint, dilated superficial venous collaterals across the anterior chest wall. No lower-extremity edema. Hands and fingers are non-edematous with capillary refill under two seconds bilaterally. Vital signs: T 37.1 °C, HR 92, BP 138/82 (right arm), RR 20, SpO₂ 94% on 2 L nasal cannula. Lung auscultation: diminished breath sounds in the right upper lobe; no wheezes, crackles, or stridor. Heart sounds: regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs, no pericardial rub. Neurological: no headache reported, pupils equal and reactive, no papilledema on fundoscopic exam, no upper-extremity neurological deficits. Neck circumference measured at the cricoid cartilage: 44 cm.

**LABORATORY DATA**
WBC 11,200/µL, Hgb 13.8 g/dL, platelets 268,000/µL, sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, chloride 101 mEq/L, CO₂ 24 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 118 mg/dL, albumin 3.4 g/dL, INR 1.0, PTT 28 seconds, D-dimer 0.8 µg/mL (mildly elevated). ABG on 2 L nasal cannula: pH 7.42, PaCO₂ 36 mmHg, PaO₂ 72 mmHg, HCO₃ 23 mEq/L, SaO₂ 94%.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — Admission to 6 hours. The patient is settled in bed at 45 degrees. Over the first six hours the nurse notes that the facial edema has not worsened and the patient is resting comfortably, though he becomes mildly dyspneic when he attempts to lie flatter to adjust his pillow. When the head of bed is inadvertently lowered to 20 degrees during a linen change, the patient immediately reports a sensation of "head fullness" and his face visibly reddens further; SpO₂ drops from 94% to 91% within two minutes. Restoring the head of bed to 45 degrees resolves the desaturation over five minutes. Repeat vitals at hour 6: T 37.2 °C, HR 94, BP 140/84, RR 22, SpO₂ 94% at 45 degrees on 2 L nasal cannula. The right forearm IV site is noted to have sluggish flow and mild swelling around the insertion site; the nurse flushes it and it runs but slowly. Urine output over 6 hours: 280 mL. The oncology consult has been placed but the oncologist has not yet seen the patient.

**Stage 2** — 6 to 18 hours after admission. At hour 12 the patient calls the nurse reporting worsening shortness of breath even while sitting upright. He is now speaking in short phrases rather than full sentences. His voice sounds slightly hoarse compared to admission. The facial and periorbital edema has visibly increased; the neck appears more swollen, and the nurse re-measures neck circumference at the cricoid cartilage: 47 cm (up from 44 cm at admission). The superficial chest-wall venous collaterals are more prominent. Both upper extremities now show pitting edema to the mid-forearm bilaterally. The right forearm IV has infiltrated and is discontinued. Vital signs: T 37.3 °C, HR 110, BP 134/86, RR 28, SpO₂ 90% on 2 L nasal cannula. Lung auscultation: diminished breath sounds in the right upper and middle lobes; faint inspiratory stridor is audible over the trachea on auscultation with the bell. No wheezes or crackles. The patient is anxious, gripping the side rails, and repeatedly asking "Why can't I breathe?" ABG on 2 L nasal cannula: pH 7.38, PaCO₂ 42 mmHg, PaO₂ 62 mmHg, HCO₃ 24 mEq/L, SaO₂ 90%. The oncologist has reviewed the chart but has not yet examined the patient. The admitting provider is on the floor and available.

**Stage 3** — 18 to 30 hours after admission. The nurse has escalated to the provider, oxygen has been increased to a high-flow nasal cannula at 40 L/min with FiO₂ 0.50 per order, and the head of bed remains at 45 degrees. The oncologist has now examined the patient and recommended urgent initiation of treatment; the provider has ordered dexamethasone 4 mg IV every 6 hours per oncology recommendation, and the first dose has been administered. Interventional radiology has been consulted for possible SVC stenting. At hour 24, the patient's SpO₂ is 93% on the high-flow nasal cannula. His respiratory rate has decreased to 24. He is again speaking in short-to-moderate-length phrases. The inspiratory stridor, previously faint, is no longer audible on tracheal auscultation. Neck circumference is 46 cm (down from 47 cm at Stage 2, still above the 44 cm baseline). Facial edema remains present but the periorbital component appears slightly less tense. Upper-extremity edema is unchanged. Vital signs: T 37.1 °C, HR 102, BP 130/80, RR 24, SpO₂ 93% on high-flow nasal cannula. Urine output over the preceding 6 hours: 320 mL. The patient reports he feels "a little better" but is still short of breath with any movement. A new peripheral IV has been placed in the left lower extremity after coordination with the team and per facility policy, and it is running well.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At admission and through Stage 1, the nurse reviews the full set of assessment findings — facial plethora, periorbital edema, non-collapsing jugular venous distension, dilated superficial chest-wall venous collaterals, diminished right upper lobe breath sounds, the position-dependent desaturation episode, the sluggish right forearm IV, the normal lower extremities, the normal neurological exam, and the stable temperature. Which of these findings are significant cues consistent with obstruction of venous return through the superior vena cava, and which are not meaningful cues for this clinical picture? *Correct:* The facial plethora, periorbital edema, non-collapsing jugular venous distension, dilated superficial chest-wall venous collaterals, position-dependent worsening of symptoms and desaturation when the head of bed is lowered, the sluggish upper-extremity IV flow, and the diminished right upper lobe breath sounds (consistent with the known right mediastinal mass) are all significant cues of SVC obstruction. The normal lower-extremity exam, the normal neurological findings, and the stable low-grade temperature are not meaningful cues for this syndrome — lower-extremity edema would suggest a systemic fluid-overload or inferior vena cava process rather than SVC obstruction, and the temperature is clinically insignificant. *Why:* SVC syndrome produces venous congestion above the level of obstruction — face, neck, upper extremities, and upper chest — while sparing the lower body. The non-collapsing JVD reflects fixed obstruction rather than volume overload (in volume overload, JVD would decrease with upright positioning). The sluggish upper-extremity IV reflects impaired venous drainage in the territory proximal to the obstruction. Recognizing which findings belong to the SVC-obstruction pattern and which do not is the first step in clinical judgment. *Skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse considers the cluster of findings — upper-body venous congestion sparing the lower extremities, position-dependent worsening, known mediastinal mass, and the mildly elevated D-dimer — and must determine what clinical condition best explains the pattern. The differential includes SVC syndrome from extrinsic compression by the mediastinal mass, acute decompensated heart failure, and upper-extremity deep vein thrombosis. *Correct:* The findings are most consistent with SVC syndrome from extrinsic compression by the known mediastinal malignancy. The distribution of edema (face, neck, upper chest only), the non-collapsing JVD, the visible venous collaterals across the chest wall (representing alternative venous drainage pathways), and the position-dependent worsening when the head of bed is lowered all point to mechanical obstruction of the SVC. Acute decompensated heart failure would produce bilateral lower-extremity edema, pulmonary crackles, and elevated BNP, none of which are present. Isolated upper-extremity DVT could cause unilateral arm swelling and a mildly elevated D-dimer but would not explain the bilateral facial plethora, bilateral JVD, or the chest-wall collaterals. *Why:* Analyzing the distribution and character of the venous congestion — bilateral and above the diaphragm, with collateral formation — localizes the obstruction to the SVC. The known mediastinal mass provides the mechanism. The mildly elevated D-dimer is nonspecific and does not change the dominant hypothesis when the full cue cluster points to extrinsic compression. *Skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient's condition has worsened: neck circumference has increased from 44 cm to 47 cm, he is speaking in short phrases, respiratory rate is 28, SpO₂ has dropped to 90% on 2 L nasal cannula, and faint inspiratory stridor is now audible over the trachea. The nurse must determine which of the worsening findings represents the most immediately life-threatening problem. *Correct:* The new inspiratory stridor is the most immediately life-threatening finding because it signals tracheal compression or narrowing from the expanding venous congestion and edema around the airway. While the worsening facial and neck edema, the increasing dyspnea, and the falling oxygen saturation are all concerning, stridor specifically indicates that the airway lumen is compromising — a partial upper-airway obstruction that can progress to complete obstruction. This is the finding that makes the situation an airway emergency rather than a worsening but manageable respiratory problem. *Why:* Stridor indicates turbulent airflow through a narrowed airway and is an ominous sign in SVC syndrome because the edema compressing the airway may continue to worsen. Airway patency is the highest-priority concern in any clinical framework — it precedes breathing and circulation. The other findings (worsening edema, tachycardia, desaturation) are serious but are consequences of the same process; the stridor is the sentinel that the process has reached the airway. *Skill:* Prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, with the new stridor, worsening dyspnea, and SpO₂ of 90%, the nurse must determine the set of immediate actions to take. Multiple interventions are relevant: increasing supplemental oxygen, maintaining head-of-bed elevation, activating rapid response or the facility emergency escalation process, ensuring emergency airway equipment is at the bedside, establishing non–upper-extremity IV access, and removing the infiltrated right forearm IV. *Correct:* The immediate actions are to increase supplemental oxygen delivery (apply a non-rebreather mask or prepare high-flow nasal cannula per available equipment and standing respiratory distress protocol), confirm the head of bed is at 45 degrees or higher, activate the rapid response team or facility emergency escalation process and notify the provider, emphasizing new inspiratory stridor, worsening hypoxemia, and concern for impending airway compromise, and ensure emergency airway equipment (suction, bag-valve-mask, oxygen delivery escalation, and preparation for difficult-airway support) is at the bedside. These actions address the airway-breathing threat and escalate care simultaneously. Establishing new IV access avoiding the upper extremities is important and should be done promptly because upper-extremity IV access is unreliable in SVC obstruction, but it does not take precedence over the airway and escalation actions. Removing the infiltrated IV is routine wound care and is the lowest priority in this moment. *Why:* The stridor signals a potential airway emergency. Oxygen delivery and positioning are within immediate RN scope and directly support oxygenation and venous drainage. Activating rapid response and urgent provider notification are required because the patient may need emergent intubation, corticosteroids, or procedural intervention that is outside RN scope, and the nurse should anticipate transfer to a higher level of monitoring or care. Having airway equipment at the bedside prepares for rapid deterioration. These actions are essentially concurrent — they do not have a strict sequence among themselves — but they collectively represent the priority response. The non–upper-extremity IV is a necessary next step but is a vascular-access task, not an airway intervention. *Skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse needs to place a new peripheral IV because the right forearm IV has infiltrated and upper-extremity venous return is compromised by the SVC obstruction. The nurse must decide where to place the new IV. *Correct:* Avoid upper-extremity IV placement and obtain non–upper-extremity access per facility policy/provider direction, such as lower-extremity peripheral access or other appropriate access arranged with the team. Venous drainage from the lower extremities returns to the heart via the inferior vena cava, which is not obstructed. An upper-extremity IV in either arm will have impaired flow and a high risk of infiltration or failure because venous return through the SVC is obstructed; medications and fluids infused into an upper-extremity vein may not reach central circulation reliably and may worsen local edema. *Why:* In SVC obstruction, all venous return from the upper body is impaired. Infusions into upper-extremity veins will meet high back-pressure, leading to sluggish flow, extravasation, and unreliable drug delivery — exactly what happened with the right forearm IV. The lower extremities drain through the IVC and are unaffected by the obstruction. This is a direct application of understanding the anatomy of the obstruction to a concrete nursing action, while deferring to facility policy on specific non–upper-extremity sites. *Skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, after oxygen has been increased to high-flow nasal cannula, dexamethasone has been initiated, and the head of bed has been maintained at 45 degrees, the nurse must evaluate whether the patient is improving, stable, or deteriorating. The available data include: SpO₂ 93% on high-flow nasal cannula (up from 90% on 2 L nasal cannula), respiratory rate 24 (down from 28), stridor no longer audible on tracheal auscultation (previously present), neck circumference 46 cm (down from 47 cm but still above the 44 cm baseline), facial edema slightly less tense, the patient speaking in short-to-moderate phrases (previously short phrases only), and the patient reporting he feels "a little better." *Correct:* The patient is showing early improvement after supportive oxygen/positioning and provider-directed treatment, but has not returned to baseline and remains at risk. The resolution of stridor is the most important positive finding because it indicates the airway narrowing is easing. The modest improvements in SpO₂, respiratory rate, and speech pattern support a trend toward stabilization. However, the neck circumference remains above baseline, facial edema persists, and the patient is still dyspneic with movement and requires high-flow oxygen — he is not out of danger. The appropriate evaluation is that the patient is responding to the current interventions but requires continued close monitoring of airway patency (listening for return of stridor), respiratory status (rate, effort, SpO₂), and upper-body edema (neck circumference, facial edema) with a low threshold for re-escalation if any of these worsen. *Why:* Evaluating outcomes in SVC syndrome requires tracking the specific markers of the obstruction — airway patency, respiratory function, and the degree of venous congestion above the obstruction. A single set of improved vitals does not confirm resolution; the underlying obstruction has not been definitively treated yet (initial medical therapies can reduce edema, but definitive relief of the obstruction is still pending). The nurse must recognize partial improvement while maintaining vigilance for re-deterioration. *Skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Identifying the normal lower-extremity exam as a reassuring sign that rules out a serious vascular problem, or treating the low-grade temperature as a significant finding suggesting infection — both misread the cue pattern. SVC syndrome specifically spares the lower body, so normal legs are expected, not reassuring in the way they would be in heart failure. The temperature is clinically insignificant and pursuing it as a fever workup would divert attention from the venous obstruction.

- *(Decision 1)* Dismissing the sluggish upper-extremity IV as a routine access problem unrelated to the clinical picture, rather than recognizing it as a cue consistent with impaired upper-body venous return from SVC obstruction.

- *(Decision 2)* Attributing the bilateral facial and upper-body edema to fluid overload or heart failure and requesting a diuretic — wrong analysis. The IV rate is modest (75 mL/hr), there are no crackles, no lower-extremity edema, and no history of heart failure. The distribution of edema (above the diaphragm only) and the non-collapsing JVD point to mechanical obstruction, not volume overload. Administering a diuretic could reduce intravascular volume without relieving the obstruction and could worsen perfusion.

- *(Decision 2)* Focusing on the mildly elevated D-dimer as evidence of an acute thromboembolic event and prioritizing anticoagulation workup over recognition of the mechanical compression — the D-dimer is nonspecific and mildly elevated in many conditions including malignancy; the full clinical picture overwhelmingly points to extrinsic compression.

- *(Decision 3)* Prioritizing the tachycardia (HR 110) or the falling SpO₂ as the most life-threatening finding over the stridor. While tachycardia and desaturation are concerning, they are compensatory responses and consequences of the worsening obstruction. Stridor is the finding that signals the process has reached the airway — the most immediately lethal trajectory — and must drive the urgency of the response.

- *(Decision 4)* Delaying rapid response activation or provider notification to complete a full set of repeat laboratory draws or to document the assessment in the electronic health record before escalating — documentation and labs are important but must not delay communication about a potential airway emergency. The structured urgent notification and emergency escalation are time-critical.

- *(Decision 4)* Placing the patient flat or in Trendelenburg position to improve blood pressure or facilitate IV access — this would worsen venous congestion above the obstruction, increase intracranial pressure, and further compromise the airway. Head-of-bed elevation must be maintained.

- *(Decision 5)* Placing the replacement IV in the left upper extremity because the right arm IV infiltrated, reasoning that the left arm is unaffected — wrong. SVC obstruction impairs venous return from both upper extremities because both subclavian veins drain into the SVC. A left arm IV would face the same back-pressure and unreliable flow as the right.

- *(Decision 6)* Interpreting the improved SpO₂ of 93% as evidence that the patient is stable and can be downgraded in monitoring intensity — the SpO₂ improvement occurred on significantly higher oxygen support (high-flow nasal cannula at 40 L/min and FiO₂ 0.50 versus 2 L nasal cannula), so it does not represent an equivalent comparison. The patient requires more oxygen to achieve a similar saturation, which means the underlying respiratory compromise persists. Reducing monitoring or oxygen support prematurely could lead to rapid re-deterioration.

- *(Decision 6)* Focusing on urine output as the primary marker of improvement — urine output is relevant to overall perfusion but is not the parameter that tracks the specific threat in SVC syndrome (airway compromise from upper-body edema). The priority monitoring parameters are airway patency, respiratory status, and upper-body edema trends.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

- Recognize the clinical cue cluster of superior vena cava syndrome — facial plethora, upper-body edema sparing the lower extremities, non-collapsing JVD, chest-wall venous collaterals, and position-dependent worsening — and distinguish it from heart failure and thromboembolic disease.
- Identify stridor as the sentinel sign that SVC syndrome has progressed to airway compromise, and prioritize it as the most immediately life-threatening finding.
- Generate the immediate nursing response to worsening SVC syndrome with airway threat: oxygen optimization, positioning, rapid response / emergency escalation, and airway equipment at the bedside.
- Apply understanding of the anatomy of SVC obstruction to practical nursing actions, including selecting a lower-extremity site for IV access when upper-body venous return is compromised.
- Evaluate the patient's response to treatment by tracking the specific markers of SVC obstruction — airway patency, respiratory effort, oxygen requirements in context of delivery method, and upper-body edema trends — rather than relying on a single vital sign in isolation.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 2, when the patient has developed new inspiratory stridor, worsening upper-body edema with neck circumference increasing from 44 cm to 47 cm, SpO₂ falling to 90% on 2 L nasal cannula, and speech reduced to short phrases — the full picture of SVC syndrome with impending airway compromise has declared.

- *Most likely condition:* Superior vena cava syndrome with impending airway compromise secondary to extrinsic compression by the known mediastinal malignancy. The progressive upper-body venous congestion (facial edema, neck swelling, bilateral upper-extremity edema, chest-wall collaterals), the position-dependent worsening, and now the new stridor in a patient with a documented right mediastinal mass constitute an unambiguous presentation.

- *Plausible competing conditions:* (1) Acute decompensated heart failure — wrong here because the edema is confined to the upper body with no lower-extremity edema, no pulmonary crackles, no history of cardiac disease, and the JVD does not collapse with upright positioning as it would with simple volume overload; the distribution is anatomically inconsistent with biventricular failure. (2) Acute upper-extremity deep vein thrombosis with secondary swelling — wrong because the findings are bilateral and symmetric involving the face, neck, and both arms with visible chest-wall collateral formation; unilateral DVT cannot produce this pattern, and the chest-wall collaterals indicate a central obstruction at the SVC level, not a peripheral venous thrombosis.

- *Two priority actions:* (1) Increase supplemental oxygen delivery and confirm the head of bed is maintained at 45 degrees or higher — these are the immediate within-scope interventions that optimize oxygenation and reduce venous pressure above the obstruction, directly addressing the airway and breathing threat. (2) Activate rapid response or facility emergency escalation and notify the provider, reporting the new stridor, worsening respiratory status, and concern for airway compromise — this escalates care to enable advanced airway support and provider-level interventions (corticosteroids, possible emergent intubation, interventional radiology consultation) that are outside RN scope but time-critical.

- *Two wrong actions:* (1) Lowering the head of bed to facilitate a procedure or assessment — this would increase venous pressure above the obstruction, worsen facial and airway edema, and could precipitate complete airway obstruction; the position-dependent desaturation episode in Stage 1 already demonstrated this risk in this patient. (2) Attempting to place a new IV in the opposite upper extremity before addressing the airway and escalating — this delays the time-critical response for a vascular-access task, and the replacement IV would fail in the same way because both upper extremities have impaired venous return through the obstructed SVC.

- *Two parameters to monitor:* (1) Airway patency assessed by auscultation for stridor over the trachea and observation of voice quality and speech pattern — stridor is the direct indicator of airway narrowing from peritracheal edema, and its presence, worsening, or resolution is the most critical marker of whether the patient is progressing toward or away from complete airway obstruction. (2) Upper-body edema trend assessed by serial neck circumference measurement and visual assessment of facial and periorbital edema — the degree of venous congestion above the obstruction tracks the severity of the SVC compression and correlates with the risk of further airway compromise; a rising neck circumference signals worsening obstruction, while a decreasing measurement suggests response to treatment.

- *Two irrelevant parameters:* (1) Lower-extremity edema assessment — the inferior vena cava is not involved in this patient's obstruction, so lower-extremity edema would not develop from SVC syndrome and monitoring it does not track the severity or trajectory of this condition; it is the wrong anatomic territory. (2) D-dimer trending — the mildly elevated D-dimer is a nonspecific finding common in malignancy and does not track the mechanical obstruction of the SVC or the airway threat; serial D-dimer values would not inform whether the patient is improving or deteriorating from the SVC compression.
