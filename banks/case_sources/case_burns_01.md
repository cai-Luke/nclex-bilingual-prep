**CASE TITLE**
Major thermal burn with inhalation injury requiring fluid resuscitation and airway management.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 35-year-old man with no significant past medical history is brought to the emergency department by ambulance after being rescued from a residential house fire. Paramedics report he was found in a smoke-filled bedroom, conscious but confused, approximately 40 minutes before ED arrival. He has no known allergies and takes no medications. His estimated weight is 80 kg. The fire department estimates the time of burn injury at 0600. The patient arrives at the ED at 0640.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient is awake but anxious and speaking in a hoarse, raspy voice. He reports throat tightness and difficulty swallowing. He has visible burns across his face, anterior chest, abdomen, and both anterior arms. His eyebrows and nasal hairs are singed. Carbonaceous sputum is noted when he coughs. He is breathing rapidly and appears to be in significant distress.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
Temperature 36.8 °C, heart rate 125 beats per minute, blood pressure 90/60 mmHg, respiratory rate 28 breaths per minute, SpO₂ 93% on room air. Weight estimated at 80 kg.

Head and face: diffuse facial erythema and edema with blistering across the forehead, cheeks, and chin. Singed eyebrows and nasal hairs bilaterally. Lips are edematous. Oropharyngeal inspection reveals erythematous and mildly edematous posterior pharynx with soot deposits.

Voice: markedly hoarse, with audible stridor on forced inspiration.

Chest and abdomen: deep partial-thickness burns with moist, pink-to-red wound beds, intact sensation to pinprick, and large fluid-filled blisters across the entire anterior trunk from the clavicles to the inguinal creases. Bilateral anterior arms from shoulders to wrists show similar deep partial-thickness burns with blistering and weeping. The posterior trunk, posterior arms, bilateral legs, and perineum are unburned. Lung auscultation reveals bilateral expiratory wheezes with coarse rhonchi in the upper lobes; air entry is present but diminished bilaterally.

Abdomen: soft, non-distended, bowel sounds present.

Extremities: radial pulses palpable bilaterally but thready. Capillary refill 4 seconds in the fingertips of both hands. No circumferential burns to the extremities; the burns on the arms are limited to the anterior surfaces. No burns to the legs.

Neurological: alert, oriented to person and place but not to time. Pupils equal and reactive. Moves all extremities spontaneously.

A Foley catheter is placed on arrival. Initial urine output is 20 mL of dark amber urine collected during the 40-minute transport period.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Baseline labs drawn at 0645:

Complete blood count: WBC 14,200/µL, hemoglobin 16.8 g/dL, hematocrit 52%, platelets 210,000/µL.

Basic metabolic panel: sodium 139 mEq/L, potassium 5.1 mEq/L, chloride 102 mEq/L, bicarbonate 20 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, glucose 168 mg/dL.

Arterial blood gas on room air: pH 7.32, PaCO₂ 32 mmHg, PaO₂ 68 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 20 mEq/L, lactate 4.2 mmol/L.

Carboxyhemoglobin (COHb): 18%.

Urinalysis: dark amber, specific gravity 1.035, positive for blood on dipstick, no red blood cells on microscopy.

Coagulation: PT 13.2 seconds, INR 1.1, aPTT 28 seconds.

Type and screen: sent.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1 — 0640 to 0730 (first 90 minutes in the ED; 30 to 120 minutes post-burn)**

The patient is placed on 100% oxygen via non-rebreather mask. Two large-bore (16-gauge) peripheral IVs are established in the dorsal veins of both feet, as the bilateral arm burns preclude upper-extremity access. Lactated Ringer's solution is initiated. The burn team and anesthesia are paged.

The provider performs a rapid burn assessment using the Rule of Nines to estimate total body surface area (TBSA) involvement. The burned regions are documented as follows: the entire face and anterior scalp, the entire anterior trunk, and both anterior arms from shoulders to wrists. The posterior trunk, posterior arms, legs, and perineum are confirmed unburned.

Over the next 30 minutes, the patient's hoarseness worsens. He begins to drool and reports increasing difficulty breathing. Inspiratory stridor becomes audible without a stethoscope. SpO₂ on the non-rebreather mask is 94%. Repeat vitals at 0720: HR 130, BP 88/58, RR 32. The facial and lip edema is visibly progressing.

The provider orders emergent endotracheal intubation for impending airway obstruction. The nurse prepares rapid-sequence intubation equipment and medications. Intubation is performed successfully at 0725 with a 7.5-mm endotracheal tube; placement is confirmed by end-tidal CO₂ and bilateral breath sounds. The patient is placed on mechanical ventilation with FiO₂ 1.0.

The provider writes the fluid resuscitation order: per the Parkland formula, administer lactated Ringer's solution calculated as 4 mL × patient weight in kg × percent TBSA burned. Half of the total 24-hour volume is to be infused in the first 8 hours from the time of burn (0600), and the remaining half over the subsequent 16 hours. The order specifies that the hourly urine output target is 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr (40 to 80 mL/hr for this patient), and the infusion rate should be titrated to maintain urine output within this range.

Urine output for the first hour after Foley placement (0640–0740): 15 mL of dark amber urine.

**Stage 2 — 0730 to 1400 (hours 1.5 through 8 post-burn)**

The patient is intubated and sedated. The fluid resuscitation is running. The nurse is titrating the lactated Ringer's infusion rate based on hourly urine output.

At 0800 (2 hours post-burn), urine output is 25 mL/hr. The urine remains dark amber. The nurse increases the infusion rate per the titration protocol.

At 1000 (4 hours post-burn), urine output has improved to 35 mL/hr. The urine color has lightened to dark yellow. Vitals: HR 118, BP 96/64, RR set at 16 on the ventilator, SpO₂ 99% on FiO₂ 1.0. Repeat ABG: pH 7.35, PaCO₂ 36 mmHg, PaO₂ 210 mmHg, lactate 3.0 mmol/L. COHb has decreased to 8%.

At 1200 (6 hours post-burn), urine output is 45 mL/hr. Urine is now yellow. Vitals: HR 110, BP 102/68. Repeat labs: potassium 4.6 mEq/L, creatinine 1.0 mg/dL, hematocrit 46%, lactate 2.1 mmol/L. The elevated hematocrit has begun to trend down from the admission value of 52%, consistent with intravascular volume repletion.

The burn surgery team completes a formal wound assessment. They confirm deep partial-thickness burns to the entire anterior trunk (18% TBSA), bilateral anterior arms from shoulders to wrists (4.5% each arm, 9% total), and the entire face and anterior scalp (4.5% TBSA). Total estimated TBSA: 31.5%. No full-thickness burns are identified. The burns on the anterior arms are not circumferential; the posterior arms are unburned. The anterior trunk burns extend from clavicle to inguinal crease but do not wrap around the flanks to the posterior trunk. No circumferential burns are present on any body region.

At 1400 (8 hours post-burn), the first half of the calculated 24-hour resuscitation volume should have been delivered. Urine output is 50 mL/hr. Vitals: HR 105, BP 108/70. The nurse prepares to transition to the second-half infusion rate for the remaining 16 hours.

**Stage 3 — 1400 to 2200 (hours 8 through 16 post-burn)**

The infusion rate is adjusted for the second phase of resuscitation. Urine output is monitored hourly.

At 1600 (10 hours post-burn), urine output is 55 mL/hr. Urine is clear yellow. Vitals: HR 100, BP 112/72. The patient is hemodynamically improving.

At 1800 (12 hours post-burn), the nurse notes that urine output has increased to 120 mL/hr for two consecutive hours. Vitals: HR 92, BP 118/76. The patient's face and eyelids are markedly more edematous than at admission, which is expected during the resuscitation phase, but the extremities are also becoming edematous. The nurse auscultates the lungs and hears new bilateral basilar crackles that were not present earlier. SpO₂ remains 99% on the ventilator. Repeat labs: sodium 134 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, hematocrit 38%, albumin 2.1 g/dL.

At 2000 (14 hours post-burn), urine output remains elevated at 110 mL/hr. The bilateral basilar crackles persist. Peak airway pressures on the ventilator have increased from 22 cmH₂O to 30 cmH₂O. Abdominal girth has increased by 4 cm from the 1400 measurement. The nurse notes that the patient's hands, which were previously unburned and had palpable radial pulses with 4-second capillary refill, now have tense edema with capillary refill of 5 seconds. Radial pulses remain palpable but are weaker.

The burn team is notified of the findings. They review the total volume infused and note that the actual volume delivered has exceeded the Parkland-calculated volume by approximately 20%, likely from the aggressive early titration needed to achieve adequate urine output during the first 4 hours. The team discusses the risk of fluid creep — excessive resuscitation beyond the calculated volume leading to complications including pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome, and extremity compartment syndrome.

At 2200 (16 hours post-burn), after the infusion rate has been reduced, urine output is 70 mL/hr. The basilar crackles are unchanged. Peak airway pressures are 28 cmH₂O. Abdominal girth is stable. Capillary refill in the hands is 4 seconds, and radial pulses are palpable. Bladder pressure, measured per protocol via the Foley catheter transducer, is 18 mmHg (normal < 12 mmHg; intra-abdominal hypertension is defined as sustained pressure ≥ 12 mmHg; abdominal compartment syndrome is sustained pressure ≥ 20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction).

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At the initial presentation, the nurse reviews the patient's burn distribution and must calculate the total body surface area burned using the Rule of Nines. The burned areas are the entire face and anterior scalp, the entire anterior trunk, and both anterior arms from shoulders to wrists. The posterior trunk, posterior arms, legs, and perineum are unburned. *Correct action:* The TBSA is calculated as follows — face and anterior scalp: 4.5%; anterior trunk: 18%; right anterior arm: 4.5%; left anterior arm: 4.5%. Total: 31.5% TBSA. *Why it is correct:* The Rule of Nines assigns 9% to the entire head (4.5% anterior, 4.5% posterior), 18% to the anterior trunk, 18% to the posterior trunk, 9% to each entire arm (4.5% anterior, 4.5% posterior), 18% to each entire leg, and 1% to the perineum. Only the anterior surfaces of the arms are burned, so each arm contributes 4.5%, not 9%. The posterior trunk and legs are unburned and contribute 0%. Accurate TBSA calculation is essential because it directly determines the fluid resuscitation volume. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation and assessment findings.

2. *Situation:* At the initial presentation and into Stage 1, the nurse must identify which cluster of findings indicates the need for emergent intubation rather than continued management with a non-rebreather mask. The patient has singed nasal hairs, carbonaceous sputum, facial burns with progressive edema, a hoarse voice that is worsening, audible inspiratory stridor, oropharyngeal soot and erythema, and a history of enclosed-space smoke exposure. SpO₂ is 94% on the non-rebreather. *Correct action:* The combination of progressive hoarseness, audible stridor, worsening facial and lip edema, carbonaceous sputum, and enclosed-space exposure constitutes impending upper-airway obstruction from inhalation injury. The nurse should anticipate and prepare for emergent endotracheal intubation. Waiting for further SpO₂ decline or respiratory failure is unsafe because upper-airway edema in burn patients can progress rapidly to complete obstruction, making delayed intubation technically impossible. *Why it is correct:* Hoarseness and stridor in a burn patient indicate laryngeal and supraglottic edema. These are late signs of progressive airway compromise. Once the airway becomes fully edematous, oral intubation may fail, and a surgical airway may be required. Early intubation while the airway is still patent is the standard of care. SpO₂ may remain near-normal until obstruction is nearly complete because the patient is hyperventilating and on supplemental oxygen — a normal SpO₂ does not rule out impending obstruction. The COHb of 18% also means the pulse oximeter is overestimating true oxygen saturation, since standard pulse oximetry cannot distinguish carboxyhemoglobin from oxyhemoglobin. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues and prioritize hypotheses. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation and Stage 1 data.

3. *Situation:* After the TBSA has been determined at 31.5% and the patient's weight is 80 kg, the nurse must calculate the Parkland formula fluid resuscitation volume and determine the correct infusion rate for the first 8 hours. The provider order specifies: administer lactated Ringer's solution calculated as 4 mL × weight in kg × percent TBSA burned; half of the total in the first 8 hours from the time of burn (0600), and the remaining half over the next 16 hours. *Correct action:* Total 24-hour volume = 4 mL × 80 kg × 31.5% = 10,080 mL of lactated Ringer's. Half (5,040 mL) is to be infused in the first 8 hours from the time of burn (0600 to 1400). The remaining 5,040 mL is infused over the next 16 hours (1400 to 0600 the following day). The first-half rate is 5,040 mL ÷ 8 hours = 630 mL/hr as a starting rate. However, because the patient arrived at 0640 — already 40 minutes into the 8-hour window — the nurse must account for the time already elapsed. By 0640, approximately 40 minutes of the first 8-hour window have passed. The remaining time in the first half is 7 hours and 20 minutes (from 0640 to 1400). The volume to deliver in that remaining time is 5,040 mL minus whatever was infused during transport (which was minimal — the paramedics did not have IV access established with LR running at the calculated rate). Practically, the starting infusion rate will need to be higher than 630 mL/hr to deliver the first-half volume by 1400, and the rate is then titrated hourly to the urine output target of 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr (40 to 80 mL/hr). *Why it is correct:* The Parkland formula is the standard initial estimate for crystalloid resuscitation in major burns. The critical concept is that the 8-hour clock starts at the time of burn injury, not at the time of hospital arrival. Failing to anchor the calculation to the time of burn results in under-resuscitation during the period of greatest capillary leak (the first 8 hours), when fluid requirements are highest. The formula provides a starting estimate; actual delivery is titrated to urine output. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Initial presentation (weight, TBSA) and Stage 1 (provider order with formula and time-of-burn anchor).

4. *Situation:* During Stage 2, the nurse is titrating the lactated Ringer's infusion rate hourly. At 0800 (2 hours post-burn), urine output is 25 mL/hr — below the target of 40 to 80 mL/hr. At 1000, urine output has improved to 35 mL/hr but remains below target. The urine has lightened from dark amber to dark yellow. The nurse must decide the appropriate action. *Correct action:* The nurse should increase the infusion rate because urine output remains below the minimum target of 40 mL/hr. The urine color change from dark amber to dark yellow and the clearing of the dipstick-positive blood (which indicated possible myoglobinuria) are positive trends, but the hourly volume is still inadequate. The nurse should continue to titrate upward in measured increments and reassess hourly. The nurse should also notify the provider if urine output does not reach the target range within the next hour despite rate increases, as persistently inadequate output despite aggressive fluid resuscitation may indicate the need for additional interventions. *Why it is correct:* In burn resuscitation, urine output is the most reliable real-time indicator of end-organ perfusion and adequacy of fluid replacement. The target of 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr reflects adequate renal perfusion. Below this target, the patient is under-resuscitated and at risk for acute kidney injury, worsening shock, and organ failure. The dark amber urine with positive blood on dipstick but no RBCs on microscopy at baseline was consistent with myoglobinuria from muscle injury, which increases the risk of renal tubular damage and makes adequate urine output even more critical. The improving urine color suggests the myoglobin is being cleared, but the volume must reach target. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 2 data.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3 (1800, 12 hours post-burn), the nurse notes urine output has risen to 120 mL/hr for two consecutive hours — well above the target of 40 to 80 mL/hr. New bilateral basilar crackles are present. Extremity edema is developing in previously unburned hands. Peak airway pressures are rising. Abdominal girth has increased. The hematocrit has dropped from 52% to 38%, and albumin is 2.1 g/dL. The nurse must identify the emerging complication and determine the priority nursing action. *Correct action:* The findings are consistent with fluid creep — excessive crystalloid resuscitation beyond the Parkland-calculated volume, resulting in third-spacing, pulmonary edema, and risk of abdominal compartment syndrome and extremity compartment syndrome. The priority nursing actions are to reduce the infusion rate (the urine output of 120 mL/hr far exceeds the 40–80 mL/hr target, indicating over-resuscitation at the current rate), notify the burn team of the new pulmonary crackles, rising airway pressures, increasing abdominal girth, and extremity edema, and initiate serial monitoring of bladder pressure (intra-abdominal pressure), compartment assessments of the hands (capillary refill, pulses, sensation), and ventilator parameters. *Why it is correct:* Fluid creep is a recognized complication of burn resuscitation in which the actual volume infused exceeds the Parkland estimate, often because aggressive early titration to achieve urine output targets results in cumulative over-resuscitation. The consequences include pulmonary edema (crackles, rising airway pressures), abdominal compartment syndrome (increasing girth, elevated bladder pressure), and extremity compartment syndrome in unburned limbs (edema, compromised perfusion). The urine output of 120 mL/hr is a cue that the infusion rate is now too high — the resuscitation has overshot. The dropping hematocrit from 52% to 38% reflects hemodilution from the large crystalloid volume, and the low albumin reflects both dilution and capillary leak of protein. The nurse must titrate down, not continue at the current rate, and must monitor for compartment complications. The bladder pressure of 18 mmHg at 2200 confirms intra-abdominal hypertension (≥ 12 mmHg) but has not yet reached the threshold for abdominal compartment syndrome (≥ 20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction), so continued monitoring and volume reduction are appropriate rather than emergent surgical decompression. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3 data.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

Decision point 1: Counting each entire arm as 9% rather than recognizing that only the anterior surfaces are burned (4.5% per arm). This error would yield a TBSA of 40.5% instead of 31.5%, resulting in a Parkland calculation of 12,960 mL instead of 10,080 mL — a 28% overestimate that would contribute to fluid creep and its complications.

Decision point 1: Including the posterior trunk or legs in the TBSA calculation despite the assessment clearly documenting these areas as unburned. Inaccurate TBSA estimation in either direction — over or under — directly harms the patient through over- or under-resuscitation.

Decision point 2: Relying on the SpO₂ of 94% on the non-rebreather mask as reassurance that the airway is stable and intubation can be deferred. In the presence of hoarseness, stridor, and progressive facial edema, SpO₂ is a lagging indicator of airway compromise. Additionally, with a COHb of 18%, the pulse oximeter is falsely elevated because it reads carboxyhemoglobin as oxyhemoglobin. Waiting for SpO₂ to drop further risks complete airway obstruction.

Decision point 2: Treating the SpO₂ of 93–94% as the primary problem and focusing on oxygen delivery (increasing flow rate, repositioning the mask) rather than recognizing the airway obstruction pattern. The issue is not oxygenation failure alone — it is impending mechanical obstruction of the upper airway, which supplemental oxygen cannot overcome.

Decision point 3: Calculating the Parkland formula correctly but starting the 8-hour clock at the time of ED arrival (0640) rather than the time of burn (0600). This error delays the delivery of the first-half volume by 40 minutes, resulting in under-resuscitation during the critical early period of maximum capillary permeability. The first half of the volume must be delivered by 1400 (8 hours after the 0600 burn), not by 1440.

Decision point 3: Using normal saline instead of lactated Ringer's for the resuscitation. The Parkland formula specifies lactated Ringer's solution. Large-volume normal saline resuscitation causes hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, which would worsen the patient's already-low bicarbonate of 20 mEq/L and pH of 7.32.

Decision point 4: Interpreting the dark amber urine with positive blood on dipstick as hematuria and focusing on a genitourinary injury workup rather than recognizing the pattern of myoglobinuria (positive dipstick blood with no RBCs on microscopy). This misinterpretation could delay the recognition that aggressive fluid resuscitation is needed to prevent myoglobin-induced renal tubular injury.

Decision point 4: Seeing the urine output improve from 15 mL/hr to 35 mL/hr and interpreting this as adequate because it is trending upward. The trend is positive, but 35 mL/hr remains below the minimum target of 40 mL/hr. Titration must continue until the target range is achieved, not merely until the trend is favorable.

Decision point 5: Interpreting the high urine output of 120 mL/hr as a sign that resuscitation is successful and the patient is improving, rather than recognizing it as evidence of over-resuscitation. In the context of new crackles, rising airway pressures, increasing abdominal girth, and extremity edema, excessive urine output signals that the infusion rate has overshot the target and must be reduced.

Decision point 5: Attributing the bilateral basilar crackles and rising airway pressures to worsening inhalation injury rather than to pulmonary edema from fluid overload. While inhalation injury can cause lower-airway inflammation, the timing (12 hours into aggressive crystalloid resuscitation), the concurrent peripheral edema in unburned tissue, the dropping hematocrit, and the urine output far above target all point to fluid creep as the cause. Misattributing the crackles to inhalation injury could lead to continued aggressive fluid administration, worsening the overload.

Decision point 5: Failing to monitor bladder pressure (intra-abdominal pressure) when abdominal girth is increasing during large-volume resuscitation. Abdominal compartment syndrome is a life-threatening complication of fluid creep in burn resuscitation, and serial bladder pressure measurement is the standard monitoring method. A nurse who monitors only urine output and vitals without assessing intra-abdominal pressure may miss the progression from intra-abdominal hypertension to compartment syndrome.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**

Accurately calculate total body surface area burned using the Rule of Nines, distinguishing anterior from posterior surface involvement and applying the correct percentage to each body region.

Identify the clinical indicators of impending upper-airway obstruction from inhalation injury — hoarseness, stridor, progressive facial edema, carbonaceous sputum, and enclosed-space exposure — and recognize that early intubation is required before complete obstruction occurs, regardless of current SpO₂.

Apply the Parkland formula to calculate the initial crystalloid resuscitation volume, correctly anchoring the 8-hour first-half window to the time of burn injury rather than the time of hospital arrival, and titrate the infusion rate to the hourly urine output target.

Evaluate the adequacy of burn fluid resuscitation using hourly urine output as the primary end point, recognizing both under-resuscitation (output below target with dark concentrated urine) and over-resuscitation (output far above target with signs of fluid overload).

Recognize the clinical manifestations of fluid creep — pulmonary edema, rising airway pressures, increasing abdominal girth, extremity edema in unburned tissue, and excessive urine output — and respond by reducing the infusion rate and initiating monitoring for abdominal and extremity compartment syndrome.

**BOW-TIE SYNTHESIS**
Synthesis moment: Stage 3, at 1800 (12 hours post-burn), after the resuscitation has achieved hemodynamic improvement but new findings of pulmonary crackles, rising airway pressures, increasing abdominal girth, extremity edema, and urine output of 120 mL/hr have emerged.

Most likely condition: Fluid creep — iatrogenic over-resuscitation with crystalloid beyond the Parkland-calculated volume, resulting in third-spacing, pulmonary edema, and intra-abdominal hypertension. The total volume infused has exceeded the calculated Parkland volume by approximately 20%. The urine output of 120 mL/hr far exceeds the 40–80 mL/hr target. The hematocrit drop from 52% to 38% reflects hemodilution. The new bilateral crackles, rising peak airway pressures, increasing abdominal girth, and edema in previously unburned hands all confirm excessive interstitial fluid accumulation from over-resuscitation.

Plausible competing conditions: (1) Worsening inhalation injury with lower-airway inflammation — wrong here because the timing and pattern do not fit isolated inhalation injury progression. The crackles are bilateral and basilar (consistent with fluid overload), not diffuse or associated with worsening bronchospasm. The concurrent peripheral edema in unburned tissue, the dropping hematocrit, and the excessive urine output all point to volume overload rather than primary pulmonary parenchymal injury. The patient's oxygenation on the ventilator remains adequate. (2) Sepsis or systemic inflammatory response from wound infection — wrong here because it is far too early (12 hours post-burn) for burn wound infection to manifest. The patient has no fever, the WBC elevation is consistent with the stress response to major burns, and the hemodynamic trajectory is improving (HR decreasing, BP increasing), which is the opposite of septic deterioration. The findings are explained entirely by the volume status.

Two priority actions: (1) Reduce the lactated Ringer's infusion rate — the urine output of 120 mL/hr is 50% above the upper target of 80 mL/hr, and continuing the current rate will worsen pulmonary edema, increase intra-abdominal pressure, and risk compartment syndrome. The rate should be titrated downward to bring urine output back into the 40–80 mL/hr range. (2) Notify the burn team and initiate serial bladder pressure monitoring — the increasing abdominal girth and the bladder pressure of 18 mmHg (confirmed at 2200) indicate intra-abdominal hypertension, and the team must be alerted to assess for progression toward abdominal compartment syndrome and to evaluate whether the extremity edema is compromising perfusion in the hands.

Two wrong actions: (1) Maintaining or increasing the current infusion rate because the Parkland formula calls for continued resuscitation over 24 hours — wrong because the Parkland volume is a starting estimate, not a fixed prescription. The formula explicitly requires titration to urine output. Urine output of 120 mL/hr signals that the current rate is excessive, and continuing it will worsen the fluid overload complications that are already manifesting. Blind adherence to the calculated rate when the clinical end point shows over-resuscitation is a dangerous error. (2) Attributing the crackles to inhalation injury and requesting an increase in ventilator FiO₂ or PEEP without addressing the volume overload — wrong because the crackles are from pulmonary edema secondary to fluid creep, not from primary lung injury. Increasing ventilator support without reducing the fluid input treats the symptom while perpetuating the cause, and the rising airway pressures will continue to worsen.

Two parameters to monitor: (1) Hourly urine output — this is the primary titration end point for burn resuscitation. A return to the 40–80 mL/hr target range after rate reduction confirms that the resuscitation is being appropriately recalibrated and that renal perfusion remains adequate without ongoing over-resuscitation. (2) Bladder pressure (intra-abdominal pressure) — serial measurement directly tracks whether the intra-abdominal hypertension (18 mmHg) is stable, improving with volume reduction, or progressing toward the abdominal compartment syndrome threshold of 20 mmHg or greater with organ dysfunction, which would require emergent intervention.

Two irrelevant parameters: (1) Carboxyhemoglobin level — the COHb has already decreased from 18% to 8% with high-flow oxygen therapy and is trending toward normal. It is no longer the active clinical problem at this synthesis moment; the crisis is fluid overload, not carbon monoxide toxicity. Tracking COHb does not evaluate whether the fluid creep is resolving. (2) Serum potassium — the potassium has normalized from 5.1 to 4.2 mEq/L with resuscitation and is not the parameter that tracks the trajectory of the current complication. While potassium monitoring remains part of routine care, it does not evaluate the pulmonary edema, the intra-abdominal hypertension, or the adequacy of the volume titration, which are the active concerns at this moment.

---REVIEWER-CURRENCY-NOTES---

Claim: The Parkland formula uses 4 mL × kg × %TBSA with lactated Ringer's, half in the first 8 hours from time of burn.
Where it appears: Key Decision Point 3 and Stage 1 provider order.
Reviewer check to run: Confirm that the Parkland (Baxter) formula remains the standard initial crystalloid resuscitation estimate taught in current burn care guidelines (ABA Practice Guidelines, ATLS 10th edition). Some centers have moved toward modified formulas or colloid-inclusive protocols; verify that 4 mL/kg/%TBSA with LR remains the standard NCLEX-level expectation.

Claim: Intra-abdominal hypertension is defined as sustained bladder pressure ≥ 12 mmHg; abdominal compartment syndrome is ≥ 20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction.
Where it appears: Stage 3 clinical course and Key Decision Point 5.
Reviewer check to run: Confirm these thresholds against the current World Society of the Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (WSACS) consensus definitions. The 12 mmHg and 20 mmHg thresholds were established in the 2013 WSACS guidelines; verify they have not been revised.

Claim: Standard pulse oximetry cannot distinguish carboxyhemoglobin from oxyhemoglobin, leading to falsely elevated SpO₂ readings in CO exposure.
Where it appears: Key Decision Point 2 rationale.
Reviewer check to run: This is a well-established physiologic principle, but verify whether newer-generation pulse oximeters with multi-wavelength CO-oximetry capability (e.g., Masimo Rad-57) have become standard enough in EDs that the blanket statement needs qualification. The case does not specify the oximeter type, so the claim should hold for conventional two-wavelength devices.
