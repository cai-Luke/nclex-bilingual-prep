**CASE TITLE**
Refeeding syndrome risk in a newly admitted patient with severe anorexia nervosa.

**PATIENT BACKGROUND**
A 22-year-old woman is admitted to a medical unit from the emergency department with a diagnosis of severe anorexia nervosa, restricting type. She has a three-year history of progressive caloric restriction and excessive exercise. Her outpatient psychiatrist referred her to the ED after a syncopal episode at home and a reported intake of fewer than 400 kcal/day for the past several weeks. Her BMI on admission is 14.8 kg/m². She has no prior inpatient admissions for her eating disorder and no history of purging behaviors. She takes no medications and has no known drug allergies. Her last menstrual period was approximately nine months ago.

Admission orders include continuous telemetry monitoring, strict intake and output, daily weights obtained in a hospital gown after voiding and before breakfast, a supervised refeeding protocol starting at 1,200 kcal/day divided into three meals and three snacks with a registered dietitian consult, IV normal saline at 75 mL/hr for the first 12 hours, thiamine 200 mg IV daily for the first three days then oral supplementation, a multivitamin with minerals daily, and electrolyte panels every 12 hours for the first 72 hours. The provider's refeeding protocol note specifies: "If phosphorus falls below 2.5 mg/dL or potassium below 3.5 mEq/L, hold caloric advancement and notify provider immediately. Phosphorus replacement per pharmacy protocol: potassium phosphate IV for levels below 2.0 mg/dL, oral sodium phosphate for levels 2.0–2.5 mg/dL." A one-to-one sitter is ordered for meal supervision and for one hour after each meal. Activity is restricted to the unit; no unsupervised ambulation.

**INITIAL PRESENTATION**
The patient is alert and cooperative but visibly emaciated. She states she feels "fine" and that the syncopal episode was because she "stood up too fast." She is reluctant to discuss her eating habits in detail and asks when she can go home. She expresses concern about the caloric content of the refeeding plan, stating it is "way too much food." She is oriented to person, place, time, and situation. She denies suicidal ideation. She is wearing multiple layers of clothing and requests extra blankets.

**ASSESSMENT FINDINGS**
T 35.8 °C (96.4 °F), HR 52, BP 88/54, RR 14, SpO₂ 99% on room air. Weight 39.8 kg, height 164 cm. She appears cachectic with prominent bony landmarks, temporal wasting, dry and flaking skin, fine lanugo hair on her forearms and face, and brittle nails. Oral mucosa is dry. Capillary refill 4 seconds in fingers. Extremities are cool and mottled to mid-forearm bilaterally. Peripheral pulses are palpable but weak. No peripheral edema. Heart sounds are regular but bradycardic; no murmur. Lungs are clear bilaterally. Abdomen is scaphoid, soft, non-tender, with hypoactive bowel sounds. She has mild proximal muscle weakness — difficulty rising from a seated position without using her arms. Neurologically intact; cranial nerves II–XII grossly normal. Telemetry shows sinus bradycardia at 52 bpm, normal QTc 420 ms, no ST changes.

**LABORATORY DATA**
Sodium 137 mEq/L, potassium 3.6 mEq/L, chloride 100 mEq/L, CO₂ 26 mEq/L, BUN 18 mg/dL, creatinine 0.9 mg/dL, glucose 62 mg/dL, calcium 8.8 mg/dL, magnesium 1.9 mg/dL, phosphorus 3.1 mg/dL, albumin 3.0 g/dL, prealbumin 11 mg/dL (reference 20–40), AST 48 U/L, ALT 52 U/L, WBC 3,200/µL, Hgb 10.8 g/dL, Hct 32%, platelets 130,000/µL, TSH 2.4 mIU/L. Urinalysis: specific gravity 1.028, no protein, no glucose, no ketones. ECG: sinus bradycardia, QTc 420 ms, no ectopy.

**CLINICAL COURSE**

**Stage 1** — 18 hours after admission, refeeding has begun. The patient ate approximately 60% of her first three meals under sitter supervision. She was observed tearing food into very small pieces and rearranging it on the plate. She attempted to stand and pace in her room between meals; the sitter redirected her to bed rest per orders. She reports bloating, mild nausea, and abdominal cramping after eating. Vitals: T 36.0 °C, HR 58, BP 90/58, RR 16, SpO₂ 99%. Telemetry: sinus bradycardia, QTc 430 ms. Urine output over the past 12 hours is 350 mL. Repeat labs at 12 hours: potassium 3.4 mEq/L, phosphorus 2.4 mg/dL, magnesium 1.7 mg/dL, glucose 78 mg/dL, calcium 8.6 mg/dL. The nurse notes the phosphorus has dropped from 3.1 to 2.4 mg/dL and the potassium from 3.6 to 3.4 mEq/L since admission.

**Stage 2** — 36 hours after admission. Phosphorus replacement was initiated per protocol (oral sodium phosphate for the level of 2.4 mg/dL). Caloric advancement was held per the refeeding protocol. The patient is now tearful and states, "I knew this would happen — the food is making me sick. I want to stop eating." She reports worsening bloating and constipation. She has not had a bowel movement since admission. She is observed hiding food in her napkin during lunch; the sitter intervenes. Vitals: T 36.2 °C, HR 64, BP 92/60, RR 18, SpO₂ 98%. Telemetry now shows occasional premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), 3–5 per minute, and the QTc has prolonged to 480 ms. Repeat labs at 36 hours: potassium 3.1 mEq/L, phosphorus 2.1 mg/dL, magnesium 1.4 mg/dL, glucose 88 mg/dL, calcium 8.2 mg/dL. Urine output over the preceding 12 hours is 600 mL. The patient's weight this morning is 40.6 kg — a gain of 0.8 kg in less than 36 hours.

**Stage 3** — 60 hours after admission. After aggressive IV electrolyte replacement (potassium phosphate IV per pharmacy protocol for phosphorus below 2.0 mg/dL, IV magnesium sulfate 2 g, and IV potassium chloride 40 mEq), the provider was notified of the QTc prolongation and PVCs and ordered the replacements stat. Caloric intake remains held at the initial 1,200 kcal/day without advancement. The patient is calmer after a visit from the psychiatric liaison team, who initiated supportive counseling and discussed the medical necessity of refeeding. She is tolerating meals with less resistance, eating approximately 75% of offered food. She reports the bloating is "a little better." Vitals: T 36.4 °C, HR 68, BP 96/62, RR 16, SpO₂ 99%. Telemetry: sinus rhythm, rate 68, QTc 440 ms, PVCs resolved. Repeat labs at 60 hours: potassium 3.8 mEq/L, phosphorus 2.8 mg/dL, magnesium 1.8 mg/dL, glucose 92 mg/dL, calcium 8.7 mg/dL. Weight is 40.9 kg. Urine output over the preceding 12 hours is 550 mL. Peripheral edema is now noted — mild, 1+ pitting to the ankles bilaterally. Lungs remain clear. The provider is considering a cautious caloric advancement to 1,400 kcal/day beginning tomorrow if electrolytes remain stable on the next draw.

**KEY DECISION POINTS**

1. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the nurse reviews the 12-hour repeat labs and sees that phosphorus has dropped from 3.1 to 2.4 mg/dL and potassium from 3.6 to 3.4 mEq/L. The nurse must determine the significance of these changes and the required nursing actions per the admission refeeding protocol. *Correct action:* Recognize that the phosphorus of 2.4 mg/dL has crossed the protocol threshold of 2.5 mg/dL, triggering two required actions: hold caloric advancement and notify the provider immediately. Initiate oral sodium phosphate replacement per the pharmacy protocol (the level is between 2.0 and 2.5 mg/dL). The potassium of 3.4 mEq/L has also crossed the 3.5 mEq/L threshold, requiring provider notification. *Why it is correct:* Refeeding drives extracellular phosphorus and potassium into cells as insulin secretion rises with carbohydrate intake. Hypophosphatemia is the hallmark electrolyte derangement of refeeding syndrome and can cause respiratory failure, cardiac dysfunction, rhabdomyolysis, and death if not caught early. The protocol thresholds exist to trigger intervention before the decline becomes dangerous. Applying the stated protocol — not waiting for the next scheduled draw or for symptoms — is the safe action. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Recognize cues, take action. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

2. *Situation:* At Stage 1, the patient reports bloating, nausea, and abdominal cramping after eating. She is visibly distressed and asks the nurse to reduce her meal portions. The nurse must determine whether these GI symptoms warrant modifying the refeeding plan or whether they are expected and manageable. *Correct action:* Acknowledge the patient's discomfort, provide reassurance that mild GI symptoms are expected during early refeeding after prolonged starvation, and implement comfort measures (positioning, small sips of warm fluid between meals if permitted, reporting to the provider if symptoms escalate to vomiting or severe pain). Do not independently reduce caloric intake or skip meals — the refeeding prescription is a medical order. Document the symptoms and communicate them to the provider and dietitian for evaluation. *Why it is correct:* Prolonged starvation causes gastric atrophy, slowed motility, and reduced digestive enzyme production. Mild bloating, early satiety, nausea, and cramping are expected during the first days of refeeding and do not, by themselves, indicate a complication requiring caloric reduction. Reducing intake without a provider order undermines the treatment plan and may reinforce the patient's illness-driven desire to restrict. However, severe or escalating GI symptoms (distension, vomiting, absent bowel sounds) would require urgent provider notification because of the risk of superior mesenteric artery syndrome or gastric dilation. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues. *Answerable from:* Stage 1.

3. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the nurse reviews the telemetry and sees new PVCs at 3–5 per minute and a QTc that has prolonged from 420 ms at baseline to 480 ms. Simultaneously, the labs show potassium 3.1 mEq/L, phosphorus 2.1 mg/dL, and magnesium 1.4 mg/dL — all worsening despite oral phosphorus replacement. The nurse must prioritize the most urgent action. *Correct action:* Notify the provider immediately of the cardiac rhythm changes (new PVCs, QTc prolongation to 480 ms) in the context of worsening hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, and hypomagnesemia. Anticipate stat IV electrolyte replacement orders — particularly IV potassium and IV magnesium, because hypomagnesemia makes hypokalemia refractory to potassium replacement alone, and both contribute to QTc prolongation and arrhythmia risk. The phosphorus of 2.1 mg/dL now falls below 2.0 mg/dL rounding threshold and warrants IV potassium phosphate per the pharmacy protocol. *Why it is correct:* QTc prolongation beyond 470 ms in a female patient with active electrolyte depletion represents a significant risk for torsades de pointes. The combination of hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypophosphatemia is synergistically dangerous for cardiac conduction. Magnesium must be repleted concurrently because it is a cofactor for the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump; without adequate magnesium, potassium replacement will be ineffective. The oral phosphorus replacement was insufficient, indicating the need to escalate to IV replacement. Provider notification with the full clinical picture — rhythm change plus the three declining electrolytes — is the priority over any single replacement action. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Prioritize hypotheses, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

4. *Situation:* At Stage 2, the patient is tearful, states the food is making her sick, and asks to stop eating. She has also been observed hiding food. The nurse must respond therapeutically while maintaining the treatment plan. *Correct action:* Use a therapeutic, non-judgmental communication approach: validate the patient's emotional distress ("I can see this is very difficult for you") without agreeing that the food is causing harm. Gently and factually explain that the medical team is monitoring her closely and adjusting the plan to keep her safe. Do not agree to stop the refeeding. Do not use controlling or punitive language. Document the food-hiding behavior objectively and communicate it to the treatment team (provider, dietitian, psychiatric liaison). Ensure the sitter continues meal supervision per protocol. *Why it is correct:* Anorexia nervosa involves intense fear of weight gain and distorted body perception. The patient's request to stop eating is driven by the illness, not by a medical indication to discontinue nutrition. Agreeing to stop refeeding would be medically dangerous in a patient with a BMI of 14.8 and active refeeding electrolyte shifts. However, a confrontational or dismissive response damages the therapeutic alliance and increases resistance. The food-hiding behavior is clinically significant — it means actual caloric intake is lower than recorded, which affects the accuracy of the refeeding plan and electrolyte monitoring. The psychiatric liaison team is the appropriate resource for ongoing psychological support. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Take action (therapeutic communication and safety maintenance). *Answerable from:* Stage 2.

5. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the patient's electrolytes have improved, the QTc has shortened to 440 ms, and PVCs have resolved. However, the nurse notes new bilateral ankle edema and a cumulative weight gain of 1.1 kg over 60 hours. The nurse must determine whether these findings represent a complication requiring intervention or an expected phenomenon of early refeeding. *Correct action:* Recognize that mild peripheral edema and modest weight gain during early refeeding are expected refeeding edema — caused by insulin-mediated sodium and water retention as carbohydrate intake resumes — and do not, in the absence of pulmonary congestion, indicate heart failure or fluid overload requiring diuretics. Assess lung sounds (which are clear), check for jugular venous distension, and monitor respiratory status. Document the edema and weight trend and report to the provider. Do not administer or request diuretics. Continue monitoring intake and output and daily weights. *Why it is correct:* Refeeding edema is a well-recognized, usually self-limiting phenomenon in malnourished patients resuming nutrition. Insulin secretion increases renal sodium reabsorption, and low albumin (3.0 g/dL in this patient) reduces oncotic pressure, both contributing to fluid shifting into the interstitial space. Diuretic use in this context is dangerous: it would worsen the already tenuous electrolyte balance (further depleting potassium, magnesium, and phosphorus) and could cause intravascular volume depletion in a patient who is already hypotensive. The key distinction is between peripheral edema with clear lungs (expected, monitor) and edema with pulmonary crackles, tachypnea, or rising oxygen requirements (fluid overload or cardiac decompensation, requiring urgent intervention). *Clinical-judgment skill:* Evaluate outcomes. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

6. *Situation:* At Stage 3, the provider is considering advancing calories from 1,200 to 1,400 kcal/day. The nurse must determine whether the patient currently meets the protocol criteria for safe caloric advancement. *Correct action:* Evaluate the current electrolyte values against the protocol thresholds: phosphorus is 2.8 mg/dL (above the 2.5 mg/dL hold threshold), potassium is 3.8 mEq/L (above 3.5 mEq/L), and the QTc has improved. The protocol criteria for advancement — electrolytes above threshold on the next draw — have not yet been confirmed because the provider specified "if electrolytes remain stable on the next draw." The nurse should communicate to the provider that the current values meet the thresholds but that the protocol condition requires one more confirmatory set before advancement. This is appropriate clarification, not refusal of an order. *Why it is correct:* Caloric advancement in refeeding must be cautious and stepwise. The patient just experienced a significant electrolyte nadir requiring IV replacement and cardiac rhythm changes. A single improved set of labs does not confirm stability — the protocol wisely requires a confirmatory draw. The nurse's role is to apply the stated protocol accurately and advocate for the patient's safety by ensuring the advancement criteria are fully met before increasing caloric load. *Clinical-judgment skill:* Analyze cues, generate solutions. *Answerable from:* Stage 3.

**COMMON NURSING ERRORS**

- *(Decision 1)* Noting the phosphorus drop but waiting for the next scheduled lab draw or for clinical symptoms before acting — the protocol threshold has been crossed, and early intervention prevents progression to dangerous hypophosphatemia. Waiting for symptoms (muscle weakness, respiratory compromise) means the window for safe early correction has been missed.

- *(Decision 1)* Recognizing the low phosphorus but overlooking the potassium of 3.4 mEq/L because it is "only slightly low" — in the context of active refeeding with a downward trend, a potassium that has crossed the protocol threshold of 3.5 mEq/L requires the same notification. Electrolyte shifts during refeeding can accelerate rapidly.

- *(Decision 2)* Independently reducing meal portions or allowing the patient to skip a meal to relieve GI discomfort — this modifies a medical order without authorization and reinforces the patient's restriction behavior. Mild GI symptoms during early refeeding are expected and manageable; the nurse should report them but not unilaterally change the nutrition prescription.

- *(Decision 3)* Replacing potassium alone without addressing the magnesium of 1.4 mg/dL — hypomagnesemia renders potassium replacement ineffective because magnesium is required for normal function of the renal potassium-conserving channels. The potassium will continue to waste renally until magnesium is corrected. Failing to communicate the full electrolyte picture (all three low values plus the rhythm change) to the provider risks an incomplete replacement order.

- *(Decision 3)* Treating the PVCs as benign or attributing the QTc prolongation to the baseline bradycardia rather than to the electrolyte derangements — in a patient with active hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and hypophosphatemia, new PVCs and QTc prolongation are ominous signs of myocardial electrical instability, not incidental findings.

- *(Decision 4)* Responding to the patient's distress by agreeing that the food is harmful and advocating to the provider to stop refeeding — this aligns with the patient's illness-driven cognition rather than with medical safety. Alternatively, responding with a rigid or punitive tone ("You have to eat or we'll place a feeding tube") damages the therapeutic relationship and increases the patient's anxiety and resistance without addressing the underlying fear.

- *(Decision 5)* Interpreting the ankle edema and weight gain as fluid overload and requesting or administering diuretics — diuretics would exacerbate the electrolyte depletion that has been the central danger throughout this admission. Refeeding edema with clear lungs is managed with monitoring, elevation, and reassurance, not diuresis.

- *(Decision 5)* Attributing the weight gain to "too many calories" and recommending caloric reduction — the weight gain is fluid redistribution, not adipose tissue accumulation. Reducing calories based on this misinterpretation would slow nutritional rehabilitation without addressing the edema.

- *(Decision 6)* Advancing calories without verifying that the confirmatory lab draw has been completed and meets the protocol criteria — premature advancement after a significant electrolyte nadir risks triggering another dangerous decline. The nurse's role includes ensuring protocol criteria are fully satisfied before implementing the next step.

**EXPECTED LEARNING OBJECTIVES**
- Apply a refeeding protocol by recognizing electrolyte threshold crossings and initiating the correct protocol-driven actions (hold advancement, notify provider, initiate replacement).
- Differentiate expected early refeeding symptoms (mild GI discomfort, refeeding edema) from complications requiring urgent intervention (cardiac rhythm changes with electrolyte derangements).
- Prioritize the clinical significance of concurrent electrolyte deficiencies — particularly the relationship between magnesium depletion and refractory hypokalemia — in the context of cardiac monitoring changes.
- Demonstrate therapeutic communication with a patient whose illness drives resistance to the treatment plan, maintaining both the therapeutic alliance and adherence to medical orders.
- Evaluate readiness for caloric advancement by applying stated protocol criteria to current clinical and laboratory data.
